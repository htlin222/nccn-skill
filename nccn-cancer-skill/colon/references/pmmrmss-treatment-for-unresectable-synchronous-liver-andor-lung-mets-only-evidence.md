# pMMR/MSS: Treatment for Unresectable Synchronous Liver and/or Lung Metastases Only (COL-7) -- Evidence Summary

<!-- Source: NCCN Colon Cancer v2.2026, page 15 -->

## Clinical Presentation

Patients with pMMR/MSS colon cancer with unresectable synchronous liver and/or lung metastases only [p.15].

## Treatment Strategy

### Assessment for Conversion to Resectable Disease [p.15]

The primary approach includes:

- **Re-evaluate for conversion to resectable disease every 2 months if conversion to resectability is a reasonable goal** [p.15]

### Systemic Therapy Options [p.15]

Patients should receive **systemic therapy (COL-D)** with the following options:

- **FOLFIRI or FOLFOX or CAPEOX or FOLFIRINOX ± bevacizumab**, OR [p.15]
- **FOLFIRI or FOLFOX ± cetuximab or panitumumab (KRAS/NRAS/BRAF WT and left-sided tumors only)** [p.15]

### Surgical Considerations [p.15]

- **Consider colon resection only if imminent risk of obstruction, significant bleeding, perforation, or other significant tumor-related symptoms** [p.15]

## Treatment Outcomes

### Converted to Resectable Disease [p.15]

For patients who achieve conversion to resectable disease:

- **Synchronized or staged resection (preferred) and/or local therapy of colon and metastatic cancer** [p.15]

### Remains Unresectable [p.15]

For patients who remain unresectable:

- **Systemic therapy ± biologic therapy (Category 2B for biologic therapy) (COL-D)**, OR [p.15]
- **Consider observation or shortened course of chemotherapy** [p.15]

## Adjuvant Treatment [p.15]

Following treatment of resected metastatic disease (up to 6 months perioperative treatment):

- **Same systemic therapy options as initial treatment, based on response and tolerability** [p.15]

## Surveillance [p.15]

After completion of treatment, patients proceed to **Surveillance (COL-8)** [p.15].

## Special Considerations

### Bevacizumab Timing [p.15]

- There should be at least a 6-week interval between the last dose of bevacizumab and elective surgery [p.15]
- Re-initiation of bevacizumab should occur at least 6-8 weeks postoperatively [p.15]
- There is an increased risk of stroke and other arterial events, especially in those aged ≥65 years [p.15]
- Bevacizumab may interfere with wound healing [p.15]

### Anti-EGFR Antibody Use [p.15]

- There are conflicting data regarding the use of FOLFOX + cetuximab in patients with potentially resectable liver metastases [p.15]
- **Cetuximab or panitumumab should only be used for left-sided tumors** [p.15]
- The Panel defines the left side of the colon as splenic flexure to rectum [p.15]
- Evidence suggests that tumors originating on the right side of the colon (hepatic flexure through cecum) are unlikely to respond to cetuximab and panitumumab [p.15]
- Data on the response to cetuximab and panitumumab in primary tumors originating in the transverse colon (hepatic flexure to splenic flexure) are lacking [p.15]
- Patients with BRAF mutations other than V600E may be considered for anti-EGFR therapy [p.15]

### Biologic Therapy [p.15]

Biologic therapy is only appropriate for continuation of favorable response from conversion therapy [p.15].

### Local Therapy Preferences [p.15]

**For liver metastases:** Resection is preferred over locally ablative procedures [p.15]. For small liver lesions (≤3 cm), thermal ablation is equivalent to resection [p.15]. Other local techniques can be considered for liver oligometastases when thermal ablation is not indicated [p.15].

**For lung metastases:** Surgery, thermal ablation, and SBRT can be considered [p.15].

### Hepatic Artery Infusion [p.15]

Hepatic artery infusion ± systemic chemotherapy (VEGF inhibitor contraindicated) **(Category 2B)** is also an option at institutions with experience in both the surgical and medical oncologic aspects of this procedure [p.15].

---

*All recommendations are category 2A unless otherwise indicated* [p.15]
