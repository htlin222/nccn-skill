## Summary of Key Evidence

Primary appendiceal neoplasms are rare and most often found incidentally during an appendectomy following clinical presentation of acute appendicitis [p.84]. Management and treatment of primary appendiceal cancers are dependent on classification, grading, and staging of these neoplasms [p.84].

## Pathologic and Molecular Classification

Appendiceal neoplasms can be histopathologically classified as [p.84]:
- Neuroendocrine neoplasms (NENs)
- Mucinous neoplasms
- Goblet cell adenocarcinomas (GCAs)
- Colonic-type adenocarcinomas (non-mucinous)
- Signet ring cell carcinomas (epithelial origin)

Mucinous neoplasms can be further subclassified into [p.84]:
- Low-grade appendiceal mucinous neoplasms (LAMNs)
- High-grade appendiceal mucinous neoplasms (HAMNs)
- Mucinous adenocarcinomas with or without signet ring cells

Both colonic-type adenocarcinoma and colorectal adenocarcinoma develop from precancerous adenomas and are managed and treated similarly [p.84]. Goblet cell carcinomas (GCAs) are primarily composed of epithelial and neuroendocrine elements and characteristically contain goblet cells [p.84].

## Appendiceal Adenocarcinomas

### Mucinous Neoplasm Classification

Histopathologic features of LAMN present as circumferential proliferation of low-grade mucinous epithelium with a pushing pattern into the lamina propria, submucosa, muscularis propria, and into the subserosa [p.84]. LAMN can also present with fibrosis of the submucosa, pushing patterns resembling diverticulum, and mucin and/or cells from the neoplasm outside of the appendix [p.85].

HAMNs have similar features to LAMN but have more extensive and complex atypical cytologic features [p.85]. Comparably, both LAMN- and HAMN-classified tumors lack infiltrative invasion and are both maintained within the appendix [p.85].

### Molecular Characteristics

Data suggest that appendiceal mucinous neoplasms (AMNs) develop from the same mutational sequence as colorectal carcinomas beginning with a point mutation in KRAS [p.85]. Genetic mutations in GNAS and RNF43 are found in both LAMN and HAMN, which further supports the theory that HAMN tumors progress from LAMN tumors [p.85].

In contrast, HAMN tumors have been shown to harbor additional mutations in TP53, ATM, and APC, which may be linked to their more aggressive phenotype [p.85].

### Mucinous Adenocarcinoma

Mucinous adenocarcinomas resemble LAMNs and HAMNs but with the presence of infiltrative invasion (instead of the signature pushing pattern seen in the mucinous neoplasms) [p.85].

Mucinous adenocarcinomas can be further subclassified based on the presence of signet ring cells [p.85]:
- ≤50% signet ring cells: Classified as mucinous adenocarcinoma with signet ring cells [p.85]
- >50% signet ring cells: Classified as signet ring cell carcinoma [p.85]

The cells that compose the mucinous adenocarcinomas produce an excess of extra- and intracellular mucin; the intracellular mucin displaces the cell's nuclei resulting in its characteristic ring-like appearance [p.85].

#### Signet Ring Cell Prognosis

The presence of signet ring cells leads to a poorer prognosis: 10% to 40% of patients with G3 (poorly differentiated) mucinous adenocarcinoma with signet ring cells have a 5-year OS [p.85].

### AJCC Staging Terminology

The 8th edition of the AJCC Staging Manual now uses the following terminology to further characterize appendiceal neoplasms [p.85]:
- G1: Well differentiated
- G2: Moderately differentiated
- G3: Poorly differentiated

### Molecular Subtypes and Clinical Outcomes

A recent molecular analysis was performed on patients diagnosed with mucinous appendiceal adenocarcinomas (MAAs). Out of 164 MAAs tested, 24 were predominantly RAS-mutated with GNAS and TP53 wild-type [p.85].

RAS-mutant predominant tumors:
- Significantly fewer mutations and chromosomal alterations compared to GNAS or TP53 mutation predominance [p.85]
- Improved OS when compared to GNAS-mutant (P = .05) and TP53-mutant (P = .04) tumors [p.85]
- Reduced tumor bulk (P = .04) and stromal invasion (P < .01) [p.85]
- Responded more to first-line chemotherapy (50%) compared to GNAS-mutant predominant (6%, P = .03) tumors [p.85]

Three molecular subtypes identified [p.85]:
1. **RAS-mutant/GNAS-wild-type/TP53-wild-type**: Typically clinically indolent [p.85]
2. **GNAS-mutant predominance**: Shows chemotherapy resistance [p.85]
3. **TP53-mutant predominance**: Highly aneuploid and aggressive [p.85]

This clinical behavior was observed regardless of histopathology [p.85].

## Goblet Cell and Neuroendocrine Carcinomas of the Appendix

### Appendiceal Goblet Cell Carcinomas

Appendiceal goblet cell carcinomas (GCCs) account for approximately 14% to 19% of primary appendiceal neoplasms and consist of both glandular epithelial cells and neuroendocrine components [p.85]. GCCs are considered mixed adenoneuroendocrine carcinomas (MANECs) and display the immunohistochemistry staining consistent with neuroendocrine markers but behave more aggressively like an adenocarcinoma [p.85].

For this reason, it is recommended to clinically treat GCCs as an adenocarcinoma [p.85].

### Appendiceal Neuroendocrine Carcinomas

Appendiceal neuroendocrine carcinomas (ANCs) can develop in the jejunum/ileum, appendix, or cecum [p.85]. They are composed of enterochromaffin-like cells (ECLs) in the bowel wall and can often produce serotonin [p.85].

They are usually indolent tumors when found in the appendix or the rectum but progress more aggressively when found in the colon [p.85]. ANCs are usually asymptomatic and found incidentally if tumor development occurs at the tip of the appendix, but can cause symptomatic obstruction and appendicitis if found in the mid or proximal portions [p.85].

Progression and OS of the patient is dependent on the histologic subtype of the appendiceal neoplasm [p.85].

## Clinical Presentation

### Presenting Symptoms

Patients typically present with symptoms resembling appendicitis, which include but are not limited to [p.86]:
- Abdominal pain in the right lower quadrant
- Vomiting
- Change in bowel habits
- Intestinal obstruction
- Compression of the ureters
- Nausea

### High-Risk Clinical Features

There should be increased suspicion of an appendiceal neoplasm if the patient is [p.86]:
- >50 years of age with a family history of inflammatory bowel disease, colon cancer, and/or unexplained anemia

Additionally, if appendicitis is treated nonoperatively (typically with antibiotics), repeat interval imaging is crucial to ensure that the imaging findings resolve [p.86]. Lack of resolution may suggest an appendiceal malignancy [p.86].

### Diagnostic Approach

Diagnosing neoplasms of the appendix is challenging and symptoms may overlap with colon cancer, gynecologic cancers, or varying abdominal pathologies [p.86]. Diagnostic tests to be considered at initial presentation include [p.86]:
- Imaging studies such as CT/MRI
- Endoscopy
- Tumor biopsy
- In some cases surgery
- Thorough medical history and physical examination

An appendix >15 mm on a CT or MRI with an irregular or thickened wall is suggestive of appendiceal carcinoma [p.86]. Non-specific abdominal bloating/distention and/or postprandial discomfort may also be observed with mucinous peritoneal involvement [p.86].

### Colonoscopy Recommendation

Colonoscopy is recommended if the patient has been diagnosed with mucinous adenocarcinoma of the appendix as there is an increased risk of colonic polyps and neoplasia [p.86].

### Tumor Biomarkers

Tumor biomarkers CEA and CA 19-9 can be evaluated and used as prognostic indicators for patients receiving cytoreductive surgery and hyperthermic intraperitoneal chemotherapy (CRS/HIPEC) [p.86].

In a recent study, a multivariate analysis was performed to establish predictors of disease progression using tumor biomarkers [p.86]:
- When CA 19-9 was elevated preoperatively: Unfavorable PFS [p.86]
- When CEA was elevated preoperatively: Unfavorable OS [p.86]
- CA-125 level: Inconclusive in this study; however, other studies suggest normal serum levels of CA-125 and CA 19-9 correspond to an increase in survival and a decrease in recurrence [p.86]

Tumor differentiation and classification were the strongest predictors of both PFS and OS [p.86].

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Localized Appendiceal Adenocarcinoma -- Workup and Management

A screening colonoscopy is recommended in all patients diagnosed with appendiceal adenocarcinoma (AA) to rule out synchronous large polyps or cancer [p.86]. CT of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis is also recommended for evaluation of the primary tumor and any possibility of metastatic disease [p.86].

#### Early-Stage Disease Management

Tis- and T1-staged tumors may be managed with appendectomy alone if negative margins are obtained during resection and there is no evidence of angiolymphatic invasion [p.86].

T1 and T2 tumors with unfavorable characteristics such as angiolymphatic invasion or positive margins should be considered for a right hemicolectomy and removal of ≥12 lymph nodes for accurate resection and staging [p.86].

#### Adjuvant Chemotherapy for Stage II and III Disease

Extrapolating from CRC, patients with stage III (nodal involvement) or stage II colonic-type AA should be considered for adjuvant systemic chemotherapy [p.86].

A univariate and multivariate analysis was performed on patients diagnosed with stage II and stage III GCC who received surgical resection and adjuvant chemotherapy to evaluate the impact of adjuvant chemotherapy on OS [p.86]:
- Out of 619 patients, adjuvant chemotherapy was administered in 9.4% (N = 48) of stage II and 47.7% (N = 51) of stage III individuals [p.86]
- Stage II with adjuvant chemotherapy: 96.9% 5-year OS [p.86]
- Stage II without adjuvant chemotherapy: 89.1% 5-year OS (P = .236) [p.86]
- Stage III with adjuvant chemotherapy: 77.1% 5-year OS [p.86]
- Stage III without adjuvant chemotherapy: 42.8% 5-year OS (P = .003) [p.86]

This study concluded that administration of adjuvant chemotherapy was associated with better OS in patients with stage III GCC [p.86].

#### Disease Monitoring

In certain patients who are candidates for disease monitoring, surveillance imaging should occur at least annually and may occur more frequently in patients with acellular mucinous spread noted during surgery [p.87].

If evidence of appendiceal rupture and dissemination of tumor cells were found outside of the appendix, imaging may not be a reliable source for accurate disease monitoring [p.87]. If there is clinical suspicion of active disease (e.g., symptoms, elevated tumor markers) despite unremarkable imaging, laparoscopy should be considered [p.87].

### Metastatic Appendiceal Adenocarcinoma -- Workup and Management

The heterogeneity of the appendiceal neoplasms causes a varying risk of metastasis across histologic tumor types [p.87]. Distant metastasis in colonic-type adenocarcinomas has been reported in 23% to 37% of cases with the most common site being dissemination to the peritoneum, with metastasis to the liver and lung being less common [p.87].

The pathologic M stage distinguishes between [p.87]:
- M1a: Intraperitoneal acellular mucin only
- M1b: Intraperitoneal mucinous epithelium
- M1c: Non-intraperitoneal metastasis

If metastasis spreads beyond the peritoneum, then treatment should follow the NCCN Guidelines for Colon Cancer recommendations for metastatic disease [p.87].

#### Peritoneal Metastasis and Mucinous Carcinoma

Appendiceal neoplasms (mucinous and non-mucinous) tend to metastasize within the peritoneal cavity through various ways of dissemination [p.87]. Metastasis of a mucinous adenocarcinoma can arise from the excess secretion of mucin from neoplastic epithelial cells [p.87]. This results in appendiceal rupture and spread of mucin and tumor cells within the peritoneal cavity leading to neoplastic epithelial cells adhering to the peritoneal surface and causing varying sized lesions [p.87].

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### Systemic Chemotherapy for Metastatic Disease

Cytotoxic chemotherapy with efficacy against CRC is used to treat appendiceal adenocarcinoma (AA) [p.87]. It is reasonable to use other targeted therapy options for MSI-high (MSI-H), BRAF mutation, or HER2 status, which is consistent with recommendations within the NCCN Guidelines for Colon Cancer and NCCN Guidelines for Rectal Cancer [p.87].

There is no clear evidence for anti-epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) therapy even if the patient's tumor is RAS/RAF wild-type [p.87].

#### Adjuvant Chemotherapy and Complete Cytoreduction

A retrospective medical record review of patients with appendiceal adenocarcinoma was conducted to test for differentials in OS when adjuvant chemotherapy followed complete cytoreduction [p.87].

In total, 103 patients with AA were enrolled in the study, and 68 patients (66%) achieved a cytoreductive score of 0–1 [p.87]:
- 26 of 68 patients received adjuvant chemotherapy [p.87]
- Median OS with adjuvant chemotherapy: 9.03 years [p.87]
- Median OS without adjuvant chemotherapy: 2.88 years [p.87]
- P = .02 [p.87]

This increase in OS was only observed in patients who did not have low-grade AA [p.87]. This study suggests that adjuvant chemotherapy does not have a benefit in patients with LAMN but does show an increase of OS in patients with other histologic tumor types [p.87].
