## Literature Search Methodology

### Search Parameters

Literature in the field of CRC was identified from the PubMed database, published since the previous Guidelines update, using the following search terms: colon cancer, colorectal cancer, and rectal cancer [p.78]. The PubMed database was chosen as it remains the most widely used resource for medical literature and indexes peer-reviewed biomedical literature [p.78].

### Selection Criteria

The search results were narrowed by selecting studies in humans published in English [p.78]. Results were confined to the following article types [p.78]:
- Clinical Trial, Phase II
- Clinical Trial, Phase III
- Clinical Trial, Phase IV
- Guideline
- Practice Guideline
- Randomized Controlled Trial
- Meta-Analysis
- Systematic Reviews
- Validation Studies

### Evidence Inclusion

The data from key PubMed articles as well as articles from additional sources deemed as relevant to these Guidelines as discussed by the Panel during the Guidelines update have been included in this version of the Discussion section [p.78]. Recommendations for which high-level evidence is lacking are based on the Panel's review of lower-level evidence and expert opinion [p.78].

## Language Standards

### Goals and Principles

NCCN Guidelines strive to use language that advances the goals of equity, inclusion, and representation [p.78]. NCCN Guidelines endeavor to use language that is [p.78]:
- Person-first
- Not stigmatizing
- Anti-racist, anti-classist, anti-misogynist, anti-ageist, anti-ableist, and anti-weight-biased
- Inclusive of individuals of all sexual orientations and gender identities

### Organ-Specific and Non-Gendered Language

NCCN Guidelines incorporate non-gendered language, instead focusing on organ-specific recommendations [p.78]. This language is both more accurate and more inclusive and can help fully address the needs of individuals of all sexual orientations and gender identities [p.78].

### Use of Gender and Sex Terms

NCCN Guidelines will continue to use the terms men, women, female, and male when citing statistics, recommendations, or data from organizations or sources that do not use inclusive terms [p.78]. Most studies do not report how sex and gender data are collected and use these terms interchangeably or inconsistently [p.78].

If sources do not differentiate gender from sex assigned at birth or organs present, the information is presumed to predominantly represent cisgender individuals [p.78].

### Future Research Direction

NCCN encourages researchers to collect more specific data in future studies and organizations to use more inclusive and accurate language in their future analyses [p.78].

## Risk Assessment Overview

### Familial Risk

Approximately 20% of colon cancer cases are associated with familial clustering, and first-degree relatives of patients with colorectal adenomas or invasive CRC are at increased risk for CRC [p.78].

Genetic susceptibility to CRC includes well-defined inherited syndromes, such as [p.78]:
- Lynch syndrome (also known as hereditary nonpolyposis CRC [HNPCC])
- Familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP)

### Recommendations

It is recommended that all patients with colon cancer be queried regarding their family history and considered for risk assessment, as detailed in the NCCN Guidelines for Colorectal Cancer Screening [p.78].

### Screening Intervals

Results from a randomized controlled trial (RCT) suggest that most individuals without a personal history of CRC and with one first-degree relative with CRC diagnosed before age 50 years or two first-degree relatives with CRC diagnosed at any age can safely be screened with colonoscopy every 6 years [p.78].

## CRC Molecular Classification

### Overview of CMS Classification

CRC is a heterogeneous disease [p.78]. An international consortium recently reported a molecular classification, defining four different subtypes [p.78]:

#### CMS1 (Microsatellite Instability Immune)
- Hypermutated, microsatellite unstable
- Strong immune activation
- --> See Lynch Syndrome and Microsatellite Instability [p.78]

#### CMS2 (Canonical)
- Epithelial, chromosomally unstable
- Marked WNT and MYC signaling activation [p.78]

#### CMS3 (Metabolic)
- Epithelial
- Evident metabolic dysregulation [p.78]

#### CMS4 (Mesenchymal)
- Prominent transforming growth factor β activation
- Stromal invasion
- Angiogenesis [p.78]

### Clinical Application

This classification is not yet recommended in clinical practice [p.78].
