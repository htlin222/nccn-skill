## Summary of Key Evidence

Nonmetastatic colon cancer management involves comprehensive workup and surgical resection with appropriate adjuvant chemotherapy based on disease stage and prognostic factors [p.88-97].

## Workup and Management of the Malignant Polyp

### Definition and Classification

A malignant polyp is defined as one with cancer invading the submucosa (pT1) [p.88]. Conversely, polyps classified as carcinoma in situ (pTis) have not penetrated the submucosa and are therefore not considered capable of regional nodal metastasis [p.88].

### Initial Management

The Panel recommends marking the polyp site during colonoscopy or within 2 weeks of the polypectomy, if appropriate [p.88]. Testing for MMR/MSI should be done during the initial workup to help with diagnosis of Lynch syndrome and inform treatment decision-making [p.88].

### Endoscopic Resection Decision-Making

Before making a decision about surgical resection for an endoscopically resected adenomatous polyp or adenoma, physicians should review the pathology and consult with the patient [p.88].

In patients with invasive cancer in a pedunculated or sessile polyp (adenoma), no additional surgery is required if the polyp has been completely resected and has favorable histologic features [p.88].

### Favorable Histologic Features

Favorable histologic features include [p.88]:
- Lesions of grade 1 or 2
- No angiolymphatic invasion
- Negative resection margin

### Sessile Polyp Consideration

In addition to the option of observation, the Panel includes the option of colectomy in patients with a completely removed, single-specimen, sessile polyp with favorable histologic features and clear margins [p.89]. This option is included because the literature indicates that patients with sessile polyps may have a significantly greater incidence of adverse outcomes, including disease recurrence, mortality, and hematogenous metastasis compared with those with pedunculated polyps [p.89]. This increased incidence likely occurs because of the high probability of a positive margin after endoscopic removal [p.89].

### Fragmented Specimens and Unfavorable Pathology

If the polyp specimen is fragmented, the margins cannot be assessed [p.89]. If the specimen shows unfavorable histopathology, additional workup including [p.89]:
- Complete blood count (CBC)
- Chemistry profile
- Carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA) determination
- Chest/abdomen/pelvis CT
- Consideration of pelvic MRI

This workup should be performed to better assess for local staging and extent of disease [p.89]. If appropriate following workup, colectomy with en bloc removal of lymph nodes is recommended [p.89]. Laparoscopic surgery is an option [p.89].

### Unfavorable Histopathologic Features

Unfavorable histopathologic features for malignant polyps include [p.89]:
- Grade 3 or 4
- Angiolymphatic invasion
- Positive margin of resection

### Definition of Positive Margin

No consensus currently exists as to the definition of what constitutes a positive margin of resection [p.89]. A positive margin has been defined as [p.89]:
- The presence of tumor within 1 to 2 mm of the transected margin
- The presence of tumor cells within the diathermy of the transected margin

### Tumor Budding as Adverse Feature

Several studies have shown that tumor budding is an adverse histologic feature associated with adverse outcome and may preclude polypectomy as an adequate treatment of endoscopically removed malignant polyps [p.89].

### Surveillance After Polypectomy

All patients who have malignant polyps removed by transanal excision or transabdominal resection should undergo total colonoscopy to rule out other synchronous polyps and should subsequently undergo appropriate follow-up surveillance endoscopy [p.89]. Adjuvant chemotherapy is not recommended for patients with stage I lesions [p.89].

## Workup and Management of Invasive Nonmetastatic Colon Cancer

### Complete Staging Workup

Patients who present with invasive colon cancer appropriate for resection require a complete staging workup, including [p.89]:
- Biopsy
- Pathologic tissue review
- Total colonoscopy
- CBC
- Chemistry profile
- CEA determination
- Baseline CT scans of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis

Testing for MMR/MSI should be done at diagnosis to help with detection of Lynch syndrome and to inform treatment decision-making [p.89].

### CT Imaging Protocol

CT should be with intravenous (IV) and oral contrast [p.89]. If the CT of the abdomen and pelvis is inadequate or if CT with IV contrast is contraindicated, an abdomen/pelvis MRI with contrast plus a non-contrast chest CT should be considered [p.89].

### Pulmonary Metastases

The chest CT can identify lung metastases, which occur in approximately 4% to 9% of patients with colon and rectal cancer [p.89].

One series of 378 patients found that resection of pulmonary metastases resulted in [p.89]:
- 3-year relapse-free survival (RFS): 28%
- 3-year OS: 78%

### PET/CT Imaging

The consensus of the Panel is that a PET/CT scan is not indicated at baseline for preoperative workup [p.89]. PET/CT scans are usually done without contrast and multiple slicing and do not obviate the need for a contrast-enhanced diagnostic CT scan [p.89].

If abnormalities are seen on CT or MRI scan that are considered suspicious but inconclusive for metastases, then a PET/CT scan may be considered to further delineate that abnormality, if this information will change management [p.89].

A PET/CT scan is not indicated for assessing subcentimeter lesions, because these are routinely below the level of PET/CT detection [p.89].

### Fertility Preservation

Fertility risks should be discussed with appropriate patients prior to treatment and referral for and/or counseling on fertility preservation options should be done if indicated [p.89].

## Management of Obstructed Colon Cancer

### Surgical Options

For resectable colon cancer that is causing overt obstruction, the following options are available [p.90]:
- One-stage colectomy with en bloc removal of regional lymph nodes
- Resection with diversion
- Diversion or stent (in selected cases) followed by colectomy

### Stent Placement

Stents are generally reserved for cases of distal lesions in which a stent can allow decompression of the proximal colon with later elective colostomy with primary anastomosis [p.90].

A meta-analysis found that oncologic outcomes were similar for surgery and for stenting followed by elective surgery [p.90].

The ESCO trial, an RCT from Europe, reported similar outcomes between colonic stenting as a bridge to surgery compared to emergency surgery for malignant colon obstruction [p.90].

### Diversion Followed by Colectomy

Another meta-analysis of comparative studies compared colectomy to diversion followed by colectomy [p.90]. Although 30-day mortality and morbidity were the same between the groups, the diversion group was less likely to have a permanent colostomy [p.90]:
- OR for permanent colostomy: 0.22 (95% CI, 0.11–0.46) [p.90]

### Stoma Management

Preoperative stoma education and marking of the site by an enterostomal therapist have been shown to improve outcomes and are therefore recommended for patients who are expected to receive a stoma following surgery [p.90].

## Neoadjuvant Therapy for Resectable Colon Cancer

### Bulky Nodal Disease and T4b Tumors

Bulky nodal disease or clinical T4b may benefit from neoadjuvant therapy prior to resection [p.90]:
- Oxaliplatin-based chemotherapy for MMR-proficient (pMMR)/microsatellite-stable (MSS) disease [p.90]
- Either chemotherapy or a checkpoint inhibitor for cT4b dMMR/MSI-H disease [p.90]

If the cancer is locally unresectable or the patient is medically inoperable, systemic therapy, radiation, and/or chemoradiation is recommended, possibly with the goal of converting the lesion to a resectable state [p.90].

### FOxTROT Trial

The randomized phase III FOxTROT trial is assessing whether neoadjuvant chemotherapy improves DFS [p.90].

#### Feasibility Phase Results (2012)

Results from the feasibility phase were reported in 2012 [p.90]:
- 150 patients with T3 (with ≥5 mm invasion beyond the muscularis propria) or T4 tumors [p.90]
- Randomly assigned to three cycles of preoperative therapy (5-FU/LV/oxaliplatin), surgery, and nine additional cycles of the same therapy, or surgery with 12 cycles of the same therapy given postoperatively [p.90]
- Preoperative therapy resulted in significant downstaging compared with postoperative therapy (P = .04), with acceptable toxicity [p.90]

#### Mature Results

Mature results from the FOxTROT trial [p.90]:
- 1053 total randomized patients
- 699 randomized to neoadjuvant chemotherapy
- 354 to control group
- Primary outcome of 2-year residual or recurrent disease: 16.9% with neoadjuvant chemotherapy vs. 21.5% in control group [p.90]
- 28% lower recurrence rate with neoadjuvant chemotherapy [p.90]

#### Downstaging and Survival

The neoadjuvant chemotherapy group showed [p.90]:
- Marked T and N downstaging
- Histologic tumor regression
- More often histopathologically complete resection (94% vs. 89%; P < .001) [p.90]

### Neoadjuvant Immunotherapy for dMMR/MSI-H Colon Cancer

For the dMMR/MSI-H population, the NICHE and NICHE-2 studies have shown high rates of pathologic response with neoadjuvant immunotherapy in early-stage colon cancers prior to resection [p.90].

#### NICHE-2 Study Results

The NICHE-2 study reported results from 115 enrolled patients with nonmetastatic dMMR colon cancer treated with ipilimumab plus nivolumab [p.90]:
- In the efficacy population, 109 of 111 (98%; 95% CI, 94–100) were observed to have pathologic disease response [p.90]
- 95% major pathologic responses [p.90]
- 68% pathologic complete responses [p.90]
- At a median follow-up of 26 months, no cases of disease recurrence were reported [p.90]
- Grade 3–4 immune-related AEs: 4% of patients [p.90]

#### Preferred Approach for dMMR/MSI-H

While neoadjuvant chemotherapy with FOLFOX or CAPEOX is considered an option by the NCCN Panel for cT4b dMMR/MSI-H disease, it is important to note that the FOxTROT trial results reported little benefit from neoadjuvant chemotherapy for patients with dMMR tumors, leading checkpoint inhibitor therapy to be the preferred approach in this setting [p.90-91].

## Surgical Management

### Standard Procedure

For resectable nonmetastatic colon cancer, the preferred surgical procedure is colectomy with en bloc removal of the regional lymph nodes [p.91].

The extent of colectomy should be based on [p.91]:
- Tumor location
- Resecting the portion of the bowel and arterial arcade containing the regional lymph nodes
- Other nodes, such as those at the origin of the vessel feeding the tumor (apical lymph node) [p.91]
- Suspicious lymph nodes outside the field of resection [p.91]

Resection must be complete to be considered curative, and positive lymph nodes left behind indicate an incomplete (R2) resection [p.91].

### Complete Mesocolic Excision (CME)

There has been some attention focused on the quality of colectomy [p.91].

#### ESCME Trial

The phase III ESCME trial compared the outcomes of patients who had undergone CME to those who had a non-CME [p.91]:
- Five-year local RFS: Similar between the groups [p.91]
- Absolute risk reduction of 5-year cumulative death and disease progression after CME: 9.1% for UICC stage I–III and 16.1% for UICC stage III [p.91]

#### Observational Studies

A retrospective observational study found a possible OS advantage for surgery in the mesocolic plane over surgery in the muscularis propria plane [p.91].

A comparison of resection techniques by expert surgeons in Japan and Germany showed that CME with central vascular ligation resulted in greater mesentery and lymph node yields than the Japanese D3 high tie surgery [p.91].

#### Population-Based Evidence

A retrospective, population-based study in Denmark supports the benefit of a CME approach in patients with stage I–III colon cancer, with a significant difference in 4-year DFS [p.91]:
- CME resection: 85.8% (95% CI, 81.4–90.1) [p.91]
- Conventional resection: 75.9% (95% CI, 72.2–79.7) [p.91]
- P = .001 [p.91]

A systematic review found that four of nine prospective studies reported improved lymph node harvest and survival with CME compared with non-CME colectomy; the other studies reported improved specimen quality [p.91].

## Minimally Invasive Approaches to Colectomy

### Laparoscopic Colectomy

Laparoscopic colectomy is an option in the surgical management of colon cancer [p.91].

#### Barcelona Trial

In a small European randomized trial (Barcelona), the laparoscopic approach seemed to be associated with [p.91]:
- Some modest survival advantage
- Significantly faster recovery
- Shorter hospital stays

#### COLOR Trial

A similar but larger trial (COLOR trial) of 1248 patients with colon cancer randomly assigned to curative surgery with either a conventional open approach or laparoscopic-assisted surgery [p.91]:
- Nonsignificant absolute difference of 2.0% in 3-year DFS favoring open colectomy [p.91]
- Noninferiority of the laparoscopic approach could not be established because of study limitations [p.91]
- Ten-year outcomes showed similar rates of DFS, OS, and recurrence between open and laparoscopic surgery [p.91]

#### CLASICC Study

In the CLASICC study of 794 patients with CRC, no statistically significant differences in 3-year rates of OS, DFS, and local recurrence were observed between surgical approaches [p.91].

Long-term follow-up of participants in the CLASICC trial (median 62.9 months) showed that the lack of differences in outcomes between arms continued [p.91].

#### COST Study

In another trial (COST study) of 872 patients with colon cancer randomly assigned to undergo either open or laparoscopic-assisted colectomy for curable colon cancer, similar outcomes were seen [p.91]:
- Similar 5-year recurrence rates [p.91]
- Similar 5-year OS rates [p.91]
- Median follow-up of 7 years [p.91]

#### Australian and New Zealand Trial

A similar RCT in Australia and New Zealand found no differences in disease outcomes [p.91].

#### Meta-Analyses

Results of several meta-analyses have supported the conclusion that the two surgical approaches provide similar long-term outcomes with respect to local recurrence and survival in patients with colon cancer [p.91].

### Hospital Case Volume Impact

A subanalysis of results from the COLOR trial evaluating short-term outcomes based on hospital case volume indicated that these outcomes were statistically significantly more favorable when laparoscopic surgery was performed at hospitals with high case volumes [p.92].

A meta-analysis of 18 studies (6153 patients) found a lower rate of cardiac complications with laparoscopic colectomy compared with open resection [p.92].

### Perioperative Care and Enhanced Recovery

In recent years, perioperative care has improved, with reductions in the average length of hospital stay and complication rates after surgery [p.92].

The multicenter, randomized, controlled EnROL trial compared conventional and laparoscopic colectomy with an enhanced recovery program in place [p.92]:
- Outcomes were the same in both arms [p.92]
- Exception: Median length of hospital stay was significantly shorter in the laparoscopic group (5 vs. 7 days; P = .033) [p.92]

### Robotic Colectomy

Robotic colectomy has been compared to the laparoscopic approach, mostly with observational cohort studies [p.92].

In general, the robotic approach [p.92]:
- Results in longer operating times
- More expensive
- May be associated with less blood loss
- Shorter time to recovery of bowel function
- Shorter hospital stays
- Lower rates of complications and infections

### Panel Recommendations

The Panel recommends that minimally invasive colectomy be considered only by surgeons experienced in the techniques [p.92]. A thorough abdominal exploration is required as part of the procedure [p.92].

Routine use of minimally invasive colon resection is generally not recommended for [p.92]:
- Tumors that are acutely obstructed or perforated
- Tumors that are clearly locally invasive into surrounding structures (T4)

Patients at high risk for prohibitive abdominal adhesions should not have minimally invasive colectomy, and those who are found to have prohibitive adhesions during exploration should be converted to an open procedure [p.92].

## Adjuvant Chemotherapy for Resectable Colon Cancer

### Stage-Based Treatment Recommendations

Choices for adjuvant therapy for patients with resected, nonmetastatic colon cancer depend on the stage of disease [p.92]:

#### Stage I Disease

Patients with stage I disease do not require any adjuvant therapy [p.92].

#### Stage II Disease - MSI-H

Patients with MSI-H, stage II disease do not require any adjuvant therapy [p.92].

#### Low-Risk Stage II Disease

Patients with low-risk stage II disease that is MSS or pMMR can be [p.92]:
- Observed without adjuvant therapy
- Considered for capecitabine or 5-FU/LV

Based on results of the MOSAIC trial and the possible long-term sequelae of oxaliplatin-based chemotherapy, the Panel does not consider FOLFOX to be an appropriate adjuvant therapy option for patients with stage II disease without high-risk features [p.92].

#### High-Risk Stage II Disease

Patients with stage II disease that is MSS/pMMR and at high risk for systemic recurrence can be considered for [p.92]:
- 6 months of adjuvant chemotherapy with 5-FU/LV, capecitabine, or FOLFOX
- 3 months of adjuvant chemotherapy with CAPEOX

High-risk stage II is defined as those with poor prognostic features [p.92]:
- T4 tumors (stage IIB/IIC)
- Poorly differentiated/undifferentiated histology
- Lymphovascular invasion
- PNI
- Tumor budding
- Bowel obstruction
- Lesions with localized perforation or close, indeterminate, or positive margins
- Inadequately sampled nodes (<12 lymph nodes)

Observation without adjuvant therapy is also an option in this population [p.92].

#### Low-Risk Stage III Disease

For patients with low-risk (T1–3, N1) stage III disease, the preferred adjuvant treatment options are [p.93]:
- 3 months of CAPEOX
- 3 to 6 months of FOLFOX

Other treatment options include [p.93]:
- 6 months of single-agent capecitabine
- 5-FU/LV in patients for whom oxaliplatin therapy is believed to be inappropriate

#### High-Risk Stage III Disease

For patients with high-risk (T4, N1–2 or any T, N2) stage III disease, the preferred adjuvant treatment options are [p.93]:
- 6 months of FOLFOX
- 3 to 6 months of CAPEOX

Other treatment options include [p.93]:
- 6 months of single-agent capecitabine
- 5-FU/LV in patients for whom oxaliplatin therapy is believed to be inappropriate

### Clinical Evidence for Adjuvant Therapy Benefit

Population and institutional studies have shown that patients with resected colon cancer treated with adjuvant therapy have a survival advantage over those not treated with adjuvant therapy [p.93].

#### National Cancer Database Analysis

Patients from the National Cancer Database with stage III or high-risk stage II disease treated according to NCCN Guidelines had a survival advantage over patients whose treatment did not adhere to these guidelines [p.93].

#### Memorial University Medical Center Study

A retrospective cohort study of 852 patients with any stage of colon or rectal cancer treated at Memorial University Medical Center in Savannah, Georgia found that concordance with the recommendations in NCCN Guidelines for Colon Cancer resulted in a lower risk of death [p.93].

## Endpoints for Adjuvant Chemotherapy Clinical Trials

### ACCENT Collaborative Group Analysis

The Adjuvant Colon Cancer End Points (ACCENT) collaborative group evaluated the appropriateness of various endpoints for adjuvant chemotherapy trials in colon cancer [p.93].

Results of an analysis of individual patient data from 20,898 patients in 18 randomized colon adjuvant clinical trials suggested that DFS after 2 and 3 years of follow-up are appropriate endpoints for clinical trials involving treatment of colon cancer with 5-FU–based chemotherapy in the adjuvant setting [p.93].

### Timing of Recurrence

An update of this analysis showed that most relapses occur within 2 years after surgery [p.93], and that recurrence rates were [p.93]:
- <1.5% per year after 5 years
- <0.5% per year after 8 years

### Updated OS Analysis

A further update of the data suggested that the association between 2- or 3-year DFS and 5-year OS was reduced when patient survival after recurrence was hypothetically prolonged to match current survival times with modern combination therapies (2 years) [p.93].

This indicated that >5 years may now be required to evaluate the effect of adjuvant therapies on OS [p.93].

### Combination Therapy Analysis

Further confirmation comes from a new analysis by the ACCENT group of data from 12,676 patients undergoing combination therapies from six trials [p.93]:
- 2- and 3-year DFS correlated with 5- and 6-year OS in stage III disease [p.93]
- Did not correlate in stage II disease [p.93]
- Correlation of DFS to OS was strongest at 6-year follow-up [p.93]

This suggests that at least 6 years are required for adequate assessment of OS in modern adjuvant colon cancer trials [p.93].

## Adjuvant Chemotherapy in Stage II Disease

### Meta-Analysis of Stage II Adjuvant Therapy

Results from a 2015 meta-analysis of 25 high-quality studies showed [p.93-94]:
- 5-year DFS in stage II without adjuvant therapy: 81.4% (95% CI, 75.4–87.4) [p.94]
- 5-year DFS in stage II with adjuvant chemotherapy: 79.3% (95% CI, 75.6–83.1) [p.94]

In contrast, for stage III colon cancer [p.94]:
- 5-year DFS without adjuvant chemotherapy: 49.0% (95% CI, 23.2–74.8) [p.94]
- 5-year DFS with adjuvant chemotherapy: 63.6% (95% CI, 59.3–67.9) [p.94]

These results suggest that the benefit of adjuvant therapy is greater in patients at higher risk because of nodal status [p.94].

### QUASAR Trial

In contrast to results from most other trials, the QUASAR trial indicated a small but statistically significant survival benefit for patients with stage II disease treated with 5-FU/LV compared to patients not receiving adjuvant therapy [p.94]:
- RR of recurrence at 2 years: 0.71 (95% CI, 0.54–0.92; P = .01) [p.94]

However, approximately 64% of patients had <12 lymph nodes sampled, and thus actually may have been patients with higher risk disease who were more likely to benefit from adjuvant therapy [p.94].

### Oxaliplatin in Stage II Disease

#### MOSAIC Trial

Results from a post-hoc exploratory analysis of the MOSAIC trial did not show a significant DFS benefit of FOLFOX over 5-FU/LV for patients with stage II disease at a follow-up of 6 years [p.94]:
- HR: 0.84 (95% CI, 0.62–1.14; P = .258) [p.94]

After longer follow-up, no difference in 10-year OS was observed in the stage II subpopulation [p.94]:
- FOLFOX: 79.5%
- 5-FU/LV: 78.4%
- HR: 1.00 (P = .98) [p.94]

#### High-Risk Stage II Analysis

In addition, patients with high-risk stage II disease receiving FOLFOX did not have improved DFS compared with those receiving infusional 5-FU/LV [p.94]:
- HR: 0.72 (95% CI, 0.50–1.02; P = .063) [p.94]

Furthermore, no OS benefit was seen in the stage II population overall or in the stage II population with high-risk features [p.94].

#### C-07 Trial

Similar results were seen in the C-07 trial, which compared FLOX to 5-FU/LV in patients with stage II and III disease [p.94].

Results of a large population-based study also support the lack of benefit of the addition of oxaliplatin to adjuvant regimens for patients with stage II colon cancer [p.94].

### SEER Database Analysis

Using the SEER databases, a 2002 analysis of outcomes of patients with stage II disease based on whether or not they had received adjuvant chemotherapy showed no statistically significant difference in 5-year OS [p.94]:
- No adjuvant therapy: 78%
- With adjuvant chemotherapy: 75%
- HR: 0.91 (95% CI, 0.77–1.09) [p.94]

In contrast, a 2016 analysis of 153,110 patients with stage II colon cancer from the National Cancer Database found that adjuvant treatment was associated with improved survival [p.94]:
- HR: 0.76 (P < .001) [p.94]
- Adjusted for comorbidity and unplanned hospital readmissions [p.94]

### Population-Level Analysis from Netherlands

Results of another population-level analysis from the Netherlands published in 2016 suggest that the benefit of adjuvant therapy in patients with stage II colon cancer may be limited to those with pT4 tumors [p.94].

### Individualized Decision-Making

Decision-making regarding the use of adjuvant therapy for patients with stage II disease should incorporate patient/physician discussions individualized for the patient [p.94]. This should include explanations of [p.94]:
- Specific characteristics of the disease and its prognosis
- Evidence related to the efficacy and possible toxicities associated with treatment
- Centering on patient choice

Observation and participation in a clinical trial are options that should be considered [p.94].

### Prognostic Uncertainty

Patients with average-risk stage II colon cancer have a very good prognosis, so the possible benefit of adjuvant therapy is small [p.94]. Patients with disease with high-risk features, on the other hand, traditionally have been considered more likely to benefit from adjuvant chemotherapy [p.94].

However, the current definition of high-risk stage II colon cancer is clearly inadequate [p.94]:
- Many patients with disease with high-risk features do not have a recurrence [p.94]
- Some patients with disease deemed to be average-risk do [p.94]

Furthermore, no data point to features that are predictive of benefit from adjuvant chemotherapy, and no data correlate risk features and selection of chemotherapy in patients with high-risk stage II disease [p.94].

### ASCO Panel and NCCN Consensus

Overall, the NCCN Panel supports the conclusion of a 2022 ASCO Panel and believes that it is reasonable to accept the relative benefit of adjuvant therapy in stage III disease as indirect evidence of benefit for stage II disease, especially for those with high-risk features [p.94].

Additional information that may influence adjuvant therapy decisions for stage II and/or stage III disease includes MSI status, multigene assays, and the influence of patient age [p.94-95].

## Microsatellite Instability (MSI)

### Genetic Basis

MSI is an important piece of information to consider when deciding whether to use adjuvant chemotherapy in patients with stage II disease [p.95].

Mutation of MMR genes or modifications of these genes (e.g., methylation) can result in MMR protein deficiency and MSI [p.95].

### MSI Classification

Tumors showing the presence of MSI are classified as either [p.95]:
- MSI-H (high)
- MSI-L (low)

Tumors without this characteristic are classified as MSS (microsatellite stable) [p.95].

Patients with tumors determined to have dMMR status are biologically the same population as those with MSI-H status [p.95].

### Lynch Syndrome

Germline mutations in the MMR genes MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, and/or PMS2 or EpCAM are found in individuals with Lynch syndrome, which is responsible for 2% to 4% of colon cancer cases [p.95].

Somatic MMR defects have been reported to occur in approximately 19% of colorectal tumors [p.95], whereas others have reported somatic hypermethylation of the MLH1 gene promoter, which is associated with MLH1 gene inactivation, in as many as 52% of colon tumors [p.95].

### Stage-Specific MSI Prevalence

Data from the PETACC-3 trial showed that tumor specimens characterized as MSI-H are more common in stage II disease than in stage III disease [p.95]:
- Stage II: 22%
- Stage III: 12%
- P < .0001 [p.95]

In another large study, the percentage of stage IV tumors characterized as MSI-H was only 3.5% [p.95].

These results suggest that MSI-H (dMMR) tumors have a decreased likelihood to metastasize [p.95].

### MSI as Prognostic Factor

Substantial evidence shows that in patients with stage II disease, a deficiency in MMR protein expression or MSI-H tumor status is a prognostic marker of a more favorable outcome [p.95].

In contrast, the favorable impact of dMMR on outcomes seems to be more limited in stage III colon cancer and may vary with primary tumor location [p.95].

### MSI and Adjuvant Therapy Response

Some studies show that a deficiency in MMR protein expression or MSI-H tumor status may be a predictive marker of decreased benefit and possibly a detrimental impact from adjuvant therapy with a fluoropyrimidine alone in patients with stage II disease [p.95].

#### Retrospective Study

A retrospective study involving long-term follow-up of patients with stage II and III disease evaluated according to MSI tumor status showed [p.95]:
- MSI-L or MSS patients: Improved outcomes with 5-FU adjuvant therapy [p.95]
- MSI-H patients: Did not show a statistically significant benefit from 5-FU after surgery, instead exhibiting a lower 5-year survival rate than those undergoing surgery alone [p.95]

#### Sargent et al. Study

Results from another retrospective study of pooled data from adjuvant trials by Sargent et al showed that in tumors characterized as dMMR [p.95]:
- Adjuvant 5-FU chemotherapy seemed to be detrimental in patients with stage II disease [p.95]
- Not in those with stage III disease [p.95]

#### QUASAR Study

In contrast to the findings of Sargent et al., a study of 1913 patients with stage II CRC from the QUASAR study showed [p.95]:
- dMMR was prognostic (recurrence rate: 11% for dMMR vs. 26% for pMMR) [p.95]
- Did not predict benefit or detrimental impact of chemotherapy [p.95]

#### CALGB 9581 and 89803 Trials

A study of patients in the CALGB 9581 and 89803 trials came to a similar conclusion [p.95]. MMR status was prognostic but not predictive of benefit or detrimental impact of adjuvant therapy (irinotecan plus bolus 5-FU/LV) in patients with stage II colon cancer [p.95].

### Panel Recommendations for MSI Testing

The Panel recommends universal MMR or MSI testing for all patients with a personal history of colon or rectal cancer [p.95] to:
- Identify individuals with Lynch syndrome [p.95]
- Inform use of immunotherapy in patients with metastatic disease [p.95]
- Inform decisions for patients with stage II disease [p.95]

#### MSI-H Stage II Disease

Patients with stage II MSI-H tumors may have a good prognosis and do not benefit from 5-FU adjuvant therapy [p.96]. Adjuvant therapy should not be given to patients with low-risk stage II MSI-H tumors [p.96].

An exception is with T4b (stage IIC) dMMR/MSI-H tumors, which may carry a higher risk compared to other dMMR/MSI-H stage II tumors [p.96]. For these patients, fluoropyrimidine-based adjuvant therapy, with or without oxaliplatin, may be considered [p.96].

#### Poorly Differentiated Histology in MSI-H Disease

It should be noted that poorly differentiated histology is not considered a high-risk feature for patients with stage II disease whose tumors are MSI-H [p.96].
## Multigene Assays, Immunoscore, and Circulating Tumor DNA

### Oncotype DX Colon Cancer Assay

Oncotype DX colon cancer assay quantifies the expression of seven recurrence-risk genes and five reference genes as a prognostic classifier of low, intermediate, or high likelihood of recurrence [p.96].

#### Clinical Validation

Clinical validation in patients with stage II and III colon cancer from QUASAR and NSABP C-07 trials showed that recurrence scores are prognostic for recurrence, DFS, and OS in stage II and III colon cancer [p.96], but are not predictive of benefit to adjuvant therapy [p.96].

For the low, intermediate, and high recurrence risk groups, recurrence at 3 years was [p.96]:
- Low: 12%
- Intermediate: 18%
- High: 22%

Multivariate analysis showed that recurrence scores were related to recurrence independently from TNM staging, MMR status, tumor grade, and number of nodes assessed in both stage II and III disease [p.96].

Similar results were found in a prospectively designed study that tested the correlation between recurrence score using the Oncotype DX colon cancer assay and the risk of recurrence in patients from the CALGB 9581 trial (stage II disease) [p.96].

An additional prospectively designed clinical validation study in patients from the NSABP C-07 trial found that the assay results correlated with recurrence, DFS, and OS [p.96]. This study also found some evidence that patients with higher recurrence scores may derive more absolute benefit from oxaliplatin, although the authors noted that the recurrence score is not predictive of oxaliplatin efficacy [p.96].

An additional study validated the recurrence score in patients with stage II/III colon cancer treated with surgery alone [p.96].

### ColoPrint

ColoPrint quantifies the expression of 18 genes as a prognostic classifier of low versus high recurrence risk [p.96].

In a set of 206 patients with stage I–III CRC, the 5-year RFS rates were [p.96]:
- Low risk: 87.6% (95% CI, 81.5–93.7)
- High risk: 67.2% (95% CI, 55.4–79.0)

In patients with stage II disease in particular, the HR for recurrence between the high and low groups was 3.34 (P = .017) [p.96].

This assay was further validated in a pooled analysis of 416 patients with stage II disease, 301 of whom were assessed as a T3/MSS subset [p.96]. In the T3/MSS subset, patients classified as low risk and high risk had a 5-year risk of relapse of [p.96]:
- Low risk: 22.4%
- High risk: 9.9%
- HR: 2.41 (P = .005)

As with the Oncotype DX colon cancer assay, recurrence risk determined by ColoPrint is independent of other risk factors, including T stage, perforation, number of nodes assessed, and tumor grade [p.96].

### ColDx

ColDx is a microarray-based multigene assay that uses 634 probes to identify patients with stage II colon cancer at high risk of recurrence [p.96].

In a 144-sample independent validation set, the HR for identification of patients with high-risk disease was [p.96]:
- Recurrence: 2.53 (95% CI, 1.54–4.15; P < .001)
- Cancer-related death: 2.21 (95% CI, 1.22–3.97; P = .0084)

A cohort study of patients in the C9581 trial found that patients with stage II colon cancer identified as high risk by ColDx had a shorter recurrence-free interval than those identified as low-risk [p.96]:
- Multivariable HR: 2.13 (95% CI, 1.3–3.5; P < .01)

Similar to the other assays described here, the recurrence risk determined by ColDx is independent of other risk factors [p.96].

### Immunoscore

An international study led by the Society for Immunotherapy of Cancer aimed to validate Immunoscore, a scoring system reported as percentiles of CD3+ and CD8+ immune cell densities in prespecified regions of the tumor sample by dedicated software [p.96].

This study reported that patients with the highest Immunoscore showed the lowest risk of recurrence [p.96]:
- 3-year RFS rates:
  - Low: 56.9%
  - Medium: 65.9%
  - High: 76.4%
- HR (high vs. low): 0.48 (95% CI, 0.32–0.71; P = .0003)

A high Immunoscore also correlated with prolonged [[p.96]:
- Time to recurrence
- OS
- DFS
- All P < .001

The benefit of adjuvant chemotherapy was also associated with a high Immunoscore for both [[p.96]:
- High-risk tumors (P = .0015)
- Low-risk tumors (P = .0011)

The same was not true for tumors with a low Immunoscore (P > .12) [p.96].

### Circulating Tumor DNA (ctDNA)

Post-surgical circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) has also been studied as a marker for an elevated risk of recurrence in stage I–III colon cancer [p.96].

#### Prospective Multicenter Study

A prospective, multicenter study of 130 patients with stage I–III colon cancer detected ctDNA by multiplex, PCR-based next-generation sequencing (NGS) [p.96]:
- 30 days after surgery: Patients with positive ctDNA assays were 7 times more likely to experience disease relapse than patients with negative ctDNA assays [p.96]
  - HR: 7.2 (95% CI, 2.7–19.0; P < .001)
- After adjuvant chemotherapy: Patients with ctDNA-positive assays were 17 times more likely to have disease relapse [p.96]
  - HR: 17.5 (95% CI, 5.4–56.5; P < .001)

#### Additional Prospective Study

Another prospective study of 150 patients with localized colon cancer detected ctDNA with NGS following surgery [p.97]. In this study, detection of ctDNA was also associated with poorer DFS [p.97]:
- ctDNA post-surgery: HR, 17.56 (log rank P = .0014)
- ctDNA in serial plasma samples during follow-up: HR, 11.33 (log rank P = .0001)

#### DYNAMIC Study

In the DYNAMIC study, 455 patients with stage II colon cancer were randomized to either ctDNA-guided management or standard management [p.97].

After a median follow-up of 37 months [[p.97]:
- Lower percentage of patients in ctDNA-guided management group received adjuvant chemotherapy compared to standard management: 15% vs. 28%
- RR: 1.82 (95% CI, 1.25–2.65)
- 2-year RFS: Noninferior in ctDNA-guided group (93.5% vs. 92.4%)

An abstract reporting 5-year results from the DYNAMIC study showed similar results, including mature OS data [p.97]:
- 5-year RFS: 88% (ctDNA-guided) vs. 87% (standard management)
- 5-year OS: 93.8% (ctDNA-guided) vs. 93.3% (standard management)

#### CIRCULATE-Japan Study

The GALAXY observational arm from the ongoing CIRCULATE-Japan study has analyzed presurgical and postsurgical ctDNA in 1039 patients with stage II–IV resectable CRC [p.97].

With a median follow-up of 16.74 months [p.97]:
- Postsurgical ctDNA positivity at 4 weeks after surgery: Associated with higher recurrence risk [p.97]
  - HR: 10.0 (P < .0001)
- Identified patients with stage II or III CRC: Who derived a benefit from adjuvant chemotherapy [p.97]
  - HR: 6.59 (P < .0001)

### Panel Recommendations on Molecular Assays

In summary, the information from these tests can further inform the risk of recurrence over other risk factors, but the Panel questions the value added [p.98].

Furthermore, evidence of predictive value in terms of the potential benefit of chemotherapy is lacking [p.98]. Therefore, the Panel believes that there are insufficient data to recommend the use of multigene assays, Immunoscore, or post-surgical ctDNA to estimate risk of recurrence or determine adjuvant therapy [p.98].

ESMO has released similar recommendations regarding these assays, stating that their role in predicting chemotherapy benefit is uncertain [p.98].

The NCCN Panel encourages enrollment in clinical trials to help with the generation of additional data on these assays [p.98].

## Adjuvant Chemotherapy in Older Patients

### Utilization Decline with Age

Adjuvant chemotherapy usage declines with the age of the patient [p.98].

Questions regarding the safety and efficacy of chemotherapy in older patients have been difficult to answer, because older patients are underrepresented in clinical trials [p.98].

### Population Studies - Benefit of 5-FU/LV

Population studies have found that adjuvant therapy is beneficial in older patients [p.98].

A retrospective analysis of 7263 patients from the linked SEER-Medicare Databases found a survival benefit for the use of 5-FU/LV in patients ≥65 years with stage III disease [p.98]:
- HR: 0.70 (P < .001)

Another analysis of 5489 patients aged ≥75 years diagnosed with stage III colon cancer between 2004 and 2007 from four datasets, including the SEER-Medicare Databases and the NCCN Outcomes Database, showed a survival benefit for adjuvant chemotherapy in this population [p.98]:
- HR: 0.60 (95% CI, 0.53–0.68)

This study also looked specifically at the benefit of the addition of oxaliplatin to adjuvant therapy in patients ≥75 years with stage III disease, and found only a small, non-significant benefit [p.98].

Analysis of almost 12,000 patients from the ACCENT database also found a reduced benefit to the addition of oxaliplatin to fluoropyrimidines in the adjuvant setting in patients ≥70 years [p.98].

### Subset Analyses of Major Trials

Subset analyses of major adjuvant therapy trials show a lack of benefit to the addition of oxaliplatin in older patients [p.98].

#### NSABP C-07 Trial

Subset analysis of the NSABP C-07 trial showed that the addition of oxaliplatin to 5-FU/LV gave no survival benefit in patients ≥70 years with stage II or III colon cancer (n = 396) [p.98]:
- HR: 1.18 (95% CI, 0.86–1.62)
- With a trend towards decreased survival

#### MOSAIC Trial

Similarly, in a subset analysis of the MOSAIC trial, 315 patients aged 70 to 75 years with stage II or III colon cancer derived no benefit from the addition of oxaliplatin [p.99]:
- OS HR: 1.10 (95% CI, 0.73–1.65)

### Pooled Analysis of Oxaliplatin in Patients ≥70 Years

Another pooled analysis aimed to determine if patients aged ≥70 years derive a benefit with oxaliplatin-based adjuvant chemotherapy for treatment of high-risk stage II–III colon or rectal cancer [p.99].

This analysis included OS data from 1985 patients and showed [[p.99]:
- Patients <70 years: Oxaliplatin-based adjuvant chemotherapy reduced mortality risk by 26%
- Patients ≥70 years: Did not yield an improvement

Similar results were seen for DFS [p.99].

### Conflicting Evidence - NSABP C-08, XELOXA, X-ACT, and AVANT Trials

However, a pooled analysis of individual patient data from the NSABP C-08, XELOXA, X-ACT, and AVANT trials found that DFS and OS were improved with adjuvant CAPEOX or FOLFOX over 5-FU/LV in patients ≥70 years [p.99]:
- DFS: HR, 0.77 (95% CI, 0.62–0.95; P = .014)
- OS: HR, 0.78 (95% CI, 0.61–0.99; P = .045)

### TOSCA Trial Subgroup Analysis

A subgroup analysis of the phase III TOSCA trial (part of the IDEA collaboration) found that once the multivariable analysis was corrected for sex, performance status, tumor site, grade, treatment, treatment duration, and dose reduction [[p.99]:
- No significant difference in time to tumor recurrence between patients ≥70 years compared to those <70 years when treated with oxaliplatin-based adjuvant therapy [p.99]
  - HR: 1.19 (95% CI, 0.98–1.44; P = .082)
- Worse prognostic factors and higher rate of treatment discontinuation had a negative impact on efficacy measures of DFS and OS in the population ≥70 years [p.99]

### Risk of Early Mortality with Adjuvant Therapy

A pooled analysis of 37,568 patients from adjuvant trials in the ACCENT database found that the likelihood of early mortality after adjuvant treatment increased with age in a nonlinear fashion [p.99]:
- P < .001

For instance, the ORs for 30-day mortality for patients aged 70 and 80 years compared to patients aged 60 years were [[p.99]:
- Age 70: 2.58 (95% CI, 1.88–3.54)
- Age 80: 8.61 (95% CI, 5.34–13.9)

Patients aged 50 years, on the other hand, had a corresponding OR of 0.72 (95% CI, 0.47–1.10) [p.99].

However, the absolute risk of early mortality was very small, even for older patients [[p.99]:
- 30-day mortality for 80-year-olds: 1.8%

### Overall Recommendations for Older Patients

Overall, the benefit and toxicities of 5-FU/LV as adjuvant therapy seem to be similar in older and younger patients [p.99]. However, the Panel cautions that a benefit for the addition of oxaliplatin to 5-FU/LV in patients ≥70 years has not been proven in stage II or stage III colon cancer [p.99].

## Timing of Adjuvant Therapy

### Delay Impact on Overall Survival

A systematic review and meta-analysis of 10 studies involving >15,000 patients examined the effect of timing of adjuvant therapy after resection [p.99].

Results of this analysis showed that each 4-week delay in chemotherapy results in a 14% decrease in OS, indicating that adjuvant therapy should be administered as soon as the patient is medically able [p.99].

These results are consistent with other similar analyses [p.99].

### National Cancer Database Analysis

In addition, a retrospective study of 7794 patients with stage II or III colon cancer from the National Cancer Database found that a delay of >6 weeks between surgery and adjuvant therapy reduced survival after adjustment for clinical-, tumor-, and treatment-related factors [p.99].

### Netherlands Cancer Registry

Another retrospective study of 6620 patients with stage III colon cancer from the Netherlands Cancer Registry also found that starting adjuvant therapy after 8 weeks beyond resection was associated with worse survival [p.99].

### Confounding Factors in Timing Analysis

However, some critics have pointed out that this type of analysis is biased by confounding factors such as comorbidities, which are likely to be higher in patients with a longer delay before initiation of chemotherapy [p.99].

In fact, the registry study found that patients who started therapy after 8 weeks were more likely to be [[p.99]:
- >65 years
- Have had an emergency resection
- Have a prolonged postoperative admission

### ECOG-ACRIN E1291 Trial

Additionally, a phase III intergroup trial of the ECOG-ACRIN Cancer Research Group (E1291) reported no significant difference in OS or DFS for perioperative therapy versus no perioperative therapy with 5-FU, although this trial was terminated early due to slow accrual [p.99].

## Leucovorin Shortage

### Background

A shortage of LV has existed in the United States [p.99]. No specific data are available to guide management under these circumstances, and all proposed strategies are empiric [p.99].

### Panel Recommendations for LV Shortage

The Panel recommends several possible options to help alleviate the problems associated with this shortage [p.99]:

#### Option 1: Levoleucovorin

One is the use of levoleucovorin, which is commonly used in Europe [p.99]. A dose of 200 mg/m2 of levoleucovorin is equivalent to 400 mg/m2 of standard LV [p.99].

Use of levoleucovorin should only be considered during times of LV shortage since levoleucovorin is substantially more expensive than LV [p.99].

#### Option 2: Lower Doses of LV

Another option is for practices or institutions to use lower doses of LV for all doses in all patients, because the Panel feels that lower doses are likely to be as efficacious as higher doses, based on several studies [p.99].

The QUASAR study found that 175 mg of LV was associated with similar survival and 3-year recurrence rates as 25 mg of LV when given with bolus 5-FU as adjuvant therapy to patients after R0 resections for CRC [p.99].

Another study showed no difference in response rate or survival in patients with mCRC receiving bolus 5-FU with either high-dose (500 mg/m2) or low-dose (20 mg/m2) LV [p.99].

Furthermore, the Mayo Clinic and North Central Cancer Treatment Group (NCCTG) determined that no therapeutic difference was seen between the use of high-dose (200 mg/m2) or low-dose (20 mg/m2) LV with bolus 5-FU in the treatment of advanced CRC, although the 5-FU doses were different in the treatment arms [p.100].

#### Option 3: Treatment Without LV

Finally, if none of the above options are available, treatment without LV would be reasonable [p.100]. For patients who tolerate this without grade II or higher toxicity, a modest increase in 5-FU dose (in the range of 10%) may be considered [p.100].

## Adjuvant FOLFOX and Infusional 5-FU/LV

### MOSAIC Trial

The European MOSAIC trial compared the efficacy of FOLFOX and 5-FU/LV in the adjuvant setting in 2246 patients with completely resected stage II and III colon cancer [p.100].

Although this initial trial was performed with FOLFOX4, mFOLFOX6 has been the control arm for all recent and current National Cancer Institute (NCI) adjuvant studies for CRC [p.100], and the Panel believes that mFOLFOX6 is the preferred FOLFOX regimen for adjuvant and metastatic treatments [p.100].

Results of this study have been reported with median follow-ups of up to 9.5 years [p.100].

#### Stage III Disease Results

For patients with stage III disease [[p.100]:
- 5-year DFS: 58.9% (5-FU/LV) vs. 66.4% (FOLFOX) [p.100]
  - P = .005
- 10-year OS: Statistically significantly increased with FOLFOX compared with 5-FU/LV [p.100]
  - 67.1% (FOLFOX) vs. 59.0% (5-FU/LV)
  - HR: 0.80 (P = .016)

#### Neuropathy Outcomes

Although the incidence of grade 3 peripheral sensory neuropathy was 12.4% for patients receiving FOLFOX and only 0.2% for patients receiving 5-FU/LV [[p.100]:
- Long-term safety results showed a gradual recovery for most of these patients [p.100]
- However, neuropathy was present in 15.4% of examined patients at 4 years (mostly grade 1) [p.100]
- Suggesting that oxaliplatin-induced neuropathy may not be completely reversible in some patients [p.100]

### Community Setting Data

An analysis of five observational data sources, including the SEER-Medicare and NCCN Outcomes Databases, showed that the addition of oxaliplatin to 5-FU/LV gave a survival advantage to the general stage III colon cancer population treated in the community [p.100].

Another population-based analysis found that the harms of oxaliplatin in the Medicare population with stage III colon cancer were reasonable, even in patients ≥75 years [p.100].

In addition, a pooled analysis of individual patient data from four RCTs revealed that the addition of oxaliplatin to capecitabine or 5-FU/LV improved outcomes in patients with stage III colon cancer [p.100].

Furthermore, analysis of data from 12,233 patients in the ACCENT database of adjuvant colon cancer trials supports the benefit of oxaliplatin in patients with stage III disease [p.100].

## Adjuvant Capecitabine and CAPEOX

### Single-Agent Capecitabine

Single-agent oral capecitabine as adjuvant therapy for patients with stage III colon cancer was shown to be at least equivalent to bolus 5-FU/LV (Mayo Clinic regimen) with respect to DFS and OS in the X-ACT trial [[p.100]:
- DFS: HR, 0.87 (95% CI, 0.75–1.00; P < .001)
- OS: HR, 0.84 (95% CI, 0.69–1.01; P = .07)

Final results of this trial were subsequently reported [p.100]. After a median follow-up of 6.9 years, the equivalencies in DFS and OS were maintained in all subgroups, including those ≥70 years [p.100].

### CAPEOX in Stage III Disease

Capecitabine was also assessed as adjuvant therapy for stage III colon cancer in combination with oxaliplatin (CAPEOX) in the NO16968 trial [p.100].

This trial showed an improved 3-year DFS rate compared with bolus 5-FU/LV [[p.100]:
- CAPEOX: 70.9%
- 5-FU/LV: 66.5%

Final results of this trial showed that OS at 7 years was improved in the CAPEOX arm compared with the 5-FU/LV arm [[p.100]:
- CAPEOX: 73%
- 5-FU/LV: 67%
- HR: 0.83 (95% CI, 0.70–0.99; P = .04)

### CAPEOX vs. mFOLFOX6

Another phase III trial compared CAPEOX to mFOLFOX6 in 408 patients with stage III or high-risk stage II colon cancer [p.100]:
- No significant differences were seen in 3-year DFS and 3-year OS [p.100]

In addition, a pooled analysis of individual patient data from four RCTs revealed that the addition of oxaliplatin to capecitabine or 5-FU/LV improved outcomes in patients with stage III colon cancer [p.101].

## Duration of Adjuvant Therapy

### IDEA Collaboration Overview

The IDEA collaboration investigated whether limiting adjuvant treatment to 3 months of FOLFOX or CAPEOX—which would markedly decrease the incidence of neuropathy—would compromise oncologic outcomes [p.101].

IDEA included 12,834 patients in an international effort that pooled data from six concurrently conducted, randomized phase III trials to assess the noninferiority of 3 months compared with 6 months of adjuvant FOLFOX or CAPEOX in patients with stage III colon cancer [p.101].

The median follow-up was 39 months [p.101].

### Neurotoxicity Reduction

Importantly, grade 3+ neurotoxicity rates were lower in the 3-month versus 6-month treatment arms [[p.101]:
- FOLFOX: 3% vs. 16% (P < .0001)
- CAPEOX: 3% vs. 9% (P < .0001)

Grade 2 neurotoxicity rates were also lower [[p.101]:
- FOLFOX: 14% vs. 32%
- CAPEOX: 12% vs. 36%
- P < .0001

Grade 2 and grade 3/4 diarrhea rates were also lower with the shorter duration of therapy [[p.101]:
- P < .0001 for FOLFOX
- P = .01 for CAPEOX

### Primary Endpoint - DFS at 3 Years

The primary endpoint of 3-year DFS did not meet the prespecified cutoff for noninferiority in the overall population [[p.101]:
- 3 months: 74.6%
- 6 months: 75.5%
- HR: 1.07 (95% CI, 1.00–1.15)

The small absolute difference of 0.9% is of questionable clinical significance [p.101].

However, noninferiority was observed within certain subgroups [[p.101]:
- Low-risk (T1–3, N1) subgroup:
  - 3 months of CAPEOX: Noninferior to 6 months (HR, 0.85; 95% CI, 0.71–1.01)
  - 3 months vs. 6 months of FOLFOX: Noninferiority could not be proven (HR, 1.10; 95% CI, 0.96–1.26)
- High-risk (T4 and/or N2) subgroup:
  - 3 months of FOLFOX: Inferior to 6 months (HR, 1.20; 95% CI, 1.07–1.35)
  - 3 months vs. 6 months of CAPEOX: Noninferiority could not be proven (HR, 1.02; 95% CI, 0.89–1.17)

### Final Analysis Results

Results of the final analysis of IDEA were reported after an overall median survival follow-up of 72.3 months [p.101].

In the final analysis [[p.101]:
- 5-year OS: 82.4% (3 months) vs. 82.8% (6 months) [p.101]
  - HR: 1.02 (95% CI, 0.95–1.11; P = .058)
- 5-year OS HR by treatment:
  - CAPEOX: 0.96 (3 vs. 6 months)
  - FOLFOX: 1.07 (3 vs. 6 months)
- Long-term DFS HRs:
  - CAPEOX: 0.98 (3 vs. 6 months)
  - FOLFOX: 1.16 (3 vs. 6 months)

The authors of this study concluded that, while the differences in OS did not meet the statistical assumptions for noninferiority [[p.101]:
- The overall 0.4% difference in 5-year OS should be placed in clinical context [p.101]
- Especially considering the marked reduction in toxicity associated with the shorter duration of therapy [p.101]

### High-Risk Stage II Disease

A pooled analysis of patients with high-risk stage II colon cancer in the IDEA collaboration did not show noninferiority of 3 months compared to 6 months of adjuvant treatment based on 5-year DFS [[p.101]:
- 3 months: 80.7%
- 6 months: 83.9%
- HR: 1.17 (80% CI, 1.05–1.31)

Similar to stage III, the duration of therapy was associated with a small, not statistically significant difference in 5-year DFS between 3 and 6 months of CAPEOX [[p.101]:
- HR: 1.02 (80% CI, 0.88–1.17)

Two of the published trials within the IDEA collaboration reported similar results for high-risk stage II disease [p.101].

#### TOSCA Trial

For the TOSCA trial [[p.101]:
- 5-year RFS: Similar between 3 and 6 months of CAPEOX [p.101]
- Difference more pronounced between 3 and 6 months of FOLFOX (8.56% difference favoring 6 months) [p.101]
- OS analysis at median follow-up of 7 years:
  - HR: 1.09 for OS in 3- versus 6-month arms (95% CI, 0.93–1.26; P for superiority = .288) [p.101]

#### HORG-IDEA Trial

In the Hellenic Oncology Research Group (HORG)-IDEA trial [[p.101-102]:
- 3-year DFS with FOLFOX: 76.7% (3 months) vs. 79.3% (6 months) [p.102]
  - HR: 1.21 (95% CI, 0.54–2.70)
- 3-year DFS with CAPEOX: 85.4% (3 months) vs. 83.8% (6 months) [p.102]
  - HR: 0.99 (95% CI, 0.59–1.67)

### ACHIEVE Trial

ACHIEVE was another phase III trial that investigated similar questions regarding duration of adjuvant therapy for 1313 patients of Asian descent with stage III colon cancer [p.102].

The results of ACHIEVE were consistent with IDEA [[p.102]:
- Incidence of long-lasting peripheral neuropathy: Significantly lower with 3 months compared to 6 months [p.102]
  - 9.7% vs. 24.3% (P < .001)
- DFS rates: Similar between 3- and 6-month arms [p.102]
  - HR: 0.95 (95% CI, 0.76–1.20)

Final results from the ACHIEVE trial showed comparable 5-year OS results [[p.102]:
- 3-month treatment: 87.0%
- 6-month treatment: 86.4%

Long-term peripheral neuropathy was more common following 6 months of treatment [[p.102]:
- 16% vs. 8%

### ACHIEVE-2 Trial

ACHIEVE-2 was a randomized phase III trial comparing adjuvant chemotherapy duration of 3 versus 6 months for 525 patients with high-risk stage II colon cancer [p.102].

Similar to the other studies [[p.102]:
- 3-year DFS with CAPEOX: 88.2% (3-month arm) vs. 88.4% (6-month arm) [p.102]
- Lower discontinuation rate with 3 months: 15% vs. 35% (P < .0001) [p.102]
- Lower rate of grade 2 or higher peripheral sensory neuropathy with 3 months: 16% vs. 43% (P < .0001) [p.102]

### Other Trials

Other trials, including the phase III KCSG CO09-07 trial, have reported similar results when comparing 3 to 6 months of adjuvant therapy in patients with stage II–III colon cancer [p.102].

### NCCN Recommendations Based on Duration Data

Based on these data [[p.102]:
- 3 months of CAPEOX or 3 to 6 months of FOLFOX: Listed as preferred adjuvant therapy options for patients with low-risk stage III colon cancer [p.102]
- 3 to 6 months of CAPEOX or 6 months of FOLFOX: Listed as preferred adjuvant therapy options for patients with high-risk stage III colon cancer [p.102]
- 6 months of infusional 5-FU/LV or single-agent capecitabine: Included as other adjuvant therapy options for low- or high-risk stage III colon cancer [p.102]
- For stage II colon cancer at high risk for systemic recurrence:
  - Recommended options: 6 months of capecitabine, 5-FU/LV, or FOLFOX, or 3 months of CAPEOX [p.102]
  - Observation may also be an appropriate option [p.102]
  - No adjuvant treatment option is preferred over the others [p.102]
