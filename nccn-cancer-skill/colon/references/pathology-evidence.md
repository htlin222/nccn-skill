## Summary of Key Evidence

Colorectal cancers are usually staged after surgical exploration of the abdomen and pathologic examination of the surgical specimen [p.81].

## Pathologic Examination Criteria

The criteria that should be included in the report of the pathologic evaluation [p.81]:

- Grade of the cancer
- Depth of penetration and extension to adjacent structures (T)
- Number of regional lymph nodes evaluated
- Number of positive regional lymph nodes (N)
- Assessment of the presence of distant metastases to other organs, to the peritoneum or an abdominal structure, or in non-regional lymph nodes (M)
- Status of proximal, distal, radial, and mesenteric margins
- Lymphovascular invasion
- Perineural invasion (PNI)
- Tumor deposits

The prefixes "p" and "yp" used in TNM staging denote "pathologic staging" and "pathologic staging after neoadjuvant therapy and surgery," respectively [p.82].

## Margins

### Radial Margin Assessment

In colon cancer, the radial margin (or circumferential resection margin, CRM) represents the adventitial soft tissue closest to the deepest penetration of the tumor [p.82]. It is created surgically by blunt or sharp dissection of the retroperitoneal aspect, and it corresponds to any aspect of the colon that is not covered by a serosal layer of mesothelial cells [p.82]. The serosal (peritoneal) surface does not constitute a surgical margin [p.82].

The radial margins should be assessed in all colonic segments with non-peritonealized surfaces [p.82]. In segments of the colon that are completely encased by peritoneum, such as the transverse colon, the mesenteric resection margin is the only relevant radial margin [p.82].

#### Surgeon Recommendation

On pathologic examination, it is difficult to appreciate the demarcation between the peritonealized surface and the non-peritonealized surface. The surgeon is therefore encouraged to mark the area of non-peritonealized surface with a clip or suture [p.82].

#### Clinical Significance of CRM Status

In a study of 608 patients with rectal cancer, a positive radial margin was shown to be a negative prognostic factor for both local recurrence and OS [p.82]:
- CRM-positive resections: 38.2% local recurrence rate [p.82]
- CRM-negative resections: 10.0% local recurrence rate [p.82]

## Lymph Nodes

### Number of Lymph Nodes Examined

The number of lymph nodes evaluated is important to note on the pathology report [p.82]. A secondary analysis of patients from the Intergroup Trial INT-0089 showed that an increase in the number of lymph nodes examined was associated with increased survival for patients with both node-negative and node-positive disease [p.82].

Results from population-based studies show an association between improvement in survival and examination of ≥12 lymph nodes [p.82].

#### Mechanism and Factors Influencing Lymph Node Yield

The mechanism for this correlation is poorly understood [p.82]. Several factors influence lymph node harvest:
- The extent and quality of surgical resection can have an impact on the node harvest [p.82]
- The number of regional lymph nodes retrieved also varies with age of the patient, sex, and tumor grade or site [p.82]
- Patients with a strong anti-cancer immune response may have more easily found lymph nodes and improved prognosis [p.82]
- MSI and wild-type KRAS/BRAF have been associated with improved prognosis and increased lymph node retrieval [p.82]

### Panel Recommendation for Lymph Node Examination

The Panel recommends examination of ≥12 lymph nodes [p.82]. This recommendation is supported by CAP and the 8th edition of the AJCC Cancer Staging Manual [p.82].

Notably, emerging evidence suggests that a greater number of nodes may need to be examined in some situations, particularly for T4 lesions, to provide an adequate assessment of disease stage [p.82].

For stage II (pN0) colon cancer, it is recommended that the pathologist go back to the specimen and submit more tissue of potential lymph nodes if <12 nodes were initially identified [p.82]. Patients considered to have N0 disease but for whom <12 nodes have been examined are suboptimally staged and should be considered to be at higher risk [p.82].

### Lymph Node Ratio

The ratio of positive lymph nodes to the total number of lymph nodes examined is also being evaluated for possible prognostic impact [p.83].

A systematic review and meta-analysis of 33 studies that included >75,000 patients with node-positive CRC concluded that a higher lymph node ratio was significantly associated with shorter OS and disease-free survival (DFS) [p.83]. However, analysis of the SEER database suggests that the lymph node ratio does not adequately represent the different effects of both the number of positive lymph nodes and the number of lymph nodes examined [p.83].

### Sentinel Lymph Node Evaluation

The potential benefit of sentinel lymph node evaluation for colon cancer has mostly been associated with providing more accurate staging of nodal pathology through detection of micrometastatic disease in the sentinel node(s) [p.83].

Results of studies evaluating the sentinel node for micrometastatic disease through use of hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining to identify small foci of tumor cells and the identification of particular tumor antigens through immunohistochemistry (IHC) have been reported [p.83].

### Micrometastases and Isolated Tumor Cells

There is potential benefit of assessing regional lymph nodes for micrometastases and isolated tumor cells [p.83].

The 8th edition of the AJCC Cancer Staging Manual considers clusters of 10 to 20 tumor cells, or clumps of tumor that measure ≥0.2 mm in diameter, but <2 mm in diameter, to be micrometastases [p.83]. Such micrometastases have been shown to be a poor prognostic factor.

#### Clinical Significance of Micrometastases

One study of 312 consecutive patients with pN0 disease found that positive cytokeratin staining was associated with a higher risk of recurrence [p.83]:
- Relapse in patients with positive nodes: 14%
- Relapse in patients with negative nodes: 4.7%
- HR: 3.00 (95% CI, 1.23–7.32; P = .013) [p.83]

A 2012 systematic review and meta-analysis found decreased survival in patients with pN0 tumors with IHC or reverse transcriptase polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) evidence of tumor cells in regional nodes [p.83].

A 2014 meta-analysis found that the presence of micrometastases increases the likelihood of disease recurrence [p.83].

## Tumor Deposits

Tumor deposits, also called extranodal tumor deposits, peritumoral deposits, or satellite nodules, are irregular discrete tumor deposits in the pericolic or perirectal fat that show no evidence of residual lymph node tissue but are within the lymphatic drainage of the primary tumor [p.83]. They are not counted as lymph nodes replaced by tumor [p.83].

Most tumor deposits are thought to arise from lymphovascular invasion or, occasionally, perineural invasion [p.83].

The number of tumor deposits should be recorded in the pathology report, because they have been shown to be associated with reductions in DFS and OS [p.83].

### Prognostic Impact of Tumor Deposits

Multivariate survival analysis in one study showed [p.83]:
- pN0 tumors without satellite nodules: 91.5% 5-year survival
- pN0 tumors with satellite nodules: 37.0% 5-year survival
- P < .0001 [p.83]

## Perineural Invasion

Several studies have shown that the presence of perineural invasion (PNI) is associated with a significantly worse prognosis [p.83].

### Clinical Outcomes with PNI

One retrospective analysis of 269 consecutive patients who had colorectal tumors resected at one institution found a four-fold greater 5-year survival in patients with tumors without PNI versus patients whose tumors invaded nearby neural structures [p.83].

Multivariate analysis of patients with stage II rectal cancer showed that patients with tumors with PNI have a significantly worse 5-year DFS compared with those without PNI [p.83]:
- With PNI: 29%
- Without PNI: 82%
- P = .0005 [p.83]

Similar results were seen for patients with stage III disease [p.83].

### Meta-Analysis of PNI

A meta-analysis that included 58 studies and 22,900 patients found that PNI is associated with [p.83]:
- Worse 5-year OS: RR, 2.09 (95% CI, 1.68–2.61) [p.83]
- Worse 5-year DFS: RR, 2.35 (95% CI, 1.66–3.31) [p.83]

PNI is therefore included as a high-risk factor for systemic recurrence [p.83].
