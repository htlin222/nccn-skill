## Summary of Key Evidence

All recommendations are category 2A unless otherwise indicated. [p.23]

## Imaging Principles

### Initial Staging Assessment

- **C/A/P CT** (chest/abdomen/pelvis computed tomography) [p.23]
- Consider **abdomen/pelvis MRI** to assist with diagnosis of rectal cancer versus colon cancer (e.g., low-lying sigmoid tumor). [p.23]
- Consider **MRI of liver** for liver metastases if potentially resectable. [p.23]
- **FDG-PET/CT** is not routinely indicated. [p.23]

### Metastatic Disease Assessment

- **C/A/P CT with contrast** [p.24]
- **FDG-PET/CT**, **FDG-PET/MRI**, or **contrast-enhanced MRI** can be considered for assessment of response and liver recurrence after image-guided liver-directed therapy. [p.25]
- If liver-directed therapy or surgery is contemplated, a hepatic **MRI with intravenous routine extracellular or hepatobiliary GBCA** is preferred over CT. [p.24]

## Pathologic Assessment

### Malignant Polyps (pT1 Disease)

A malignant polyp is defined as one with cancer invading through the muscularis mucosa and into the submucosa (pT1). pTis is not considered a malignant polyp. [p.27]

**Favorable histologic features:** [p.27]
- Low-grade histology (according to WHO 2019; formerly well to moderately differentiated)
- No angiolymphatic invasion
- Adequate depth of invasion [p.27]

**Unfavorable histologic features:** [p.27]
- High-grade histology (according to WHO 2019; formerly poorly differentiated)
- Angiolymphatic invasion
- Significant depth of submucosa invaded [p.27]

### Pathologic Parameters

The following parameters should be reported: [p.27]

**Radial (Circumferential) Margin Evaluation:** The serosal surface (peritoneal) does not constitute a surgical margin. In colon cancer, the circumferential margin should be evaluated. [p.28]

**Perineural Invasion (PNI):** The presence of PNI is associated with a significantly worse prognosis. In multivariate analysis, PNI has been shown to be an independent adverse (high-risk) factor. [p.28]

**Tumor Deposits:** Irregular discrete tumor deposits in pericolic or perirectal fat away from the leading edge of the tumor and showing no evidence of residual lymph node tissue are considered positive findings. [p.28]

**Tumor Budding:** High tumor budding has been identified as a new prognostic factor in colon cancer. Several studies have shown that high-tier tumor budding in pT1 colorectal cancers (CRCs), including malignant polyps, is associated with an increased risk of lymph node metastasis. [p.28]

## Lymph Node Evaluation

- The **AJCC** and **College of American Pathologists** recommend examination of a minimum of 12 lymph nodes to accurately stage colon cancers. [p.29]
- For **stage II (pN0) colon cancer**, if <12 lymph nodes are initially identified, it is recommended that the pathologist go back to the specimen and resubmit more tissue. If 12 lymph nodes are still not identified, a comment in the report should indicate that an extensive search for lymph nodes was undertaken. [p.29]

**Isolated Tumor Clusters (ITC):** Defined as single cells or small groups of cells measuring <0.2 mm. The biologic significance of ITCs is currently not well characterized. Lymph nodes with ITC are defined as negative (N0) in the 9th edition of the AJCC staging system. [p.29]

**Micrometastatic Tumor Deposits:** Measure between 0.2 mm–2.0 mm and lymph nodes involved with micrometastatic deposits are considered positive lymph nodes. [p.29]

## Molecular Testing Methods

- Testing can be performed on **formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded tissue** or **cytology material (preferred)** or **blood-based circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA)/RNA**. [p.29]
- **Repeat molecular testing** should not be performed after standard cytotoxic chemotherapy, as significant molecular changes are rarely observed. [p.29]

## KRAS, NRAS, and BRAF Testing

- **All patients with metastatic CRC** should have tumor genotyped for **RAS (KRAS and NRAS)** and **BRAF mutations** as part of MGPT at initial diagnosis of metastatic disease. [p.29]
- Patients with any known **KRAS mutation (exons 2, 3, and 4)** or **NRAS mutation (exons 2, 3, and 4)** should not be treated with either **cetuximab** or **panitumumab**, unless given as part of a regimen targeting a **KRAS G12C mutation**. [p.29]
- **BRAF V600E mutation** makes response to panitumumab or cetuximab highly unlikely unless given with a **BRAF inhibitor**. [p.29]
- **BRAF V600E mutation testing** via **immunohistochemistry (IHC)** is also an option. [p.29]
- Testing must be performed only in laboratories certified under the **Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments of 1988 (CLIA-88)**. [p.29]

## Microsatellite Instability (MSI) or Mismatch Repair (MMR) Testing

- **Universal MMR or MSI testing** is recommended in all newly diagnosed patients with colon cancer. [p.30]
- **Stage II (MSI-H) cancers** may have a good prognosis and do not benefit from fluorouracil adjuvant therapy. [p.30]
- **MMR or MSI testing** should be performed only in **CLIA-approved laboratories**. [p.30]

### Testing Methodologies

**MSI Testing:** Involves looking for a change in the number of microsatellite repeats in tumor DNA compared to non-neoplastic tissue. This may be accomplished by multiplex polymerase chain reaction (PCR) followed by capillary electrophoresis or as part of a validated MGPT. [p.30]

**IHC for MMR:** Interrogates tumor tissue for protein expression of four MMR genes: MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, and PMS2. [p.30]
- **pMMR:** Tumors that show nuclear expression of all four MMR proteins. [p.30]
- **dMMR:** Tumors that reveal loss of expression of any MMR protein. [p.30]

**BRAF V600E Mutation:** In the setting of MLH1 absence, approximately 1% of cancers with BRAF V600E mutations (and loss of MLH1) are Lynch syndrome (LS). Caution should be exercised in excluding patients with a strong family history from germline screening. [p.30]

## HER2 Testing

- **Diagnostic testing** is via **IHC**, **fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH)**, or **next-generation sequencing (NGS)**. [p.31]
- **Positive by IHC** is defined as: **3+ staining in >50% of tumor cells**. [p.31]
- **Anti-HER2 therapy** with signal transduction inhibition (e.g., trastuzumab/pertuzumab, trastuzumab/tucatinib) is indicated for patients with **HER2 (ERBB2) positive tumors**. [p.31]
- **Fam-trastuzumab deruxtecan-nxki** is only indicated in **HER2-overexpressed tumors (IHC 3+)**. [p.31]

## NTRK Gene Fusions

- **NTRK 1/2/3 gene fusions** are extremely rare in CRCs. The overall incidence is approximately **0.35%** in a cohort of 2314 CRCs. [p.31]
- **TRK inhibitors** have been shown to have activity ONLY in those cases with NTRK 1/2/3 gene fusions, and NOT with NTRK 1/2/3 gene point mutations. [p.31]
- Methodologies for detecting NTRK 1/2/3 gene fusions are **IHC**, **FISH**, **DNA-based NGS**, and **RNA-based NGS**. [p.31]

## POLE/POLD1 Mutations

- **Polymerase genes, POLE and POLD1**, encode proteins tasked with proofreading functions to recognize and correct mispaired bases. [p.32]
- **Somatic POLE mutations** occur in approximately **2%–8%** of patients with predominately MSS/pMMR CRC while **somatic POLD1 mutations** are rare. [p.32]
- **NGS of CRCs** in patients with either germline or somatic exonuclease domain (ED) pathogenic variants demonstrate an **ultra-hypermutated phenotype** with extremely high **TMB (>50 mut/Mb)**. [p.32]
- **POLE/POLD1 mutations** can be identified through **single gene assays (PCR or Sanger sequencing)**. However, **TMB calculation requires a MGPT**. [p.32]
- **Patients with CRC harboring POLE/POLD1 mutations** have a more favorable prognosis, likely secondary to immune responses stimulated by the ultra-hypermutated phenotype. [p.32]

## RET Gene Fusions

- **RET** is a receptor tyrosine kinase that plays a critical role in the development and maintenance of neural and genitourinary tissues. [p.32]
- **Somatic activating alterations in RET** include point mutations as well as gene fusions. [p.32]
- In patients with CRC, **activating RET gene fusions** involving the C-terminal kinase domain lead to constitutive upregulation of RET kinase activity. [p.32]
- **Selpercatinib** (RET-targeted inhibitor) is FDA-approved for patients with solid tumors harboring activating RET gene fusions. [p.32]
- The presence of **RET gene fusions** can be interrogated through a variety of techniques, including **IHC**, **FISH**, **PCR**, and either **DNA- or RNA-based NGS**. [p.32]
- Caution should be used in interpreting **RET gene fusion results** from ctDNA/RNA assays following targeted therapy with **EGFR inhibitors**. [p.32]

## Surgical Principles

### Colectomy

**Lymphadenectomy:** [p.36]
- Proper technique for lymphadenectomy requires **proximal ligation of the associated vascular pedicle(s)** with **en bloc removal of the colonic segment and its mesentery**. [p.36]
- **Clinically positive lymph nodes** outside the standard field of resection that are considered suspicious should be **biopsied or removed**, if possible. [p.36]
- **Positive nodes left behind** indicate an **incomplete (R2) resection**. [p.36]
- **A minimum of 12 lymph nodes** should be examined to adequately establish N stage. [p.36]
- **Resection needs to be complete** to be considered curative. [p.36]

### Minimally Invasive Approaches

**Laparoscopic or robot-assisted colectomy** may be performed with the following considerations: [p.36]
- The surgeon has experience performing minimally invasive colorectal operations [p.36]
- **Preoperative localization** is performed (e.g., radiographic identification, preoperative endoscopic marking, endoscopic landmarks) [p.36]
- **Thorough abdominal exploration and assessment** can be performed [p.36]
- **Generally not indicated** for:
  - Locally advanced cancer (e.g., invasion into adjacent structure) [p.36]
  - Acute bowel obstruction or perforation from cancer [p.36]
  - May be considered with appropriate surgeon experience [p.36]

### Surgical Considerations for Hereditary Syndromes

## Locoregional Therapies

### Image-Guided Tumor Ablation

**Thermal Ablation:** Creates tumor cell death through deposition of tumoricidal heat (radiofrequency or microwave) or cold (cryoablation) in the tumor and surrounding margins. [p.36]

**Non-thermal Ablation:** Irreversible electroporation creates tumor cell death through electrical pulses that create irreversible membrane pores and cellular lysis/destruction. [p.36]

### Liver Tumor Ablation

**Thermal Ablation:** [p.36-37]
- Can be considered **alone, or in conjunction with surgery**, in appropriately selected patients with small metastases that can be treated with margins
- **All original sites of disease** need to be amenable to thermal ablation or resection
- **Image-guided thermal ablation** may be considered in selected surgical candidates or medically nonsurgical candidates with small tumors that can be completely ablated with margins
- Can be considered in selected patients with **recurrence after hepatectomy or ablation** as long as **all visible disease can be ablated with margins**

**Non-thermal Ablation (Irreversible Electroporation):** [p.37]
- Can be considered in patients whose tumors **cannot be safely resected or ablated with margins** due to proximity to central bile ducts or other structures that cannot be protected

### Lung Tumor Ablation

**Thermal Ablation:** [p.37]
- **Ablative techniques** may be considered **alone or in conjunction with resection** for resectable disease
- **All original sites of disease** need to be amenable to thermal ablation or resection
- Can be considered when **unresectable and amenable to complete thermal ablation**
- May be considered in selected surgical candidates with small tumors that can be completely ablated with margins
- May be considered for **recurrences after surgery or prior ablation** as long as all visible disease is amenable to thermal ablation

### Arterially Directed Embolic Therapy

**Hepatic Transarterial Radioembolization (TARE) with Yttrium-90 (Y-90) Microspheres:** [p.37]

- **Y-90 radioembolization (radiation lobectomy approach)** can be considered instead of portal vein embolization when:
  - Hepatic metastatic disease is not optimally resectable based on insufficient remnant liver volume [p.37]
  - Borderline resectable disease that would benefit from tumor downsizing and remnant hypertrophy [p.37]

- **Hepatic TARE with Y-90 microspheres** can be considered in:
  - Selected patients with chemotherapy-resistant/refractory disease and predominant hepatic metastases [p.37]

- **Radiation segmentectomy approach** can be considered for small tumors non-eligible for resection or thermal ablation [p.37]

**Transarterial Chemoembolization (TACE):** [p.37]

- **TACE** involves **hepatic artery catheterization** to **locally deliver chemotherapy in combination with arterial embolization** [p.37]
- Can be considered in **highly selected patients** with:
  - Chemotherapy-resistant/refractory disease [p.37]
  - Preserved liver function [p.37]
  - Predominant hepatic metastases [p.37]

**Drug-Eluting Bead TACE (DEB-TACE):** [p.37]
- **Most commonly accepted variation** for treatment of metastatic CRC
- Uses **irinotecan** as the chemotherapeutic agent (DEBIRI) [p.37]
- **DEBIRI** can be used along with **irinotecan-based chemotherapy** for **unresectable liver-dominant disease** [p.37]

## Surveillance Recommendations

- Surveillance recommendations can be found on COL-8 [p.67]
- Long-term surveillance should be carefully managed with routine good medical care and monitoring, including cancer screening, routine health care, and preventive care [p.67]
- Routine CEA monitoring and routine CT scanning are not recommended beyond 5 years [p.67]

## Survivorship Care Planning

The oncologist and primary care provider should have defined roles in the surveillance period, with roles communicated to patient [p.67]

Develop survivorship care plan that includes [p.67]:

- Overall summary of treatment, including all surgeries, radiation treatments, and chemotherapy received
- Description of possible expected time to resolution of acute toxicities, long-term effects of treatment, and possible late sequelae of treatment
- Surveillance recommendations
- Delineation of appropriate timing of transfer of care with specific responsibilities identified for primary care physician and oncologist
- Health behavior recommendations
- Fertility counseling

## Management of Late/Long-Term Sequelae of Disease or Treatment

For issues related to distress, pain, neuropathy, fatigue, or sexual dysfunction, see NCCN Guidelines for Survivorship [p.67]

### Chronic Diarrhea or Incontinence

Consider the following approaches [p.67]:

- Anti-diarrheal agents
- Bulk-forming agents
- Diet manipulation
- Pelvic floor rehabilitation
- Protective undergarments

### Management of an Ostomy

Consider the following [p.67]:

- Participation in an ostomy support group or coordination of care with a health care provider specializing in ostomy care (e.g., ostomy nurse)
- Screen for distress around body changes (NCCN Guidelines for Distress Management)
- Precautions around involvement with physical activity (see page SPA-C in the NCCN Guidelines for Survivorship)

### Oxaliplatin-Induced Neuropathy

Treatment options [p.67]:

- Consider duloxetine for painful neuropathy only; not effective for numbness, tingling, or cold sensitivity [p.67]
- Consider non-pharmacologic therapies such as heat or acupuncture [p.67]
- Pregabalin or gabapentin are not recommended [p.67]

## Counseling Regarding Healthy Lifestyle and Wellness

Per NCCN Guidelines for Survivorship [p.67]:

- Undergo all age- and gender-appropriate cancer and preventive health screenings as per national guidelines [p.67]
- Maintain a healthy body weight throughout life [p.67]
- Adopt a physically active lifestyle. Discuss and facilitate options to achieve a goal of at least 150 minutes of moderate to vigorous physical activity per week. Activity recommendations may require modification based on treatment sequelae (e.g., ostomy, neuropathy) [p.67]
- Consume a healthy diet with emphasis on plant sources. Diet recommendations may be modified based on severity of bowel dysfunction [p.67]
- Consider daily aspirin 325 mg for secondary prevention [p.67]
- Drink alcohol sparingly, if at all [p.67]
- Receive smoking cessation counseling as appropriate (NCCN Guidelines for Smoking Cessation) [p.67]

Additional health monitoring and immunizations should be performed as indicated under the care of a primary care physician. Survivors are encouraged to maintain a therapeutic relationship with a primary care physician throughout their lifetime [p.67].
