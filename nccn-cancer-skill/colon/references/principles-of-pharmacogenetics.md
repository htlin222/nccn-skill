# Principles of Pharmacogenetics (COL-J)

<!-- Source: NCCN Colon Cancer v2.2026, pages 69-69 -->

## Overview

Pharmacogenetics (PGx) is the study of how genetic factors influence an individual's response to drugs [p.69]. PGx aims to inform both the choice of drug and dosage in an effort to maximize efficacy and minimize toxicity [p.69].

Drugs given at the same dose in the same schedule for the same disease may have different effects on patients due to inherent differences in drug disposition and metabolism related to genetic alterations [p.69]. These differences may affect the anti-tumor efficacy of agents and/or may alter the risk and degree of toxicity [p.69].

## Testing Considerations

PGx testing may be considered prior to the initiation of chemotherapies known to be subject to variable metabolism that might predictably change the relative risk of severe toxicity or drug exposure [p.69].

Historically, there have been barriers to implementing global clinical PGx testing, particularly the concern of delaying the initiation of therapy while awaiting results [p.69]. Additionally, it is necessary to have consensus on an optimal testing platform, the expertise to interpret what is often highly complex data, and to offer providers guidance in applying the information to a given patient [p.69].

**Consider consulting a clinical pharmacist or PGx specialist if available to aid in drug dose adjustments based on interpretation and evaluation of PGx test results [p.69].**

## UGT1A1: Irinotecan

### Genetic Variants

- **UGT1A1*6 allele**: Represents a mutation in the coding sequence (c.211G>A) that results in decreased function [p.69]
- **UGT1A1*28 allele**: Represents a dinucleotide repeat (TA7) in the promoter region that also results in decreased function [p.69]
- **WT UGT1A1 genotype**: Individuals with wild-type UGT1A1 genotype are considered to have normal UGT1A1 function, represented as the *1 allele [p.69]. This designation is dependent on which regions of UGT1A1 have been tested [p.69].

### Increased Risk of Severe Toxicity

Studies show that patients with the following diplotypes are at increased risk of severe neutropenia during treatment with irinotecan [p.69]:

- Homozygous UGT1A1*28/*28 and UGT1A1*6/*6
- Compound heterozygous UGT1A1*28/*6

See FDA website for additional information [p.69].

These patients are considered UGT1A1 poor metabolizers and have increased exposure to the active metabolite of irinotecan, SN-38, due to decreased UGT1A1 function [p.69].

### Dosing Recommendations

**Poor Metabolizers (UGT1A1*28/*28):**

Consider dose reduction of the initial dose of irinotecan by 30% for patients with UGT1A1*28/*28 alleles [p.69]. The dose may be titrated upwards in subsequent cycles if it is well-tolerated [p.69].

**Intermediate Metabolizers (UGT1A1*1/*28, UGT1A1*1/*6):**

Patients who are heterozygous for either the UGT1A1*28 or *6 alleles (*1/*28, *1/*6) are considered intermediate metabolizers and may also have a risk of severe neutropenia [p.69]. However, current evidence does not support a preemptive dose reduction for UGT1A1 intermediate metabolizers [p.69].

### Clinical Availability

Currently, PGx testing for UGT1A1 is commercially available [p.69].
