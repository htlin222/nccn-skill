## Familial Risk and Inherited Syndromes

### Epidemiology of Familial CRC

Approximately 20% of colon cancer cases are associated with familial clustering, and first-degree relatives of patients with colorectal adenomas or invasive CRC are at increased risk for CRC [p.78].

Genetic susceptibility to CRC includes well-defined inherited syndromes, such as [p.78]:
- Lynch syndrome (also known as hereditary nonpolyposis CRC [HNPCC])
- Familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP)

### Risk Assessment Recommendations

It is recommended that all patients with colon cancer be queried regarding their family history and considered for risk assessment, as detailed in the NCCN Guidelines for Colorectal Cancer Screening [p.78].

Results from a randomized controlled trial (RCT) suggest that most individuals without a personal history of CRC and with one first-degree relative with CRC diagnosed before age 50 years or two first-degree relatives with CRC diagnosed at any age can safely be screened with colonoscopy every 6 years [p.78].

## CRC Molecular Classification

### Heterogeneous Disease Biology

CRC is a heterogeneous disease [p.78]. An international consortium recently reported a molecular classification, defining four different subtypes [p.78]:

#### CMS1 (Microsatellite Instability Immune)
- Hypermutated, microsatellite unstable
- Strong immune activation
- --> See Lynch Syndrome and Microsatellite Instability [p.78]

#### CMS2 (Canonical)
- Epithelial, chromosomally unstable
- Marked WNT and MYC signaling activation [p.78]

#### CMS3 (Metabolic)
- Epithelial
- Evident metabolic dysregulation [p.78]

#### CMS4 (Mesenchymal)
- Prominent transforming growth factor β activation
- Stromal invasion
- Angiogenesis [p.78]

### Clinical Application Status

This classification is not yet recommended in clinical practice [p.78].

## Lynch Syndrome

### Definition and Epidemiology

Lynch syndrome is the most common form of genetically determined colon cancer predisposition, accounting for 2% to 4% of all CRC cases [p.79].

This hereditary syndrome results from germline mutations in DNA mismatch repair (MMR) genes [p.79]:
- MLH1
- MSH2
- MSH6
- PMS2

### Diagnostic Testing

Identifying a germline mutation in an MMR gene through sequencing is definitive for Lynch syndrome [p.79]. However, patients usually undergo selection by considering family history and performing an initial test on tumor tissue before sequencing [p.79].

#### Initial Tumor-Based Testing

Two different initial tests can be performed on CRC specimens to identify individuals who might have Lynch syndrome [p.79]:

1. **Immunohistochemical (IHC) Analysis:** Tests for MMR protein expression, which is often diminished because of mutation [p.79]

2. **Microsatellite Instability (MSI) Analysis:** Results from MMR deficiency (dMMR) and is detected as changes in the length of repetitive DNA elements in tumor tissue caused by the insertion or deletion of repeated units [p.79]

#### BRAF and MLH1 Promoter Testing

Testing the BRAF gene for mutation is indicated when IHC shows that MLH1 expression is absent in the tumor [p.79]. The presence of a BRAF mutation indicates that MLH1 expression is down-regulated through somatic methylation of the promoter region of the gene and not through a germline mutation [p.79].

Testing for MLH1 promoter methylation may also be used to determine this [p.79].

### Universal Screening Approach

Many NCCN Member Institutions and other comprehensive cancer centers now perform IHC and sometimes MSI testing on all newly diagnosed colorectal and endometrial cancers regardless of family history to determine which patients should have genetic testing for Lynch syndrome [p.79].

The cost effectiveness of this approach, referred to as universal or reflex testing, has been confirmed for CRC [p.79]. This approach has been endorsed by [p.79]:
- The Evaluation of Genomic Applications in Practice and Prevention (EGAPP) working group at the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)
- The American Society for Clinical Pathology (ASCP)
- College of American Pathologists (CAP)
- Association for Molecular Pathology (AMP)
- American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO)

The U.S. Multi-Society Task Force on Colorectal Cancer also recommends universal genetic testing of tumors of all patients with newly diagnosed CRC, as does the American Gastroenterological Association [p.79]. The Cleveland Clinic recently reported on its experiences implementing such a screening approach [p.79].

### NCCN Panel Recommendation

**The NCCN Colon/Rectal Cancer Panel endorses universal MMR or MSI testing of all patients with a personal history of colon or rectal cancer to identify individuals with Lynch syndrome [p.79].**

#### Clinical Relevance

This testing is also relevant for [p.79]:
- Adjuvant therapy planning for stage II disease
- Treatment selection in stage IV disease
- --> See Microsatellite Instability and Checkpoint Inhibitor Immunotherapy for dMMR/MSI-H or POLE/POLD1 Mutation-Positive Disease in the First-Line and Non-First-Line Settings

An infrastructure needs to be in place to handle the screening results [p.79]. A more detailed discussion is available in the NCCN Guidelines for Colorectal Cancer Screening [p.79].

## The Role of Vitamin D in CRC

### Evidence from Observational Studies

Prospective studies have suggested that vitamin D deficiency may contribute to CRC incidence and/or that vitamin D supplementation may decrease CRC risk [p.79].

Furthermore, several prospective studies have shown that low vitamin D levels are associated with increased mortality of patients with CRC [p.79].

### Meta-Analyses on Vitamin D and Mortality

A systematic review and meta-analysis of five studies totaling 2,330 patients with CRC compared the outcomes of patients in the highest and lowest categories of vitamin D levels and found [p.79]:
- **Overall Survival (OS):** HR = 0.71; 95% CI, 0.55–0.91 (better survival with higher vitamin D levels)
- **Disease-Specific Mortality:** HR = 0.65; 95% CI, 0.49–0.86 (better survival with higher vitamin D levels)

Another meta-analysis determined that the relationship between vitamin D levels and mortality is linear [p.79].

### Intervention Trial Results

#### Adenoma Recurrence Study

A recent randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial, however, showed that supplementation with vitamin D and/or calcium had no effect on the recurrence of colorectal adenomas within 3 to 5 years after removal of adenomas in 2,259 participants [p.80].

A later analysis of the same study reported that the effect of vitamin D supplementation on recurrence of advanced adenomas varied significantly based on the genotype of the vitamin D receptor, indicating that only individuals with specific vitamin D receptor alleles may benefit from vitamin D supplementation for prevention of advanced adenomas [p.80].

#### SUNSHINE Trial (Advanced CRC)

The randomized, double-blind, phase II SUNSHINE trial reported a longer progression-free survival (PFS) for patients with previously untreated mCRC randomized to standard treatment plus high-dose vitamin D supplementation compared to those randomized to standard treatment plus low-dose vitamin D supplementation [p.80]:
- High-dose: 13.0 months
- Low-dose: 11.0 months
- HR = 0.64; 95% CI, 0–0.90; P = 0.02

However, this difference was not significant [p.80]. There was also no significant difference between high- and standard-dose vitamin D supplementation for overall response rate (ORR) or overall survival (OS) [p.80].

### Current Recommendations

In a 2010 report, the Institute of Medicine (now known as the National Academy of Medicine) concluded that data supporting a role for vitamin D were only conclusive in bone health, and not in cancer and other diseases [p.80].

**Citing this report and the lack of level 1 evidence, the Panel does not currently recommend routine screening for vitamin D deficiency or supplementation of vitamin D in patients with CRC [p.80].**

## Other Risk Factors for CRC

### Inflammatory Bowel Disease

It is well-recognized that individuals with inflammatory bowel disease are at an increased risk for CRC [p.80]:
- Ulcerative colitis
- Crohn's disease

### Lifestyle and Dietary Risk Factors

Possible risk factors for the development of CRC include [p.80]:
- Smoking
- Consumption of red and processed meats
- Alcohol consumption
- Diabetes mellitus
- Low levels of physical activity
- Metabolic syndrome
- Obesity/high body mass index (BMI)

#### Healthy Lifestyle Factors Study

In the EPIC cohort of almost 350,000 individuals, those who adhered to five healthy lifestyle factors had an HR for the development of CRC of 0.63 (95% CI, 0.54–0.74) compared with those who adhered to one or fewer of the factors [p.80]. The five healthy lifestyle factors are [p.80]:
- Healthy weight
- Physical activity
- Non-smoking
- Limited alcohol consumption
- Healthy diet

Other large studies support the conclusion that adherence to healthy lifestyle factors can reduce the risk of CRC [p.80].

### Dairy Consumption

Some data suggest that consumption of dairy may lower risk for the development of CRC [p.80]. However, a systematic review and meta-analysis of 15 cohort studies (>900,000 patients; >5,200 cases of CRC) only found an association between risk for colon cancer in males and the consumption of nonfermented milk [p.80].

No association was seen for [p.80]:
- Rectal cancer in males
- Colon or rectal cancer in females
- Either cancer in either sex with consumption of solid cheese or fermented milk

### Other Dietary Factors and Medication

Large cohort studies and meta-analyses suggest that other dietary factors may lower the risk for CRC, including the consumption of fish and legumes [p.80].

Furthermore, the use of aspirin or nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) may also decrease the risk for CRC, although evidence supporting this association is limited and variable [p.80].

#### Aspirin for Prevention

While the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) guidance previously recommended daily low-dose aspirin for CRC prevention, the 2022 update concluded that there was insufficient evidence that aspirin use reduces CRC incidence [p.80].

### Risk Factors Associated with Poor Prognosis

Some data suggest that smoking, metabolic syndrome, obesity, and red/processed meat consumption are associated with a poor prognosis [p.80].

Conversely, post-diagnosis fish consumption may be associated with a better prognosis [p.80].
