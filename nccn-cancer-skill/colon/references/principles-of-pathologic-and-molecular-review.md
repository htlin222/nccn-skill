## Endoscopically Removed Malignant Polyps

### Definition and Features [p.27]

A malignant polyp is defined as one with cancer invading through the muscularis mucosa and into the submucosa (pT1). pTis is not considered a "malignant polyp" [p.27].

### Favorable Histologic Features [p.27]

- Low-grade histology (according to WHO 2019; formerly well to moderately differentiated) [p.27]
- No angiolymphatic invasion [p.27]
- Depth of submucosal invasion <1 mm [p.27]
- Low tumor budding [p.27]
- Negative margin of resection [p.27]
- Positive margin definition: tumor cells present at or <1 mm from the transected margin [p.27]

### Unfavorable Histologic Features [p.27]

- High-grade histology (according to WHO 2019; formerly poorly differentiated) [p.27]
- Angiolymphatic invasion [p.27]
- Depth of submucosal invasion >1 mm [p.27]
- High tumor budding [p.27]
- "Positive margin" (tumor cells at or <1 mm from transected margin) [p.27]
- High tumor budding has been associated with adverse outcome and may preclude polypectomy as adequate treatment of malignant polyps [p.27]

### Sessile Malignant Polyps [p.27]

There is controversy as to whether malignant colorectal polyps with a sessile configuration can be successfully treated by endoscopic removal. Endoscopically removed sessile malignant polyps have a significantly greater incidence of adverse outcomes (residual disease, recurrent disease, mortality, and hematogenous metastasis, but not lymph node metastasis) than pedunculated malignant polyps [p.27].

However, endoscopically removed malignant sessile polyps with low-grade histology, <1-mm submucosal invasion, negative margins, and no lymphovascular invasion can be successfully treated with endoscopic polypectomy [p.27].

## Colon Cancer Appropriate for Resection

### Histologic Confirmation [p.27]

Histologic confirmation of primary colonic malignant neoplasm is required [p.27].

## Pathologic Stage

### Reporting Parameters [p.27]

The following parameters should be reported [p.27]:
- Grade of the cancer [p.27]
- Depth of invasion (T) [p.27]
- Number of lymph nodes evaluated and number positive (N) [p.27]
- Status of proximal, distal, radial, and mesenteric margins [p.27]
- Lymphovascular invasion [p.27]
- Perineural invasion (PNI) [p.27]
- Tumor deposits [p.27]

### Radial (Circumferential) Margin Evaluation [p.28]

The serosal surface (peritoneal) does not constitute a surgical margin. In colon cancer, the circumferential (radial) margin represents the adventitial soft tissue closest to the deepest penetration of tumor, created surgically by blunt or sharp dissection of the retroperitoneal aspect [p.28].

The radial margins should be assessed in all colonic segments with non-peritonealized surfaces. The circumferential resection margin corresponds to any aspect of the colon not covered by a serosal layer of mesothelial cells and must be dissected from the retroperitoneum [p.28].

The mesenteric resection margin is the only relevant circumferential margin in segments completely encased by the peritoneum [p.28].

### Perineural Invasion (PNI) [p.28]

The presence of PNI is associated with significantly worse prognosis. In multivariate analysis, PNI has been shown to be an independent prognostic factor for cancer-specific, overall, and disease-free survival (DFS) [p.28].

For stage II carcinoma, those with PNI have significantly worse 5-year DFS compared to those without PNI (29% vs. 82%; P = .0005) [p.28].

### Tumor Deposits [p.28]

Irregular discrete tumor deposits in pericolic or perirectal fat away from the leading edge of the tumor and showing no evidence of residual lymph node tissue, but within the lymphatic drainage of the primary carcinoma, are considered peritumoral deposits or satellite nodules and are not counted as lymph nodes replaced by tumor [p.28].

Most examples are due to lymphovascular invasion or, more rarely, PNI. Because these tumor deposits are associated with reduced DFS and overall survival, their number should be recorded in the surgical pathology report [p.28].

This poorer outcome has also been noted in patients with stage III carcinoma [p.28].

### Tumor Budding [p.28]

In recent years, high tumor budding has been identified as a new prognostic factor in colon cancer. An international consensus conference on tumor budding reporting established standards [p.28].

A tumor bud is defined as a single cell or a cluster of ≤4 cells identified on hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) at the advancing edge of the invasive carcinoma. The total number of buds are reported from a selected hot spot measuring 0.785 mm (20x ocular in most microscopes) [p.28].

Budding is separated into three tiers [p.28]:
- Low tier (0-4 buds)
- Intermediate tier (5-9 buds)
- High tier (≥10 buds)

Two recent studies using this scoring system have shown tumor budding to be an independent prognostic factor for stage II colon cancer. ASCO guideline for stage II colon cancer designates tumor budding as an adverse (high-risk) factor [p.28].

Several studies have shown that high-tier tumor budding in pT1 colorectal cancers (CRCs), including malignant polyps, is associated with increased risk of lymph node metastasis [p.28].

## Lymph Node Evaluation

### Minimum Lymph Node Requirements [p.29]

The AJCC and College of American Pathologists recommend examination of a minimum of 12 lymph nodes to accurately stage colon cancers [p.29].

The literature lacks consensus as to the minimum number of lymph nodes to accurately identify stage II cancer. The minimum number has been reported as >7, >9, >13, >20, and >30 [p.29].

The number of lymph nodes retrieved can vary with patient age, gender, tumor grade, and tumor site [p.29].

For stage II (pN0) colon cancer, if <12 lymph nodes are initially identified, it is recommended that the pathologist go back to the specimen and resubmit more tissue of potential lymph nodes [p.29].

If 12 lymph nodes are still not identified, a comment in the report should indicate that an extensive search for lymph nodes was undertaken. The pathologist should attempt to retrieve as many lymph nodes as possible [p.29].

It has been shown that the number of negative lymph nodes is an independent prognostic factor for patients with stage IIIB and IIIC colon cancer [p.29].

### Isolated Tumor Clusters (ITC) [p.29]

Isolated tumor clusters (ITC) are defined as single cells or small groups of cells measuring <0.2 mm. The biologic significance of ITCs is currently not well characterized [p.29].

Lymph nodes with ITC are defined as negative (N0) in the 9th edition of the AJCC staging system. However, the number of lymph nodes involved by ITC should be documented in the pathology report [p.29].

### Micrometastatic Tumor Deposits [p.29]

Micrometastatic tumor deposits measure between 0.2 mm-2.0 mm. Lymph nodes involved with micrometastatic deposits are considered positive lymph nodes [p.29].

## Methods of Testing

### Sample Types and Repeat Testing [p.29]

Testing can be performed on [p.29]:
- Formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded tissue or cytology material (preferred)
- Blood-based circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA)/RNA

Repeat molecular testing should not be performed after standard cytotoxic chemotherapy, as significant molecular changes are rarely observed [p.29].

Changes in the molecular profile can more commonly be seen after targeted therapies, and repeat testing may be considered to guide future targeted therapy decisions [p.29].

## KRAS, NRAS, and BRAF Testing

### Clinical Significance [p.29]

All patients with metastatic CRC should have tumor genotyped for RAS (KRAS and NRAS) and BRAF mutations as part of multi-gene panel testing (MGPT) at initial diagnosis of metastatic disease [p.29].

Patients with any known KRAS mutation (exons 2, 3, and 4) or NRAS mutation (exons 2, 3, and 4) should not be treated with either cetuximab or panitumumab, unless given as part of a regimen targeting a KRAS G12C mutation [p.29].

BRAF V600E mutation makes response to panitumumab or cetuximab highly unlikely unless given with a BRAF inhibitor [p.29].

### Testing Methodology [p.29]

BRAF V600E mutation testing via immunohistochemistry (IHC) is also an option [p.29].

Testing for KRAS, NRAS, and BRAF mutations should be performed only in laboratories certified under the Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments of 1988 (CLIA-88) as qualified to perform high-complexity clinical laboratory (molecular pathology) testing. No specific methodology is recommended [p.29].

When using MGPT, it is recommended to test the metastasis for additional genomic alterations. However, testing can be performed on primary CRCs, as literature has shown that driver KRAS, NRAS, and BRAF mutations are similar in both specimen types [p.29].

## Microsatellite Instability (MSI) or Mismatch Repair (MMR) Testing

### Universal Testing Recommendations [p.30]

Universal MMR or MSI testing is recommended in all newly diagnosed patients with colon cancer [p.30].

### BRAF V600E and MLH1 Loss [p.30]

The presence of a BRAF V600E mutation in the setting of MLH1 absence would preclude the diagnosis of Lynch syndrome (LS) in the vast majority of patients. However, approximately 1% of cancers with BRAF V600E mutations (and loss of MLH1) are LS [p.30].

Caution should be exercised in excluding patients with a strong family history from germline screening in the case of BRAF V600E mutation [p.30].

### Stage II MSI-H Disease Prognosis [p.30]

Stage II (MSI-H) cancers may have a good prognosis and do not benefit from fluorouracil adjuvant therapy [p.30].

### Testing Standards [p.30]

MMR or MSI testing should be performed only in CLIA-approved laboratories [p.30].

### Microsatellite Instability (MSI) Testing [p.30]

Testing for MSI involves looking for a change in the number of microsatellite repeats in tumor DNA compared to non-neoplastic tissue as evidence of dMMR mechanisms. This may be accomplished by [p.30]:
- Multiplex polymerase chain reaction (PCR) followed by capillary electrophoresis
- As part of a validated MGPT, especially in patients with metastatic disease requiring genotyping of RAS and RAF gene families [p.30]

### Immunohistochemistry (IHC) for MMR [p.30]

IHC for MMR looks at the protein expression of four MMR genes: MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, and PMS2 [p.30].

Patients with pathogenic germline variants in these genes have Lynch syndrome (LS). However, alterations in these genes also arise in sporadically acquired CRC [p.30].

**Interpretation Results:**
- Tumors that show nuclear expression of all four MMR proteins are considered **pMMR** [p.30]
- Tumors that reveal loss of expression of any MMR protein are considered **dMMR** [p.30]
- To ensure clarity, interpretation of IHC result should be reported as either "retained for all MMR proteins," or "loss of expression of (one or more of the MMR proteins)" [p.30]
- The words "positive" or "negative" should be avoided, although there are a few caveats such as isolated loss of MSH6 following neoadjuvant treatment [p.30]

### MMR Protein Heterodimers [p.30]

MMR proteins function as heterodimers: MLH1/PMS2 and MSH2/MSH6. Therefore, loss of expression of one protein will usually result in the loss of the heterodimer partner [p.30].

The pattern of lost protein expression guides further testing such as MLH1 promoter hypermethylation, or BRAF V600E or germline testing to assess for LS [p.30].

## HER2 (ERBB2) Overexpression/Amplification Testing

### Diagnostic Testing Methods [p.31]

Diagnostic testing is via [p.31]:
- Immunohistochemistry (IHC)
- Fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH)
- Next-generation sequencing (NGS)

### IHC Scoring [p.31]

Positive by IHC is defined as: 3+ staining in >50% of tumor cells [p.31].

3+ staining is defined as intense membrane staining that can be circumferential, basolateral, or lateral [p.31].

Those who have a HER2 score of 2+ should be reflexed to FISH testing [p.31].

### FISH and NGS Results [p.31]

HER2 (ERBB2) amplification detected by FISH is considered positive when the HER2 (ERBB2):CEP17 ratio is ≥2 in >50% of the cells [p.31].

NGS is another methodology for testing for HER2 (ERBB2) amplification [p.31].

### Anti-HER2 Therapy [p.31]

Anti-HER2 therapy with signal transduction inhibition (e.g., trastuzumab/pertuzumab, trastuzumab/tucatinib) is indicated for patients with [p.31]:
- HER2 (ERBB2) overexpression/amplification (IHC 3+, IHC 2+/ISH+, or NGS detected)
- Tumors that are also KRAS, NRAS, and BRAF WT [p.31]

### Fam-Trastuzumab Deruxtecan [p.31]

Fam-trastuzumab deruxtecan-nxki is only indicated in HER2-overexpressed tumors (IHC 3+) [p.31].

## NTRK 1/2/3 Gene Fusions

### Clinical Significance [p.31]

NTRK 1/2/3 gene fusions are extremely rare in CRCs. The overall incidence is approximately 0.35% in a cohort of 2314 CRCs, with NTRK 1/2/3 gene fusions confined to tumors that are pan-WT KRAS, NRAS, and BRAF [p.31].

In one study of 8 CRCs harboring NTRK 1/2/3 gene fusions, 7 were found in the small subset that were dMMR (MLH1)/MSI-H. NTRK 1/2/3 gene fusions are more frequently found among patients with dMMR [p.31].

### TRK Inhibitor Response [p.31]

TRK inhibitors have been shown to have activity ONLY in those cases with NTRK 1/2/3 gene fusions, and NOT with NTRK 1/2/3 gene point mutations [p.31].

### Detection Methodologies [p.31]

Methodologies for detecting NTRK 1/2/3 gene fusions are [p.31]:
- IHC
- FISH
- DNA-based NGS
- RNA-based NGS

In one study, DNA-based sequencing showed an overall sensitivity of 81.1% and specificity of 99.9% for detection of NTRK 1/2/3 gene fusions when compared to RNA-based sequencing [p.31].

IHC showed an overall sensitivity of 87.9% and specificity of 81.1% [p.31].

Since approximately 1 in 5 tumors identified as having an NTRK 1/2/3 gene fusion by IHC will be a false positive, tumors that test positive by IHC should be confirmed by RNA-based NGS [p.31].

RNA-based sequencing appears to be the optimal way to approach NTRK 1/2/3 gene fusions, because the splicing out of introns simplifies the technical requirements of adequate coverage and provides direct evidence of functional transcription [p.31].

Selection of the appropriate assay for NTRK 1/2/3 gene fusion detection depends on tumor type and genes involved, as well as consideration of other factors such as available material, accessibility of various clinical assays, and whether MGPT is needed concurrently [p.31].

## POLE/POLD1

### Function and Pathogenic Variants [p.32]

Polymerase genes, POLE and POLD1, encode proteins tasked with proofreading functions to recognize and correct mispaired bases incorporated during DNA replication [p.32].

Pathogenic variants (PVs) within the exonuclease domains (ED) of POLE and POLD1 result in loss of this proofreading function leading to subsequent acquisition of numerous single nucleotide variants (SNVs) [p.32].

### Germline and Somatic POLE/POLD1 Mutations [p.32]

Germline PVs within the ED of POLE and POLD1 predispose patients to multiple colorectal adenomas and carcinomas, resulting in polymerase proofreading-associated polyposis (PPAP) [p.32].

Somatic POLE mutations occur in approximately 2%-8% of patients with predominately MSS/pMMR CRC while somatic POLD1 mutations are extremely rare [p.32].

### Ultra-Hypermutated Phenotype and Tumor Mutation Burden (TMB) [p.32]

NGS of CRCs in patients with either germline or somatic ED PVs demonstrate an ultra-hypermutated phenotype identified as extremely high TMB (>50 mut/Mb) [p.32].

TMB is calculated as the total number of somatic mutations per coding area of the tumor genome. Although calculations vary according to assay performed, TMB >10 mut/Mb is generally regarded as TMB-high (TMB-H) [p.32].

### Detection and Prognosis [p.32]

POLE/POLD1 mutations can be identified through single gene assays (PCR or Sanger sequencing). However, TMB calculation requires MGPT, which often includes concurrent POLE/POLD1 sequencing [p.32].

Performing MGPT on CRC tumor tissue has the advantage of not only identifying POLE/POLD1 mutations but also provides direct evidence of loss of proofreading function (TMB-H) [p.32].

Patients with CRC harboring POLE/POLD1 mutations have a more favorable prognosis, likely secondary to immune responses stimulated by the presence of numerous neoantigens produced as a consequence of aberrant proofreading function [p.32].

For these patients, disease responds well to immune checkpoint inhibitor therapy [p.32].

## RET Gene Fusions

### Molecular Function [p.32]

RET is a receptor tyrosine kinase that plays a critical role in the development and maintenance of neural and genitourinary tissues, primarily through downstream MAPK and PI3K signaling pathways [p.32].

### Clinical Context [p.32]

Germline activating PVs in RET lead to multiple endocrine neoplasia type 2 (MEN2) and loss of function PVs are associated with Hirschsprung disease and congenital abnormalities of the kidney and urinary tract [p.32].

Somatic activating alterations in RET include point mutations as well as gene fusions and have been identified in a variety of tumors [p.32].

### RET Fusions in CRC [p.32]

In patients with CRC, activating RET gene fusions involving the C-terminal kinase domain lead to constitutive upregulation of RET kinase activity and subsequent promotion of cell proliferation and survival [p.32].

The most common gene fusion partners reported include KIF5B, CCDC6, and NCOA4 [p.32].

### Targeted Therapy [p.32]

The RET-targeted inhibitor, selpercatinib, is FDA-approved for patients with solid tumors harboring activating RET gene fusions [p.32].

### Detection Methods [p.32]

The presence of RET gene fusions can be interrogated through a variety of techniques, including [p.32]:
- IHC
- FISH
- PCR
- DNA-based NGS assays
- RNA-based NGS assays

RNA-based NGS assays are gene fusion agnostic and as such have the advantage of identifying RET gene fusions involving any partner gene [p.32].

Caution should be used in interpreting RET gene fusion results from ctDNA/RNA assays following targeted therapy with EGFR inhibitors [p.32].
## Principles of Surgery and Locoregional Therapies

### Hepatic Resection [p.39]

**General Principles:**
- Hepatic resection is the treatment of choice for resectable liver metastases from CRC [p.39]
- Complete resection must be feasible based on anatomic grounds and the extent of disease; maintenance of adequate hepatic function is required [p.39]
- There should be no unresectable extrahepatic sites of disease [p.39]
- Partial debulking (less than an R0 resection) is not recommended [p.39]

**Primary Tumor and Metastases:**
- Patients with resectable metastatic disease and a primary tumor in place should have both sites resected with curative intent [p.39]
- These can be resected in one operation or as a staged approach, depending on the complexity of the hepatectomy or colectomy, comorbid diseases, surgical exposure, and surgeon expertise [p.39]
- Staged procedures can be performed as liver-first or primary-first approaches [p.39]

**Surgical Technique:**
- In the setting of neoadjuvant therapy, placement of pre-treatment fiducial marker(s) in smaller lesions may be considered [p.39]
- Re-resection and re-ablation can be considered in selected patients [p.39]

**Conversion Strategies:**
- When hepatic metastatic disease is not optimally resectable based on insufficient remnant liver volume, approaches using preoperative portal vein embolization, staged liver resection, or Y-90 radioembolization can be considered [p.39]

**Ablative Approaches:**
- At the time of surgery, ablative techniques may be considered alone or in conjunction with resection. All original sites of disease should be amenable to thermal ablation or resection [p.39]
- Thermal ablation may be considered in selected surgical candidates with small tumors that can be completely ablated with margins [p.39]
- For liver metastases, in general, resection may be prioritized over local thermal ablation. However, for select patients with small lesions (≤3 cm), thermal ablation should be prioritized, when feasible, due to equivalent outcomes and reduced toxicity compared to surgery. SBRT can be considered in select cases where other local therapy options are not possible or in patients who prefer a noninvasive approach [p.39]

**Arterially Directed Therapy:**
- Arterially directed catheter therapy, and in particular Y-90 microsphere radioembolization, is an option in selected patients with chemotherapy-resistant/refractory disease and with predominant hepatic metastases [p.39]
- Ablative EBRT may be considered in selected cases or in the setting of a clinical trial and should not be used indiscriminately in patients with potentially surgically resectable disease or disease that can be percutaneously ablated with margins [p.39]

### Lung Resection [p.39]

**Resectability Criteria:**
- Complete resection based on the anatomic location and extent of disease with maintenance of adequate function is required [p.39]
- The primary tumor must have been resected for cure (R0) [p.39]
- Resectable extrapulmonary metastases do not preclude resection [p.39]

**Surgical Approaches:**
- Re-resection and re-ablation can be considered in selected patients [p.39]
- Ablative techniques may be considered alone or in conjunction with resection for resectable disease. All original sites of disease need to be amenable to thermal ablation or resection [p.39]
- Ablative techniques can also be considered when unresectable and amenable to complete thermal ablation [p.39]
- Thermal ablation may be considered in selected surgical candidates with small tumors that can be completely ablated with margins [p.39]

**Synchronous Metastases:**
- Patients with resectable synchronous metastases can be resected synchronously or using a staged approach [p.39]

**Treatment Options:**
- For lung metastases, surgery, thermal ablation, and SBRT can be considered based on multidisciplinary discussion and patient preference [p.39]
- Ablative EBRT may be considered in selected cases or in the setting of a clinical trial and should not be used indiscriminately in patients with potentially surgically resectable disease or disease that can be percutaneously ablated [p.39]

### Evaluation for Conversion to Resectable or Ablatable Disease [p.40]

- Re-evaluation for resection and/or ablation should be considered in otherwise unresectable patients after 2 months of preoperative chemotherapy and every 2 months thereafter [p.40]
- Metastatic tumor(s) with a higher likelihood of being converted to resectable and/or ablatable are those in which the initial disease is confined to limited sites [p.40]
- When considering whether disease has been converted to resectable and/or ablatable, all original sites need to be amenable to treatment [p.40]
- Preoperative chemotherapy regimens with high response rates should be considered for patients with potentially convertible disease [p.40]

## Principles of Systemic Therapy for Advanced or Metastatic Disease (COL-D)

### General Principles [p.44]

**Biosimilars:**
- An FDA-approved biosimilar is an appropriate substitute for any recommended systemic biologic therapy in the NCCN Guidelines [p.44]

### Continuum of Care [p.44]

**Oxaliplatin Management:**
- Discontinuation of oxaliplatin should be strongly considered after 3 to 4 months of therapy (or sooner for unacceptable neurotoxicity) while maintaining other agents until time of progression [p.44]
- Oxaliplatin may be reintroduced if it was discontinued for neurotoxicity rather than for disease progression [p.44]

**Single-Agent Fluoropyrimidine:**
- A single-agent fluoropyrimidine is not recommended after progression on a fluoropyrimidine-containing regimen [p.44]

**Anti-Angiogenic Therapy:**
- Bevacizumab is the preferred anti-angiogenic agent and can be continued into the second line of therapy and beyond [p.44]

**EGFR Inhibitors:**
- EGFR inhibitors include cetuximab and panitumumab [p.44]
- An EGFR inhibitor should not be continued beyond progression of disease [p.44]
- There is no efficacy benefit in changing the EGFR inhibitor at the time of progression [p.44]

**Other Anti-Angiogenic Agents:**
- Ziv-aflibercept and ramucirumab have only shown activity when given in conjunction with FOLFIRI in FOLFIRI-naive patients [p.44]
- There are no data to suggest activity of FOLFIRI-ziv-aflibercept or FOLFIRI-ramucirumab in a patient whose disease has progressed on FOLFIRI-bevacizumab, or vice versa [p.44]

**Arterially Directed Therapy:**
- Arterially directed catheter therapy, and in particular Y-90 microsphere selective internal radiation, is an option in highly selected patients with chemotherapy resistant/refractory disease and with predominant hepatic metastases [p.44]

**Monitoring:**
- C/A/P CT with contrast or chest CT and abdomen/pelvis MRI with contrast are recommended to monitor progress of therapy [p.44]
- FDG-PET/CT should not be used. See Principles of Imaging (COL-A) [p.44]

**Rechallenge:**
- If patients had therapy stopped for reasons other than progression (e.g., cumulative toxicity, elective treatment break, patient preference), rechallenge is an option at time of progression [p.44]

### Toxicity Risk Assessment [p.44]

**DPYD Variant Testing:**
- The FDA added a Boxed warning recommending to test patients for genetic variants of DPYD prior to initiating capecitabine or fluorouracil unless immediate treatment is necessary [p.44]
- Use should be avoided in patients with certain homozygous or compound heterozygous DPYD variants that result in complete DPD deficiency [p.44]
- However, no specific test is recommended and there are insufficient data to inform dose adjustments for many of the DPYD variants [p.44]

**Irinotecan Dosing:**
- Irinotecan should be used with caution in patients with Gilbert syndrome or elevated serum bilirubin [p.44]
- There is a commercially available test for UGT1A1. Guidelines for use in clinical practice have not been established [p.44]

**Anti-Angiogenic Therapy Perioperative Management:**
- Anti-angiogenic therapy should be held for at least 28 days prior to elective surgery [p.44]
- Do not administer for 28 days following major surgery and until adequate wound healing [p.44]

**Infection Risk:**
- For infection risk, monitoring, and prophylaxis recommendations for targeted therapies, see INF-A in the NCCN Guidelines for Prevention and Treatment of Cancer-Related Infections [p.44]

### Checkpoint Inhibitor Immunotherapy [p.44]

**Nivolumab and Hyaluronidase-nvhy:**
- Nivolumab and hyaluronidase-nvhy is not approved for concurrent use with IV ipilimumab [p.44]
- For nivolumab monotherapy, nivolumab and hyaluronidase-nvhy subcutaneous injection may be substituted for IV nivolumab [p.44]
- Nivolumab and hyaluronidase-nvhy has different dosing and administration instructions compared to IV nivolumab [p.44]

**Pembrolizumab and Berahyaluronidase Alfa-pmph:**
- Pembrolizumab and berahyaluronidase alfa-pmph subcutaneous injection may be substituted for IV pembrolizumab [p.44]
- Pembrolizumab and berahyaluronidase alfa-pmph has different dosing and administration instructions compared to IV pembrolizumab [p.44]

**Atezolizumab and Hyaluronidase-tqjs:**
- Atezolizumab and hyaluronidase-tqjs subcutaneous injection may be substituted for IV atezolizumab [p.44]
- Atezolizumab and hyaluronidase-tqjs has different dosing and administration instructions compared to IV atezolizumab [p.44]

**Duration of Therapy:**
- If disease response on checkpoint inhibitor, consider discontinuing checkpoint inhibitor after 2 years of treatment [p.44]

## Important Notes

**General Footnotes [p.50]:**
- Left-sided colon is defined as splenic flexure to rectum [p.50]
- Tumors originating on the right side of the colon (hepatic flexure through cecum) are unlikely to respond to cetuximab and panitumumab in first-line therapy for metastatic disease [p.50]
- Data on the response to cetuximab and panitumumab in primary tumors originating in the transverse colon (hepatic flexure to splenic flexure) are lacking [p.50]
- Cetuximab or panitumumab are recommended in combination with irinotecan-based therapy or as single-agent therapy for patients who cannot tolerate irinotecan [p.50]
- If no previous treatment with HER2 inhibitor [p.50]
- Some activity was seen after previous treatment with a HER2-targeted regimen. May not be indicated in patients with underlying lung issues due to lung toxicity [p.50]
- Patients with BRAF V600E-mutant cancers should receive an encorafenib-containing regimen during one line of therapy. A chemotherapy-containing option is preferred in the first-line metastatic setting [p.50]
- BRAF V600E regimen may be given with FOLFOX as subsequent-line therapy if no previous treatment with oxaliplatin or BRAF-targeting regimen [p.50]
- Combination PD-L1/CTLA-4 blockade has been associated with improved survival in unresectable or metastatic CRC, but also higher toxicity [p.50]
