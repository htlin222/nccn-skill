## Epidemiology and Burden of Disease

### General Epidemiology

Colorectal cancer (CRC) is the fourth most frequently diagnosed cancer and the second leading cause of cancer death in the United States [p.77]. In 2024, an estimated 106,590 new cases of colon cancer and 46,220 cases of rectal cancer will occur [p.77]. During the same year, an estimated 53,010 people will die of colon and rectal cancer combined [p.77].

Despite these high numbers, the incidence of colon and rectal cancers per 100,000 people has decreased from 60.5 in 1976 to 46.4 in 2005 and, more recently, 38.7 in 2016 [p.77]. In addition, mortality from CRC has been decreasing for decades (since 1947 in females and since 1980 in males) and is currently down by more than 50% from peak mortality rates [p.77].

These improvements in incidence and mortality from CRC are thought to be a result of cancer prevention and earlier diagnosis through screening and better treatment modalities [p.77].

### Age-Specific Trends

#### Older Adults (≥65 years)
Recent data show continued rapid declines in incidence among those aged ≥65 years, with a decrease of 3.3% annually between 2011 and 2016 [p.77].

#### Younger Adults
Conversely, incidence has increased among those <65 years, with [p.77]:
- 1% annual increase in those aged 50 to 64 years
- 2% annual increase in those <50 years

CRC death rates also showed age-dependent trends [p.77]:
- 3% annual decline for those ≥65 years
- 0.6% annual decline for individuals aged 50 to 64 years
- 1.3% annual increase for individuals <50 years

#### Early-Onset CRC (EOCRC) Projections
A retrospective cohort study of the SEER CRC registry found that the incidence of CRC in patients <50 years has been increasing [p.77]. Authors estimate that incidence rates for colon and rectal cancers will increase by 90.0% and 124.2%, respectively, for patients 20 to 34 years of age by 2030 [p.77].

**Clinical Significance:** The cause of this trend is currently unknown [p.77]. One review suggests that CRC occurring in patients <45 years may be clinicopathologically and genetically different from CRC in adults ≥45 years, although this has not been confirmed broadly [p.77]. If cancer in this population is different, there would be a need to develop specific treatment strategies for this population [p.77].

### Racial and Ethnic Disparities

CRC incidence and mortality rates vary by race and ethnicity with the highest rates in non-Hispanic Black individuals and lowest in Asian Americans/Pacific Islanders [p.77]. The magnitude of inequity in mortality rates is double that of incidence rates [p.77].

**Contributing Factors:** Reasons for these racial inequities include [p.77]:
- Differences in risk factor prevalence
- Access to health care and other social determinants of health
- Comorbidities
- Tumor characteristics

### Outcomes in Early-Onset CRC

In a cohort study of 1,959 patients with metastatic CRC (mCRC), patients who developed mCRC at a younger age (<50 years) showed worse survival outcomes and unique adverse event (AE) profiles, which the authors partially attribute to distinct genetic profiles [p.77].

## Scope of These Guidelines

This Discussion summarizes the NCCN Clinical Practice Guidelines in Oncology (NCCN Guidelines) for Colon Cancer [p.77]. These guidelines begin with the clinical presentation of the patient to the primary care physician or gastroenterologist, and address [p.77]:
- Diagnosis
- Pathologic staging
- Surgical management
- Perioperative treatment
- Surveillance
- Management of recurrent and metastatic disease
- Survivorship

### Key Points for Clinicians

When reviewing these guidelines, clinicians should be aware of several key points [p.77]:

1. **TNM Staging System:** These guidelines adhere to the TNM (tumor, node, metastasis) staging system [p.77]

2. **Clinical Trial Participation:** Although the guidelines are believed to represent the optimal treatment strategy, the Panel believes that, when appropriate, patients should preferentially be included in a clinical trial over standard or accepted therapy [p.77]
