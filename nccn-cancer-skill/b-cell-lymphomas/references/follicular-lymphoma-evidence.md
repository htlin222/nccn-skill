## Summary of Key Evidence

- FL is the most common subtype of indolent NHL, accounting for ~22% of newly diagnosed NHLs; approximately 90% have t(14;18) translocation juxtaposing BCL2 with IGH locus [p.163]
- WHO5 classification distinguishes classic FL (cFL) with t(14;18) from FL with uncommon features (uFL), and reclassifies FL3B as follicular large B-cell lymphoma (FLBL) requiring DLBCL-like treatment [p.163]
- Pathologic grading (FL1-2 vs. FL3A vs. FL3B) is considered a clinical predictor of outcome; FLIPI-2 risk stratification shows 5-year PFS of 98%, 88%, and 77% for low-, intermediate-, and high-risk disease in rituximab-treated patients [p.165]
- Active surveillance remains standard for advanced-stage, low-tumor-burden FL without treatment indications; rituximab monotherapy delays time to next treatment but does not improve overall survival compared to watchful waiting [p.169-170]
- RT is effective for stage I-II disease with >90% long-term disease control; ISRT (24-30 Gy) is preferred, with 10-year PFS and OS rates of 40-59% and 58-86%, respectively [p.167]
- For high-tumor-burden disease, chemoimmunotherapy with CD20-directed mAb (bendamustine, CHOP, or CVP) and lenalidomide + rituximab are preferred first-line regimens with category 2A recommendation [p.171-172]
- Obinutuzumab-based chemoimmunotherapy shows significantly longer PFS (63% vs. 56% at 7 years, P=.006) compared to rituximab-based chemoimmunotherapy, though OS is similar (89% vs. 87%, P=.36) [p.172]

## Diagnosis and Classification

FL has characteristic immunophenotype including CD20+, CD10+, BCL2+, CD23+/-, CD43-, CD5-, CCND1-, and BCL6+; immunophenotyping is essential to distinguish FL from mantle cell lymphoma, marginal zone lymphoma, or SLL [p.164]. IHC for Ki-67, IRF4/MUM1, cyclin D1, and FISH for t(14;18), BCL6, 1p36, and IRF4/MUM1 rearrangements may be useful; however, high Ki-67 (≥30%) has not been established as a guide for therapy selection [p.164-165].

Special entities include:
- **FL with BCL2-R negative/CD23-positive pattern**: Typically grade 1-2 with pelvic/inguinal involvement and good prognosis; managed as cFL [p.164]
- **LBCL with IRF4 rearrangement**: Occurs in children and young adults with Waldeyer's ring, cervical, or GI involvement; managed as DLBCL [p.163-164]
- **Pediatric-type FL (PTFL)**: Lacks BCL2, BCL6, and IRF4 rearrangements; MAP2K1 and TNFRSF14 mutations most frequent [p.164]
- **In situ follicular neoplasia (ISFN)**: Presence of FL-like B-cells in reactive lymph node germinal centers with BCL2 staining and t(14;18); development to overt lymphoma reported in 5-6% of cases [p.164]

## Prognostic Assessment

The Follicular Lymphoma International Prognostic Index (FLIPI) divides patients into three prognostic groups based on age, Ann Arbor stage, number of nodal sites, hemoglobin, and LDH [p.165]. FLIPI-2 was developed in the rituximab era and shows 5-year PFS rates of 79%, 51%, and 20% for low-, intermediate-, and high-risk disease, respectively [p.165]. In patients treated with rituximab-containing regimens, 5-year PFS rates are 98%, 88%, and 77% for low-, intermediate-, and high-risk disease [p.165].

The m7-FLIPI incorporating mutation status of seven genes (EZH2, ARID1A, MEF2B, EP300, FOXO1, CREBBP, CARD11), FLIPI, and ECOG performance status improves risk stratification; prognostic significance was retained in RCHOP and RCVP/obinutuzumab but not in bendamustine-based regimens [p.165].

## Imaging and Surveillance

PET/CT scans demonstrate high sensitivity (94-98%) and specificity (88-100%) for indolent lymphomas compared to CT alone [p.166]. Higher baseline SUVmax was not associated with subsequent histologic transformation in GALLIUM study, indicating PET alone is insufficient for transformation diagnosis [p.166]. Post-treatment PET/CT negativity is associated with superior PFS; median PFS was 48 months if PET/CT negative vs. 17 months if positive in FL patients treated with RCHOP [p.166].

End-of-treatment PET/CT is now standard for post-treatment response evaluation; however, routine surveillance PET scans are not recommended for patients achieving complete response, as earlier relapse detection did not translate to improved survival [p.167].

## First-Line Therapy: Stage I-II Disease

ISRT (24-30 Gy) is the preferred treatment for stage I or contiguous stage II disease, resulting in 10-year PFS and OS rates of 40-59% and 58-86%, respectively [p.367]. A multicenter retrospective study by the International Lymphoma Radiation Oncology Group confirmed RT alone (≥24 Gy) as potentially curative, with 5-year FFP and OS rates of 69% and 96% [p.367].

Addition of systemic therapy (rituximab, chemotherapy, or chemoimmunotherapy) to IFRT improves FFS and PFS but does not impact OS in early-stage disease [p.367-368]. In the TROG 99.03 trial (n=150), IFRT followed by RCVP was associated with superior 10-year PFS compared to IFRT alone (59% vs. 41%); however, 10-year OS was not significantly different (95% vs. 87%) [p.368].

Retrospective studies comparing active surveillance, RT, rituximab monotherapy, chemoimmunotherapy, and combined modality with RT showed no differences in OS outcomes between approaches [p.368]. Carefully selected patients requiring large abdominal radiation fields, advanced age, concern for xerostomia, or patient refusal may be observed with comparable outcomes to RT-treated patients [p.369].

**Current recommendations**: ISRT (24-30 Gy) is preferred; CD20-directed mAb ± chemotherapy is an alternate option; active surveillance appropriate when toxicity outweighs benefit [p.369].

For non-contiguous stage II disease, CD20-directed mAb ± chemotherapy is recommended; ISRT can be used with systemic therapy for local palliation; active surveillance may be appropriate [p.369].

## First-Line Therapy: Stage III-IV Low-Tumor-Burden Disease

Multiple prospective randomized trials demonstrated no OS benefit with immediate treatment versus watchful waiting in advanced-stage, low-tumor-burden, asymptomatic FL [p.169]. Although rituximab monotherapy results in significantly longer median time to initiation of new treatment versus watchful waiting, it does not improve OS [p.169].

Analysis of the F2-study registry (n=107 "watch and wait"; n=242 rituximab-containing) showed 4-year freedom from treatment failure (FFTF) of 79% vs. 69% (not significant after adjusting for baseline factors) and similar 5-year OS (87% vs. 88%) [p.169-170].

A randomized phase III intergroup trial (187 watchful waiting; 192 maintenance rituximab; 84 induction rituximab) showed at 15 years, percentage not needing new treatment was 34%, 48%, and 65%, respectively (p<.0001 for both treatment arms) [p.170]. Four-year PFS rates were higher with rituximab (57% induction, 78% maintenance) vs. watchful waiting (32%), but no OS differences were observed [p.170]. The National LymphoCare Study (n=386 watchful waiting, n=296 rituximab monotherapy, n=1072 rituximab + chemotherapy) confirmed no significant OS difference; estimated 8-year OS rates were 74%, 67%, and 72%, respectively [p.170].

**Current recommendation**: Active surveillance is standard for advanced-stage, low-tumor-burden FL without treatment indications [p.170]. Treatment should only be initiated for: symptoms attributable to FL; threatened end-organ function; clinically significant or progressive cytopenia; clinically significant bulky disease (diameter ≥7 cm); or steady/rapid progression [p.170].

## First-Line Therapy: High-Tumor-Burden Disease - Rituximab Monotherapy

While active surveillance is reasonable, rituximab monotherapy is a proactive and effective strategy supported by multiple studies [p.171]. Long-term follow-up from an international randomized trial showed rituximab induction effectively delayed time to next treatment (TTNT) with superior PFS compared to watchful waiting [p.171].

A short course (4 weekly doses) of rituximab, as compared with extended treatment in the RESORT trial, may optimize benefit/risk ratio with similar long-term survival [p.171]. The phase III LYSA study (n=100 subcutaneous rituximab; n=100 intravenous rituximab) showed 4-year PFS of 58% and 41%, respectively (P=.0076); CR rates were 59% and 36% (P=.001) [p.171]. TTNT and OS were not significantly different; improved outcomes strongly associated with drug exposure in first 3 months [p.171].

While rituximab monotherapy is a preferred regimen for low-tumor-burden FL, it has not demonstrated proven benefit over active surveillance in terms of OS or decreased histologic transformation risk [p.171].

## First-Line Therapy: High-Tumor-Burden Disease - Chemoimmunotherapy

Bendamustine, CHOP, or CVP with obinutuzumab or rituximab, and lenalidomide + rituximab are included as preferred regimens for advanced-stage, high-tumor-burden FL [p.171].

**FOLL-05 Trial** (Italian Lymphoma Group, n=534): Compared RCVP, RCHOP, and RFM (rituximab, fludarabine, mitoxantrone) as first-line treatment [p.171]. After median follow-up of 34 months, 3-year time to treatment failure (TTF) was 46% for RCVP vs. 62% (P=.003) for RCHOP and 59% (P=.006) for RFM [p.171]. Three-year PFS rates were 52%, 68%, and 63%, respectively (P=.011) [p.172]. No significant differences in ORR, CR rates, or 3-year OS rate (95% for all) [p.172]. Grade 3-4 neutropenia: 28% (RCVP), 50% (RCHOP), 64% (RFM); secondary malignancies: 2% (RCVP), 3% (RCHOP), 8% (RFM) [p.172].

**StiL NHL1 Study**: BR (bendamustine + rituximab, n=not specified) vs. RCHOP [p.172]. At median follow-up of 45 months, median PFS was 69 months vs. 31 months (P<.0001) [p.172]. ORR similar (93% BR vs. 91% RCHOP); CR rate higher with BR (40% vs. 30%, P=.021) [p.172]. Grade 3-4 neutropenia: 29% (BR) vs. 69% (RCHOP); infections (any grade): 37% vs. 50% [p.172]. Erythema and allergic skin reactions more common with BR (16%, 15% vs. 9%, 6%) [p.172]. Secondary malignancies similar (8% vs. 9%); estimated 10-year survival: 71% (BR) vs. 66% (RCHOP), not significantly different [p.172].

**BRIGHT Trial** (n=419; 154 FL randomized to BR, 160 to RCHOP/RCVP): BR non-inferior to RCHOP/RCVP for CR and PFS [p.172]. CR rates: 30% (BR) vs. 25% (RCHOP/RCVP, P=.02) [p.172]. At 5-year follow-up: PFS 70% (BR) vs. 62% (RCHOP/RCVP, P=.05); OS 82% vs. 85% (P=.5461) [p.172]. Opportunistic infections and secondary malignancies slightly higher with BR [p.173].

**GALLIUM Trial** (n=1202; 1:1 obinutuzumab vs. rituximab with chemotherapy): Planned interim analysis (median 34 months) and final analysis (median 8 years) showed obinutuzumab-based chemoimmunotherapy associated with significantly longer PFS and lower progression/relapse risk [p.172]. Estimated 7-year PFS: 63% (obinutuzumab) vs. 56% (rituximab, P=.006) [p.172]. Response rates at end of induction not significantly different (88% vs. 87%); 7-year OS similar (89% vs. 87%, P=.36) [p.172-173]. Grade ≥3 treatment-emergent adverse events higher with obinutuzumab: infections 20% vs. 16%, neutropenia 46% vs. 39%, infusion reactions 12% vs. 7% [p.173]. Bendamustine associated with higher grade 3-5 infections and secondary cancers; non-relapse–related fatal adverse events 6% (bendamustine + obinutuzumab) and 4% (bendamustine + rituximab) vs. 2% (CHOP/CVP) [p.173].

**Panel Consensus**: Chemoimmunotherapy with CD20-directed mAb (obinutuzumab or rituximab) is appropriate first-line therapy; in absence of randomized data showing OS superiority of one regimen over another, all chemoimmunotherapy regimens designated as preferred with category 2A recommendation [p.173].

Selection should be individualized by age, disease extent, comorbidities, and therapy goals; avoid excessively myelotoxic regimens in candidates for future HDT/ASCR [p.173]. HBV testing (HBsAg and HBcAb) required prior to initiation; routine viral load monitoring for positive results [p.173]. Empiric antiviral therapy or upfront prophylaxis should be incorporated [p.173]. Prophylaxis for pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PJP) and varicella zoster virus (VZV) recommended for bendamustine recipients [p.173].

## First-Line Therapy: Lenalidomide + Rituximab

**RELEVANCE Trial** (n=1030; n=513 lenalidomide + rituximab vs. n=517 chemotherapy + rituximab [RCHOP, RCVP, or BR]) with maintenance rituximab [p.173]. ORR: 84% (lenalidomide + rituximab) vs. 89% (chemotherapy + rituximab) [p.173]. CR rate at 120 weeks: 48% vs. 53% (P=.13) [p.173]. Estimated 6-year PFS: 60% vs. 59% [p.173]. OS: 89% for both groups [p.173]. Grade 3-4 neutropenia: 32% (lenalidomide + rituximab) vs. 50% (chemotherapy + rituximab) [p.173]. Febrile neutropenia: 2% vs. 7% [p.173]. Grade 3-4 cutaneous reactions: 7% vs. 1% [p.173].

Although RELEVANCE (designed as superiority trial) did not show lenalidomide + rituximab superior to chemotherapy + rituximab for efficacy, confirmed as effective alternative to chemoimmunotherapy [p.173]. Panel consensus: lenalidomide + rituximab included as preferred regimen with category 2A recommendation [p.173].

## Initial Workup

Workup should include thorough physical examination, performance status evaluation, constitutional symptoms assessment, CBC with differential, comprehensive metabolic panel, and serum LDH measurement [p.165]. HBV testing required for all patients due to viral reactivation risk [p.165]. Measurement of uric acid, serum beta-2 microglobulin (for FLIPI-2 calculation), and HCV testing may be useful in certain circumstances [p.166].

Bone marrow biopsy with aspirate is essential to document clinical stage I-II disease; however, in patients with low-tumor-burden indolent lymphoma and radiographic stage III disease, initial staging bone marrow evaluation can be deferred if active surveillance is the initial option [p.166]. Chest/abdomen/pelvis CT with contrast and/or whole-body PET/CT recommended; PET/CT essential if RT planned for stage I-II disease [p.166]. MUGA scan or echocardiogram recommended for patients receiving anthracycline-containing regimens [p.166].
## Summary of Key Evidence

- Lenalidomide + obinutuzumab as first-line induction achieves 92% ORR with 82% 3-year PFS in the GALEN study (n = 100) [p.174]
- Rituximab maintenance provides PFS benefit but not OS advantage; PRIMA trial showed 75% 3-year PFS with maintenance vs. 58% observation [p.174]
- Obinutuzumab-based chemoimmunotherapy with maintenance significantly improves PFS over rituximab-based regimens in GALLIUM trial, though OS not significantly different [p.174]
- Second-line therapy options include lenalidomide + rituximab (AUGMENT: 39-month median PFS), tafasitamab + lenalidomide + rituximab (inMIND: PFS not reached), and CD20-directed chemoimmunotherapy [p.176-177]
- Third-line options include CAR T-cell therapies (ZUMA-5, ELARA, TRANSCEND FL with ORRs 86-97%), bispecific antibodies (mosunetuzumab, epcoritamab with ORRs 78-82%), and tazemetostat [p.179-181]
- Prognostic indicator: POD ≤24 months after diagnosis and failure to achieve EFS12 predict poor survival; 5-year OS 50% with POD ≤2 years vs. 90% with POD >2 years [p.175]

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Lenalidomide + Obinutuzumab

GALEN study (phase II, n = 100) evaluated lenalidomide + obinutuzumab as first-line induction therapy with results showing:
- ORR: 92% (20% CR) [p.174]
- Median follow-up: 4 years; 3-year PFS: 82%; 3-year OS: 94% [p.174]
- Most common grade ≥3 adverse event: Neutropenia (47% of patients) [p.174]
- **NCCN Recommendation:** Category 2B [p.174]

### First-Line Consolidation or Extended Therapy

**Rituximab Maintenance**

RESORT study (phase III randomized trial, E4402; n = 289) compared rituximab maintenance versus rituximab retreatment in previously untreated low-burden FL patients responding to rituximab induction:
- Primary endpoint: TTF (time to treatment failure)
- At 5-year median follow-up: Estimated TTF similar in both arms (4 years; P = .54) [p.174]
- 3-year freedom from cytotoxic therapy: 95% (maintenance) vs. 84% (retreatment; P = .03) [p.174]
- Conclusion: Rituximab retreatment provides comparable disease control to rituximab maintenance in low-burden FL; long-term data confirmed no OS advantage with maintenance rituximab [p.174]

PRIMA trial (phase III randomized, n = 1018) evaluated rituximab maintenance in patients with FL responding to first-line chemoimmunotherapy (RCVP, RCHOP, or RFCM):
- Patients randomized to observation only or rituximab maintenance for 2 years [p.174]
- At 36-month median follow-up: 3-year PFS 75% (maintenance) vs. 58% (observation; P = .0001) [p.174]
- Two years after randomization: 71% of maintenance patients in CR/CRu vs. 52% observation [p.174]
- OS: No significant difference between groups at initial analysis [p.174]
- Long-term follow-up (10 years): Median PFS 10.5 years (maintenance) vs. 4 years (observation); OS estimates identical at 80% in both arms [p.174]
- PFS benefit was significant regardless of response quality to chemoimmunotherapy [p.174]
- **NCCN Recommendation:** Category 1 for patients with high tumor burden treated with RCVP/RCHOP based on PRIMA study [p.175]
- **Note:** Insufficient data to support rituximab maintenance after first-line BR in CR patients; real-world analysis showed PFS improvement only in PR patients [p.175]

GALLIUM trial (discussed above) demonstrated obinutuzumab-based chemoimmunotherapy + obinutuzumab maintenance resulted in significantly longer PFS than rituximab-based chemoimmunotherapy + rituximab maintenance, but OS not significantly different [p.174]

**Obinutuzumab Maintenance**
- **NCCN Recommendation:** Category 2A based on GALLIUM trial results [p.175]

**Consolidation with Rituximab**
- Should be considered if initially treated with single-agent rituximab [p.175]

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### Relapsed or Progressive Disease - Assessment Criteria

Progressive disease requires histologic documentation to exclude transformation, especially with:
- Raised LDH levels
- Disproportional growth in single area
- Development of extranodal disease
- New constitutional symptoms
- PET/CT SUV >13 raises suspicion for transformation [p.175]

**Prognostic Factors:**
- POD ≤24 months after diagnosis: Identifies poor survival indicator [p.175]
- EFS12 (event-free survival at 12 months): Patients achieving EFS12 have OS similar to age/sex-matched general population; those not achieving EFS12 have lower OS [p.175]
- National LymphoCare Study: 5-year OS 50% for POD ≤2 years after RCHOP vs. 90% for POD >2 years [p.175]

**Treatment Indications for Relapsed/Progressive Disease:**
- Symptoms attributable to FL (not limited to B symptoms)
- Threatened end-organ function
- Clinically significant/progressive cytopenia secondary to lymphoma
- Clinically significant bulky disease (nodal/extranodal mass ≥7 cm)
- Steady or rapid progression [p.175]

### Second-Line Therapy: Preferred Regimens

**Chemoimmunotherapy with CD20-Directed Monoclonal Antibody**

Bendamustine + Rituximab (BR):
- Phase III randomized study (n = 230; FL patients): BR vs. FR (fludarabine + rituximab) [p.176]
- At 96-month median follow-up: Median PFS 34 months (BR) vs. 12 months (FR) [p.176]

**CHOP + Obinutuzumab:**
- Randomized study (n = 56) in relapsed/refractory FL: CHOP + obinutuzumab vs. FC + obinutuzumab [p.176]
- ORR: 96% (CHOP + obinutuzumab) vs. 93% (FC + obinutuzumab) [p.176]
- CR rates: 39% vs. 50%, respectively [p.176]
- In rituximab-refractory subgroup: 25% (CHOP) and 30% (FC) achieved CR; all achieved ≥PR [p.176]
- More TEAEs with FC + obinutuzumab [p.176]

**GADOLIN Trial (Bendamustine + Obinutuzumab):**
- Phase III randomized trial (n = 413; 335 had FL): Bendamustine + obinutuzumab vs. bendamustine monotherapy [p.176]
- Median follow-up: 32 months; Median PFS significantly longer with combination: 26 vs. 14 months (P < .001) in FL patients [p.176]
- Median OS: Not reached (combo) vs. 54 months (monotherapy) [p.176]
- Grade ≥3 TEAEs: Neutropenia (33% vs. 26%), thrombocytopenia (11% vs. 16%), anemia (8% vs. 10%), infusion-related reactions (11% vs. 6%) [p.176]
- **Note:** BR not recommended for second-line if used as first-line due to concerns for opportunistic infections and secondary malignancies [p.176]

**Lenalidomide + Rituximab**

AUGUMENT trial (phase III, multicenter, double-blind, randomized; n = 358; 295 with FL):
- Lenalidomide + rituximab vs. rituximab monotherapy in previously treated indolent lymphomas [p.177]
- At 28-month median follow-up in FL subgroup:
  - Median PFS: 39 months (lenalidomide + rituximab) vs. 14 months (rituximab; P < .0001) [p.177]
  - ORR: 80% (35% CR) vs. 55% (20% CR; P < .0001) [p.177]
  - 2-year OS: 95% vs. 86%, respectively [p.177]
- Adverse events (lenalidomide + rituximab vs. rituximab): Infections (63% vs. 49%), cutaneous reactions (32% vs. 12%), constipation (26% vs. 14%), thrombocytopenia (15% vs. 4%), tumor flare reaction (11% vs. 1%); Grade 3-4 neutropenia (50% vs. 13%) and leukopenia (7% vs. 2%) most common [p.177]
- **NCCN Recommendation:** Preferred second-line option [p.175]

**Tafasitamab + Lenalidomide + Rituximab**

inMIND trial (phase III randomized; n = 548) in relapsed/refractory FL:
- Tafasitamab + lenalidomide + rituximab (n = 272) vs. lenalidomide + rituximab + placebo (n = 275) [p.177]
- At 14-month median follow-up:
  - Median PFS: Not reached vs. 16 months (P < .0001) [p.177]
  - ORR: 84% vs. 72% (P = 0.0014); CR rate: 49% vs. 40% (P = .029) [p.177]
  - Improved DOR: 21 months vs. 14 months; longer TTNT: Not reached vs. 29 months [p.177]
- PFS benefit consistent across subgroups including POD24 and refractory to multiple therapies [p.177]
- TEAEs including grade 3-4 neutropenia and infection rates similar between arms [p.177]
- **NCCN Recommendation:** Preferred option for relapsed/refractory FL in lenalidomide + rituximab candidates [p.177]
- **Important consideration:** Loss of CD19 expression possible mechanism of resistance; further follow-up needed to confirm incidence of relapse and CD19 loss. Rebiopsy recommended to confirm CD19 positivity before CD19-directed CAR T-cell therapy [p.177]

### Second-Line Therapy: Other Recommended Regimens

**Lenalidomide Monotherapy**

Phase II trial (n = 43) in relapsed/refractory indolent NHL:
- Overall ORR: 23% (7% CR) [p.177]
- FL subgroup (n = 22): ORR 27% [p.177]
- Median DOR: >16 months (not reached); Median PFS: 4 months [p.177-178]
- **NCCN Recommendation:** For patients not candidates for CD20-directed mAb therapy [p.178]

**Lenalidomide + Obinutuzumab**

GALEN study (phase II, n = 89) in relapsed/refractory FL including early relapse patients:
- Evaluable for efficacy (n = 86): ORR 87% (38% CR) [p.178]
- 3-year median follow-up: 2-year PFS 65%; 2-year OS 87% [p.178]
- Most common TEAEs: Asthenia (61%), neutropenia (43%; grade ≥3, 5%), bronchitis (41%), diarrhea (40%), muscle spasms (39%) [p.178]

**Rituximab or Obinutuzumab Monotherapy**

Rituximab monotherapy study (multiinstitutional, n = 166) in relapsed/refractory FL:
- Response rate: 48% [p.178]
- 12-month median follow-up; Estimated median time to progression: 13 months [p.178]
- Grade ≥3 infusion-related reactions: 12% [p.178]

GAUSS trial (phase II randomized; n = 175; FL, n = 149) in relapsed indolent lymphomas with prior response to rituximab-containing regimen:
- ORR: 45% (obinutuzumab) vs. 33% (rituximab) in FL (P = .08) [p.178]
- No improvement in PFS: 2-year PFS rates 46% (median 18 months) vs. 50% (median 25 months) [p.178]
- Higher infusion-related reactions with obinutuzumab: 74% vs. 51% [p.178]

### Second-Line Consolidation or Extended Dosing

**Rituximab Maintenance**

GLSG study (phase III randomized, German Low Grade Lymphoma Study Group; n = 81):
- Rituximab maintenance after second-line RFCM in recurring/refractory FL [p.178]
- Median PFS not reached (maintenance) vs. 26 months (observation; P = .035) [p.178]
- **NCCN Recommendation:** Category 1; one dose every 12 weeks up to 2 years [p.179]

EORTC 20981 trial (phase III randomized Intergroup; n = 334) in relapsed/resistant FL responding to CHOP or RCHOP:
- Rituximab maintenance significantly improved median PFS: 4 years vs. 1 year (P < .001) [p.178]
- 6-year median follow-up: 5-year OS not significantly different (74% vs. 64%) [p.178]
- Benefit observed regardless of induction therapy (CHOP or RCHOP) [p.178]

**Rituximab Maintenance vs. Retreatment:**
- Randomized study (n = 114) in previously chemotherapy-treated indolent lymphomas [p.178]
- Rituximab maintenance: 31 months PFS vs. 7 months retreatment (P = .007) [p.178-179]
- Duration of benefit similar (31 vs. 27 months) [p.179]
- **NCCN Recommendation:** Either maintenance or rituximab retreatment at progression appropriate [p.179]

**Obinutuzumab Maintenance**

GADOLIN study: Obinutuzumab maintenance following bendamustine + obinutuzumab improved PFS in rituximab-refractory FL [p.178]
- **NCCN Recommendation:** Category preference; 1 g every 8 weeks for 12 doses in rituximab-refractory disease including POD on/within 6 months of prior rituximab [p.179]

**HDT/ASCR**
- **NCCN Recommendation:** As consolidation therapy for second or third remission in relapsed/refractory disease [p.179]

## Third-Line Therapy Evidence

### CAR T-Cell Therapy (Preferred Regimens)

Three FDA-approved CD19-directed CAR T-cell therapies for relapsed/refractory FL after ≥2 lines of systemic therapy:

**Axicabtagene Ciloleucel (ZUMA-5)**

Phase II trial (n = 127 FL; 31 MZL) after ≥2 prior lines of systemic therapy [p.179]:
- Efficacy in 104 evaluable patients (FL, n = 84): ORR 92% (74% CR) [p.179]
- FL-specific: ORR 94% (80% CR); Median age 61 years; 38% ECOG PS 1; 86% stage III/IV [p.179]
- Grade ≥3 TEAEs: Cytopenias (70%), infections (18%); Grade ≥3 CRS (7%), neurologic events (19%); Treatment-related deaths (3%) [p.179]
- 42-month median follow-up: ORR 94%, median PFS 40 months [p.179]
- 5-year updated analysis: Median PFS 62 months; 60-month PFS rate 50% (consistent with high-risk features including POD24); 60-month OS 69% [p.179-180]

**Tisagenlecleucel (ELARA)**

Phase II trial in relapsed/refractory FL after ≥2 lines including CD20-directed mAb and alkylating agent or disease relapse after HDT/ASCR [p.180]:
- 94 evaluable patients: ORR 86% (68% CR); Median PFS not reached [p.180]
- 24-month PFS: 57%; 24-month OS: 88% [p.180]
- Elevated baseline tumor burden, POD24, and >4 nodal areas associated with inferior long-term outcomes [p.180]
- 17-month median follow-up: Grade ≥3 CRS (49%), neurological events (37%), ICANS (4%); no safety signals with longer follow-up [p.180]

**Lisocabtagene Maraleucel (TRANSCEND FL)**

Phase II trial (n = 101) in relapsed/refractory FL after ≥2 prior lines [p.180]:
- 19-month median follow-up: ORR 97% (94% CR) [p.180]
- Median DOR and median PFS not reached (17-month and 18-month follow-up, respectively) [p.180]
- 12-month endpoints: Response maintained in 82%; 12-month PFS 81%; 12-month OS 96% [p.180]
- Grade ≥3 TEAEs: Neutropenia (65%), prolonged cytopenia (22%), infection (5%), CRS (grade 3; 58%), neurological events (grade 3; 15%) [p.180]
- 2-year follow-up confirmed safety and efficacy [p.180]

**NCCN Recommendation:** All three CAR T-cell therapies included as options for third-line and subsequent therapy [p.180]

### Bispecific Antibodies (Preferred Regimens)

**Mosunetuzumab (CD3 x CD20)**

FDA-approved for relapsed/refractory FL [p.180]

Phase II single-arm multicenter study (n = 90) in relapsed/refractory FL after ≥2 lines including CD20-directed mAb and alkylating agent [p.180]:
- Fixed-duration intravenous treatment [p.180]
- IRC-assessed: ORR 80% (60% CR) [p.180]
- Dosing: CR patients completed 8 cycles; PR/stable disease patients received 17 total cycles [p.180]
- Favorable response across all subgroups including high-risk disease and EZH2-mutated FL [p.180]
- 18-month median follow-up: IRC median PFS 18 months; estimated EFS 61%; estimated OS 90% [p.180]
- Efficacy superior to patient's last prior therapy [p.180]
- Updated follow-up (37-month median): Investigator-assessed ORR 78% (60% CR); median PFS 24 months; 36-month OS 82% [p.180]
- Peripheral blood B-cell depletion in all patients; B-cell recovery after median 18 months post-treatment [p.180]
- Most common Grade 1-2 TEAEs: CRS (42%), fatigue (37%), headache (30%), pyrexia (28%) [p.180-181]
- Grade 3-4 neutropenia: 13% [p.180]
- CRS management: Predominantly cycle 1; grade 3 in 2% (resolved); corticosteroids/tocilizumab used in 15%/8% respectively; combination in 10% [p.181]
- ICANS: Very rare, all low grade; grade 1-2 confusion in 3%, no aphasia/seizures/encephalopathy/cerebral edema [p.181]
- Safety profile enables outpatient administration [p.181]
- **Important:** Loss of CD20 expression possible mechanism of resistance; rebiopsy recommended to confirm CD20 positivity before initiation [p.181]

**Epcoritamab (CD3 x CD20)**

EPCORE NHL-1 trial (n = 128) in relapsed/refractory FL after ≥2 prior lines [p.181]:
- Subcutaneous administration; fixed-duration [p.181]
- ORR 82% (63% CR); consistent across high-risk subgroups [p.181]
  - Double refractory FL: 76% (56% CR) [p.181]
  - Refractory to last prior therapy: 74% (51% CR) [p.181]
  - POD24: 79% (64% CR) [p.181]
- 18-month follow-up: PFS rate 49% (all patients), 74% (CR patients) [p.181]
- Grade 1-2 TEAEs: CRS (65%), injection-site reaction (57%), fatigue (28%), pyrexia (23%), headache (20%), diarrhea (25%) [p.181]
- Grade 3-4 neutropenia: 13% each; grade 3 diarrhea (2%), pyrexia (2%), fatigue (2%) [p.181]
- ICANS: 6%, resolved without discontinuation [p.181]
- **Important:** Activity in CD20-negative lymphomas unclear; rebiopsy recommended to confirm CD20 positivity [p.181]

### Third-Line Therapy: Other Recommended Regimens

**Tazemetostat (EZH2 Inhibitor)**

FDA-approved for relapsed/refractory FL after ≥2 prior systemic therapies [p.181]

Phase II trial (n = 99) in relapsed/refractory FL after ≥2 therapies including PI3K inhibitor or immunomodulatory drug [p.181]:
- 45 EZH2-mutated; 54 EZH2 wild-type patients [p.181]
- EZH2-mutated cohort (22-month median follow-up): ORR 69% (13% CR, 56% PR, 29% stable disease) [p.181]
- EZH2 wild-type cohort: ORR 35% (4% CR, 31% PR, 33% stable disease) [p.181]
- 36-month median follow-up: Median PFS 14 months (EZH2-mutated) vs. 11 months (wild-type); Median OS not reached in either [p.181]
- ORR higher in EZH2-mutated across subgroups:
  - POD24: 63% vs. 25% [p.181]
  - Double-refractory: 78% vs. 27% [p.181]
  - Refractory to rituximab: 59% vs. 31% [p.181]
- Grade ≥3 TEAEs: Thrombocytopenia (3%), neutropenia (3%), anemia (2%); serious TEAEs in 4% [p.181]
- **NCCN Recommendation:** Option for third-line and subsequent therapy irrespective of EZH2 mutation status; testing for EZH2 mutation recommended using paraffin-embedded tissue and approved sequencing assay [p.182]

**Zanubrutinib + Obinutuzumab**

ROSEWOOD trial (phase II randomized; n = 217) in relapsed/refractory FL after ≥2 prior systemic therapies [p.182]:
- Zanubrutinib + obinutuzumab (n = 145) vs. obinutuzumab (n = 72) [p.182]
- ORR: 69% vs. 46%; CR: 39% vs. 19% [p.182]
- 20-month median follow-up: Median PFS 28 vs. 10 months [p.182]
- Grade ≥3 TEAEs higher with zanubrutinib + obinutuzumab: Thrombocytopenia (15% vs. 7%), pneumonia (10% vs. 4%), diarrhea (3% vs. 1%), dyspnea (2% vs. 0%) [p.182]
- Similar: Grade ≥3 neutropenia (24% vs. 23%), anemia (5% vs. 6%) [p.182]
- Lower with combination: Grade ≥3 infusion-related reactions and pyrexia; atrial fibrillation (3%), hypertension (3%) [p.182]
- **NCCN Recommendation:** Option for third-line and subsequent therapy [p.182]

**Loncastuximab Tesirine + Rituximab**

Phase II single-arm trial (n = 39) with relapsed/refractory FL, some with POD24 after first-line and transformed FL [p.182]:
- 92% met GELF criteria; 51% had POD24 after first-line [p.182]
- CR rate: 67% [p.182]
- 18-month median follow-up: Median DOR 16 months; 12-month PFS 95% [p.182]
- Grade ≥3 toxicities: Lymphopenia (21%), neutropenia (13%) [p.182]
- **Panel note:** Single-arm design with small sample size; some Panel members advocated non-inclusion pending prospective randomized trial confirmation [p.182]
- **NCCN Recommendation:** Category 2B for third-line therapy (other recommended regimens); long-term follow-up needed to confirm no CD19 expression loss. LOTIS-2 trial in DLBCL showed CD19 antigen loss after loncastuximab tesirine not common, suggesting does not preclude responses to subsequent CD19-directed CAR T-cell therapy, but needs confirmation in larger cohort. Rebiopsy recommended to confirm CD19 positivity before CD19-directed CAR T-cell therapy [p.182-183]

## Special Considerations

### Comparison of Third-Line Therapies

**CAR T-Cell vs. Bispecific Antibodies vs. Small Molecule Inhibitors:**
- CAR T-cell: Single infusion; requires bridging chemotherapy in majority; common TEAEs include CRS, ICANS, prolonged cytopenias (70% cytopenias in ZUMA-5) [p.182]
- Bispecific antibodies: "Off-the-shelf" immunotherapies; better overall safety profile; very few ICANS incidences; more readily available; no bridging chemo needed; require step-up dosing schedule over time to mitigate CRS; step-up can be challenging in rapidly progressing disease [p.182-183]
- Concerns: Loss of CD20 expression possible mechanism of resistance for CD3 x CD20 bispecific therapy; activity in CD20-negative lymphomas unclear [p.183]

### PI3K Inhibitors (No Longer Recommended)

Copanlisib, duvelisib, idelalisib, and umbralisib previously received accelerated FDA approval but underwent voluntary withdrawal based on substantial toxicity with detrimental impact on OS in recent clinical trials [p.176]
- **NCCN Recommendation:** Not recommended for treatment of relapsed/refractory FL [p.176]

### Allogeneic Hematopoietic Cell Transplant

- Results in lower relapse rates than HDT/ASCR but associated with high transplant-related mortality (TRM) [p.176]
- May be considered as third-line consolidation in selected patients [p.176]

### Treatment for Older or Infirm Patients

**Rituximab Monotherapy:**
- Preferred first-line for patients unable to tolerate chemoimmunotherapy [p.183]
- Single-agent activity demonstrated in previously untreated low and advanced-stage FL [p.183]
- Demonstrated activity in relapsed/refractory disease [p.183]

**Chlorambucil + Rituximab:**
- Retrospective study (n = 75) in newly diagnosed FL: ORR 97% (75% CR); 5-year EFS 71%; 5-year OS 98% [p.183]

**Alkylating Agents:**
- Cyclophosphamide or chlorambucil with or without rituximab [p.183]
- Single-agent cyclophosphamide associated with similar OS and CR rates as cyclophosphamide-based combination [p.183]

**Second-Line and Subsequent:**
- Rituximab monotherapy: Preferred [p.183]
- Tazemetostat: Preferred for older/infirm patients [p.183]
- Cyclophosphamide with or without rituximab: Alternate option [p.183]

### Pediatric-Type Follicular Lymphoma (PTFL) in Adults

**Characteristics:**
- Lack of BCL2, BCL6, and IRF4 rearrangements [p.183]
- BCL2 expression in approximately 40-50% of cases; BCL6 expression in majority [p.183]
- Histology: Large expansive follicles with "starry sky" pattern, effacement of architecture, absence of diffuse area, high grade (grade 3), high proliferation index [p.183]

**Prognostic Distinction:**
- Without BCL2 rearrangement but with high proliferation index: Highly indolent course (stage I, no progression/relapse) [p.183]
- With BCL2 rearrangement and/or low proliferation index (Ki-67 <30%): Stage III/IV disease, majority experience progression/recurrence [p.183-184]

**Management:**
- PTFL without BCL2 rearrangements: Generally favorable prognosis; primarily managed with excision (preferred) or involved site radiation therapy (ISRT) [p.184]
- Extensive local disease not candidates for excision/ISRT: RCHOP recommended [p.184]
- Restaging with PET/CT recommended after ISRT or RCHOP completion [p.184]
- No further treatment needed following excision or CR to ISRT/RCHOP [p.184]
- No maintenance therapy data available [p.184]
- Excellent prognosis patients: No surveillance imaging necessary [p.184]
- Less responsive disease (<CR): Treat as progressive FL [p.184]

## Summary of Key Evidence

- **Pediatric follicular lymphoma** is biologically distinct from adult-type, with characteristic MAPK pathway mutations and low genetic complexity [p.187]
- **Follicular lymphoma in situ** (FL-IS) is a precursor lesion that may infrequently progress to manifest disease and is associated with other overt lymphomas [p.186]
- **Prognostic indices** including FLIPI (Follicular Lymphoma International Prognostic Index), FLIPI-2, and m7-FLIPI incorporate genetic mutations and clinical parameters to stratify risk [p.187]
- **PET-CT imaging** after induction therapy is highly predictive of patient outcome and supports detection of histological transformation [p.188]
- **Radiotherapy** for stage I-II follicular lymphoma yields durable long-term remissions, with combined modality therapy (rituximab + radiation) improving progression-free survival [p.189]
- **Rituximab maintenance** following initial induction therapy significantly prolongs response duration and progression-free survival compared to standard dosing or observation alone [p.190-192]
- **Advanced therapy options** for relapsed/refractory disease include PI3K inhibitors (idelalisib, copanlisib, duvelisib, umbralisib), CAR-T cell therapy (axicabtagene ciloleucel, tisagenlecleucel, lisocabtagene maraleucel), bispecific antibodies (mosunetuzumab, epcoritamab), and novel agents (tazemetostat, zanubrutinib) [p.192-195]

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Radiotherapy for Early-Stage Disease

**Localized follicular lymphoma (Stage I-II)** can be effectively managed with involved-field or involved-node radiotherapy alone or with combined modality approaches:

- **Single-agent radiotherapy**: Long-term follow-up studies demonstrate durable disease control in early-stage patients treated at Stanford [p.189, ref.60] and multicenter series [p.189, ref.61]
- **Involved regional vs. involved node radiotherapy**: Comparative analysis shows similar long-term outcomes between regional and node-restricted approaches [p.189, ref.62]
- **Radiotherapy with PET-CT staging**: Retrospective collaborative study by ILROG confirms efficacy of definitive radiotherapy for localized disease staged by (18)F-FDG PET-CT [p.189, ref.63]
- **Combined modality therapy**: Addition of rituximab to involved-field radiotherapy improves progression-free survival in stage I-II patients [p.189, ref.69]

### Systemic Therapy with Rituximab

**First-line immunochemotherapy and rituximab-based approaches**:

- **Rituximab monotherapy schedules**: Prolonged treatment (extended schedule beyond standard weekly x 4) significantly increases event-free survival and response duration compared to standard dosing [p.190, ref.93]
- **Rituximab maintenance**: 2-year maintenance rituximab in high tumor burden patients receiving rituximab plus chemotherapy (PRIMA trial) demonstrates sustained progression-free survival benefit [p.190, ref.97]
- **Obinutuzumab** (anti-CD20 glycoengineered monoclonal antibody): First-line obinutuzumab for advanced follicular lymphoma [p.190, ref.88]
- **Obinutuzumab vs. Rituximab**: GALLIUM study final results compare obinutuzumab versus rituximab immunochemotherapy in previously untreated indolent NHL [p.190, ref.89]

### Combination Approaches

- **Rituximab plus lenalidomide (R2)**: RELEVANCE trial 6-year results show efficacy of lenalidomide plus rituximab versus rituximab-chemotherapy followed by rituximab maintenance in untreated advanced disease [p.190, ref.91]
- **Obinutuzumab plus lenalidomide**: LYSA study demonstrates efficacy in advanced, previously untreated follicular lymphoma patients requiring systemic therapy [p.190, ref.92]

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### Relapsed/Refractory Disease Management

**Salvage immunochemotherapy**:

- **Obinutuzumab plus chemotherapy**: GAUDI study combining obinutuzumab with CHOP or FC in relapsed/refractory follicular lymphoma [p.192, ref.103]
- **Obinutuzumab plus bendamustine**: GADOLIN trial demonstrates superiority of obinutuzumab plus bendamustine versus bendamustine monotherapy in rituximab-refractory patients, with overall survival benefit [p.192, ref.104-105]
- **Bendamustine-rituximab**: Multicentre randomized trial comparing bendamustine plus rituximab versus fludarabine plus rituximab in relapsed indolent lymphomas [p.193, ref.120]

### Novel Targeted Agents

**PI3K inhibitors**:

- **Idelalisib**: PI3Kdelta inhibition effective in relapsed indolent lymphoma and high-risk follicular lymphoma with early relapse [p.192, ref.113-114]
- **Copanlisib**: Long-term safety and efficacy in relapsed/refractory indolent lymphoma with 2-year follow-up from CHRONOS-1 study [p.192, ref.111]
- **Duvelisib (IPI-145)**: Phase II DYNAMO study demonstrates activity in refractory indolent NHL [p.192, ref.112]
- **Umbralisib**: Dual PI3Kdelta/CK1epsilon inhibitor effective in relapsed or refractory indolent lymphoma [p.192, ref.115]

**EZH2 inhibitors**:

- **Tazemetostat**: Open-label single-arm phase 2 trial in relapsed/refractory follicular lymphoma [p.195, ref.148]

### Cell Therapy Approaches

**Chimeric antigen receptor T-cell (CAR-T) therapy**:

- **Axicabtagene ciloleucel (axi-cel)**: ZUMA-5 trial demonstrates durable responses in relapsed/refractory indolent NHL with 5-year follow-up data [p.194, ref.138-140]
- **Tisagenlecleucel**: ELARA trial shows durable response in adults with relapsed/refractory follicular lymphoma [p.194, ref.141]
- **Lisocabtagene maraleucel (liso-cel)**: TRANSCEND FL phase 2 trial demonstrates efficacy; 2-year follow-up available [p.194-195, ref.142-143]

### Bispecific Antibodies

**CD20/CD3 bispecific antibodies**:

- **Mosunetuzumab**: Single-arm multicenter phase 2 study in relapsed/refractory follicular lymphoma showing safety and efficacy, with 3-year follow-up demonstrating long-term outcomes [p.195, ref.144-145]
- **Epcoritamab**: Phase 2 EPCORE NHL-1 cohort demonstrates monotherapy efficacy in relapsed/refractory follicular lymphoma [p.195, ref.147]

**Mechanisms of resistance**: Loss of CD20 expression identified as resistance mechanism to mosunetuzumab in relapsed/refractory B-cell lymphomas [p.195, ref.146]

### Stem Cell Transplantation

**Autologous transplant for early therapy failure**:

- **Autologous SCT in early progression**: National LymphoCare Study and CIBMTR analysis demonstrate efficacy in follicular lymphoma with early therapy failure [p.192, ref.108]
- **Autologous vs. allogeneic transplant**: Comparative analysis of autologous versus allogeneic transplantation in patients with early treatment failure [p.192, ref.110]
- **Long-term outcomes**: Studies document long-term safety and efficacy after high-dose therapy and autologous stem cell transplantation [p.193, ref.137]

**Allogeneic transplant**: EBMT registry matched study demonstrates lower relapse rate with allogeneic compared to autologous transplant, though with higher procedure-related mortality [p.193, ref.117]

### Maintenance Therapies

- **Rituximab maintenance post-salvage**: Prolongs response duration after salvage therapy with rituximab-containing regimens in recurring/refractory disease [p.193, ref.129]
- **Prolonged subcutaneous rituximab**: MabCute phase III study demonstrates safety and efficacy of prolonged maintenance with SC rituximab in relapsed/refractory patients [p.193, ref.132]

### Early Prognostic Markers

- **Progression of disease (POD) at 24 months (POD24)**: Validation across 5225 patients in 13 clinical trials establishes POD24 as robust early clinical endpoint predicting poor survival [p.191, ref.102]
- **Early event status**: Post-induction PET-CT response predicts subsequent outcome in newly diagnosed disease [p.190, ref.101]

## Special Considerations

### Pathologic Variants

**Follicular lymphoma in situ**:

- FL-IS has variable clinical significance and natural history; may infrequently progress but frequently precedes manifest follicular lymphoma [p.186, ref.28]
- FL-IS provides insights into clonal evolution from in situ to overt disease [p.186, ref.29]

**Pediatric-type follicular lymphoma**:

- Genetically and biologically distinct from adult follicular lymphoma with low genetic complexity and frequent TNFRSF14 alterations [p.186-187, ref.20-22]
- Predominantly presents in children and young adults [p.186]

**High proliferation index**:

- Low-grade histology with high proliferation index represents distinct clinical-pathologic subset [p.187, ref.31]
- Intra-follicular tumor cell proliferative rate has independent prognostic significance [p.187, ref.32]

### Risk Stratification

**FLIPI and FLIPI-2 scores**:

- Follicular Lymphoma International Prognostic Index (FLIPI) established as standard prognostic tool [p.187, ref.35-36]
- FLIPI-2 developed to improve prognostication in modern treatment era [p.187, ref.37]

**Molecular prognostication**:

- Integration of gene mutations (EZH2, TP53, ARID1A, MLL2) in m7-FLIPI improves risk stratification for first-line immunochemotherapy patients [p.187, ref.39]
- EZH2 mutation status may predict differential efficacy of chemotherapy in GALLIUM trial [p.187, ref.40]

### Imaging Assessment

**PET-CT in diagnosis and surveillance**:

- 18F-FDG PET/CT visualizes follicular lymphoma across all grades [p.187, ref.43]
- Intensity of 18F-FDG uptake distinguishes between indolent and aggressive NHL [p.187, ref.44]
- Baseline SUVmax did not predict histological transformation in GALLIUM phase 3 study [p.188, ref.49]

**Transformation detection**:

- FDG-PET/CT imaging guides biopsies in detection of histological transformation [p.188, ref.45]
- Majority of transformed lymphomas have high SUVs similar to DLBCL [p.188, ref.46]
- FDG-PET/CT-guided rebiopsy may identify clinically unsuspected transformation [p.188, ref.48]

**Post-induction PET-CT**:

- Highly predictive of patient outcome following induction therapy (PRIMA trial subset analysis) [p.188, ref.55]
- Impact on high-tumor burden patients treated with immunochemotherapy (GOELAMS study) [p.188, ref.56]
- Prognostic role confirmed in FOLL05 trial subset analysis [p.188, ref.57]

### Follow-up Imaging

- CT abdomen/pelvis provides limited additional benefit for surveillance in stages I-III disease [p.188, ref.58]
- 18F-FDG PET-CT scan has established role in follow-up of lymphoma [p.188-189, ref.59]
