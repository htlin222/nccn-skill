## Tumor Lysis Syndrome (TLS)

### Laboratory Hallmarks [p.117]

* High potassium [p.117]
* High uric acid [p.117]
* High phosphorous [p.117]
* Low calcium [p.117]

### Clinical Symptoms [p.117]

* Nausea and vomiting [p.117]
* Shortness of breath [p.117]
* Irregular heartbeat [p.117]
* Clouding of urine [p.117]
* Lethargy [p.117]
* Joint discomfort [p.117]

### Risk Factors for TLS Prophylaxis [p.117]

* Histologies of Burkitt lymphoma (BL) and lymphoblastic lymphoma (LL); occasionally diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL) [p.117]
* Spontaneous TLS [p.117]
* Elevated white blood cell (WBC) count [p.117]
* Bone marrow involvement [p.117]
* Pre-existing elevated uric acid [p.117]
* Ineffectiveness/intolerance of allopurinol [p.117]
* Renal disease or renal involvement by tumor [p.117]

### TLS Management Strategy [p.117]

TLS is best managed if anticipated and treatment is started prior to chemotherapy [p.117].

Centerpiece of treatment includes: [p.117]

* Rigorous hydration [p.117]
* Management of hyperuricemia [p.117]
* Frequent monitoring of electrolytes and aggressive correction (essential) [p.117]

### Hyperuricemia Management: First-Line and At Retreatment [p.117]

**Testing Required** [p.117]

* Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) testing is required prior to use of rasburicase [p.117]
* Rasburicase is contraindicated in patients with a history consistent with G6PD [p.117]
* In G6PD-positive patients, rasburicase should be substituted with allopurinol [p.117]

**Low-Risk Disease** [p.117]

* Allopurinol or febuxostat (Category 2A) [p.117]
  * Beginning 2–3 days prior to chemoimmunotherapy [p.117]
  * Continued for 10–14 days [p.117]

**Intermediate-Risk Disease: Stage I/II and LDH <2X ULN** [p.117]

* Allopurinol or febuxostat (Category 2A) [p.117]
  → OR
* Rasburicase (Category 2A) if renal dysfunction and uric acid, potassium, and/or phosphate >ULN [p.117]

**High-Risk Disease: Stage III/IV and/or LDH ≥2X ULN** [p.117]

* Rasburicase (Category 2A) [p.117]

### Rasburicase Dosing and Re-dosing [p.117]

* Doses of 3–6 mg are usually effective [p.117]
* One dose of rasburicase is frequently adequate [p.117]
* Re-dosing should be individualized and is indicated for patients with any of the following: [p.117]
  * Urgent need to initiate therapy in a high-bulk patient [p.117]
  * Situations where adequate hydration may be difficult or impossible [p.117]
  * Acute renal failure [p.117]

### Consequences of Untreated TLS [p.117]

If TLS is untreated, its progression may cause: [p.117]

* Acute kidney failure [p.117]
* Cardiac arrhythmias [p.117]
* Seizures [p.117]
* Loss of muscle control [p.117]
* Death [p.117]

## Viral Reactivation

### Hepatitis B Virus (HBV) [p.118]

**Risk and Screening** [p.118]

* Anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody (mAb)-based chemoimmunotherapy is associated with risk of HBV reactivation [p.118]
* Hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg) and hepatitis B core antibody (HBcAb) testing for all patients receiving anti-CD20 mAb therapy [p.118]
* Quantitative hepatitis B viral load by PCR and surface antibody only if one of the screening tests is positive [p.118]
* Note: Patients receiving IV immunoglobulin (IVIG) may be HBcAb-positive as a consequence of IVIG therapy [p.118]

**Prophylactic Antiviral Therapy** [p.118]

* Prophylactic antiviral therapy with entecavir is recommended for any patient who is HBsAg-positive and receiving anti-lymphoma therapy [p.118]
* If there is active disease (PCR+), it is considered treatment/management and not prophylactic therapy [p.118]

**HBcAb Positive Patients** [p.118]

* Prophylactic antiviral therapy is preferred [p.118]
* However, if there is concurrent high-level hepatitis B surface antibody, these patients may be monitored with serial hepatitis B viral load [p.118]

**Antiviral Agent Selection** [p.118]

* **Entecavir** (preferred) (Category 2A) [p.118]
* Avoid lamivudine due to risks of resistance development [p.118]
* Other antivirals including adefovir, telbivudine, and tenofovir are proven active treatments and are acceptable alternatives [p.118]

**Monitoring During and After Treatment** [p.118]

* Monitor hepatitis B viral load with PCR monthly through treatment and every 3 months thereafter [p.118]
  * If viral load is consistently undetectable, treatment is considered prophylactic [p.118]
  * If viral load fails to drop or previously undetectable PCR becomes positive, consult hepatologist and discontinue anti-CD20 mAb therapy [p.118]
* Maintain prophylaxis up to 12 months after oncologic treatment ends [p.118]
  * Consult with hepatologist for duration of therapy in patient with active HBV [p.118]

### Hepatitis C Virus (HCV) [p.118]

**Epidemiology** [p.118]

* New evidence from large epidemiologic studies, molecular biology research, and clinical observation supports an association between HCV and B-cell non-Hodgkin lymphoma (NHL) [p.118]
* Recently approved direct-acting antiviral (DAA) agents for chronic carriers of HCV with genotype 1 demonstrated a high rate of sustained viral responses [p.118]

**Low-Grade B-Cell NHL** [p.118]

* According to the American Association for the Study of Liver Diseases, combined therapy with DAA agents should be considered in asymptomatic patients with HCV genotype 1 [p.118]
* This therapy can result in regression of lymphoma [p.118]

**Aggressive B-Cell NHL** [p.118]

* Patients should be initially treated with chemoimmunotherapy regimens as outlined in these guidelines [p.118]
* Liver functional tests and serum HCV RNA levels should be closely monitored during and after chemoimmunotherapy for development of hepatotoxicity [p.118]
* Antiviral therapy should be considered in patients in complete remission after completion of lymphoma therapy [p.118]

### John Cunningham Virus (JCV) Reactivation [p.118]

**Associated Drug** [p.118]

* Brentuximab vedotin (anti-CD30 antibody-drug conjugate) can cause JCV reactivation and progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML) [p.118]

**Clinical Course** [p.118]

* PML is usually fatal [p.118]

**Clinical Presentation** [p.118]

Clinical indications may include: [p.118]

* Changes in behavior such as confusion [p.118]
* Dizziness or loss of balance [p.118]
* Difficulty talking or walking [p.118]
* Vision problems [p.118]

**Diagnosis** [p.118]

* Made by PCR of cerebrospinal fluid and in some cases brain biopsy [p.118]

**Treatment** [p.118]

* No known effective treatment [p.118]

## Additional Rare Complications of Monoclonal Antibody Therapy [p.119]

### Mucocutaneous Reactions [p.119]

* Rare complications such as mucocutaneous reactions including paraneoplastic pemphigus, Stevens-Johnson syndrome, lichenoid dermatitis, vesiculobullous dermatitis, and toxic epidermal necrolysis can occur [p.119]
* Expert consultation with dermatology is recommended [p.119]

### Re-Challenge with Anti-CD20 Monoclonal Antibodies [p.119]

* Re-challenge with the same mAb is not recommended in patients experiencing rare complications to chosen anti-CD20 mAb (rituximab, obinutuzumab, or ofatumumab) [p.119]
* An alternative anti-CD20 mAb (e.g., obinutuzumab) could be used for patients with intolerance to rituximab, including those experiencing severe hypersensitivity reactions requiring discontinuation of chosen anti-CD20 mAb, regardless of histology [p.119]
* It is unclear that the use of alternative anti-CD20 mAb poses the same risk of recurrence [p.119]

### Rituximab and Obinutuzumab Rapid Infusion [p.119]

* If no infusion reactions were experienced with prior cycle of rituximab or obinutuzumab, a rapid infusion over 90 minutes can be used [p.119]

## Rituximab-Related Neutropenia [p.119]

* Late-onset neutropenia (weeks to months after last exposure) occurs in up to 20% of patients [p.119]
* It can be severe, but patients usually do not present with infections and can be initially observed for spontaneous recovery [p.119]
* A short course of G-CSF is indicated for prolonged neutropenia [p.119]
* IVIG has been anecdotally successful in patients with neutropenia that is refractory to G-CSF [p.119]

## Obinutuzumab-Related Neutropenia [p.119]

* Late-onset neutropenia can be seen in up to 20% of patients [p.119]
* Early-onset neutropenia is a risk with obinutuzumab monotherapy as well as with combination chemotherapy or targeted agents [p.119]
* Neutropenia is responsive to growth factors [p.119]

## Management of Methotrexate Toxicity [p.119]

### Glucarpidase Indication [p.119]

Glucarpidase is strongly recommended when: [p.119]

* Plasma methotrexate concentrations are two standard deviations above the mean expected plasma concentration as determined by MTXPK.org [p.119]
  → OR
* Plasma methotrexate level is >30 μM at 36 hours, >10 μM at 42 hours, or >5 μM at 48 hours [p.119]

### Glucarpidase Administration [p.119]

* Optimal administration of glucarpidase is within 48 to 60 hours from the start of methotrexate infusion [p.119]
* Leucovorin should be continued for at least 2 days following glucarpidase administration [p.119]
* Leucovorin should be administered at least 2 hours before or 2 hours after the dose of glucarpidase [p.119]

## Special Considerations for Adolescent and Young Adult (AYA) Patients with B-Cell Lymphomas [p.120]

* See NCCN Guidelines for Adolescent and Young Adult (AYA) Oncology for comprehensive initial evaluations and more details on fertility/fertility preservation and psychosocial assessments in AYA patients [p.120]
* The use of dexrazoxane as a cardioprotectant in combination with first-line therapy is not recommended in AYA patients >18 years [p.120]
* Consider toxicity of radiation therapy (RT), particularly in young females with mediastinal disease [p.120]
* Younger AYA patients may be eligible to participate in pediatric clinical trials [p.120]

## Immunizations [p.120]

* See NCCN Guidelines for Survivorship - General Principles of Immunizations [p.120]
* COVID-19 vaccination: See CDC for Use of COVID-19 Vaccines in the US [p.120]

## Anti-Infective Prophylaxis [p.120]

* Prophylaxis for Pneumocystis Jiroveci Pneumonia (PJP) and Varicella-Zoster Virus (VZV): See NCCN Guidelines for Prevention and Treatment of Cancer-Related Infections [p.120]
* Pemivibart for pre-exposure prophylaxis of COVID-19 for individuals with moderate to severe immunocompromise [p.120]

## Hypogammaglobulinemia [p.120]

* Patients receiving anti-CD20 mAb and CAR T-cell (CD19–directed) therapy may experience hypogammaglobulinemia [p.120]
* Patients with recurrent infections may benefit from IVIG replacement [p.120]

## Bone Health: Recommendations for Patients Who Have Received Steroid-Containing Regimens [p.121]

### Therapy [p.121]

**Vitamin D Management** [p.121]

* If vitamin D 25-OH is deficient, then replete [p.121]
* In patients with lymphoma with current elevations in 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D, deficient 25(OH)D levels should not be aggressively replaced [p.121]

**Calcium Intake** [p.121]

* Calcium intake from food (plus supplements if necessary) should be commensurate with National Academy of Medicine recommendations [p.121]
  * Exception: cases of lymphoma-induced hypercalciuria/hypercalcemia due to excessive 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D production [p.121]
* In patients receiving corticosteroid-containing chemotherapy regimens, adequate calcium intake is of paramount importance since corticosteroids block calcium absorption and increase fracture risk [p.121]

**Pharmacologic Treatment for Osteoporosis** [p.121]

* Patients with osteoporotic BMD, with a history of hip or vertebral fractures, or with asymptomatic vertebral compression deformity (as seen on CT scan or other imaging) should be started on therapy as per National Osteoporosis Foundation guidelines [p.121]
* Referral to an endocrinologist with expertise in bone health is recommended [p.121]

**Special Populations** [p.121]

* In appropriate patients with premature menopause, hormone replacement therapy up until the expected time of natural menopause, or raloxifene could be considered [p.121]

**First-Line Pharmacologic Treatment** [p.121]

* Bisphosphonates should be used as first-line pharmacologic treatment for osteoporosis [p.121]

**Alternative Agent for Bisphosphonate Intolerance** [p.121]

* In patients who cannot tolerate or whose symptoms do not improve with bisphosphonate therapy, denosumab is an effective alternative medication to prevent osteoporotic fractures [p.121]

**Contraindicated Agent** [p.121]

* Teriparatide is contraindicated in patients with a history of RT [p.121]
* Theoretical concerns in patients with a recent history of cancer exist [p.121]

### Evaluation [p.121]

**Initial Testing** [p.121]

* Vitamin D, 25-OH level [p.121]

**Post-Treatment Bone Mineral Density (BMD) Evaluation** [p.121]

* BMD evaluation 1 year following therapy [p.121]
* Greatest risk in patients with chemotherapy-induced premature menopause [p.121]

**If Osteopenic (T-score between -1.1 and -2.4)** [p.121]

* Use Fracture Risk Assessment Tool (FRAX) to determine if drug therapy is necessary (https://www.sheffield.ac.uk/FRAX/) [p.121]
* Treatment thresholds: 20% risk for any major osteoporotic fracture or 3% risk for hip fracture [p.121]

**If T-score -2 to -2.4 (at any site) or ongoing glucocorticoid exposure** [p.121]

* Repeat BMD every 1–2 years, as long as risk factors persist [p.121]

**If T-score -1.5 to -1.9 (at any site) with no risk factors** [p.121]

* Repeat BMD in 5 years [p.121]

### Avascular Necrosis [p.121]

* Consider diagnosis of avascular necrosis (AVN) in patients who have received corticosteroids presenting with hip pain [p.121]
* See NCCN Guidelines for Survivorship [p.121]
