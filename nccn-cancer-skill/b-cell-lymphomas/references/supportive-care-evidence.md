## Summary of Key Evidence

- Tumor lysis syndrome (TLS) is a serious complication of anticancer therapy that typically occurs within 12 to 72 hours after starting chemoimmunotherapy [p.148]. Laboratory TLS is defined as a 25% increase in serum uric acid, potassium, or phosphorus, or a 25% decrease in calcium levels [p.148].

- For TLS management, rasburicase is highly effective for intermediate-risk and high-risk disease, with single fixed doses (3 mg or 6 mg) or weight-based dosing (0.05-0.15 mg/kg) demonstrated as effective in adult patients [p.148]. Rasburicase is contraindicated in G6PD-deficient patients due to risk of methemoglobinemia or hemolysis [p.148].

- Hepatitis B virus (HBV) reactivation occurs in patients with B-cell lymphomas receiving rituximab-containing regimens, particularly those with positive HBcAb testing (with or without HBsAb positivity), even among patients with negative HBsAg prior to treatment initiation [p.149].

- Entecavir prophylaxis is recommended for patients with HBV infection (HBsAg positive) or HBsAg-negative/HBcAb-positive patients receiving anti-lymphoma therapy, with surveillance and prophylaxis continued for up to 12 months after treatment completion [p.149-150].

- Hepatitis C virus (HCV) seropositivity is strongly associated with increased incidence of B-cell lymphomas, particularly DLBCL and marginal zone lymphoma [p.150]. Initial antiviral therapy should be considered for asymptomatic patients with HCV-positive low-grade B-cell lymphomas [p.150].

- Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML) is a rare but serious CNS infection caused by JC polyomavirus reactivation that can occur in patients with B-cell lymphomas treated with rituximab, brentuximab vedotin, or chemotherapy with purine analogs [p.151]. No effective treatment exists; careful monitoring for neurologic symptoms is essential [p.151].

- Steroid-containing systemic therapy regimens increase risk of fractures and treatment-induced bone loss [p.152]. Evaluation of vitamin D levels and post-treatment bone mineral density assessment is recommended for patients receiving steroid-based therapy, with bisphosphonates as first-line pharmacologic treatment for osteoporosis [p.152].

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Tumor Lysis Syndrome Management

Tumor lysis syndrome (TLS) is a potentially serious metabolic complication characterized by abnormalities including hyperkalemia, hyperuricemia, hyperphosphatemia, and hypocalcemia [p.148]. Clinical symptoms include nausea and vomiting, diarrhea, seizures, shortness of breath, renal insufficiency, and cardiac arrhythmias. Untreated TLS can result in profound metabolic changes, cardiac arrhythmias, seizures, loss of muscle control, acute renal failure, and death [p.148].

**Risk Factors and Prophylaxis:**
Histologies of Burkitt lymphoma (BL), lymphoblastic lymphoma, and occasionally diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL), bone marrow involvement, bulky chemosensitive tumors, elevated leukocyte count, pretreatment elevated lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), pre-existing elevated uric acid, renal disease, or renal tumor involvement are considered risk factors [p.148]. TLS prophylaxis should be considered for patients with any of these risk factors, and frequent electrolyte monitoring with aggressive correction is essential [p.148].

**Hyperuricemia Management:**
Allopurinol, febuxostat, and rasburicase are highly effective for managing hyperuricemia [p.148]. Allopurinol or febuxostat is recommended for low-risk or intermediate-risk disease and should be started 2 to 3 days prior to chemoimmunotherapy initiation and continued for 10 to 14 days [p.148].

Rasburicase is recommended for intermediate-risk disease (if renal dysfunction and uric acid, potassium, and/or phosphate greater than upper limit of normal) or high-risk disease [p.148]. A single dose of rasburicase (3 mg or 6 mg) is adequate in most circumstances [p.148]. Repeat dosing should be individualized based on bulky disease requiring immediate therapy, inability to achieve adequate hydration, or acute renal failure [p.148].

**G6PD Testing:** Rasburicase is contraindicated in patients with glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency due to increased risk of methemoglobinemia or hemolysis [p.148]. G6PD testing should be considered prior to rasburicase initiation, with allopurinol substitution if G6PD deficiency is present [p.148-149].

### Hepatitis B Virus Reactivation Prevention

**Epidemiology and Risk:**
Patients with malignancies and HBV infection (HBsAg-positive or HBcAb-positive) are at risk for HBV reactivation with cytotoxic chemotherapy [p.149]. A meta-analysis of FDA safety reports demonstrated that HBcAb positivity was correlated with increased incidence of rituximab-associated HBV reactivation [p.149]. In patients with B-cell lymphomas treated with rituximab-containing regimens, HBV reactivation occurred in patients with positive HBcAb testing (with or without HBsAb positivity), even among patients with negative HBsAg prior to treatment initiation [p.149].

False-negative HBsAg results may occur in patients with chronic liver disease; therefore, patients with a history of hepatitis requiring chemoimmunotherapy should be assessed by viral load measurement [p.149].

**Testing Recommendations:**
The Panel recommends HBsAg and HBcAb testing for all patients with B-cell lymphomas planned for treatment with anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody (mAb)-containing regimens [p.149]. In individuals who test positive for HBsAg and/or HBcAb, baseline quantitative polymerase chain reaction (PCR) for HBV DNA should be obtained to determine viral load, though a negative baseline PCR does not preclude the possibility of reactivation [p.149].

HBsAb positivity is generally equated with protective immunity, although reactivated HBV disease may occur in the setting of significant immunosuppression in individuals with HBV infection that is HBcAb positive [p.149]. Vaccination against HBV should be strongly considered in patients without HBV infection (negative for HBsAg, HBsAb, and HBcAb) [p.149].

**Antiviral Prophylaxis:**
Entecavir prophylaxis is recommended for patients with HBV infection (HBsAg positive) receiving anti-lymphoma therapy [p.149]. Entecavir prophylaxis is also the preferred approach for patients with HBV infection (HBsAg negative but HBcAb positive); however, in the presence of concurrently high levels of HBsAb, appropriate supportive care interventions such as monitoring with serial HBV viral load measurements and treatment with pre-emptive antivirals upon increasing viral load may be necessary [p.149].

Lamivudine has been shown to reduce risks for HBV reactivation in patients with HBV infection (HBsAg-positive) and hematologic malignancies treated with immunosuppressive cytotoxic agents [p.149]. However, lamivudine should be avoided due to risks for the development of resistance [p.149]. Entecavir has been shown to be more effective than lamivudine in preventing rituximab-associated HBV reactivation [p.149].

Other antivirals such as adefovir, telbivudine, and tenofovir have also demonstrated antiviral efficacy in patients with chronic HBV infection and are acceptable alternatives [p.150]. The optimal choice of prophylactic antiviral therapy will be driven by institutional standards or recommendation from hepatology or an infectious disease consultant [p.150].

**Duration and Monitoring:**
Surveillance and antiviral prophylaxis should be continued for up to 12 months after the completion of oncologic treatment [p.150]. During the treatment period, viral load should be monitored monthly with PCR and then every 3 months after treatment completion [p.150]. If viral load is consistently undetectable, prophylaxis with antivirals should be continued [p.150]. If viral load fails to drop or a previously undetectable PCR becomes positive, consultation with a hepatologist and discontinuation of anti-CD20 mAb therapy is recommended [p.150].

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### Hepatitis C Virus-Associated B-Cell Lymphomas

**Epidemiology:**
Large population-based and multicenter case-control studies have demonstrated a strong association between seropositivity for hepatitis C virus (HCV) and development of B-cell lymphomas [p.150]. The prevalence of HCV seropositivity was consistently increased among patients with diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL) and marginal zone lymphoma (MZL) [p.150]. In a retrospective study, persistent HCV infection remained a significant independent factor associated with development of malignant lymphomas, and achievement of sustained virologic response (SVR) with interferon-based therapy may reduce the incidence of malignant lymphoma in people with HCV infection [p.150].

**Treatment Response:**
Several published reports suggested that treatment with antivirals (typically interferon with or without ribavirin) led to regression of lymphoma in people with HCV infection, providing evidence for HCV involvement in pathogenesis of lymphoproliferative diseases [p.150]. In patients with HCV infection and indolent B-cell lymphomas not requiring immediate treatment with chemoimmunotherapy, initial treatment with interferon (with or without ribavirin) appeared to induce lymphoma regression in a high proportion of patients [p.150]. In patients with HCV-infection and B-cell lymphomas in remission after anti-lymphoma therapy, subsequent antiviral therapy may be associated with lower risk of disease relapse [p.150].

**Outcomes in Aggressive Lymphomas:**
In patients with aggressive B-cell lymphomas, an earlier analysis of pooled data from GELA clinical studies (prior to the rituximab era) suggested that HCV seropositivity in patients with DLBCL was associated with significantly decreased survival outcomes, due in part to severe hepatotoxicity among those with HCV infection [p.150]. Subsequent studies in the rituximab era showed that HCV seropositivity was not predictive of outcomes in terms of progression-free survival (PFS) or overall survival (OS) in patients with DLBCL [p.150]. However, the incidence of hepatotoxicity with chemoimmunotherapy was higher among people with HCV infection, confirming observations from the GELA studies [p.150].

**Advanced Antiviral Therapy:**
The treatment of chronic HCV infection has improved with newer antiviral agents, especially those targeting HCV genotype 1 carriers. Direct-acting antiviral agents (DAA) administered in combination with standard antivirals (pegylated interferon and ribavirin) have shown significantly higher rates of SVR compared with standard therapy alone in chronic carriers of HCV genotype 1 [p.150]. Telaprevir and boceprevir are DAA approved by the FDA for treatment (in combination with pegylated interferon and ribavirin) of patients with HCV genotype 1 infection [p.150]. The updated guidelines from the American Association for the Study of Liver Diseases (AASLD) recommended that DAA be incorporated into standard antiviral therapy for patients infected with HCV genotype 1 [p.150].

**Panel Recommendations:**
The Panel recommends initial antiviral therapy in asymptomatic patients with HCV-positive low-grade B-cell lymphomas [p.150]. For those with HCV genotype 1, triple antiviral therapy with inclusion of direct-acting antivirals should be considered as per AASLD guidelines [p.150-151].

Patients with HCV-positive aggressive B-cell lymphomas should initially be treated with appropriate chemoimmunotherapy regimens according to the NCCN Guidelines for B-Cell Lymphomas [p.151]. Liver function and serum HCV RNA levels should be closely monitored for the development of hepatotoxicity during and after chemoimmunotherapy [p.151]. Antiviral therapy should then be considered in patients who achieve a complete response (CR) after completion of chemoimmunotherapy [p.151].

### Progressive Multifocal Leukoencephalopathy

Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML) is a rare but serious and usually fatal CNS infection caused by reactivation of the latent JC polyomavirus [p.151]. PML generally occurs in individuals who are severely immunocompromised, as in the case of people with HIV and in patients with low CD4+ T-cells prior to or during anti-lymphoma treatment [p.151].

**Reported Cases:**
PML has been reported in patients with B-cell lymphomas treated with rituximab (usually in combination with chemotherapy), brentuximab vedotin, or chemotherapy with purine analogs or alkylating agents [p.151].

**Clinical Presentation and Diagnosis:**
PML is clinically suspected based on neurologic signs and symptoms that may include confusion, motor weakness or poor motor coordination, visual changes, and/or speech changes [p.151]. PML is usually diagnosed with PCR of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) or, in certain circumstances, by the analysis of brain biopsy material [p.151].

**Management and Monitoring:**
There is no effective treatment for PML [p.151]. Patients should be carefully monitored for the development of any neurologic symptoms [p.151]. There is currently no consensus on pretreatment evaluations that can be undertaken to predict the subsequent development of PML [p.151].

### Methotrexate Toxicity

Glucarpidase should be used for patients with significant renal dysfunction receiving high-dose methotrexate [p.151]. The guidelines recommend the use of a web-based tool to optimize the administration of glucarpidase (based on the plasma concentrations of methotrexate) for patients receiving high-dose methotrexate [p.151].

## Special Considerations

### Anti-CD20 Monoclonal Antibody Therapy Intolerance

**Mucocutaneous Reactions:**
Rare complications such as mucocutaneous reactions including paraneoplastic pemphigus, Stevens-Johnson syndrome, lichenoid dermatitis, vesiculobullous dermatitis, and toxic epidermal necrolysis can occur in patients treated with anti-CD20 mAb [p.151]. Expert consultation with a dermatologist is recommended [p.151].

**Re-Challenge and Alternatives:**
Re-challenge with the same anti-CD20 mAb is not recommended in patients experiencing mucocutaneous complications to chosen anti-CD20 mAb (rituximab or obinutuzumab) [p.151]. An alternative anti-CD20 mAb (obinutuzumab) could be used for patients with intolerance to rituximab, including those experiencing severe hypersensitivity reactions requiring discontinuation of chosen anti-CD20 mAb, regardless of histology [p.151]. However, it is unclear if such a substitution poses the same risk of recurrence [p.151].

**Biosimilars and Subcutaneous Formulations:**
An FDA-approved biosimilar is an appropriate substitute for rituximab in all subtypes of B-cell lymphomas [p.151]. Rituximab and hyaluronidase injection for subcutaneous use is approved by the FDA for the treatment of patients with previously untreated and relapsed/refractory follicular lymphoma (FL) and previously untreated diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL), only for patients who have received at least one full dose of intravenous rituximab [p.151].

The guidelines recommend that rituximab and hyaluronidase injection for subcutaneous use may be substituted for intravenous rituximab after patients have received the first full dose of rituximab by intravenous infusion [p.151]. Switching to subcutaneous rituximab is not recommended until a full intravenous dose of rituximab is successfully administered without experiencing severe adverse reactions [p.151].

### Bone Health in Patients Receiving Steroid-Containing Regimens

**Risk and Assessment:**
Steroid-containing systemic therapy regimens have been associated with increased risk of fractures and treatment-induced bone loss in patients with B-cell lymphomas [p.152]. The risk of treatment-induced bone loss is higher among young females with chemotherapy-induced premature menopause and older patients receiving chemotherapy [p.152]. Patients with newly diagnosed B-cell lymphomas are also at risk of low bone mineral density (BMD), which may worsen during treatment with steroid-based systemic therapy [p.152].

**Evaluation and Referral:**
Referral to an endocrinologist with expertise on bone health and initiation of treatment as per National Osteoporosis Foundation guidelines is recommended for patients with osteoporotic BMD, with a history of hip or vertebral fractures, or with asymptomatic vertebral compression deformity (as seen on imaging studies) [p.152].

Evaluation of vitamin D levels and post-treatment bone mineral density (BMD) evaluation using a fracture risk assessment tool is recommended for patients receiving steroid-based systemic therapy [p.152].

**Nutritional and Pharmacologic Management:**
Adequate calcium intake is essential since corticosteroids block calcium absorption and increase the risk of fracture [p.152]. Raloxifene or hormone replacement therapy up until the expected time of natural menopause could be considered in appropriate patients with premature menopause [p.152].

Bisphosphonates should be used as first-line pharmacologic treatment for osteoporosis [p.152]. Denosumab is an effective alternative option to prevent osteoporotic fractures in patients who cannot tolerate or whose symptoms do not improve with bisphosphonate therapy [p.152].

## Summary of Key Evidence

- Hepatitis B reactivation is a serious risk in lymphoma patients receiving chemotherapy and immunotherapy; prophylactic antiviral agents (lamivudine, entecavir) are recommended [p.159-160] [91-94]
- Entecavir demonstrates superior viral DNA reduction compared to lamivudine and adefovir in preventing HBV reactivation [p.160] [93, 102]
- Hepatitis C virus-associated B-cell lymphomas may regress with antiviral therapy alone, and treatment of HCV infection can be effective in indolent lymphomas [p.159-160] [103-109]
- Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML) is a rare but serious adverse effect of rituximab and brentuximab vedotin requiring monitoring [p.160-161] [119-122]
- Alternative anti-CD20 antibodies (obinutuzumab, ofatumumab) or desensitization protocols can be considered for patients with rituximab hypersensitivity [p.161] [125-126]
- Subcutaneous rituximab administration offers equivalent efficacy to intravenous rituximab with improved tolerability [p.161] [127-128]
- Bone health monitoring and prevention strategies are essential, as chemotherapy and corticosteroids significantly reduce bone mineral density in lymphoma patients [p.161-162] [129-134]

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Hepatitis B Prophylaxis

**Prophylactic Lamivudine:** Meta-analysis shows prophylactic lamivudine reduces chemotherapy-associated hepatitis B reactivation in lymphoma patients [p.159] [91]. However, lamivudine resistance with emergence of YMDD mutations has been documented as a treatment failure mechanism [p.159] [96].

**Entecavir Prophylaxis:** Randomized controlled trial (RCT) of entecavir versus lamivudine for HBV prophylaxis in rituximab-treated lymphoma patients with resolved HBV infection showed benefit with entecavir [p.159] [92]. A second RCT comparing entecavir vs. lamivudine in untreated DLBCL patients receiving R-CHOP demonstrated superior performance of entecavir in preventing HBV reactivation [p.159] [93]. Meta-analysis confirms entecavir superiority over lamivudine in preventing HBV reactivation in chemotherapy-treated lymphoma patients [p.160] [94].

### Hepatitis C Management

**Antiviral Treatment Response:** Retrospective analysis demonstrated regression of splenic lymphoma with villous lymphocytes after hepatitis C virus treatment [p.160] [103]. Response to antiviral treatment has been documented in HCV-associated marginal zone lymphomas [p.160] [104]. Systematic review showed regression of lymphoproliferative disorders after HCV treatment [p.160] [106]. Multicenter Italian study demonstrated role of anti-HCV treatment in HCV-related low-grade B-cell NHL [p.160] [107]. Case report evidence suggests antiviral therapy after complete chemotherapy response may be efficacious in HCV-positive NHL [p.160] [108]. Prospective study of indolent B-cell lymphomas associated with HCV infection treated with antiviral agents demonstrated clinical benefit [p.160] [109].

**DLBCL and HCV:** Japanese multicenter analysis showed hepatic toxicity and prognosis in HBV-infected DLBCL patients treated with rituximab-containing regimens [p.160] [112]. Single-center experience in DLBCL with HCV infection in the rituximab era demonstrated improved outcomes [p.160] [113].

**HCV Protease Inhibitors:** Boceprevir and telaprevir were assessed for previously treated and untreated chronic HCV genotype 1 infection with application to NHL patients [p.160] [114-117].

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### Management of Rituximab Adverse Effects

**Progressive Multifocal Leukoencephalopathy (PML):** Multicenter adverse event analysis reported 57 cases of PML after rituximab therapy in HIV-negative lymphoma patients from the RADAR project [p.160] [119]. Additional case reports document PML in follicular lymphoma patients during rituximab-containing chemotherapy with low IgG levels and low CD4+ count [p.161] [120-121]. Similarly, 5 cases of PML were associated with brentuximab vedotin therapy reported to SONAR project [p.161] [122].

**Rituximab Hypersensitivity:** Alternative anti-CD20 antibody therapy versus desensitization for patients requiring discontinuation of rituximab, obinutuzumab, or ofatumumab due to drug hypersensitivity reactions was explored [p.161] [125]. Successful obinutuzumab administration was documented in patients with previous rituximab intolerance [p.161] [126].

### Subcutaneous Rituximab Administration

**SABRINA Trial:** Randomized, open-label, phase 3 trial (n=NR) comparing subcutaneous vs. intravenous rituximab for first-line follicular lymphoma treatment demonstrated equivalent efficacy with improved safety profile [p.161] [127].

**MabEase Trial:** Randomized trial of subcutaneous and intravenous rituximab plus cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, vincristine, and prednisone in first-line DLBCL treatment showed comparable efficacy [p.161] [128].

## Special Considerations

### Bone Health Management

**Bone Loss Risk:** R-CHOP treatment significantly reduces CT-assessed vertebral bone density in DLBCL patients; single-center study of 111 patients demonstrated substantial bone mineral density (BMD) reduction [p.161] [131]. Chemotherapy and intermittent corticosteroids both contribute to BMD loss in lymphoma patients [p.161] [132]. Elderly NHL patients receiving chemotherapy are at higher risk for osteoporosis and fractures [p.161] [133]. Prospective pilot study documented alterations in BMD and bone turnover markers in newly diagnosed lymphoma patients receiving chemotherapy over 1-year follow-up [p.161-162] [134].

**Bone Protective Strategies:** Pamidronate administration reduced new vertebral fractures and improved BMD in malignant lymphoma patients receiving chemotherapy [p.162] [138]. Zoledronic acid in a prospective, randomized controlled phase III trial prevented bone loss in lymphoma patients receiving primary therapy [p.162] [139]. Denosumab was studied for fracture prevention in postmenopausal osteoporosis [p.162] [140].

**Clinical Guidelines:** Clinician's guide recommendations for prevention and treatment of osteoporosis address cancer treatment-related bone health [p.162] [135]. Special attention to bone care in high-dose steroid-treated lymphoma survivors is recommended [p.162] [136]. Cancer patient bone fragility prevention and treatment strategies are outlined in specialized guidance [p.162] [137].

### High-Dose Methotrexate Management

**Pharmacokinetic Monitoring:** MTXPK.org clinical decision support tool evaluates high-dose methotrexate pharmacokinetics to inform post-infusion care and glucarpidase use [p.161] [123].

### Rituximab Intolerance

**Waldenstrom Macroglobulinemia:** Rituximab intolerance in patients with Waldenstrom macroglobulinemia has been documented [p.161] [124].
