## Summary of Key Evidence

- Post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorders (PTLD) are a heterogeneous group of lymphomas occurring after solid organ transplant (SOT) or allogeneic hematopoietic cell transplant (HCT) [p.340], related to immunosuppression and often Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) [p.340]. PTLD following SOT are typically of recipient origin, whereas PTLD following allogeneic HCT are usually of donor origin [p.340].

- Incidence varies significantly by transplanted organ: kidney transplants 0.8%-2.5%, pancreatic 0.5%-5%, liver 1%-5.5%, heart 2.0%-8%, lung 3%-10%, and multiorgan/intestinal transplants ≤20% [p.340]. Following allogeneic HCT, incidence varies with HLA matching and T-cell depletion, with highest rates following haploidentical transplants with selective T-cell depletion (>20%) [p.340].

- Established risk factors for developing PTLD include EBV serology mismatch (recipient EBV-negative, donor EBV-positive), type of transplanted organ, intensity of induction immunosuppression, and type of immunosuppression [p.340]. Use of anti-thymocyte globulin (ATG) or anti-CD3 monoclonal antibody (mAb), T-cell depletion, and unrelated or HLA-mismatched allografts are associated with increased risks [p.340-341].

- Monomorphic PTLD appears to be the most common subtype, with diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL) being the most frequent [p.341]. EBV-negative PTLD are more likely to be of germinal center B-cell (GCB) type and more likely associated with late-onset PTLD (>1 year after transplant) [p.340].

- Reduction in immunosuppression (RI) remains the initial step for nearly all patients, leading to regression in 20%-80% of patients with polyclonal and monoclonal PTLD [p.343]. Rituximab monotherapy as second-line therapy results in overall response rates (ORR) of 44% with 28% complete response (CR) in retrospective/prospective phase II studies [p.344], with 5-year disease-specific survival of 94% in patients achieving CR [p.344].

- Sequential chemoimmunotherapy (rituximab followed by CHOP) in the prospective PTLD-1 trial demonstrated ORR of 90% (68% CR) in patients receiving both agents versus 60% (20% CR) with rituximab alone [p.344-345], with 5-year PFS and OS of 50% and 55% respectively [p.345].

- Risk-stratified treatment approach (PTLD-1 trial) in 126 patients achieved ORR of 88% (70% CR) with estimated 3-year OS of 70% [p.345], where low-risk patients (CR to initial rituximab) received consolidation monotherapy while high-risk patients received R-CHOP-21 [p.345].

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Reduction in Immunosuppression

Reduction in immunosuppression (RI) remains the initial step in comprehensive care of nearly all patients with PTLD [p.346-347]. RI leads to regression of PTLD in 20% to 80% of patients with polyclonal and monoclonal PTLD [p.343]. EBV-negative disease is less responsive to RI, but responses have been reported [p.343].

Initial management strategies include reduction of calcineurin inhibition (cyclosporin or tacrolimus) by 50% and discontinuation of antimetabolic agents (azathioprine or mycophenolate mofetil) [p.346-347]. Discontinuation of all non-steroidal immunosuppression should be considered in patients who are critically ill with extensive and life-threatening disease [p.346-347]. Response rates of 20%-80% occur in polyclonal or monoclonal subtypes, with patients requiring close monitoring [p.346-347]. Graft monitoring is essential to allow early detection of allograft rejection [p.346-347].

In a prospective phase II study of 16 eligible patients with PTLD following SOT using sequential approach (RI first, then interferon-alfa for less than CR, then multiagent chemotherapy if less than CR to interferon), RI resulted in one partial response (PR) [p.343].

### Rituximab Monotherapy

Rituximab monotherapy efficacy in B-cell PTLD has been confirmed in phase II studies and retrospective analyses [p.344]. In a prospective multicenter phase II study of 43 eligible patients with PTLD after SOT, rituximab monotherapy resulted in ORR of 44% (28% CR) with 1-year overall survival (OS) of 67% [p.344].

A prospective multicenter phase II study of 38 patients with PTLD after SOT demonstrated that risk-adapted extended treatment with 4 additional doses of rituximab increased CR rate from 34% to 61% without increasing treatment-emergent adverse events (TEAEs) [p.344]. Among patients who could not achieve CR with rituximab monotherapy and subsequently received rituximab in combination with chemotherapy (R-CHOP or R-EPOCH; n = 8), six patients achieved CR (75%) [p.344]. At median follow-up of 13 years, disease-specific survival (DSS) at 5 years and 10 years were 94% and 88%, respectively, for patients who achieved CR [p.344].

A multicenter retrospective analysis of 80 patients with PTLD following SOT suggests that inclusion of rituximab as part of initial therapy significantly improved both progression-free survival (PFS) and OS [p.344]. All patients initially underwent RI, and 74% were treated with rituximab with or without chemotherapy [p.344]. The 3-year PFS and OS rates for all patients were 57% and 62%, respectively [p.344]. The 3-year PFS and OS rates were 70% and 73%, respectively, for patients who received rituximab-based therapy as part of initial treatment compared to 21% and 33%, respectively, for those who did not receive rituximab-based therapy [p.344].

### Chemotherapy with or without Rituximab

Anthracycline-based chemotherapy with or without rituximab has been effective in treatment of patients with PTLD [p.344]. In a retrospective analysis of 26 patients with PTLD after SOT with disease unresponsive to RI alone, CHOP (cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, vincristine, and prednisone) induced ORR of 65% (50% CR) [p.344]. With median follow-up of nearly 9 years, median OS was 14 months, with high treatment-related mortality rate of 31% [p.344].

Chemotherapy and RI, with or without rituximab, have been reported to induce durable CR with reduced risk of graft impairment when used as first-line treatment [p.344]. Efficacy of chemoimmunotherapy for PTLD not responding to RI was demonstrated in the PTLD-1 trials [p.344].

Polatuzumab vedotin, an anti-CD79b antibody drug conjugate, in combination with R-CHP (rituximab, cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, prednisone), a modified R-CHOP regimen, is included as an option for concurrent chemoimmunotherapy, based on results of POLARIX trial [p.344].

## Sequential Chemoimmunotherapy

The prospective multicenter phase II study (PTLD-1 sequential treatment [PTLD-1-ST]) demonstrated safety and efficacy of sequential chemoimmunotherapy (4 weekly doses of rituximab followed by 4 cycles of CHOP-21) with granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF) in patients with PTLD not responding to initial RI [p.344-345]. This trial included 74 patients with 70 evaluable, where large majority presented with monomorphic PTLD (primarily DLBCL), and 44% were EBV positive [p.345].

ORR with rituximab was 60% (20% CR), which improved to 90% (68% CR) in patients who received CHOP chemotherapy following rituximab [p.345]. The 5-year PFS and OS rates were 50% and 55%, respectively [p.345]. The most common grade 3 or 4 toxicities included leukopenia (68%) and infectious events (41%) [p.345]. Treatment-related mortality associated with CHOP was reported in 11% of patients [p.345].

Subsequent analysis of this trial showed that CR after 4 cycles of rituximab-based regimen and low-risk IPI (in patients with PR after 4 cycles of rituximab-based regimen) was associated with lower risk of disease progression [p.345].

### Risk-Stratified Sequential Treatment (PTLD-1)

A risk-stratified treatment strategy based upon initial response to rituximab was evaluated in a subsequent prospective, international, multicenter phase II trial (PTLD-1, risk-stratified sequential treatment) [p.345]. In this trial, 152 patients with PTLD after SOT unresponsive to RI received induction therapy with 4 weekly doses of rituximab [p.345].

Patients in the low-risk group (defined as those achieving CR after initial rituximab) received consolidation with rituximab monotherapy on days 50, 72, 94, and 116 [p.345]. Patients in the high-risk group (defined as non-CR after initial rituximab) received chemoimmunotherapy with R-CHOP-21 (4 cycles) combined with G-CSF [p.345]. Pneumocystis jirovecii prophylaxis was recommended [p.345].

Among the 126 patients enrolled in the risk-stratified protocol, ORR was 88% (70% CR) [p.345]. The estimated 3-year OS rate was 70%, which compared favorably to OS rate of 61% [p.345]. Treatment-related mortality was 8% and median OS was 7 years [p.345]. The estimated 3-year time to progression (TTP) was 89% for patients in the low-risk group treated with rituximab consolidation [p.345]. Response to rituximab remained a prognostic factor for OS despite risk stratification [p.345].

This risk-stratified sequential treatment strategy obviates the need for chemoimmunotherapy for patients with low-risk disease achieving CR to rituximab, while incorporating more effective chemoimmunotherapy regimen (R-CHOP) for patients with high-risk disease [p.345].

### Modified Risk-Stratified Strategy (PTLD-2)

The PTLD-2 trial evaluated a modified risk-stratified strategy with use of subcutaneous rituximab in 60 patients stratified into three risk groups: low-risk (n = 21), high-risk (n = 28), very-high-risk (n = 9) [p.345-346].

Low-risk group included patients achieving CR after initial rituximab as well as those achieving PR and IPI less than 3, and very-high-risk group included lung transplant recipients with disease progression after induction therapy with rituximab [p.346]. Patients in the three risk groups received consolidation with rituximab monotherapy, chemoimmunotherapy with R-CHOP-21 (4 cycles), and 6 cycles of alternating R-CHOP/RDHA-oxaliplatin, respectively [p.346].

After median follow-up of 3 years, ORR was 94% for entire study population [p.346]. The estimated 2-year TTP was 78% and 2-year OS rate was 68% [p.346]. The 2-year event-free survival (EFS) rate in the low-risk group was not significantly higher in historical comparison to EFS rate of patients who received consolidation with CHOP in PTLD-1-ST trial (66% vs. 52%; P = .432), suggesting rituximab monotherapy consolidation in expanded low-risk group is not superior to consolidation with CHOP chemotherapy [p.346].

In the very-high-risk group, estimated 2-year PFS rate was 11% and median OS was 7 months [p.346]. Given that TEAEs were also substantial in this group, results of this trial showed that intensification of treatment is not associated with survival benefit in very-high-risk group of patients [p.346].

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### Chemotherapy for Specific Histologic Subtypes

Other indications for chemotherapy include specific histologic subtypes, such as peripheral T-cell lymphoma, not otherwise specified (PTCL, NOS); Burkitt lymphoma (BL); classic Hodgkin lymphoma (CHL); and other uncommon lymphomas including primary CNS lymphomas [p.346]. These must be managed with treatment approaches associated with improved outcomes in specific histologic subtypes [p.346].

In a retrospective multiinstitutional analysis of 84 patients with primary CNS PTLD, 93% of patients received RI [p.346]. Additional first-line therapy included high-dose methotrexate (48%), high-dose cytarabine (33%), whole brain radiotherapy (WBRT; 24%), and/or rituximab (44%) [p.346]. ORR was 60% [p.346]. At median follow-up of 42 months, 3-year PFS and OS rates were 32% and 43%, respectively [p.346].

Another retrospective multicenter analysis of 14 patients with primary CNS PTLD showed that combination of RI, WBRT, and concurrent systemic rituximab was effective treatment, although more toxic [p.346].

### Relapse After Initial Diagnosis and Treatment

The comprehensive care of patients with relapsed PTLD after chemoimmunotherapy is complex [p.346]. Retransplantation is feasible in selected patients with PTLD following SOT [p.346]. Waiting for at least 1 year from control of PTLD to retransplantation is recommended to minimize risk of PTLD recurrence [p.346].

In a French cohort study of 52 patients with kidney transplants who underwent 55 retransplantations, median time elapsed from PTLD to retransplantation was 90 months and only one patient developed PTLD recurrence after retransplantation [p.346].

A retrospective analysis of 21 patients from the Lymphoma Working Party of the EBMT suggests that although autologous hematopoietic cell transplant (HCT) is a potential therapeutic approach for PTLD following SOT, it is associated with high non-relapse mortality (NRM), primarily due to infectious complications [p.346]. After median follow-up of 64 months following autologous HCT, 3-year PFS and OS rates were 62% and 61%, respectively [p.346]. There were four deaths related to autologous HCT and 1-year NRM was 24% [p.346].

### CAR T-Cell Therapy

Anti-CD19 chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy is an effective treatment approach for relapsed/refractory DLBCL [p.346]. However, there is only limited evidence supporting its use in patients with relapsed/refractory PTLD [p.346].

In a multicenter, retrospective analysis of 22 patients with relapsed/refractory PTLD following SOT, CAR T-cell therapy resulted in ORR of 64% (55% CR) [p.346]. The 2-year PFS and OS rates were 35% and 58%, respectively [p.346]. Allograft rejection after CAR T-cell infusion was reported in 14% of patients [p.346].

CAR T-cell therapy appears to be feasible in patients with relapsed/refractory PTLD but requires multidisciplinary team and close monitoring [p.346]. Additional data are needed to confirm safety and efficacy [p.346].

### EBV-Specific Cytotoxic T-Cell Therapy

Autologous or allogeneic EBV-specific cytotoxic T-cell therapy may be a promising strategy in patients with PTLD not responding to conventional treatments [p.346-347].

A prospective multicenter phase II study evaluated allogeneic EBV-specific cytotoxic T-cell therapy for treatment of 33 patients with PTLD not responding to conventional treatments [p.347]. The majority of patients (94%) had received SOT [p.347]. All patients had RI as part of initial therapy for PTLD, and some patients had received rituximab monotherapy, anti-viral therapy, or chemotherapy [p.347]. ORR and OS rate at 6 months were 52% (42% CR) and 79%, respectively [p.347].

In another study of 114 patients who underwent allogeneic HSCT, EBV-specific cytotoxic T-cell therapy prevented PTLD in 101 patients and induced durable CR in 85% of patients in subgroup with existing PTLD (n = 13) [p.347]. During long-term follow-up, functional EBV-specific cytotoxic T-lymphocytes persisted up to 9 years [p.347].

## Special Considerations

### Risk Factors for Developing PTLD

EBV serology mismatch (recipient EBV-negative and donor EBV-positive), type of transplanted organ (highest risks for multiorgan, bowel, lung, and heart/lung transplants), intensity of induction immunosuppression, and type of immunosuppression are established risk factors for developing PTLD [p.340]. The risk is higher among children compared with adults, because primary EBV infection in EBV-negative organ recipients is the most common driver in children [p.340].

Unrelated or HLA-mismatched allografts, use of ATG or anti-CD3 mAb for prevention or treatment of graft-versus-host disease (GVHD), and T-cell depletion of allograft are associated with increased risks for PTLD in patients undergoing allogeneic HCT [p.340]. Use of ATG or anti-CD3 mAb (OKT3), as well as calcineurin inhibition with tacrolimus as primary immunosuppressive therapy along with azathioprine and new agents (e.g., belatacept in EBV-negative transplant recipients) are associated with increased risks for developing PTLD following SOT [p.340-341].

However, use of ATG solely as rejection therapy (compared to use as induction therapy or induction therapy and rejection therapy) prior to PTLD development was an independent prognostic factor for superior overall survival after PTLD diagnosis following SOT [p.341]. In an analysis of 523 patients who underwent heart transplant, switching from calcineurin inhibitor-based immunosuppression to sirolimus-based immunosuppression was associated with decreased rates of malignancies following heart transplant [p.341].

### Risk Factors for Surviving PTLD

Older age, poor performance status (Eastern Cooperative Oncology performance score of ≥2), elevated lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), organ dysfunction, multiple involved lymph nodes, multiorgancern involvement, graft organ involvement, central nervous system (CNS) involvement, number of extranodal sites (one versus more than one), type of organ transplanted, hypoalbuminemia, International Prognostic Factor Index (IPI), and comorbidities have been identified as prognostic factors for poor survival in patients with PTLD following SOT [p.341].

In the PTLD-1 trial, high-risk IPI, lung transplants, and inadequate response to rituximab induction therapy were associated with worse prognosis [p.341].

### Classification

The International Consensus Classification (ICC) has retained the 2017 WHO classification of PTLD: nondestructive PTLD, monomorphic PTLD (B-cell, T-cell, and natural killer (NK) cell type), polymorphic PTLD, and classic Hodgkin lymphoma (CHL) PTLD [p.341].

Nondestructive PTLD consist of three histologic subtypes: plasmacytic hyperplasia PTLD, infectious mononucleosis PTLD, and florid follicular hyperplasia PTLD [p.341]. Nondestructive PTLD typically develop within year of transplantation and are EBV-positive in almost all instances [p.341].

Monomorphic PTLD appear to be most common subtype, and majority are of B-cell origin with DLBCL being most frequent [p.341]. Although uncommon, Burkitt lymphoma, plasma cell myeloma, or plasmacytoma have also been reported [p.341]. Monomorphic PTLD of T-cell or NK-cell origin (although very rare) tend to occur later (after median of 6 years following transplant) [p.341]. Peripheral T-cell lymphoma, not otherwise specified (PTCL, NOS) is most prevalent subtype followed by anaplastic large cell lymphoma (ALCL) and hepatosplenic T-cell lymphoma (HSTCL) [p.341].

Polymorphic PTLD are mostly EBV positive, can be either polyclonal or monoclonal, and are most common type of PTLD among children [p.341]. They are characterized by mixed lymphoproliferation consisting of immunoblasts, plasma cells, and intermediate-sized lymphoid cells [p.342]. Immunohistochemistry (IHC) will show variable mixture of B cells and T cells [p.342]. Subdividing polymorphic PTLD is not indicated in WHO classification since this does not reliably predict clinical behavior [p.342].

CHL PTLD is almost always EBV-positive and is least common of four PTLD categories [p.342].

### Clinical Presentation and Diagnosis

The clinical presentation of PTLD is heterogeneous and is characterized by high incidence of extranodal disease, which may involve gastrointestinal tract (20%-30%), solid allografts (10%-15%), and CNS (5%-20%) [p.342]. Diagnosis of PTLD can be challenging given nonspecific clinical presentation and heterogeneity in histopathologic and immunophenotypic presentations [p.342]. Histopathology and adequate immunophenotyping are essential to confirm diagnosis of PTLD [p.342].

The recommended essential IHC panel includes CD3, CD20, CD79a, PAX5, and kappa and lambda light chains [p.342]. Flow cytometry of biopsy specimen may be useful for clonality assessment (B-Cell and T-Cell) [p.342].

Up to 50% of PTLD that develop after SOT are not associated with EBV [p.342]. EBV infection status should be evaluated using EBV-encoded RNA in situ hybridization (EBER-ISH), the most sensitive method; hematopathology review of all slides (with at least one paraffin block representative of tumor) is recommended [p.342]. Rebiopsy of lymph node (preferably most FDG-avid, accessible lymph node) should be done if consult material is nondiagnostic [p.342].

Evaluation of EBV infection status by EBER-ISH is essential component of diagnostic workup [p.342]. IHC for EBV-LMP1 and EBV-EBNA2 is useful to determine EBV latency status, which could be helpful to differentiate PTLD from other EBV-positive B-cell lymphoproliferative disorders and lymphomas arising in treatment-related immunodeficiency and non-immunodeficiency settings [p.342].

IGHV gene mutations are seen in majority of B-cell PTLD, with exception of early lesions [p.342]. Genetic alterations in MYC, NRAS, and TP53 are seen only in monomorphic PTLD, and BCL6 mutations (present in 43% of polymorphic PTLD) have been associated with shorter survival and poor response to therapy [p.342]. In certain situations, molecular analysis to detect immunoglobulin (Ig) gene rearrangements and FISH to detect MYC, BCL2, or BCL6 rearrangements could be useful [p.342].

### Initial Workup

The initial work-up for PTLD should include physical examination and evaluation of performance status [p.342]. Laboratory assessments should include standard blood work including complete blood count (CBC) with differential and metabolic panel (to include albumin, electrolytes, blood urea nitrogen [BUN], and creatinine), in addition to measurements of serum LDH levels [p.342]. Bone marrow evaluations may be useful in selected circumstances [p.342].

Chest/abdomen/pelvis CT with contrast of diagnostic quality and/or whole-body PET/CT scan are recommended as part of initial diagnostic workup [p.343]. Brain MRI may be useful in selected circumstances, especially when CNS involvement is suspected [p.343]. In addition, multigated acquisition (MUGA) scan or echocardiogram is recommended for patients being considered for treatment regimens containing anthracyclines or anthracenediones [p.343].

Hepatitis B virus (HBV) testing should be performed prior to initiation of treatment with immunotherapy (with or without chemotherapy) given potential risks for viral reactivation with such regimens [p.343].

SOT and HCT recipients who develop PTLD may have higher EBV viral load than transplant recipients without PTLD [p.343]. Evaluation of EBV viral load by quantitative polymerase chain reaction (PCR) amplification of EBV DNA can aid in diagnosis as well as monitoring of treatment responses in patients with PTLD [p.343]. The reported positive and negative predictive values for this approach have varied significantly [p.343]. Plasma or peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMC) are useful for measuring EBV viral load; however, some studies have shown viral load in plasma is more sensitive than PBMC, especially in patients with EBV-positive disease [p.343]. In recent studies, cell-free plasma EBV DNA was better marker than EBV DNA from PBMC [p.343].

CMV infection has been associated with risks for EBV-positive PTLD [p.343]. Thus, EBV PCR for measurement of cell-free plasma EBV DNA marker and CMV PCR can be useful for selected patients [p.343].

### Treatment Options Overview

Treatment approaches are largely dependent on PTLD subtype [p.343]. Published reports have included reduction in immunosuppression (RI), antiviral therapy, rituximab monotherapy, chemotherapy, and/or chemoimmunotherapy regimens [p.343]. The optimal treatment for PTLD is not well defined due to lack of randomized controlled trials and heterogeneity of disease [p.343].

In addition, survival outcomes are significantly inferior for patients with DLBCL-type PTLD who achieve event-free survival at 24 months (EFS24; defined as no progression/treatment or death related to any cause within 24 months of diagnosis) compared to survival outcomes of patients with de novo DLBCL who achieve EFS24 because of increased mortality secondary to comorbidities [p.343].

The role of antiviral therapy is controversial since majority of PTLD are associated with latent EBV [p.343]. Replicating EBV DNA has been reported in about 40% of EBV associated lymphoproliferative disorders in patients who are immunocompromised [p.343]. Antiviral drugs targeting EBV replication may be beneficial in subset of patients with early or polymorphic PTLD [p.343].

### Graft Monitoring and Management Coordination

Graft monitoring is essential to allow for early detection of allograft rejection [p.346-347]. Importantly, RI should be initiated and managed in coordination with transplant team in order to minimize risks for graft rejection [p.346-347]. Graft organ function and EBV viral load should be monitored [p.347].

In contrast to staging of lymphoma in immunocompetent patients, restaging should be performed at 2 to 4 weeks in patients receiving RI as only treatment option, since responses occur very early [p.346-347].

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Rituximab Monotherapy

Multiple phase II trials demonstrate rituximab efficacy as first-line therapy for B-cell PTLD:

- **Blaes et al. (2005)** - Phase II trial of rituximab in PTLD after solid organ transplantation with documented efficacy [p.355] [90]
- **Choquet et al. (2006)** - Prospective multicenter phase 2 study confirming safety and efficacy of rituximab in B-cell PTLD [p.355] [91]
- **Elstrom et al. (2006)** - Comparative analysis of rituximab versus chemotherapy treatment approaches [p.355] [92]
- **Jain et al. (2005)** - Long-term single-center experience with rituximab (chimeric anti-CD20 antibody) after solid organ transplantation [p.355] [93]
- **Milpied et al. (2000)** - Retrospective analysis of humanized anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody (Rituximab) in 32 post-transplant patients [p.355] [94]
- **Oertel et al. (2005)** - Effect of anti-CD20 antibody rituximab in PTLD [p.355] [95]
- **Gonzalez-Barca et al. (2007, 2021)** - Prospective phase II trial of extended rituximab treatment with long-term follow-up validation in real-world PTLD patients [p.355] [96-97]

### Reduction of Immunosuppression

Reduction of immunosuppression serves as initial therapy for posttransplant lymphoproliferative disorder:

- **Tsai et al. (2001)** - Analysis of prognostic variables and long-term follow-up of 42 adult patients receiving reduction in immunosuppression [p.354] [86]
- **Parker et al. (2010)** - BCSH and BTS management guidelines for PTLD in adult solid organ transplant recipients [p.354] [87]
- **Reshef et al. (2011)** - Reduction of immunosuppression as initial therapy in posttransplantation lymphoproliferative disorder [p.355] [88]

### Sequential Rituximab-Based Chemotherapy

- **Swinnen et al. (2008)** - Prospective study of sequential reduction in immunosuppression, interferon alpha-2B, and chemotherapy for PTLD [p.355] [89]
- **Trappe et al. (2012)** - PTLD-1 trial showing sequential rituximab followed by CHOP chemotherapy in adult B-cell PTLD with prospective international multicenter phase 2 design [p.356] [105]
- **Trappe et al. (2017)** - International prospective multicenter phase II trial demonstrating that response to rituximab induction is predictive and allows successful stratification into rituximab or R-CHOP consolidation [p.356] [106]
