## Summary of Key Evidence

- Non-Hodgkin lymphomas (NHL), Kaposi sarcoma, and lung cancer are the most common cancers in people with HIV (PWH), with expected significant decline in NHL incidence through 2030 [p.325]
- Diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL), Burkitt lymphoma (BL), and primary central nervous system lymphoma (PCNSL) are the most common B-cell lymphoma subtypes in PWH [p.325]
- Plasmablastic lymphoma (PBL) and primary effusion lymphoma (PEL) historically accounted for less than 5% of HIV-associated lymphomas, though PBL incidence may be increasing relative to DLBCL [p.325]
- Antiretroviral therapy (ART) has dramatically improved outcomes, with 2-year overall survival (OS) rates of 75% for DLBCL treated with R-CHOP in the modern ART era [p.326]
- Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) is present in at least 90% of PCNSL, approximately 50% of BL, and 40-50% of DLBCL [p.325]
- Rituximab combined with chemotherapy significantly improves response rates and survival compared to chemotherapy alone [p.328-330]

## Prognosis

### Early ART Era Outcomes

In the Collaboration of Observational HIV Epidemiological Research Europe (COHERE) study evaluating outcomes from 1998–2006, the 1-year overall survival (OS) rates were 66% for systemic lymphoma and 54% for PCNSL [p.326]. In a large cohort study, 5-year survival rates were 50% for DLBCL, 44% for BL, 23% for PCNSL, and 43% for other NHL subtypes, with older age, lower CD4 count at diagnosis, higher HIV RNA, and histologic category identified as independent risk factors for mortality [p.326].

### Modern ART Era Outcomes

In analysis of DLBCL in the modern ART era, 2-year OS and progression-free survival (PFS) were both 75% after treatment with R-CHOP, with PFS not differing from HIV-negative counterparts [p.326].

### Burkitt Lymphoma

In an international analysis of 249 HIV-associated BL patients treated from 2008 to 2019 (median follow-up 4.5 years), 3-year PFS and OS rates were 61% and 66%, respectively [p.326]. Baseline central nervous system (CNS) involvement was associated with shorter PFS (3-year PFS: 36% vs. 69% without CNS involvement; P < .001) [p.326]. Treatment with CODOX-M/IVAC was associated with longer PFS and OS compared to EPOCH or hyperCVAD after adjusting for prognostic factors [p.326].

### Primary Central Nervous System Lymphoma

PCNSL in PWH may have better prognosis and is curable with less intensive chemotherapy when combined with immune reconstitution [p.326]. Immune reconstitution with ART administered along with high-dose methotrexate or radiation therapy (RT) is associated with improved survival outcomes, even among patients with history of opportunistic infections and medical non-adherence [p.326].

### Plasmablastic Lymphoma

In a case series of 12 newly diagnosed PBL patients treated in the ART era at AIDS Malignancy Consortium (AMC) sites, 1-year survival rate was 67% with no reported deaths after 1 year at median follow-up of 73 weeks [p.326]. However, outcomes remain variable. In the German HIV-related-Lymphoma-Cohort-Study, 2-year OS rate for PBL was 43% compared to 69% for BL and 63% for DLBCL [p.327]. Age (<50 years) and early stage disease (stage I/II) were identified as prognostic factors for favorable outcomes, with EBV-positive status associated with better event-free survival compared to EBV-negative status [p.326].

### Primary Effusion Lymphoma

PEL is characterized by neoplastic effusions without detectable tumor masses and is associated with Kaposi sarcoma-associated herpesvirus (KSHV/HHV8), and may be co-infected with EBV [p.327]. PEL has poor survival compared to HIV-associated DLBCL or BL. In analysis of 10,769 HIV-associated lymphoma patients from the National Cancer Data Base, estimated 5-year OS rates were 42% for DLBCL, 45% for BL, 22% for PCNSL, and 28% for PEL [p.327]. Poor performance status, advanced stage disease, and absence of ART prior to PEL diagnosis were identified as prognostic factors for shorter survival [p.327].

## Diagnosis

Adequate immunophenotyping using immunohistochemistry (IHC) with or without flow cytometry analysis is essential to establish HIV-associated lymphoma subtype [p.327].

EBV-encoded RNA in situ hybridization (EBER-ISH) is recommended for all patients since EBV is the most commonly found oncogenic virus in HIV-associated lymphomas [p.327]. HHV8/KHSV testing is useful to confirm diagnosis of PEL [p.327].

High-grade B-cell lymphomas (HGBL) with MYC and BCL2 and/or BCL6 rearrangements and BL with 11q aberrations have been reported in PWH in frequencies similar to HIV-negative counterparts [p.327]. MUM1 expression in BL and dual expression of MYC and BCL2 coexpression in DLBCL were of prognostic significance [p.327].

Molecular analysis to detect immunoglobulin gene rearrangements and fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) to detect BCL2, BCL6, or MYC rearrangements will be useful under selected circumstances including consideration of participation in clinical trials [p.327].

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### EPOCH (Etoposide, Prednisone, Vincristine, Cyclophosphamide, Doxorubicin) + Rituximab

**AMC 034 Trial**: Patients with HIV-associated aggressive B-cell lymphomas (106 patients; 75% DLBCL; 25% BL/BL-like) were randomized to receive infusional EPOCH either concurrently with rituximab or followed sequentially [p.328]. CR rate was 73% with concurrent rituximab versus 55% with sequential rituximab [p.328]. 2-year PFS rates were 66% (concurrent) vs. 63% (sequential); 2-year OS rates were 70% vs. 67%, respectively [p.328]. Treatment-related deaths occurred in 5 patients (10%) in concurrent rituximab arm and 4 patients (7%) in sequential arm, with higher incidence of deaths among patients with baseline CD4+ count <50/mcL [p.328]. ART was given concurrently with EPOCH or delayed until chemotherapy completion per investigator choice [p.328].

**Dose-Adjusted EPOCH (DA-EPOCH)**: Overall response rate (ORR) was 87% with CR rate of 74% in untreated HIV-associated B-cell lymphomas (n=39; 79% DLBCL; 18% BL) [p.328]. At median follow-up of 53 months, PFS and OS rates were 73% and 60%, respectively [p.328]. Disease-free survival (DFS) rate was 92% with only 2 of 39 CR patients experiencing disease recurrence [p.328]. OS was decreased in patients with low baseline CD4 counts (≤100/mcL): 16% vs. 87% for higher CD4 counts [p.328]. In multivariate analysis, low CD4 counts and CNS involvement were the only significant factors associated with decreased OS [p.328].

**Short Course EPOCH with Dose-Dense Rituximab (SC-EPOCH-RR)**: CR rate of 91% in HIV-associated DLBCL (n=33); at median follow-up of 5 years, PFS and OS rates were 84% and 68%, respectively [p.329]. Addition of rituximab did not cause serious infection-related complications or deaths [p.329]. In a subsequent prospective study of 11 patients with low-risk HIV-associated BL, freedom from progression and OS rates were 100% and 90%, respectively at median follow-up of 86 months [p.329]. SC-EPOCH-RR was associated with lower incidences of fever and neutropenia than DA-EPOCH-R [p.329].

### EPOCH + Vorinostat

In a phase I study, safety and preliminary efficacy of vorinostat combined with EPOCH-R was established in high-risk HIV-associated B-cell lymphomas [p.329]. In subsequent phase II randomized trial, EPOCH demonstrated efficacy but addition of vorinostat showed no benefit [p.329]. CR rates were 74% (EPOCH) versus 68% (EPOCH + vorinostat); P = .72 [p.329]. MYC-positive DLBCL, low CD4 counts, and short diagnosis-to-treatment interval were associated with less favorable outcomes [p.329].

### EPOCH + Bortezomib for Plasmablastic Lymphoma

Retrospective study showed addition of bortezomib to EPOCH is safe and effective as front-line treatment for HIV-associated PBL [p.329].

### CHOP ± Rituximab

**CHOP Alone**: CHOP regimen induced CR rates of 30-48% with median OS of approximately 25 months [p.329].

**RCHOP (AMC 010 Trial)**: Randomized phase III trial of 150 HIV-associated B-cell lymphomas (80% DLBCL; 9% BL) [p.329]. Addition of rituximab to CHOP (RCHOP) was associated with improved CR rates: 58% vs. 47% with CHOP alone [p.329]. Longer median time to progression: 29 vs. 20 months (RCHOP vs. CHOP) [p.329]. Longer median OS: 32 vs. 25 months (RCHOP vs. CHOP) [p.329]. Median PFS was similar between groups: 10 vs. 9 months [p.329]. Maintenance rituximab following initial RCHOP was given to 35 patients but is not recommended due to lack of survival benefit shown in non-HIV trials and higher infection-related deaths during maintenance phase [p.329].

**RCHOP Phase II Trials**: 6 cycles of RCHOP resulted in CR/complete response unconfirmed (Cru) rates of 69-77% with 2-year and 3-year OS rates of 75% and 56%, respectively, in HIV-associated B-cell lymphomas (majority DLBCL) with manageable toxicities [p.329].

### CDOP (Liposomal or Pegylated Liposomal Doxorubicin) + Rituximab

**AMC 047 Trial**: 40 HIV-associated B-cell lymphomas (98% DLBCL) treated with infusional RCDOP (pegylated liposomal doxorubicin) [p.329]. ORR was 68% (CR 48%) [p.329]. 1-year PFS and OS rates were 61% and 70%, respectively [p.329]. 2-year PFS and OS were 52% and 62%, respectively [p.329]. Infectious complications in 40% of patients (grade 4 in 5%) but no infection-related deaths [p.329]. Results appeared less favorable compared to EPOCH regimen [p.330].

### CODOX-M/IVAC ± Rituximab for Burkitt Lymphoma

**Retrospective Study**: 80 BL patients comparing HIV and non-HIV populations [p.330]. CR rates were 93% (with rituximab) and 88% (without rituximab) [p.330]. 3-year PFS was 68% for both subgroups [p.330]. 3-year OS rates were 68% (with) and 72% (without rituximab) [p.330]. Trend toward improved 3-year PFS (74% vs. 61%) and OS (77% vs. 66%) with addition of rituximab [p.330].

**AMC 048 Trial**: Modified CODOX-M/IVAC with rituximab in 34 HIV-associated BL patients (2 low-risk; 32 high-risk) [p.330]. Low-risk patients treated with 3 cycles R-CODOX-M; high-risk patients with R-CODOX-M/IVAC [p.330]. Median follow-up 26 months [p.330]. 1-year PFS and OS rates were 69% and 72%, respectively [p.330]. 2-year OS rate was 69% [p.330].

### HyperCVAD ± Rituximab for Burkitt Lymphoma

HyperCVAD with or without rituximab demonstrated efficacy in BL treatment, with incorporation of rituximab associated with improved disease-related outcomes particularly for older patients [p.330]. HyperCVAD combined with ART in HIV-associated BL/leukemia and Burkitt-like lymphoma resulted in CR rates of 64-92% and median OS of 12 months [p.330].

## Special Considerations

### Workup and CD4 Counts

Baseline values for CD4 counts and HIV viral load should be obtained [p.327]. HIV-associated lymphomas are more likely to occur in PWH with advanced immunosuppression (lower CD4+ T cell counts), with exception of BL [p.327]. Among systemic HIV-associated lymphomas, BL is generally associated with higher CD4+ cell count at diagnosis compared with DLBCL [p.327]. PCNSL, PBL, and PEL are particularly associated with low CD4+ count levels [p.327]. In cases of advanced immunosuppression (CD4+ counts <200), additional work-up or prophylaxis is warranted due to increased risk of concurrent opportunistic infections [p.327].

### NCCN Recommendations for Initial Treatment

The NCCN Guidelines recommend use of ART and growth factor support along with full-dose chemotherapy with or without rituximab [p.330].

**Rituximab Considerations**: Patients on ART with persistently low CD4+ count of <50-100/mcL tend to have poorer prognosis, higher risk of infection, and cytopenias [p.330]. Omission of rituximab is strongly suggested for these patients due to higher risk of serious infectious complications [p.330]. Maximizing supportive care and close monitoring for cytopenias and infections is recommended [p.330].

**ART Drug Interactions**: ART can be administered safely with chemotherapy; however, certain antiviral drugs can interfere with cancer therapy metabolism [p.330]. Protease inhibitors cause CYP3A4 inhibition; non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors cause CYP3A4 induction [p.330]. Effective alternatives should be considered to minimize toxicities and drug-drug interactions [p.330]. Avoidance of zidovudine, cobicistat, and ritonavir is strongly recommended [p.330]. Any change in antiviral therapy should be made in consultation with an infectious disease specialist [p.330].

### DLBCL Regimens (Category 1)

EPOCH or CHOP in combination with rituximab is recommended for HIV-associated DLBCL [p.330]. **EPOCH-R is included as the preferred regimen** [p.330].

CNS prophylaxis with intrathecal methotrexate or intrathecal cytarabine is indicated for HIV-associated DLBCL with selected high-risk features including: double-hit lymphoma, involvement of 2 or more extranodal sites with elevated lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), bone marrow involvement, or other high-risk site involvement such as epidural, testicular, or paranasal sinuses [p.330].

Patients with HHV8-positive DLBCL, not otherwise specified (NOS), and PEL can be treated with same regimens as HIV-associated DLBCL [p.331]. Since majority of PEL are CD20-negative, addition of rituximab to chemotherapy is not indicated [p.331].

### Burkitt Lymphoma Regimens (Category 1)

CODOX-M/IVAC + rituximab, DA-EPOCH-R and hyperCVAD with rituximab are recommended [p.331]. **CODOX-M/IVAC + rituximab and DA-EPOCH-R are included as preferred regimens** [p.331].

### Plasmablastic Lymphoma Regimens

EPOCH (preferred), CODOX-M/IVAC, or hyper-CVAD are recommended for PBL [p.331]. Only limited data are available on management of PBL [p.331]. CD4 count of >100/µL at diagnosis, chemosensitive disease, and CR to first-line chemotherapy were associated with better outcomes [p.331].

Consolidation with high-dose therapy followed by autologous stem cell rescue (HDT/ASCR) can be considered following CR after initial therapy for patients with high-risk features including: age-adjusted IPI >2, presence of MYC gene rearrangement, or TP53 deletion [p.331].

### Primary Central Nervous System Lymphoma Regimens

Immune reconstitution with ART along with high-dose methotrexate should be considered [p.331]. RT should be reserved for patients unable to tolerate systemic therapy or those with refractory disease [p.331]. Selected patients with good performance status receiving ART may also be treated per NCCN Guidelines for Central Nervous System (CNS) Cancers [p.331].

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### Relapsed/Refractory Disease - Risk Factors

In multicenter study evaluating risk factors and incidence of relapse in large cohort with HIV-associated lymphomas (after achieving CR), unclassifiable histology, advanced stage disease, no concomitant ART during chemotherapy, and use of R-CHOP-based regimens were independently associated with higher risk of relapse in BL [p.331].

### Salvage Chemotherapy

**Bortezomib + ICE + Rituximab**: Bortezomib in combination with ifosfamide, carboplatin, etoposide (ICE) + rituximab is effective second-line therapy for relapsed/refractory HIV-associated lymphomas [p.331].

**Brentuximab Vedotin**: Case reports indicate activity in patients with PEL and plasmablastic lymphoma, which often express CD30 [p.331].

**Retrospective Analysis of Second-Line Regimens**: Analysis of 88 patients with relapsed/refractory HIV-associated lymphomas treated at 13 AMC sites [p.331]. Most commonly used second-line regimens: ICE (39%), DA-EPOCH (19%), ESHAP (13%) [p.331]. ORR was 31%; 1-year OS rate was 37% for entire population [p.331]. Baseline CD4 counts did not influence OS outcomes [p.331]. Subsequent treatment with autologous hematopoietic cell transplant (HCT) was associated with improved 1-year OS: 63% with HCT vs. 37% without HCT [p.331]. For patients experiencing response (CR or PR) after second-line therapy, no difference in 1-year OS based on HCT (88% with HCT vs. 82% without) [p.331]. Response rates and survival better for non-BL histology than BL histology [p.331]. ORR and 1-year OS for non-BL: 33% and 42%, respectively; for BL: 17% and 12%, respectively [p.331]. Patients with primary refractory disease (n=54) had significantly decreased ORR (24% vs. 56%; P = .003) and decreased 1-year OS (31% vs. 59%; P = .022) compared with relapsed disease [p.331].

### Autologous Hematopoietic Cell Transplantation

**Phase II Study**: 43 chemosensitive, relapsed/refractory HIV-associated lymphoma patients [p.332]. At median follow-up of 25 months, autologous HCT associated with estimated 1-year and 2-year OS rates of 87% and 82%, respectively [p.332]. 2-year PFS rate was 80%; 1-year transplant-related mortality was 5% [p.332]. Matched population control from Center for International Blood & Marrow Transplant Research (CIBMTR) revealed outcomes did not differ statistically from HIV-negative patients [p.332].

**Retrospective Study**: 118 HIV-associated lymphoma patients (47% DLBCL, 24% HL, 18% BL, 7% PEL) following autologous HCT in rituximab and ART era [p.332]. At median follow-up of 4 years, 3-year non-relapse mortality was 10%, incidence of relapse 27%, PFS 63%, and OS 66% [p.332]. In multivariate analysis, disease status less than PR at transplant was significant predictor of unfavorable PFS and OS [p.332].

### CAR T-Cell Therapy

**FDA-Approved Agents**: Axicabtagene ciloleucel, tisagenlecleucel, and lisocabtagene maraleucel are three anti-CD19 CAR T-cell therapies FDA-approved for relapsed/refractory DLBCL after two or more prior systemic therapy regimens [p.332]. Axicabtagene ciloleucel and lisocabtagene maraleucel also approved for primary refractory disease or relapsed disease within 12 months after first-line therapy [p.332].

**PWH Exclusion from Clinical Trials**: PWH have been excluded from all trials leading to FDA approval of CAR T-cell therapies [p.332].

**Registry Study (AMC-113/CIBMTR)**: Prospectively collected registry study (n=30; 19 DLBCL; 24% previously received HDT/ASCR) [p.332]. After median follow-up of 6 months, OS rate at 3 and 6 months was 83% and 64%, respectively [p.332]. Cytokine-release syndrome (CRS) reported in 9 patients (69%); immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS) in 3 patients (23%) [p.332].

**Ongoing Prospective Study**: AMC-112 (NCT05077527) evaluating safety and efficacy of CAR T-cell therapy with axicabtagene ciloleucel for relapsed/refractory HIV-associated aggressive B-Cell Lymphomas [p.332].

**NCCN Recommendation**: HDT/ASCR should be considered for chemosensitive relapsed/refractory HIV-associated lymphomas in transplant-eligible patients [p.332]. Patients with relapsed/refractory HIV-associated DLBCL receiving ART are suitable candidates for CAR T-cell therapies with appropriate supportive care interventions for HIV control [p.332].

## Summary of Key Evidence

- **First-line chemotherapy regimens** for HIV-associated non-Hodgkin lymphoma include rituximab plus concurrent infusional EPOCH chemotherapy [47, p.336], CHOP chemotherapy, and liposomal doxorubicin-based regimens, all safe when combined with highly active antiretroviral therapy (HAART) [56, p.336-337]

- **Dose-adjusted EPOCH** demonstrates highly effective treatment outcomes in AIDS-related lymphoma with impact from antiretroviral therapy suspension and tumor biology assessment [49, p.336]

- **Burkitt lymphoma treatment** requires intensive chemotherapy; CODOX-M/IVAC regimen with or without rituximab shows good efficacy and tolerance in the HAART era [63, 64, 65, p.337], with modified CODOX-M/IVAC-rituximab confirmed as safe and effective [65, p.337]

- **Plasmablastic lymphoma** management includes bortezomib plus EPOCH as effective frontline treatment [53, p.336], with emerging evidence for brentuximab vedotin in relapsed disease [76, p.338]

- **Second-line and salvage therapy** options include autologous hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (ASCT) for selected patients, with long-term survival data from multiple trials [80, 81, 83, p.338], and CAR T-cell therapy (axicabtagene ciloleucel, lisocabtagene maraleucel) in relapsed/refractory disease [92, 93, p.339]

- **CAR T-cell therapy** shows curative potential in refractory large B-cell lymphoma with 5-year follow-up data from ZUMA-1 [86, p.338], and long-term clinical outcomes from JULIET study [87, p.338]

- **Antiretroviral therapy management** is critical; concurrent HAART improves survival outcomes and must be carefully coordinated with chemotherapy regimens [56, 70, 71, p.337]

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Rituximab-EPOCH for Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma

**Trial Evidence:**
- **Study 47 (Sparano et al., 2010):** Rituximab plus concurrent infusional EPOCH chemotherapy demonstrated high efficacy in HIV-associated B-cell non-Hodgkin lymphoma [47, p.336]
- **Study 48 (Barta et al., 2012):** Pooled analysis of AIDS Malignancy Consortium trials evaluating rituximab plus CHOP or infusional EPOCH chemotherapy in HIV-associated non-Hodgkin lymphoma [48, p.336-337]
- **Study 50 (Dunleavy et al., 2010):** Short-course EPOCH with dose-dense rituximab (SC-EPOCH-RR) role in HIV-associated diffuse large B-cell lymphoma, with FDG-PET and tumor histogenesis assessment [50, p.336]

### Dose-Adjusted EPOCH

**Trial Evidence:**
- **Study 49 (Little et al., 2003):** Highly effective treatment of AIDS-related lymphoma with dose-adjusted EPOCH; impact of antiretroviral therapy suspension and tumor biology [49, p.336]

### CHOP and Rituximab Combinations

**Trial Evidence:**
- **Study 55 (Kaplan et al., 2005):** Randomized phase 3 trial (AIDS-Malignancies Consortium Trial 010) of CHOP with or without rituximab; rituximab did not improve clinical outcome in HIV-associated non-Hodgkin lymphoma [55, p.336]
- **Study 56 (Weiss et al., 2006):** Simultaneous treatment with combined cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, vincristine, and prednisone (CHOP) chemotherapy and HAART is safe and improves survival in AIDS-related lymphoma [56, p.336-337]
- **Study 57 (Boue et al., 2006):** Phase II trial of CHOP plus rituximab in patients with HIV-associated non-Hodgkin's lymphoma [57, p.337]
- **Study 58 (Ribera et al., 2008):** Safety and efficacy of cyclophosphamide, adriamycin, vincristine, prednisone and rituximab (CHOP-R) in HIV-associated diffuse large B-cell lymphoma; results of a phase II trial [58, p.337]

### Liposomal Doxorubicin-Based Regimens

**Trial Evidence:**
- **Study 59 (Levine et al., 2004):** Liposome-encapsulated doxorubicin in combination with standard agents (cyclophosphamide, vincristine, prednisone) in newly diagnosed AIDS-related non-Hodgkin's lymphoma; therapy results and response correlates [59, p.337]
- **Study 60 (Levine et al., 2013):** Pegylated liposomal doxorubicin, rituximab, cyclophosphamide, vincristine, and prednisone in AIDS-related lymphoma (AIDS Malignancy Consortium Study 047) [60, p.337]

### Burkitt Lymphoma First-Line Therapy

**Trial Evidence:**
- **Study 61 (Wang et al., 2003):** Intensive chemotherapy with cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, high-dose methotrexate/ifosfamide, etoposide, and high-dose cytarabine (CODOX-M/IVAC) for HIV-associated Burkitt lymphoma [61, p.337]
- **Study 62 (Barnes et al., 2011):** Evaluation of rituximab addition to CODOX-M/IVAC for Burkitt's lymphoma; retrospective analysis [62, p.337]
- **Study 63 (Rodrigo et al., 2012):** HIV-Associated Burkitt Lymphoma showing good efficacy and tolerance of intensive chemotherapy including CODOX-M/IVAC with or without rituximab in the HAART era [63, p.337]
- **Study 64 (Alwan et al., 2015):** Adding rituximab to CODOX-M/IVAC chemotherapy in HIV-associated Burkitt lymphoma is safe when used with concurrent combination antiretroviral therapy [64, p.337]
- **Study 65 (Noy et al., 2015):** AMC 048 trial: modified CODOX-M/IVAC-rituximab is safe and effective for HIV-associated Burkitt lymphoma [65, p.337]
- **Study 66 (Thomas et al., 2006):** Chemoimmunotherapy with hyper-CVAD plus rituximab for treatment of adult Burkitt and Burkitt-type lymphoma or acute lymphoblastic leukemia [66, p.337]
- **Study 67 (Samra et al., 2021):** Long-term outcome of hyper-CVAD-R for Burkitt leukemia/lymphoma and high-grade B-cell lymphoma with focus on CNS relapse [67, p.337]
- **Study 68 (Cortes et al., 2002):** Hyperfractionated cyclophosphamide, vincristine, doxorubicin, and dexamethasone with HAART for AIDS-related Burkitt lymphoma/leukemia [68, p.337]

### Other First-Line Regimens

**Trial Evidence:**
- **Study 69 (Spina et al., 2005):** Rituximab plus infusional cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, and etoposide in HIV-associated non-Hodgkin lymphoma; pooled results from 3 phase 2 trials [69, p.337]

### Plasmablastic Lymphoma First-Line Therapy

**Trial Evidence:**
- **Study 53 (Castillo et al., 2019):** Bortezomib plus EPOCH is effective as frontline treatment in patients with plasmablastic lymphoma [53, p.336]

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### Novel Chemotherapy Approaches

**Trial Evidence:**
- **Study 52 (Ramos et al., 2020):** Impact of Myc in HIV-associated non-Hodgkin lymphomas treated with EPOCH and outcomes with vorinostat (AMC-075 trial) [52, p.336]

### Brentuximab Vedotin

**Trial Evidence:**
- **Study 76 (Chang et al., 2019):** Activity of brentuximab vedotin in AIDS-related primary effusion lymphoma [76, p.338]
- **Study 77 (Holderness et al., 2013):** Brentuximab vedotin demonstrates activity in a patient with plasmablastic lymphoma arising from background chronic lymphocytic leukemia [77, p.338]

### Bortezomib-Based Approaches

**Trial Evidence:**
- **Study 75 (Reid et al., 2018):** Safety and efficacy of oncolytic viral strategy using bortezomib with ICE/R in relapsed/refractory HIV-positive lymphomas [75, p.338]

### Autologous Hematopoietic Stem Cell Transplantation (ASCT)

**Trial Evidence:**
- **Study 79 (Serrano et al., 2005):** HIV-associated lymphoma successfully treated with peripheral blood stem cell transplantation [79, p.338]
- **Study 80 (Spitzer et al., 2008):** Dose-reduced busulfan, cyclophosphamide, and autologous stem cell transplantation for HIV-associated lymphoma; AIDS Malignancy Consortium study 020 [80, p.338]
- **Study 81 (Re et al., 2009):** High-dose therapy and autologous peripheral blood stem cell transplantation as salvage treatment for AIDS-related lymphoma; long-term results of Italian Cooperative Group on AIDS and Tumors (GICAT) with prognostic factor analysis [81, p.338]
- **Study 82 (Bayraktar et al., 2012):** Outcome of patients with relapsed/refractory AIDS-related lymphoma diagnosed 1999-2008 and treated with curative intent in AIDS Malignancy Consortium [82, p.338]
- **Study 83 (Alvarnas et al., 2016):** Autologous hematopoietic cell transplantation for HIV-related lymphoma; results of BMT CTN 0803/AMC 071 trial [83, p.338]
- **Study 84 (Ayala et al., 2018):** Feasibility and efficacy of high-dose chemotherapy and autologous hematopoietic cell transplantation for HIV-associated lymphoma; single-institution experience [84, p.338]
- **Study 85 (Hubel et al., 2019):** Autologous stem cell transplantation for HIV-associated lymphoma in antiretroviral and rituximab era; retrospective study by EBMT Lymphoma Working Party [85, p.338]

### Plasmablastic Lymphoma Salvage Therapy

**Trial Evidence:**
- **Study 72 (Koizumi et al., 2016):** Clinical and pathological aspects of HIV-associated plasmablastic lymphoma; analysis of 24 cases [72, p.337]
- **Study 73 (Al-Malki et al., 2014):** Hematopoietic cell transplantation for plasmablastic lymphoma; review [73, p.337]
- **Study 74 (Cattaneo et al., 2015):** Autologous hematopoietic stem cell transplantation for plasmablastic lymphoma; European Society for Blood and Marrow Transplantation experience [74, p.338]
- **Study 78 (Pretscher et al., 2017):** Refractory plasmablastic lymphoma review of treatment options beyond standard therapy [78, p.338]

### CAR T-Cell Therapy

**Trial Evidence:**
- **Study 86 (Neelapu et al., 2023):** Five-year follow-up of ZUMA-1 supports curative potential of axicabtagene ciloleucel in refractory large B-cell lymphoma [86, p.338]
- **Study 87 (Schuster et al., 2021):** Long-term clinical outcomes of tisagenlecleucel in patients with relapsed or refractory aggressive B-cell lymphomas (JULIET): multicentre, open-label, single-arm, phase 2 study [87, p.338]
- **Study 88 (Abramson et al., 2020):** Lisocabtagene maraleucel for patients with relapsed or refractory large B-cell lymphomas (TRANSCEND NHL 001): multicentre seamless design study [88, p.339]
- **Study 89 (Sehgal et al., 2022):** Lisocabtagene maraleucel as second-line therapy in adults with relapsed or refractory large B-cell lymphoma not intended for haematopoietic stem cell transplantation (PILOT): open-label, phase 2 study [89, p.339]
- **Study 90 (Locke et al., 2022):** Axicabtagene ciloleucel as second-line therapy for large B-cell lymphoma [90, p.339]
- **Study 91 (Abramson et al., 2023):** Lisocabtagene maraleucel as second-line therapy for large B-cell lymphoma; primary analysis of phase 3 TRANSFORM study [91, p.339]
- **Study 92 (Barta et al., 2022):** Observational cohort study of people living with HIV (PWH) treated with CD19-directed CAR T-cell therapy for B-cell lymphoid malignancies; interim results of AIDS Malignancy Consortium (AMC) study AMC-113 [92, p.339]
- **Study 93 (Epstein-Peterson et al., 2022):** Trial-in-Progress: AMC 112: Axicabtagene ciloleucel in relapsed or refractory HIV-associated aggressive B-cell non-Hodgkin lymphoma (NCT05077527) [93, p.339]

## Special Considerations

### Antiretroviral Therapy Coordination

**Clinical Significance:**
- Simultaneous treatment with combined cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, vincristine, and prednisone chemotherapy and HAART is safe and improves survival [56, p.336-337]
- Concurrent HAART with bortezomib plus EPOCH is safe [53, p.336]
- Adding rituximab to CODOX-M/IVAC is safe when used with concurrent combination antiretroviral therapy [64, p.337]

### Antiretroviral Drug Selection

**Clinical Evidence:**
- Survival in HIV-infected patients with lymphoma varies according to choice of antiretroviral treatment [70, p.337]
- Outcome effects of antiretroviral drug combinations in HIV-positive patients receiving chemotherapy for lymphoma; retrospective analysis shows importance of coordinated selection [71, p.337]

### Treatment Intensity and Patient Selection

**Clinical Considerations:**
- Low-intensity therapy with EPOCH in adults with Burkitt's lymphoma shows efficacy [51, p.336]
- Plasmablastic lymphoma requires careful assessment for transplantation candidacy [73, 74, p.337-338]
