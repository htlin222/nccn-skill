## Summary of Key Evidence

- Non-Hodgkin lymphomas (NHL) are a heterogeneous group of lymphoproliferative disorders originating in B lymphocytes, T lymphocytes, or natural killer (NK) cells [p.143][1]
- In 2025, an estimated 80,350 people will be diagnosed with NHL and approximately 19,390 deaths are projected [p.143][1]
- The major B-cell lymphoma subtypes in the United States (1998-2011) were: DLBCL (32%), follicular lymphoma (17%), marginal zone lymphoma (8%), and mantle cell lymphoma (4%) [p.143][2]
- Worldwide in 2020, an estimated 544,000 people were diagnosed with NHL and approximately 260,000 died from the disease [p.143][3]
- Incidence and mortality rates of NHL have decreased since 2000 with the introduction of anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody rituximab, though mortality remains higher in resource-limited regions [p.143][4]

## Special Considerations

### HIV-Related B-Cell Lymphomas
- NHL (along with Kaposi sarcoma and lung cancer) are the most common cancer types diagnosed in people with HIV in the United States, with the largest declines in incidence rates projected for NHL and KS through 2030 [p.143][5],[6]
- DLBCL, Burkitt lymphoma, and primary central nervous system lymphoma are the most common B-cell lymphoma subtypes in people with HIV [p.143][7]

### Scope of NCCN Guidelines
- Guidelines cover diagnostic workup, treatment, and surveillance strategies for the most common B-cell lymphoma subtypes, including classification systems and supportive care considerations [p.143]
- B-cell lymphoma subtypes covered include: FL, MZLs (extranodal gastric, extranodal non-gastric, nodal, splenic), MCL, DLBCL, ALK-positive large B-cell lymphomas, MGZL, PMBL, histologic transformation to DLBCL, HGBL, BL, HIV-related B-cell lymphomas, and post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorders [p.143]
