# High-Grade B-Cell Lymphomas -- Evidence Summary

## Summary of Key Evidence

- DLBCL with MYC and BCL2 and/or BCL6 rearrangements are aggressive B-cell lymphomas with poor prognosis, characterized by elevated LDH, extranodal/bone marrow involvement, and high International Prognostic Index (IPI) [p.306][1-5]
- HGBL-DHL-BCL6 (MYC and BCL6 rearrangement) have distinct clinical and molecular features with significantly better survival outcomes compared to HGBL-DHL-BCL2, with 2-year EFS of 79% vs. 58% (median follow-up 49 months) [p.306][7-13]
- Double expressor lymphomas (DEL) with dual MYC and BCL2 expression by immunohistochemistry but negative for rearrangements have inferior prognosis compared to DLBCL-NOS but not to the same magnitude as true DHL [p.306-307][20,21]
- DA-EPOCH-R demonstrated efficacy in HGBL-DHL-BCL2 with 48-month EFS and OS rates of 71% and 77%, respectively [p.307][30]
- More intensive chemoimmunotherapy regimens are associated with better outcomes than RCHOP in DHL/THL [p.307][5,24-29]
- CAR T-cell therapies demonstrate superior outcomes over second-line chemotherapy followed by stem cell transplant for chemotherapy-sensitive relapsed/refractory disease [p.309][37,38]
- CNS relapse in HGBL-DHL-BCL2 occurs at a 2-year rate of 6%, lower than historically reported, suggesting HGBL-DHL-BCL2 should not be routinely considered a high-risk CNS relapse factor [p.308][34]

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### DA-EPOCH-R
DA-EPOCH-R is an appropriate first-line therapy option for all patients with HGBL-DHL-BCL2, HGBL-THL, or HGBL-NOS [p.308][30]. In a prospective phase II study of 53 patients with untreated aggressive B-cell lymphoma (19 with MYC rearrangement alone, 24 with DHL/THL), 48-month EFS and OS rates were 71% and 77%, respectively, after median follow-up of 56 months [p.307][30]. The Alliance A051701 trial evaluated addition of venetoclax to DA-EPOCH-R for HGBL-DHL-BCL2 but was discontinued due to excessive toxicity; however, it confirmed DA-EPOCH-R efficacy with ORR of 73% (67% CR) and estimated 14-month PFS of 65% [p.308][31].

### RCHOP
RCHOP may be associated with suboptimal outcomes and could be considered for patients with low-risk or limited-stage disease (IPI <2) [p.308]. Meta-analyses evaluating dose-intense regimens (R-hyperCVAD, R-CODOX-M/IVAC, or REPOCH) versus RCHOP in HGBL with MYC and BCL2 and/or BCL6 rearrangements showed dose-intense regimens are associated with better PFS than RCHOP, though OS was not significantly different [p.307][24,28].

### R-mini-CHOP
R-mini-CHOP may be considered for patients who are frail or older [p.308].

### R-hyperCVAD and R-CODOX-M/IVAC
R-hyperCVAD (alternating with high-dose methotrexate and cytarabine) or R-CODOX-M/IVAC are potentially toxic regimens and should be used based on performance status and presence of comorbidities [p.308]. A multicenter retrospective analysis of 106 patients with DHL/THL showed treatment with these intensive regimens resulted in superior CR and PFS compared to RCHOP [p.307][5].

### Pola-R-CHP
Pola-R-CHP (polatuzumab vedotin, rituximab, cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, and prednisone) is included as an option for first-line therapy for patients with HGBL-NOS [p.308][32]. In the POLARIX study, which included 10 patients with HGBL-NOS or HGBL-DHL/THL, there was a trend towards favorable survival for Pola-R-CHP in the HGBL subgroup, with more favorable outcomes for HGBL-NOS than for HGBL with MYC and BCL2 with or without BCL6 rearrangements [p.308][32]. The NCCN Panel added Pola-R-CHP as a treatment option with category 2B recommendation for HGBL-NOS [p.308][32].

### HGBL-NOS Treatment Outcomes
A retrospective analysis of 160 patients with HGBL-NOS reported an ORR of 80% (69% CR) with first-line chemoimmunotherapy regimens including DA-EPOCH-R (43% of patients), RCHOP (33% of patients), R-hyperCVAD with methotrexate, and R-CODOX-M/IVAC (4% and 7% of patients, respectively) [p.308][18]. The 2-year PFS and OS rates for the entire cohort were 55% and 68%, respectively, with no significant differences in ORR, PFS, and OS between regimens [p.308][18].

### Consolidative Therapy
Consolidative radiation therapy (RT) has been reported to improve response rates among patients with limited-stage disease, although there were no significant differences in survival rates [p.308][33]. The use of consolidative RT is preferred for patients with localized disease [p.308]. Consolidation with HDT/ASCR (high-dose therapy/autologous stem cell rescue) following CR to first-line therapy is not recommended since it is not associated with improved survival outcomes [p.308][5,26].

### CNS Prophylaxis
CNS prophylaxis (systemic high-dose methotrexate and/or intrathecal methotrexate and/or cytarabine during or after the course of treatment) can be considered for patients with high risk for CNS relapse based on CNS-IPI [p.308]. A population-based study of 162 patients with HGBL-DHL-BCL2 confirmed by FISH testing and without primary CNS involvement reported a 2-year CNS relapse rate of 6%, lower than that reported in earlier studies, suggesting that HGBL-DHL-BCL2 should not be considered as a risk factor for CNS relapse [p.308][34].

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### CAR T-Cell Therapy
Chimeric antigen receptor T-cell therapy with axicabtagene ciloleucel and lisocabtagene maraleucel are approved as second-line therapy options for patients with primary refractory or relapsed disease within 12 months after first-line therapy [p.309][37,38]. The ZUMA-7 trial included 70 patients with HGBL with MYC and BCL2 and/or BCL6 rearrangements (43 randomized to axicabtagene ciloleucel), and the TRANSFORM trial included 43 patients with HGBL with MYC and BCL2 and/or BCL6 rearrangements (22 randomized to lisocabtagene maraleucel), both demonstrating superiority of CAR T-cell therapy over second-line chemotherapy followed by HDT/ASCR for patients with chemotherapy-sensitive disease [p.309][37,38].

Axicabtagene ciloleucel, tisagenlecleucel, and lisocabtagene maraleucel are also FDA approved for treatment of relapsed/refractory HGBL after ≥2 prior systemic therapy regimens [p.309][39-41]. Patients with HGBL were included in small numbers in all these trials, with CAR T-cell therapies resulting in favorable clinical outcomes across all subgroups [p.309][37-41].

In a retrospective analysis comparing outcomes of patients with HGBL versus other LBCL treated with CAR T-cell therapies, the PFS (3 months vs. 5 months) and OS (15 months vs. 18 months) for patients with HGBL (n=60; DHL, THL, or HGBL-NOS) were not significantly different from patients with non-HGBL (n=135) [p.309][44]. After median follow-up of 19 months, median OS was 7 months for HGBL-THL/DHL with MYC-BCL2, 19 months for HGBL-NOS, and 14 months for HGBL-DHL with MYC-BCL6 [p.309][44].

### Bispecific Antibodies
Bispecific antibodies (epcoritamab and glofitamab) are FDA-approved for LBCL (including HGBL) after ≥2 systemic therapy regimens and are appropriate options for third-line therapy for patients with HGBL who are not candidates for CAR T-cell therapy (including patients without access to CAR T-cell therapy), or those with disease progression after CAR T-cell therapy [p.309][45,46]. However, a very small number of patients with HGBL were included in the pivotal trials leading to bispecific antibody approval, and their efficacy in patients with relapsed HGBL following CAR T-cell therapy is yet to be fully elucidated [p.309].

### General Approach for Relapsed/Refractory Disease
Relapsed/refractory disease should be treated as described for DLBCL [p.309]. Limited data are available regarding the outcome of relapsed/refractory disease following HDT/ASCR or allogeneic HCT in patients with HGBL with MYC and BCL2 and/or BCL6 rearrangements or DEL [p.309][35,36].

## Special Considerations

### Disease Classification and Prognostic Factors
In the 2017 WHO classification, DLBCL with MYC and BCL2 and/or BCL6 rearrangements were included in a unique category called high-grade large B-cell lymphomas (HGBL) with MYC and BCL2 and/or BCL6 rearrangements [p.306][6]. The 2022 WHO Classification of Hematolymphoid Tumors (WHO5) and the International Consensus Classification (ICC) have separated HGBL-DHL-BCL2 as a distinct entity from HGBL-DHL-BCL6 [p.308][16,17]. In WHO5, entities with DLBCL morphology are termed DLBCL, while ICC terms all cases as HGBL regardless of morphology [p.308].

HGBL-DHL-BCL2 are of germinal center B-cell (GCB) origin but GCB cell of origin is less common in patients with HGBL-DHL-BCL6 [p.306][7]. MYC and BCL6 rearrangements were not a poor prognostic factor of clinical outcomes for HGBL-DHL-BCL6 [p.306][12,13]. Treatment recommendations for HGBL-DHL-BCL6 are similar to that outlined for DLBCL-NOS [p.308].

The negative prognostic impact of MYC rearrangement is correlated with MYC partner gene (IG vs. non-IG), and the adverse prognostic impact was evident within 2 years after diagnosis in patients with concurrent BCL2 and/or BCL6 rearrangement with an IG partner gene [p.306][14,15].

### HGBL-NOS
Large B-cell lymphomas (LBCL) with blastoid histology or LBCL that are intermediate between DLBCL and Burkitt lymphoma (BL), but lack MYC and BCL2 with or without BCL6 rearrangement are categorized as HGBL-NOS [p.306][6,16,17]. HGBL-NOS excludes HGBL with MYC and BCL2 with or without BCL6 rearrangement or clear DLBCL. The majority of HGBL-NOS are of GCB origin and are associated with poor prognostic variables such as advanced-stage disease and elevated LDH [p.306][18,19].

### Double Expressor Lymphomas (DEL)
Double expressor lymphomas (DEL) with dual expression of both MYC and BCL2 by immunohistochemistry (IHC) but negative for associated rearrangements have an inferior prognosis compared to DLBCL-NOS, but not to the same magnitude as true DHL [p.307][20,21]. In a retrospective analysis evaluating outcomes of patients with DEL treated with RCHOP (n=61) or DA-EPOCH-R (n=94), the 3-year PFS rates were 33% and 57% for RCHOP (P=.063) and DA-EPOCH-R (P=.43), respectively [p.307][22]. The 3-year OS rates were 72% for both cohorts, with a trend towards improved PFS among patients <65 years who received DA-EPOCH-R [p.307][22]. No data currently support the use of any regimen other than RCHOP for DEL, and DEL do not warrant therapy different from that recommended for DLBCL-NOS [p.307].

### Clinical Trial Participation
Participation in clinical trials is recommended [p.308].
