## Diagnosis and Additional Testing

### Essential Immunophenotyping

- Adequate immunophenotyping to establish diagnosis [p.105]
- IHC panel: CD3, B-cell markers (CD20, CD79a, PAX5), kappa, lambda [p.105]
- EBV evaluation by EBER-ISH [p.105]
- Additional immunophenotyping depending on morphology and initial phenotyping [p.105]:
  - IHC panel for cHL: CD15, CD30, CD45, PAX5 [p.105]
  - IHC panel for DLBCL/BL: CD10, BCL6, MUM1/IRF4, MYC, TdT, Ki67, BCL2 [p.105]
  - IHC panel for plasmacytic/plasmablastic tumors: CD138, MUM1/IRF4, ALK, HHV8 [p.105]
  - IHC panel for T-cell lymphoma: CD2, CD5, CD7, CD4, CD8, ALK, TIA-1, granzyme B [p.105]
  - IHC for EBV-LMP1 and EBV-EBNA2 to determine latency status [p.105]
- Flow cytometry of biopsy specimen to include B and T cell clonality assessment [p.105]
- FISH to detect MYC, BCL2, or BCL6 rearrangements [p.105]
- Molecular analysis to detect: Ig gene rearrangements [p.105]

## Workup

### Essential Laboratory and Imaging Studies

- Performance status [p.105]
- Albumin [p.105]
- History of therapy for transplant [p.105]
- LDH, electrolytes, BUN, creatinine [p.105]
- CBC with differential [p.105]
- Hepatitis B testing [p.105] (Hepatitis B testing is indicated because of the risk of reactivation with immunotherapy + chemotherapy. Tests include HBsAg and core antibody for a patient with no risk factors. For patients with risk factors or previous history of hepatitis B, add e-antigen. If positive, check viral load and consider consult with gastroenterologist.) [p.105]
- EBV PCR for cell-free plasma EBV DNA marker [p.105] (If EBV-negative, should not be used as response marker.) [p.105]
- PET/CT scan and/or C/A/P CT with contrast [p.105]
- Pregnancy testing in patients of childbearing age (if chemotherapy or RT planned) [p.105]

### Useful in Selected Cases

- Echocardiogram or MUGA scan if anthracycline-based regimen is indicated [p.105]
- Bone marrow evaluation [p.105]
- Brain MRI with and without contrast [p.105]
- Cytomegalovirus (CMV) PCR [p.105]
- EBV serology for primary versus reactivation [p.105]
- Hepatitis C testing [p.105]
- Discuss fertility preservation options (sperm banking, semen cryopreservation, IVF, or ovarian tissue or oocyte cryopreservation) [p.105]

## PTLD Subtypes and Classification

### Non-Destructive Lesions (ICC) / Hyperplasia (WHO5)

Non-destructive lesions in the ICC Classification are of B-cell type and include PCH, infectious mononucleosis, and florid follicular hyperplasia [p.106].

### Polymorphic PTLD (B-cell type) (ICC/WHO5)

Polymorphic PTLD can present as systemic or localized disease [p.107].

### Monomorphic PTLD (B-cell and T-cell/NK-cell type) (ICC) / Lymphoma (WHO5)

Treatment is based on the unique histology [p.106].

### Classic Hodgkin Lymphoma (CHL)-type PTLD

See --> NCCN Guidelines for Hodgkin Lymphoma [p.105].

## First-Line Therapy

All recommendations are (Category 2A) unless otherwise indicated [p.105].

### Non-Destructive Lesions (ICC) / Hyperplasia (WHO5)

**Preferred:**
- Reduction of immunosuppression (RI) [p.106]

**Other Recommended:**
- Rituximab and monitor EBV PCR [p.106] (as part of a step-wise approach in patients who are not highly symptomatic or cannot tolerate chemotherapy secondary to comorbidity) [p.106]

Response to RI is variable and patients need to be closely monitored; RI should be coordinated with the transplant team. RI: Reduction in calcineurin inhibition (cyclosporin and tacrolimus), discontinuation of antimetabolic agents (azathioprine and mycophenolate mofetil), and for patients who are critically ill all non-glucocorticoid immunosuppression should be discontinued [p.106].

### Monomorphic PTLD (B-cell type)

**Preferred:**
- RI, if possible [p.106] and/or:
  - Rituximab alone [p.106] (as part of a step-wise approach in patients who are not highly symptomatic or cannot tolerate chemotherapy secondary to comorbidity) [p.106]
  - or Chemoimmunotherapy [p.106]

### Monomorphic PTLD (T-cell type) (ICC) / Lymphoma (WHO5)

- Other than reduction of RI, there are no established treatments. HDT/ASCR may not be appropriate [p.106]
- Consider chemotherapy (See NCCN Guidelines for T-Cell Lymphomas) [p.106]

### Polymorphic PTLD (B-cell type) (ICC/WHO5) -- Systemic

**Preferred:**
- RI, if possible [p.107] and:
  - Rituximab alone [p.107]
  - or Chemoimmunotherapy [p.107]

### Polymorphic PTLD (B-cell type) (ICC/WHO5) -- Localized

**Preferred:**
- RI, if possible [p.107] and:
  - ISRT ± rituximab [p.107]
  - or Surgery ± rituximab [p.107]
  - or Rituximab alone [p.107]

### Primary CNS PTLD (B-cell type)

**Preferred:**
- High-dose methotrexate + rituximab [p.108]

Note: Patients with PTLD often have renal insufficiency. High-dose methotrexate should be used with caution. Alternate regimens (cytarabine-based) should be considered [p.108].

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy

### Non-Destructive Lesions (ICC) / Hyperplasia (WHO5)

Following initial response assessment (restage in 2 to 4 weeks) [p.106]:

- **If Complete Response:** Manage immunosuppression and monitor EBV PCR, and graft organ function [p.106]
- **If Partial Response, Persistent or Progressive Disease:** Rituximab and monitor EBV PCR [p.106]

Re-escalation of immunosuppressive therapy should be individualized, taking into account the extent of initial RI and the nature of the organ allograft. These decisions should be made in conjunction with the transplant team [p.106].

### Monomorphic PTLD (B-cell type)

Following initial response assessment (restage in 2 to 4 weeks) [p.106]:

- **If Complete Response:** See appropriate histologic subtype for follow-up [p.106]
- **If Partial Response, Persistent or Progressive Disease:** [p.106]
  - If RI was initial therapy, then rituximab or chemoimmunotherapy [p.106]
  - or If rituximab monotherapy was initial therapy, then chemoimmunotherapy or consider rituximab for patients with partial response and IPI 0-2 [p.106]
  - or If chemoimmunotherapy was initial therapy, see BCEL-7 [p.106]
  - or Clinical trial [p.106]
  - or Immunotherapy with EBV-Specific Cytotoxic T-lymphocytes (EBV-CTL) (if EBV-driven) [p.106]

### Polymorphic PTLD (B-cell type) (ICC/WHO5)

Following initial response assessment (restage in 2 to 4 weeks) [p.107]:

- **If Complete Response:** [p.107]
  - Monitor EBV PCR and:
    - Active surveillance [p.107]
    - or Continue RI, if possible ± maintenance rituximab, and graft organ function monitoring [p.107]
- **If Partial Response, Persistent or Progressive Disease:** [p.107]
  - Chemoimmunotherapy [p.107]
  - or Clinical trial [p.107]
  - or EBV-CTL (if EBV driven) [p.107]

## Suggested Treatment Regimens

### Sequential Chemoimmunotherapy

(For Monomorphic PTLD (B-cell type) and Polymorphic PTLD (B-cell type)) [p.108]

- Rituximab 375 mg/m2 weekly x 4 weeks [p.108]
  - Restage with PET/CT scan (in 2-4 weeks) [p.108]
    - If PET/CT scan negative (5-PS: 1-3), rituximab 375 mg/m2 every 3 weeks x 4 cycles [p.108]
    - If PET/CT scan positive (5-PS: 4-5), RCHOP-21 every 3 weeks + G-CSF x 4 cycles [p.108]

Note: If 5-PS: 5 because of new sites of disease or clear progression, treat as refractory disease [p.108].

### Concurrent Chemoimmunotherapy

(For Monomorphic PTLD (B-cell type) and Polymorphic PTLD (B-cell type)) [p.108]

**Preferred:**
- Pola-R-CHP (polatuzumab vedotin-piiq, rituximab, cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, prednisone) (≥ IPI 2) [p.108]
- RCHOP (rituximab, cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, vincristine, prednisone) [p.108]

**Useful in Certain Circumstances:**
- For patients who are frail who cannot tolerate anthracycline, no specific regimen has been identified but options may include [p.108]:
  - RCEPP (rituximab, cyclophosphamide, etoposide, prednisone, procarbazine) [p.108]
  - RCEOP (rituximab, cyclophosphamide, etoposide, vincristine, prednisone) [p.108]
  - RCVP (rituximab, cyclophosphamide, vincristine, prednisone) [p.108]

### Primary CNS PTLD (B-cell type)

- High-dose methotrexate + rituximab [p.108]

## General Notes and Considerations

- An FDA-approved biosimilar is an appropriate substitute for any recommended systemic biologic therapy in the NCCN Guidelines [p.108]
- Rituximab and hyaluronidase human injection for subcutaneous use may be substituted for rituximab after patients have received the first full dose of rituximab by intravenous infusion [p.108]
- Consider prophylaxis for tumor lysis syndrome [p.108]
- See monoclonal antibody and viral reactivation considerations [p.108]
- Indolent small B-cell lymphomas arising in transplant recipients are not included among PTLDs, with the exception of EBV-positive marginal zone lymphomas in the ICC. Indolent lymphomas arising in transplant recipients are included in the WHO5. EBV-positive mucocutaneous ulcer (WHO5) is an indolent lymphoma [p.105]
- In the WHO5, hyperplasia includes follicular hyperplasia, infectious mononucleosis-like hyperplasia (IMH), and plasmacytic hyperplasia (PCH); in the ICC these three types are considered non-destructive [p.105]
