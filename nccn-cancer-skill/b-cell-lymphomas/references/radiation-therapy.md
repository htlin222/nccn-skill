## General Principles

All recommendations are (Category 2A) [p.125] unless otherwise indicated.

- Treatment with photons, electrons, or protons is appropriate depending upon clinical scenario [p.125]

- Advanced RT technologies such as intensity-modulated RT (IMRT)/volumetric modulated arc therapy (VMAT) [p.125], proton therapy [p.125], breath-hold [p.125] or respiratory gating [p.125], and/or image-guided therapy [p.125] may offer significant and clinically relevant advantages in specific instances to spare organs at risk (OARs) such as the heart (including coronary arteries and valves), lungs [p.125], kidneys, liver, spinal cord, esophagus, bone marrow, breasts, stomach, muscle/soft tissue, and salivary glands to decrease the risk for late, normal tissue toxicity while still achieving the primary goal of local tumor control [p.125]

- Reducing dose to normal tissues reduces the risk of late complications [p.125]. Achieving highly conformal dose distributions is especially important for patients who are being treated with curative intent or who have long life expectancies following therapy [p.125]

- For mediastinal and abdominal lymphoma, respiratory motion management such as gating or breath-hold techniques may be advantageous [p.125]. Breath-hold techniques have been shown to decrease incidental dose to the heart and lungs in many disease presentations [p.125]. Similarly, for abdominal lymphomas, reduction in radiation exposures to heart, liver, and kidneys may be achieved by motion management techniques [p.125]

- Since the advantages of these techniques include tightly conformal doses and steep gradients next to normal tissues, target definition and treatment delivery verification require careful monitoring to avoid the risk of tumor geographic miss and subsequent decrease in tumor control [p.125]. Image guidance may be required to provide this assurance [p.125]

- Randomized studies to test these concepts are unlikely to be done since these techniques are designed to decrease late effects, which take 10+ years to evolve [p.125]. In light of that, the modalities and techniques that best reduce the doses to the OARs in a clinically meaningful way without compromising target coverage should be considered [p.125]

- Radiation Dose Constraints: Recommendations for normal tissue dose constraints can be found in the Principles of Radiation Therapy section of the NCCN Guidelines for Hodgkin Lymphoma [p.125]

## Volumes

### ISRT for Nodal Disease

- ISRT is the recommended approach for volume definition and treatment planning for NHL [p.126]. Planning for ISRT requires CT-based treatment planning and incorporates volume determinations including gross tumor volume (GTV), clinical target volume (CTV), and planning target volume (PTV) [p.126]. Incorporating other imaging tests such as PET and MRI often enhances treatment volume determination [p.126]

- The pre-chemotherapy or pre-biopsy GTV provides the basis for determining the CTV [p.126]. Concerns for questionable subclinical disease and uncertainties in original imaging accuracy or localization may lead to expansion of the CTV and are determined individually using clinical judgment [p.126]. Further, adjacent uninvolved organs (e.g., lungs, bone, muscle, kidney) are excluded from the CTV when disease regresses following chemotherapy [p.126]

- For early-stage indolent NHL treated with RT alone, larger treatment volumes should be considered to encompass potential microscopic disease in adjacent lymph nodes or the immediate vicinity [p.126]. For example, the CTV definition for treating FL with RT alone will be greater than that used for DLBCL with similar disease distribution, as the latter is treated with combined modality therapy [p.126]

- Motion of the target caused by respiration as determined by 4D-CT or fluoroscopy (internal target volume [ITV]) should also influence the final CTV [p.126]

- The PTV is an additional expansion of the CTV that accounts only for setup variations and potential target motion not previously accounted for in the CTV (see International Commission on Radiation Units and Measurements [ICRU] definitions) [p.126]. Proton RT planning does not generally use a PTV, but rather robustness evaluation to ensure coverage of the CTV [p.126]

- OARs should be outlined for dose-volume analysis and optimizing treatment planning decisions [p.126]

- The treatment plan can be designed with conventional, 3D conformal, IMRT/VMAT, or proton therapy techniques using clinical treatment planning considerations of target coverage and normal tissue avoidance [p.126]

### ISRT for Extranodal Disease

- Similar principles as for ISRT nodal sites apply [p.126]

- For EMZL, the CTV generally consists of the entire affected organ (e.g., stomach, salivary gland, thyroid) [p.126]. Partial organ ISRT may be appropriate if the disease is well localized on imaging (e.g., orbit and breast) [p.126]

- For most NHL subtypes, uninvolved lymph nodes should not be targeted [p.126]

## General Dose Guidelines

### Definitive RT (1.5–2.0 Gy daily fractions)

**FL:** 24–30 Gy [p.127]

**Marginal Zone Lymphoma (MZL):** 24 Gy [p.127]
- EMZL of the stomach: 24 Gy in 16 fractions (1.5 Gy/fractions) to minimize acute GI toxicity [p.127]
  - 4 Gy in 2 fractions has been used [p.127]. However, additional 20 Gy is needed for some patients and careful follow-up is needed [p.127]
- Orbital and salivary gland MZL: 4 Gy in 2 fractions may be considered as an alternative to 24 Gy [p.127]. Careful regular follow-up (physical exam and imaging as appropriate) with radiation oncologist and ophthalmologist is essential when using this very-low-dose regimen [p.127]
- Definitive doses are recommended for incomplete response or relapsed disease [p.127]

**MCL:** [p.127]
- Primary treatment (without chemoimmunotherapy): 36 Gy [p.127]
- Consolidation after chemoimmunotherapy:
  - CR: 24–30 Gy [p.127]
  - PR: 36 Gy [p.127]

**DLBCL/HGBL/PMBL/MGZL:** [p.127]
- Primary treatment (without chemoimmunotherapy): 40 Gy [p.127]
- Consolidation after chemoimmunotherapy:
  - CR (5-PS 1–3): 30–36 Gy [p.127]
  - PR (5-PS 4): 36–50 Gy [p.127]
- Refractory disease (5-PS 4–5): 40–55 Gy [p.127]
- In combination with HCT: 20–36 Gy, depending on sites of disease and prior RT exposure [p.127]; hypofractionate as appropriate to expedite HCT procedure [p.127]
- Prophylactic testicular irradiation: 25–30 Gy [p.127]

### Palliative RT

**FL/MZL/MCL/SLL:** 2 Gy X 2 fractions or 4 Gy X 1 fraction (which may be repeated as needed) [p.127]; doses up to 30 Gy may be appropriate in select circumstances [p.127]

**DLBCL/HGBL/PMBL/MGZL and BL:** (higher doses/fraction typically appropriate) [p.127]
- 20–30 Gy in 5–10 fractions [p.127]. Standard hypofractionated palliative treatment schedules such as 20 Gy in 5 fractions and 30 Gy in 10 fractions are appropriate depending upon clinical scenario [p.127]

**HIV-related B-cell lymphomas and PTLD:** Treated based on underlying histologic subtype and treatment intent (curative vs. palliative) [p.127]

### Bridging RT with CAR T-cell Therapy

- Localized disease: 20–40 Gy [p.127] (higher doses and comprehensive coverage preferred if feasible; hypofractionate as appropriate to expedite CAR T-cell procedure) [p.127]

- Extensive disease: 20–30 Gy [p.127] (hypofractionate as outlined above) [p.127]
