## Summary of Key Evidence

- MGZL are young adult male predominant (ages 20-40) with large anterior mediastinal masses; historically associated with worse prognosis than PMBL or CHL [p.272]
- DA-EPOCH-R demonstrates superior EFS (62%) and OS (74%) at median 59 months in MGZL (n=24), significantly lower than PMBL outcomes (EFS 93%, OS 97% at 5 years; P<0.001) [p.272-273]
- CHOP-like regimens with or without rituximab outperformed ABVD, with 2-year PFS of 52% vs 22% respectively in multicenter retrospective analysis [p.272]
- Clinical trial enrollment is preferred; in absence of trials, RCHOP-21 or DA-EPOCH-R recommended for initial therapy [p.273]
- Consolidative RT strongly recommended for limited-stage disease due to historically poor chemotherapy-alone outcomes [p.273]

## Disease Definition and Epidemiology

MGZL (formerly "B-cell lymphomas unclassifiable with features intermediate between DLBCL and CHL" per 2017 WHO) are now termed mediastinal gray zone lymphomas in both ICC and WHO5 classifications [p.272]. These lymphomas show overlapping pathologic features between CHL and PMBL with poorer clinical outcomes than either entity [p.272].

MGZL typically presents in young adult males between ages 20-40 years, characterized by large anterior mediastinal mass with or without supraclavicular lymph node involvement [p.272]. Non-mediastinal gray zone lymphomas are biologically and clinically distinct and should not be considered in this diagnostic category [p.272].

Morphologic patterns include: (1) CHL-like morphology with strong expression of at least two B-cell antigens by IHC (CD20 and other B-cell markers), or (2) PMBL-like morphology with downregulated B-cell antigen profile and strong CD30/CD15 expression [p.272]. EBV is negative in vast majority; positivity should raise consideration for immune suppression or EBV-positive DLBCL, NOS [p.272].

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Chemotherapy Efficacy

MGZL appears associated with worse prognosis compared to PMBL or CHL [p.272]. In multicenter retrospective analysis (without central pathology review), patients with gray zone lymphomas treated with CHOP-like regimens with or without rituximab demonstrated superior outcomes versus those treated with ABVD (doxorubicin, bleomycin, vinblastine, dacarbazine), with 2-year PFS rates of 52% and 22%, respectively [p.272].

**DA-EPOCH-R Study:**
- Design: Prospective evaluation of 6-8 cycles DA-EPOCH-R in MGZL patients (n=24) compared to PMBL (n=51) in same study [p.272-273]
- Results in MGZL: EFS 62%, OS 74% at median follow-up 59 months [p.272]
- 5-year outcomes: EFS 62% vs. 93% (PMBL, P=0.0005); OS 74% vs. 97% (PMBL, P=0.001) [p.273]
- DA-EPOCH-R associated with improved clinical outcomes overall [p.272]

### NCCN Recommendations

No optimal treatment approach established, though multiagent chemotherapy regimens used for DLBCL are typically used for MGZL [p.273]. Patients with MGZL should receive treatment in cancer centers with experience in treating this lymphoma type [p.273].

**Preferred Initial Therapy:**
- Clinical trial (preferred option) [p.273]
- RCHOP-21 [p.273]
- DA-EPOCH-R [p.273]

### Consolidative Therapy

Given apparent inferior outcomes among MGZL treated with traditional chemoimmunotherapy regimens, consolidative RT should be strongly considered for patients with limited-stage disease amenable to RT [p.273].

## Summary of Key Evidence

- Mediastinal gray zone lymphoma (MGZL) is a distinct entity with intermediate features between classical Hodgkin lymphoma and primary mediastinal B-cell lymphoma [p.287-288]
- Gene expression and methylation profiling distinguishes MGZL as having shared elements with both classical Hodgkin lymphoma and mediastinal large B-cell lymphoma [p.287]
- Mutational landscape and clinicopathologic features provide prognostic information for patient stratification [p.288]
- Dose-adjusted EPOCH-R with mediastinal radiation therapy may improve outcomes in MGZL patients [p.288]
- Multiple bispecific antibodies and targeted therapies show efficacy in relapsed/refractory B-cell lymphomas associated with MGZL [p.284-285]

## Pathologic Definition and Classification

Mediastinal gray zone lymphoma represents a diagnostic challenge with features intermediate between two distinct lymphoma entities [p.287]. The entity was first described as the "missing link" between classical Hodgkin lymphoma and mediastinal large B-cell lymphoma [p.287]. Methylation profiling reveals distinctive molecular signatures with shared elements from both parent entities [p.287]. Gene expression profiling confirms MGZL occupies a distinct molecular space relative to primary mediastinal B-cell lymphoma and classical Hodgkin lymphoma [p.288]. The mutational landscape of MGZL has been characterized, providing molecular insights for classification and prognostication [p.288].

## First-Line and Standard Therapy Evidence

### DA-EPOCH-R with Mediastinal Radiation

The prospective study of mediastinal gray-zone lymphoma indicated that dose-adjusted EPOCH-R combined with mediastinal radiation therapy may improve outcomes for MGZL patients [p.288]. This combined modality approach addresses both the systemic disease and the characteristic mediastinal involvement seen in this lymphoma type.

## Relapsed/Refractory Therapy Options

### Bispecific Antibodies

**Epcoritamab** (CD3×CD20 bispecific antibody) demonstrates efficacy in relapsed or refractory large B-cell lymphoma [p.284-285]. The phase I/II trial showed dose expansion with durable responses [p.284]. The 3-year update from the Epcore NHL-1 trial confirmed epcoritamab leads to deep and durable responses in relapsed or refractory large B-cell lymphoma [p.284]. Epcoritamab combined with gemcitabine-oxaliplatin (GemOx) was evaluated in transplant-ineligible relapsed/refractory DLBCL in the EPCORE NHL-2 trial [p.284].

**Glofitamab** (CD3×CD20 bispecific antibody) demonstrates fixed-duration monotherapy with durable responses in patients with relapsed or refractory large B-cell lymphoma [p.284]. A 3-year follow-up from a pivotal phase II study confirmed sustained efficacy [p.284]. Glofitamab plus GemOx versus rituximab-GemOx was compared in the global phase 3 STARGLO trial for relapsed or refractory DLBCL [p.284].

### CD3×CD20 Bispecific Antibody Considerations

Consensus recommendations exist for managing toxicity associated with CD3×CD20 bispecific antibody therapy [p.287].

### Monoclonal Antibodies and ADCs

**Mosunetuzumab** demonstrates long-term 3-year follow-up benefit in relapsed or refractory follicular lymphoma after ≥2 prior therapies [p.285]. Mosunetuzumab combined with polatuzumab vedotin was evaluated in a phase 1b/2 trial for relapsed or refractory aggressive large B-cell lymphoma [p.285].

**Tafasitamab** (MOR208) plus lenalidomide showed long-term outcomes in the Phase II L-MIND study for relapsed or refractory DLBCL [p.285]. Final 5-year efficacy and safety findings confirmed sustained benefit in patients with relapsed or refractory DLBCL [p.285].

**Polatuzumab vedotin** (CD79b-directed ADC) demonstrated objective responses in relapsed or refractory DLBCL [p.285].

**Loncastuximab tesirine** (CD19-directed ADC) showed long-term efficacy and safety in the phase II LOTIS-2 study for relapsed/refractory DLBCL [p.285]. Real-world experience with loncastuximab in high-risk and heavily pretreated relapsed/refractory DLBCL from 21 US centers demonstrated clinical utility [p.285].

**Brentuximab vedotin** (CD30-directed ADC) demonstrated objective responses in phase 2 study of relapsed/refractory DLBCL with variable CD30 expression [p.285]. A phase 2 study evaluated brentuximab vedotin combinations for relapsed DLBCL [p.285].

### Emerging Issues: CD20/CD19 Antigen Loss

Loss of CD20 expression represents a mechanism of resistance to CD3×CD20 bispecific antibody therapy in relapsed/refractory B-cell lymphomas [p.285]. Post-therapeutic tumor losses of B-cell receptor components CD20/CD19 present new challenges in managing relapsed/refractory DLBCL [p.285]. CD19 antibody-drug conjugate therapy does not preclude subsequent responses to CD19-directed CAR T-cell therapy [p.286].

### Chemotherapy Regimens for Salvage

**R-ICE** (rituximab plus ifosfamide, carboplatin, etoposide) serves as second-line therapy before autologous stem cell transplantation [p.285].

**R-DHAX** (rituximab, dexamethasone, cytarabine, and oxaliplatin) is an effective salvage regimen in relapsed/refractory B-cell non-Hodgkin lymphoma [p.286].

**Oxaliplatin-based regimens** (dexamethasone, high-dose cytarabine, and oxaliplatin) with or without rituximab show effectiveness in salvage treatment for relapsed or refractory lymphoma [p.286].

**GCD-R** (gemcitabine, carboplatin, dexamethasone, and rituximab) is effective as a prospective multi-center phase II regimen by the Puget Sound Oncology Consortium [p.286].

**Comparisons of salvage regimens**: Randomized comparison of gemcitabine, dexamethasone, and cisplatin versus dexamethasone, cytarabine, and cisplatin before autologous stem-cell transplantation was conducted in the NCIC-CTG LY.12 trial [p.286]. R-GEMOX (rituximab plus gemcitabine-oxaliplatin) outcomes were evaluated in a multicenter cohort study [p.286].

**Prognostic factors in salvage**: Germinal center/activated B-cell subclassification has prognostic impact for response to salvage therapy in relapsed/refractory DLBCL in the bio-CORAL study [p.286]. MYC+ diffuse large B-cell lymphoma shows poor salvage outcomes with classical R-ICE or R-DHAP followed by BEAM plus autologous stem cell transplantation [p.286].

### Radiation Therapy

Involved-field radiotherapy before high-dose therapy and autologous stem-cell rescue provides long-term disease control in diffuse large-cell lymphoma [p.286].

### Other Agents

**Lenalidomide**: A phase 2/3 multicenter, randomized study compared lenalidomide versus investigator's choice in relapsed or refractory DLBCL [p.286].

**Selinexor** (XPO1 inhibitor): Phase 2 trial (SADAL) evaluated selinexor in patients with relapsed or refractory DLBCL [p.286].

## Special Considerations

### CAR T-Cell Therapy

Understanding and managing large B-cell lymphoma relapses after CAR T-cell therapy is critical [p.286]. Outcomes of patients with large B-cell lymphomas and progressive disease following CD19-specific CAR T-cell therapy have been characterized [p.286]. CD19 antibody-drug conjugate therapy does not preclude subsequent CAR T-cell responses [p.286].

### CAR T-Cell Toxicity Management

**Cytokine release syndrome (CRS) and immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS)**:
- Anakinra (IL-1 receptor antagonist) is used for steroid-refractory ICANS following CAR T-cell therapy [p.287]
- Anakinra is effective for refractory CRS or ICANS after chimeric antigen receptor T-cell therapy [p.287]
- How to manage refractory CRS and ICANS after CAR T-cell therapy has been addressed [p.287]
- Prophylactic anakinra with CD19 CAR T-cell therapy showed benefit in phase 2 trial interim results for relapsed or refractory lymphoma [p.287]

**Cytopenias**: Management of cytopenias after CAR T-cell therapy requires specific strategies [p.287]

**General toxicity**: ASTCT consensus grading for cytokine release syndrome and neurologic toxicity associated with immune effector cells has been established [p.287]

### ALK-Positive Large B-Cell Lymphoma

ALK activation by CLTC-ALK fusion is a recurrent event in large B-cell lymphoma [p.287]. ALK-positive DLBCL is associated with Clathrin-ALK rearrangements [p.287]. ALK-positive diffuse large B-cell lymphoma is a rare clinicopathologic entity with poor prognosis [p.287]. Systematic review of anaplastic lymphoma kinase-positive large B-cell lymphoma (ALK+ LBCL) describes clinicopathological features and management [p.287].

**ALK Inhibitor Therapy**: Precision therapy with anaplastic lymphoma kinase inhibitor ceritinib is effective in ALK-rearranged anaplastic large cell lymphoma [p.287]. Next-generation ALK inhibitors demonstrate high activity in ALK-positive large B-cell lymphoma [p.287-288].

### Prognostic and Molecular Studies

Characteristics, outcomes, and prognostication of gray zone lymphoma with features intermediate between classical Hodgkin lymphoma and DLBCL were evaluated in a large multicenter cohort [p.288]. Mutational landscape analysis of gray zone lymphoma provides molecular prognostic information [p.288].

### Associated Conditions

Regression of lymphoproliferative disorders occurs after treatment for hepatitis C infection, potentially relevant to some MGZL cases [p.288].
