## Initial Evaluation for AML

All recommendations are category 2A unless indicated as Category 1 or Category 2B.

### Laboratory and Clinical Assessment

- History and physical (H&P) [p.11]
- Complete blood count (CBC), platelets, differential, comprehensive metabolic panel (CMP), uric acid, lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) [p.11]
- B12 and folic acid evaluation [p.11]
- Prothrombin time (PT), partial thromboplastin time (PTT), fibrinogen [p.11]

### Pathology and Molecular Testing

- Bone marrow (BM) core biopsy and aspirate analyses, including immunophenotyping by immunohistochemistry (IHC) stains + flow cytometry, and the analysis of chromosomal structural variations by cytogenetics, fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH), and next generation sequencing (NGS) --> See AML-A [p.11]

- Molecular analyses for lesions that allow risk stratification as per ELN 2022 --> See AML-A [p.11]

- Comprehensive pathology report, including diagnosis of acute myeloid leukemia (AML) within the context of the diagnostic system used, blast count, cellularity, morphologic dysplasia, and mutation status if available [p.11]

- Consider additional molecular and genetic testing for heritable hematologic malignancy predisposition in a subset of patients (see MDS-D and MDS-E in the NCCN Guidelines for Myelodysplastic Syndromes) [p.11]

### Pre-Treatment Planning

- Early referral to transplant center and/or human leukocyte antigen (HLA) typing for patients with potential hematopoietic cell transplantation (HCT) in the future (except for patients with a major contraindication to HCT) [p.11]

### Imaging Assessment

- Brain CT without contrast, if central nervous system (CNS) hemorrhage suspected --> See AML-B [p.11]
- Brain MRI with and without contrast, if leukemic meningitis suspected --> See AML-B [p.11]
- Consider FDG-PET/CT in individuals with extramedullary disease [p.11]

### CNS Assessment

- Lumbar puncture (LP), if symptomatic --> See AML-B [p.11]

### Cardiac Assessment

- Evaluate myocardial function (echocardiogram or multigated acquisition [MUGA] scan) in patients with a history or symptoms of cardiac disease or prior/planned exposure to cardiotoxic drugs or radiation therapy (RT) to thorax [p.11]

### Patient Counseling and Support

- Counsel patients on infertility risk, with consideration of fertility preservation as appropriate (see NCCN Guidelines for Adolescent and Young Adult (AYA) Oncology) [p.11]

- Consider early integration of palliative care (see NCCN Guidelines for Palliative Care) [p.11]

- Refer to the NCCN Distress Thermometer and Problem List, which includes social determinants of health. See NCCN Guidelines for Distress Management [p.13]

## Diagnostic Pathway

### Multidisciplinary Diagnostic Studies (EVAL-2)

Diagnostic pathway following initial evaluation [p.12]

### APL (Acute Promyelocytic Leukemia) Pathway

If clinical or pathologic features of APL are present:
- Start all-trans retinoic acid (ATRA) upon first suspicion of APL [p.12]
- Early initiation of ATRA may prevent the lethal complication of bleeding [p.12]
- If cytogenetic and molecular testing do not confirm APL, discontinue ATRA and continue treatment as for AML [p.12]
- Consider administration of one dose of intrathecal (IT) chemotherapy (methotrexate, cytarabine, or a combination of these agents) at time of diagnostic LP --> See AML-B [p.13]

### AML Pathway

For patients with confirmed AML:
- To appropriately stratify available intensive therapy options, expedite test results of molecular and cytogenetic analyses for immediately actionable mutations or chromosomal abnormalities [p.12]

**Management of hyperleukocytosis:**
- For patients with hyperleukocytosis uncontrolled with hydroxyurea, cytarabine (0.5–2 g) may be considered prior to receiving diagnostic results [p.12]
- Leukapheresis may be considered for certain patients with symptomatic hyperleukocytosis, though data for benefit are limited [p.12]

**Blood transfusion considerations:**
- For patients who prefer not to receive blood transfusion(s), see AML-D for general considerations and supportive care [p.12]

Next diagnostic step: Risk Group and Induction (Intensive Induction Eligible) (AML-1) --> See [AML-1](aml-risk-group-and-induction-intensive-induction-eligible.md) [p.12]

### Other Diagnostic Considerations

- For suspicion of blastic plasmacytoid dendritic cell neoplasm (BPDCN), see BPDCN-INTRO [p.12]
- For myelodysplastic syndromes (MDS), see NCCN Guidelines for Myelodysplastic Syndromes [p.12]
- For B-cell or T-cell lymphoblastic leukemia/lymphoma, see NCCN Guidelines for Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia [p.12]

## Molecular Testing Notes

A variety of gene mutations are associated with specific prognoses (category 2A) and may guide medical decision-making (category 2B). Other genetic lesions may have therapeutic significance [p.13]

The field of genomics in myeloid malignancies is evolving rapidly. Mutations should be tested in all patients. Multiplex gene panels and targeted NGS analysis are recommended for the ongoing management of AML in various phases of treatment [p.13]

If a test is not available at your institution, consult the pathology team about preserving material from the original diagnostic sample for future testing at an outside reference lab. Peripheral blood may alternatively be used to detect molecular abnormalities in patients with disease with morphologically detectable, circulating leukemic blasts [p.13]

## Heritable Hematologic Malignancy Predisposition

A heritable hematologic malignancy predisposition syndrome may account for cytopenias with or without MDS in some patients (eg, GATA2 deficiency syndrome, Shwachman-Diamond syndrome, telomere biology disorders) [p.13]

Functional laboratory studies and constitutional (germline) genetic testing using large NGS panels, whole exome or whole genome sequencing, and/or laboratory analysis for copy number variants (CNVs) is recommended for certain patients --> See MDS-D and MDS-E in the NCCN Guidelines for Myelodysplastic Syndromes [p.13]

## Classification System Note

At the moment, there are discrepancies between two recognized classification systems. The NCCN Guidelines do not advocate for one over another. Providers should exercise their best clinical judgment related to these discrepancies, and the NCCN Panel recommends classification systems be written to allow for maximal clinical trial participation [p.13]

## Rare Leukemias

When presented with rare cases such as acute leukemias of ambiguous lineage (ALAL), including mixed phenotype acute leukemias (MPAL) (according to 2022 WHO classification), consultation with an experienced hematopathologist is strongly recommended [p.13]

## Clinical Setting

Patients with AML should preferably be cared for at experienced leukemia centers where clinical trials may be more available. Young adults may be eligible for pediatric trials [p.13]

ATRA should be available in all community hospitals, so appropriate therapy can be started promptly [p.13]
