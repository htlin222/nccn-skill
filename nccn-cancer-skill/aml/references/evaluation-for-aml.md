## Diagnosis

### Initial Evaluation for AML [p.11]

**Note:** All recommendations are (Category 2A) unless otherwise indicated.

**Clinical and Laboratory Assessment:**
- History and physical (H&P) [p.11]
- Complete blood count (CBC), platelets, differential, comprehensive metabolic panel (CMP), uric acid, lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) [p.11]
- B12 and folic acid evaluation [p.11]
- Prothrombin time (PT), partial thromboplastin time (PTT), fibrinogen [p.11]

**Bone Marrow and Pathology Studies:**
- Bone marrow (BM) core biopsy and aspirate analyses, including immunophenotyping by immunohistochemistry (IHC) stains + flow cytometry [p.11] (Category 1)
- Analysis of chromosomal structural variations by cytogenetics, fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH), and next generation sequencing (NGS) [p.11] (Category 1)
- Molecular analyses for lesions that allow risk stratification as per ELN 2022 [p.11] (Category 1)
- Comprehensive pathology report, including diagnosis of acute myeloid leukemia (AML) within the context of the diagnostic system used, blast count, cellularity, morphologic dysplasia, and mutation status if available [p.11]

**Genetic and Molecular Testing:**
- Consider additional molecular and genetic testing for heritable hematologic malignancy predisposition in a subset of patients [p.11]
  - See MDS-D and MDS-E in the NCCN Guidelines for Myelodysplastic Syndromes

**Note on Molecular Testing:** A variety of gene mutations are associated with specific prognoses (Category 2A) and may guide medical decision-making (Category 2B). Mutations should be tested in all patients. Multiplex gene panels and targeted NGS analysis are recommended for the ongoing management of AML in various phases of treatment [p.13]. Peripheral blood may be used to detect molecular abnormalities in patients with morphologically detectable, circulating leukemic blasts [p.13].

### Acute Promyelocytic Leukemia (APL) [p.12]

- In patients with clinical or pathologic features of APL, start all-trans retinoic acid (ATRA) upon first suspicion of APL [p.12]
- Early initiation of ATRA may prevent the lethal complication of bleeding [p.12]
- If cytogenetic and molecular testing do not confirm APL, discontinue ATRA and continue treatment as for AML [p.12]
- ATRA should be available in all community hospitals, so appropriate therapy can be started promptly [p.13]

### AML Risk Stratification [p.12]

- To appropriately stratify available intensive therapy options, expedite test results of molecular and cytogenetic analyses for immediately actionable mutations or chromosomal abnormalities [p.12]

## Workup

### Cytoreduction for Hyperleukocytosis [p.12]

- For patients with hyperleukocytosis uncontrolled with hydroxyurea, cytarabine (0.5–2 g) may be considered prior to receiving diagnostic results [p.12]
- Leukapheresis may be considered for certain patients with symptomatic hyperleukocytosis, though data for benefit are limited [p.12]

### Special Considerations [p.11–12]

- Early referral to transplant center and/or human leukocyte antigen (HLA) typing for patients with potential hematopoietic cell transplantation (HCT) in the future (except for patients with a major contraindication to HCT) [p.11]
- For patients who prefer not to receive blood transfusion(s), see AML-D for general considerations and supportive care [p.12]

## Workup — Additional Imaging and Studies [p.11]

### Central Nervous System Evaluation

- Brain CT without contrast, if central nervous system (CNS) hemorrhage suspected [p.11] (Category 1)
- Brain MRI with and without contrast, if leukemic meningitis suspected [p.11] (Category 1)
- Lumbar puncture (LP), if symptomatic [p.11] (Category 1)
- **Note:** Consider administration of one dose of intrathecal (IT) chemotherapy (methotrexate, cytarabine, or a combination of these agents) at time of diagnostic LP [p.13]

### Extramedullary Disease

- Consider FDG-PET/CT in individuals with extramedullary disease [p.11]

### Cardiac Assessment

- Evaluate myocardial function (echocardiogram or multigated acquisition [MUGA] scan) in patients with a history or symptoms of cardiac disease or prior/planned exposure to cardiotoxic drugs or radiation therapy (RT) to thorax [p.11]

### Patient Counseling and Supportive Care

- Counsel patients on infertility risk, with consideration of fertility preservation as appropriate [p.11]
  - See NCCN Guidelines for Adolescent and Young Adult (AYA) Oncology
- Consider early integration of palliative care [p.11]
  - See NCCN Guidelines for Palliative Care
- Refer to the NCCN Distress Thermometer and Problem List, which includes social determinants of health [p.13]
  - See NCCN Guidelines for Distress Management (DIS-A)

## Diagnostic Pathway

### Differential Diagnosis Considerations [p.12]

- **Acute Promyelocytic Leukemia (APL):** --> See APL Classification and Treatment Recommendations (APL-1)
- **AML:** --> See Risk Group and Induction (Intensive Induction Eligible) (AML-1)
- **Myelodysplastic Syndromes (MDS):** --> See NCCN Guidelines for Myelodysplastic Syndromes
- **B-cell or T-cell Lymphoblastic Leukemia/Lymphoma:** --> See NCCN Guidelines for Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia
- **Blastic Plasmacytoid Dendritic Cell Neoplasm (BPDCN):** --> See BPDCN-INTRO

### Classification Systems Note [p.13]

At this time, there are discrepancies between two recognized classification systems for AML. The NCCN Guidelines do not advocate for one over another. Providers should exercise their best clinical judgment related to these discrepancies, and the NCCN Panel recommends classification systems be written to allow for maximal clinical trial participation [p.13].

**For rare cases** such as acute leukemias of ambiguous lineage (ALAL), including mixed phenotype acute leukemias (MPAL) (according to 2022 WHO classification), consultation with an experienced hematopathologist is strongly recommended [p.13].

### Clinical Setting

**Patients with AML should preferably be cared for at experienced leukemia centers where clinical trials may be more available. Young adults may be eligible for pediatric trials.** [p.13]
