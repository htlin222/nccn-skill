## Risk Stratification

### ELN Risk Categories

**Favorable Risk** [p.42]
- t(8;21)(q22;q22.1)/RUNX1::RUNX1T1 (core binding factor [CBF]-AML)
- inv(16)(p13.1q22) or t(16;16)(p13.1;q22)/CBFB::MYH11
- Mutated NPM1 without FLT3-ITD
- bZIP in-frame mutated CEBPA

**Intermediate Risk** [p.42]
- Mutated NPM1 with FLT3-ITD
- Wild-type NPM1 with FLT3-ITD (without adverse-risk genetic lesions)
- t(9;11)(p21.3;q23.3)/MLLT3::KMT2A
- Cytogenetic and/or molecular abnormalities not classified as favorable or adverse

**Poor/Adverse Risk** [p.42]
- t(6;9)(p23.3;q34.1)/DEK::NUP214
- t(v;11q23.3)/KMT2A-rearranged
- t(9;22)(q34.1;q11.2)/BCR::ABL1
- t(8;16)(p11.2;p13.3)/KAT6A::CREBBP
- inv(3)(q21.3q26.2) or t(3;3)(q21.3;q26.2)/GATA2, MECOM(EVI1)
- t(3q26.2;v)/MECOM(EVI1)-rearranged
- -5 or del(5q); -7; -17/abn(17p)
- Complex karyotype or monosomal karyotype
- Mutated ASXL1, BCOR, EZH2, RUNX1, SF3B1, SRSF2, STAG2, U2AF1, and/or ZRSR2
- Mutated TP53

## Intensive Induction Therapy

### Favorable/Intermediate-Risk Groups (Intensive Induction Eligible) [p.29-30]

#### Favorable-Risk AML by Cytogenetics or Molecular Profile

**Preferred** (Category 2A unless noted) [p.29]:
- Standard 7 + 3 (daunorubicin or idarubicin) + gemtuzumab ozogamicin (CD33 positive) [p.29]

**Other Recommended** [p.29]:
- Standard 7 + 3 (daunorubicin or idarubicin)
- FLAG-IDA (fludarabine + cytarabine + granulocyte colony-stimulating factor [G-CSF] - idarubicin) + gemtuzumab ozogamicin (CD33 positive) (Category 2B; use with caution in patients >60 years) [p.29]

**Useful in Certain Circumstances** [p.29]:
- FLAG + gemtuzumab ozogamicin (CD33 positive) (for those ineligible for an anthracycline) (Category 2B)
- Standard 7 + 3 (daunorubicin or idarubicin) + midostaurin (FLT3-ITD or FLT3-TKD) (Category 1)
- Standard 7 + 3 (daunorubicin or idarubicin) + quizartinib (FLT3-ITD only) (Category 1)

#### AML with FLT3 Mutation [p.29]

Same options as favorable-risk AML above [p.29]

---

### Poor/Adverse-Risk Groups (Intensive Induction Eligible) [p.30]

#### Poor-Risk AML Without TP53 Mutation or del(17p) Abnormality

Includes: therapy-related AML (other than CBF-AML), antecedent MDS/chronic myelomonocytic leukemia (CMML), or AML-MRC

**Preferred** [p.30]:
- CPX-351/dual-drug liposomal cytarabine and daunorubicin (Category 1, preferred for age ≥60 years)
- Standard 7 + 3 (daunorubicin or idarubicin) (preferred for age <60 years)

**Other Recommended** [p.30]:
- CPX-351/dual-drug liposomal cytarabine and daunorubicin (for age <60 years)
- Standard 7 + 3 (daunorubicin or idarubicin) (for age ≥60 years)
- Decitabine (days 1-5) + venetoclax
- Azacitidine + venetoclax
- CLIA (cladribine + idarubicin + cytarabine) + venetoclax (Category 2B)
- FLAG-IDA + venetoclax (Category 2B; use with caution in patients >60 years)

#### Poor-Risk AML with TP53 Mutation or del(17p) Abnormality [p.30]

**Preferred** [p.30]:
- Clinical trial (no regimens are considered preferred)

**Other Recommended** [p.30]:
- Standard 7 + 3 (daunorubicin or idarubicin)
- CPX-351/dual-drug liposomal cytarabine and daunorubicin (Category 2B)
- FLAG-IDA (Category 2B; use with caution in patients >60 years)
- Decitabine (days 1-5) + venetoclax
- Azacitidine + venetoclax
- CLAG-M (cladribine + cytarabine + G-CSF - mitoxantrone) (Category 2B)
- CLIA + venetoclax (Category 2B)
- FLAG-IDA + venetoclax (Category 2B; use with caution in patients >60 years)

## Follow-Up and Re-Induction After Cytarabine-Based Induction

### Assessment and Response [p.32]

**Timeline** [p.32]:
- BM aspirate and biopsy: 14-21 days after start of therapy (consider D21 for cytarabine doses >100-200 mg/m²)

**Response Categories** [p.32]:
1. Hypoplasia (cellularity <20%, residual blasts <5%)
   → Await recovery

2. Response (Screening LP per AML-B; Response criteria per AML-I)
   → Consolidation or continue per risk group

3. Lack of response/Primary refractory disease
   → Re-induction options below

### Re-Induction Options [p.32]

**Cytarabine options**:
- Cytarabine monotherapy
- Standard 7 + 3 (daunorubicin or idarubicin) [p.32]
- 5 + 2 (daunorubicin or idarubicin, or mitoxantrone for age ≥60 years)
- Standard 7 + 3 + midostaurin (FLT3-ITD or FLT3-TKD)
- Standard 7 + 3 or 5 + 2 + quizartinib (FLT3-ITD only)

**HMA-based options** [p.32]:
- Azacitidine + venetoclax
- Decitabine + venetoclax

**Other intensified regimens** [p.32]:
- CPX-351/dual-drug liposomal encapsulation of cytarabine and daunorubicin (for therapy-related AML, antecedent MDS/CMML, or AML-MRC; preferred only if given in induction)

**For refractory disease** [p.32]:
- Consider regimens for relapsed/refractory disease, including targeted therapies (see AML-9)

## Lower Intensity Therapy (Intensive Induction Ineligible or Declines) [p.34-35]

### AML with IDH1 Mutation [p.34]

**Preferred** [p.34]:
- Azacitidine + venetoclax (Category 1)
- Azacitidine + ivosidenib (Category 1)

**Other Recommended** [p.34]:
- Ivosidenib

**Useful in Certain Circumstances** [p.34]:
- Low-dose cytarabine (LDAC) + venetoclax (prior HMA exposure)
- Azacitidine or decitabine (contraindication to venetoclax)
- Olutasidenib (Category 2B; not eligible for preferred regimen, not eligible for ivosidenib due to prolonged QTcF)

### AML Without IDH1 Mutation [p.34]

**Preferred** [p.34]:
- Azacitidine + venetoclax (Category 1)
- Decitabine + venetoclax

**Other Recommended** [p.34]:
- Cladribine + LDAC + venetoclax (Category 2B)

**Useful in Certain Circumstances** [p.34]:
- LDAC + venetoclax (prior HMA exposure)
- Azacitidine or decitabine (contraindication to venetoclax)
- LDAC + glasdegib
- LDAC (prior HMA exposure or contraindication to venetoclax)
- Gilteritinib ± azacitidine (FLT3-ITD or FLT3-TKD, not eligible for preferred regimen)
- Enasidenib ± azacitidine (IDH2 mutation, not eligible for preferred regimen)
- Gemtuzumab ozogamicin (CD33 positive, not eligible for preferred regimen)

## Follow-Up After Lower Intensity Induction [p.36]

### Assessment Timeline [p.36]
- BM aspirate and biopsy to document remission status (timing dependent on agent)
- MRD assessment per AML-H

### Response-Based Options [p.36]

**If Response Achieved** [p.36]:
→ Maintenance (AML-7) or Surveillance (AML-8)

**If No Response or Progression** [p.36]:
→ Therapy for Relapsed/Refractory Disease (AML-9) or Best supportive care

## Consolidation Therapy [p.37]

### For Intensive Induction-Eligible Patients [p.37]

#### Favorable-Risk AML / CBF-AML / FLT3-Mutated Favorable-Risk [p.37]

**Treatment options** [p.37]:
- Allogeneic HCT (preferred for FLT3-ITD)
- Cytarabine ± midostaurin (FLT3-ITD or FLT3-TKD)
- Cytarabine ± quizartinib (FLT3-ITD only)

**After consolidation** [p.37]:
→ Allogeneic HCT (if not previously performed) or Maintenance (AML-7) or Surveillance (AML-8)

#### Poor/Adverse-Risk AML [p.37]

**Treatment options** [p.37]:
- Allogeneic HCT (preferred)
- Cytarabine
- CPX-351/dual-drug liposomal encapsulation of cytarabine and daunorubicin (preferred only if given during induction)
- FLAG-IDA (use with caution in patients >60 years; preferred only if given during induction)
- Continuation of lower intensity regimen used for induction (e.g., HMA [azacitidine or decitabine] + venetoclax) [p.37]

#### Intermediate-Risk AML [p.37]

**First consolidation** [p.37]:
- Cytarabine ± gemtuzumab ozogamicin (CD33 positive; only if gemtuzumab ozogamicin given during induction)
- Cytarabine (5 or 7 days) ± (daunorubicin or idarubicin, or mitoxantrone for age ≥60 years)
- Cytarabine + (daunorubicin or idarubicin) + gemtuzumab ozogamicin (CD33 positive; only if given during induction)

**After consolidation** [p.37]:
→ Consider allogeneic HCT, Maintenance (AML-7), or Surveillance (AML-8)

## Maintenance Therapy [p.39]

### For Non-CBF-AML Post Chemotherapy [p.39]

**Eligibility**: Intensive chemotherapy completed, disease in remission, no allogeneic HCT planned

**Preferred** [p.39]:
- Oral azacitidine until progression or unacceptable toxicity (Category 1 for age ≥55 years)

**Useful in Certain Circumstances** [p.39]:
- Azacitidine or decitabine for maximum 12 cycles (unable to receive oral azacitidine)

### Post Allogeneic HCT - FLT3-Mutated AML [p.39]

**In remission with FLT3 mutation history** [p.39]:
- Gilteritinib (FLT3-ITD or FLT3-TKD; preferred for FLT3-ITD in CR1 without MRD negativity pre-transplant)
- Sorafenib (FLT3-ITD only)
- Midostaurin (FLT3-ITD or FLT3-TKD)
- Quizartinib (FLT3-ITD only)

### Post Allogeneic HCT - High-Risk Features [p.39]

**In remission with high-risk features** [p.39]:
- Low-dose decitabine + G-CSF (Category 2B)

### Previous FLT3 Inhibitor [p.39]

**Not pursuing allogeneic HCT** [p.39]:
- Quizartinib (FLT3-ITD only; preferred)
- Midostaurin (FLT3-ITD or FLT3-TKD)

## Surveillance and Relapsed/Refractory Disease [p.40]

### Surveillance Schedule [p.40]

**Post consolidation** (all patients in remission):
- CBC and platelets: every 1-3 months for 2 years, then every 3-6 months up to 5 years
- BM aspirate and biopsy: only if peripheral smear abnormal or cytopenias develop
- Confirm molecular remission and monitor for molecular relapse (per AML-H)

### At First Relapse [p.40]

**Initial steps** [p.40]:
- Donor search initiated concurrently with therapy institution
- Molecular profiling to determine mutation status of actionable genes (IDH1/IDH2, FLT3, KMT2A rearrangements)

**Treatment options** [p.40]:
1. Clinical trial (strongly preferred)
2. Targeted therapy ± combination followed by allogeneic HCT
3. Chemotherapy followed by allogeneic HCT
4. Allogeneic HCT (useful in certain circumstances)
5. Best supportive care

---

## Therapy for Relapsed/Refractory Disease [p.41]

### Targeted Therapy Options [p.41]

**FLT3-ITD mutation** [p.41]:
- Gilteritinib (Category 1)
- HMA (azacitidine or decitabine) + sorafenib
- Quizartinib (Category 2B)

**FLT3-TKD mutation** [p.41]:
- Gilteritinib (Category 1)

**IDH2 mutation** [p.41]:
- Enasidenib

**IDH1 mutation** [p.41]:
- Ivosidenib
- Olutasidenib

**CD33-positive AML** [p.41]:
- Gemtuzumab ozogamicin

**KMT2A rearrangement** [p.41]:
- Revumenib

**NPM1 mutation** [p.41]:
- Revumenib
- Ziftomenib

### Intensive Therapy for Appropriate Patients [p.41]

- Cladribine + cytarabine + G-CSF ± (mitoxantrone or idarubicin)
- Cytarabine ± (daunorubicin or idarubicin or mitoxantrone)
- Fludarabine + cytarabine + G-CSF ± idarubicin ± venetoclax
- Etoposide + cytarabine ± mitoxantrone
- Clofarabine ± cytarabine ± idarubicin
- CLIA (cladribine + idarubicin + cytarabine) + venetoclax (Category 2B)

### Less Intensive Therapy [p.41]

- Azacitidine or decitabine
- LDAC (Category 2B)
- (HMA or LDAC) + venetoclax

## CNS Prophylaxis and Treatment [p.43]

### Screening Indications [p.43]

**Screening LP recommended for asymptomatic patients at first remission with any of**:
- WBC count >40 x 10⁹/L at diagnosis
- Extramedullary disease
- High-risk APL
- Intraparenchymal hemorrhage at diagnosis
- FLT3 mutations
- MLLT3::KMT2A fusion
- Monocytic differentiation
- MPAL

### CNS Treatment Approaches [p.43]

**If CSF positive by morphology or immunotype** [p.43]:
- IT chemotherapy 2x/week until clear, then weekly x 4-6 weeks

**If mass effect or increased intracranial pressure present** [p.43]:
- Radiation therapy followed by IT chemotherapy 2x/week until clear, then weekly x 4-6 weeks
- OR: Cytarabine-based therapy with doses ≥2 g/m² + dexamethasone

### Radiation Therapy General Principles [p.44]

- Isolated extramedullary disease (myeloid sarcoma) or leukemia cutis: treat with systemic therapy
- Local therapy (RT or rarely surgery) may be used for residual or symptomatic disease
- CNS leukemia: RT followed by IT chemotherapy 2x/week until clear, then weekly x 4-6 weeks

## Key Clinical Pearls [p.31, p.35]

**Elevated Blast Counts** [p.31]:
- Risk for tumor lysis and leukostasis
- Measures: leukapheresis, hydroxyurea, single dose cytarabine
- Prompt definitive therapy essential

**Geriatric Assessment** [p.31]:
- Recommended for patients ≥60 years
- Consider performance status, comorbidities for induction tolerance
- Web-based tools available to evaluate CR probability and early death risk

**Gemtuzumab Ozogamicin** [p.31]:
- CD33 threshold not well-defined; may be ≥1% by flow cytometry
- Risk of sinusoidal obstruction syndrome (SOS) if transplant shortly follows administration
- Prior studies used 60-90 day interval between gemtuzumab and HCT

**Venetoclax Combinations** [p.31, p.33]:
- May be continued for patients with clinical improvement (CR/CRi)
- Consider subsequent transplant where appropriate
- Patients with cytopenias in remission: take breaks between cycles

**IDH Inhibitors** [p.35]:
- Response may take 3-5 months
- Increased risk for differentiation syndrome and hyperleukocytosis
- Monitor closely; syndrome can occur months after induction

## Supportive Care

### General Considerations and Supportive Care for Patients with AML Who Prefer Not to Receive Blood Transfusions [p.45]

**General Supportive Care**

- There is no established treatment of AML that does not require the use of blood and blood products for supportive care [p.45]
- Discuss goals of care and understanding of complications without transfusion [p.45]
- For Jehovah's Witnesses, the United States Branch of the Christian Congregation of Jehovah's Witness has a Hospital Liaison Committee that can provide helpful information about bloodless medicine: https://www.jw.org/en/medical-library/hospital-liaison-committee-hlc-contacts/united-states [p.45]
- Clarify acceptance of certain blood products (e.g., cryoprecipitate) under certain circumstances, including discussion of whether stem cells (donor or autologous) will be acceptable [p.45]
- Minimize blood loss (e.g., use of pediatric collection tubes) [p.45]
- Minimize risk of bleeding, including:
  - Consideration for use of oral contraceptive pills or medroxyprogesterone acetate in menstruating individuals [p.45]
  - Proton pump inhibitor, aggressive antiemetic prophylaxis, and stool softeners to reduce risk of gastrointestinal (GI) bleed [p.45]
  - Nasal saline sprays to reduce epistaxis [p.45]
  - Fall precautions, particularly in patients with thrombocytopenia [p.45]
- Avoid concomitant medicines or procedures that can increase the risk of bleeding or myelosuppression [p.45]
- Consider using vitamin K (to potentially reverse coagulopathy) and aminocaproic acid or tranexamic acid in patients at risk of bleeding (e.g., when platelet count drops below 30 x 10^9/L) or for management of bleeding [p.45]
- Consider use of aminocaproic acid rinses for oral bleeding or significant mucositis that could result in bleeding [p.45]
- Consider using acetaminophen to manage fever [p.45]
- Consider iron, folate, and vitamin B12 supplementation if deficient. Iron supplementation may be avoided in someone with excess iron levels [p.45]
- Consider use of erythropoiesis-stimulating agent (ESA), G-CSF, and thrombopoietin (TPO) mimetics after thorough discussion of potential risks, benefits, and uncertainties [p.45]
- Consider bed rest and supplemental oxygenation in patients with severe anemia [p.45]

**Disease-Specific Considerations**

- Test for actionable mutations and consider use of targeted agents instead of intensive chemotherapy, particularly in a non-curative setting [p.45]
- May consider use of less myelosuppressive induction including dose reduction of anthracyclines, and use of non-intensive chemotherapy [p.45]
- Consider referring to centers with experience in bloodless autologous HCT [p.45]

## Induction Therapy

### Intensive Induction Eligible (AML-1, AML-2) [p.46-48]

**Standard 7 + 3 Regimens**

**Standard 7 + 3 (Daunorubicin or Idarubicin) + Gemtuzumab Ozogamicin (CD33 positive)** (Category 2A) [p.46]
- Cytarabine 200 mg/m² continuous infusion x 7 days
- With daunorubicin 60 mg/m² or idarubicin 12 mg/m² x 3 days
- And a single dose of gemtuzumab ozogamicin 3 mg/m² (up to one 4.5-mg vial) given on day 1, 2, 3, or 4; alternatively, three total doses may be given on days 1, 4, and 7 [p.46]

**Standard 7 + 3 (Daunorubicin or Idarubicin)** (Category 2A) [p.46]
- Cytarabine 100 or 200 mg/m² continuous infusion x 7 days
- With daunorubicin 60 mg/m² or 90 mg/m² or idarubicin 12 mg/m² x 3 days [p.46]
- Note: Daunorubicin 60 mg/m² is preferred over 90 mg/m² [p.47]

**FLAG-IDA Regimens**

**FLAG-IDA + Gemtuzumab Ozogamicin (CD33 positive)** (Category 2A) [p.46]
- Fludarabine 30 mg/m² days 2-6
- Cytarabine 2 g/m² over 4 hours starting 4 hours after fludarabine infusion on days 2-6
- Idarubicin 8 mg/m² IV on days 4-6
- G-CSF subcutaneously (SC) daily days 1-7
- Plus a single dose of gemtuzumab ozogamicin 3 mg/m² in first course [p.46]

**FLAG + Gemtuzumab Ozogamicin (CD33 positive)** (Category 2A) [p.46]
- Fludarabine 30 mg/m² days 1-5
- Cytarabine 2 g/m² over 4 hours starting 3.5 hours after fludarabine infusion on days 1-5
- G-CSF SC daily starting day 1 through recovery of ANC to 1 x 10^9/L
- Plus a single dose of gemtuzumab ozogamicin 3 mg/m² on day 1 [p.46]

**FLAG-IDA** (Category 2A) [p.46]
- Fludarabine 30 mg/m² days 2-6
- Cytarabine 2 g/m² over 4 hours starting 4 hours after fludarabine infusion on days 2-6
- Idarubicin 8 mg/m² IV on days 4-6
- G-CSF SC daily days 1-7 [p.46]

## Important Clinical Notes [p.47, p.50, p.51-52]

### Dose Adjustments [p.47, p.50]
- Consider dose adjustments for cytarabine based on age and renal function [p.47]
- Doses of cytarabine ≥2 g/m² should be used with caution in patients ≥60 years and patients with renal failure due to concern for neurotoxicity [p.47]

### Substitutions [p.47]
- In times of fludarabine shortage, cladribine can be substituted for fludarabine [p.47]

### Cardiac Monitoring [p.50]
- For regimens using high cumulative doses of cardiotoxic agents, consider reassessing cardiac function prior to each anthracycline/mitoxantrone-containing course [p.50]

### Reinduction Dosing [p.50]
- If daunorubicin 90 mg/m² was used in induction, the recommended dose for daunorubicin for reinduction prior to count recovery is 45 mg/m² for no more than 2 doses [p.50]
- If idarubicin 12 mg/m² was used for induction, the early reinduction dose should be limited to 10 mg/m² for 1 or 2 doses [p.50]

### Venetoclax Principles [p.48, p.51]
- Patients with cytopenias with disease in remission should take breaks between cycles [p.48, p.51]
- See AML-J for additional principles of venetoclax use with HMA or LDAC [p.48, p.51]

### Special Populations [p.51]
- Azacitidine + ivosidenib regimen is approved for patients with newly diagnosed AML with IDH1 mutation who are: aged >75 years, have baseline ECOG performance status of 2, severe cardiac or pulmonary disease, hepatic impairment with bilirubin >1.5 times the upper limit of normal, CrCl <45 mL/min, or other comorbidity [p.51]

## Supportive Care

### General Measures [p.63]

**Blood Products** [p.63]
- Leukocyte-depleted products should be used for transfusion [p.63]
- All patients with AML are at risk for acute graft-versus-host disease (aGVHD); management should be based on institutional practice/preference. See NCCN Guidelines for Hematopoietic Cell Transplantation [p.63]
- Transfusion thresholds: red blood cell (RBC) counts for hemoglobin ≤7 to 8 g/dL or per institutional guidelines or symptoms of anemia; platelets for patients with platelets <10,000/mcL or with any signs of bleeding [p.63]
- Cytomegalovirus (CMV) screening for potential HCT candidates may be considered [p.63]

**Tumor Lysis Syndrome Prophylaxis** [p.63]
- Hydration with diuresis, and allopurinol or rasburicase [p.63]
- Rasburicase should be considered as initial treatment in patients with rapidly increasing blast counts, high uric acid, or evidence of impaired renal function [p.63]
- Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency should be checked when possible [p.63]. However, it is not always feasible to do so rapidly; if there is high suspicion of G6PD deficiency, caution is necessary as rasburicase may be contraindicated [p.63]

**Central Venous Access Device (CVAD)** [p.63]
- Unless a site-specific contraindication exists, a central venous access device with multiple lumens is recommended to allow the administration of peripherally contraindicated systemic therapies and possibly multiple infusions during higher risk periods of cytopenias related to disease and/or myelosuppressive therapy [p.63]
- CVAD routine care and maintenance should be provided as per institutional policy [p.63]
- CVAD removal and/or replacement should be determined based on individual clinical circumstances [p.63]

**Cytarabine Administration** [p.63]
- Patients receiving doses of cytarabine ≥2 g/m² (particularly those with impaired renal function), or doses of cytarabine 1–1.5 g/m² in patients >60 years of age, are at risk for cerebellar toxicity [p.63]
- Neurologic assessment, including tests for nystagmus, slurred speech, and dysmetria, should be performed before each dose of cytarabine [p.63]
- In patients exhibiting rapidly rising creatinine due to tumor lysis, doses of cytarabine ≥2 g/m² should be discontinued until creatinine normalizes [p.63]
- In patients who develop cerebellar toxicity, cytarabine should be stopped; rechallenge with doses of cytarabine ≥2 g/m² in future treatment cycles should not be attempted [p.63]
- Steroid eye drops should be administered to both eyes 4 times daily for all patients undergoing cytarabine therapy at doses of ≥2 g/m² until 24 hours post completion of cytarabine [p.63]

**Growth Factors and Antimicrobial Support** [p.63]
- Growth factors may be considered as a part of supportive care for post-remission therapy; note that such use may confound interpretation of the BM evaluation [p.63]
- Patients should be off granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF) or G-CSF for a minimum of 7 days before obtaining BM to document remission [p.63]
- Decisions regarding use and choice of antibiotics should be made by individual institutions based on the prevailing organisms and their drug resistance patterns [p.63]
- Posaconazole has been shown to significantly decrease fungal infections when compared to fluconazole and itraconazole [p.63]
- Outcomes with other azoles, such as voriconazole, echinocandins, or amphotericin B, may produce equivalent results; follow NCCN Guidelines for the Prevention and Treatment of Cancer-Related Infections and commensurate with institutional practice for antibiotic stewardship [p.63]

## Monitoring During Intensive Therapy [p.64]

### Induction [p.64]
- Complete blood count (CBC) daily (differential daily or as clinically indicated during chemotherapy and every other day after recovery of ANC >0.5 × 10⁹/L until either normal differential or persistent leukemia is documented); platelets daily while in the hospital until platelet-transfusion independent [p.64]
- Chemistry profile, including electrolytes, liver function test (LFT), blood urea nitrogen (BUN), creatinine, uric acid, and phosphorous, at least daily during active treatment until risk of tumor lysis is past [p.64]
- If the patient is receiving nephrotoxic agents, closer monitoring is required through the period of hospitalization [p.64]
- Consider reduced frequency of labs to 1–2 times per week following completion of chemotherapy based on individual circumstances, such as degree of count recovery, intensity of induction, and/or patient preference [p.64]
- Coagulation panel 1–2 times per week [p.64]
- For patients who have evidence of disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), coagulation parameters including fibrinogen should be monitored daily until resolution of DIC [p.64]
- Bone marrow (BM) aspirate/biopsy 14–21 days after start of therapy to document hypoplasia [p.64]
- If hypoplasia is not documented or indeterminate, repeat biopsy within 7 days to clarify persistence of leukemia [p.64]
- If hypoplasia, then repeat biopsy at time of hematologic recovery to document remission [p.64]
- If cytogenetics were initially abnormal, include cytogenetics as part of the remission documentation [p.64]

### Post-Remission Therapy [p.64]
- Complete blood count and platelets 2 times per week during chemotherapy [p.64]
- Chemistry profile and electrolytes daily during chemotherapy [p.64]
- Outpatient monitoring post chemotherapy: CBC, platelets, differential, and electrolytes 2–3 times per week until recovery [p.64]
- BM aspirate/biopsy only if peripheral blood counts are abnormal or if counts have not recovered within 5 weeks [p.64]
- Patients with AML with high-risk features, including poor-prognosis cytogenetics, therapy-related AML, prior MDS, or possibly 2 or more inductions to achieve a complete remission (CR) are at increased risk for relapse and should be considered for early alternate donor search [p.64]

## Measurable (Minimal) Residual Disease Assessment [p.65-68]

### General Principles [p.65]

**Evidence and Significance** [p.65]
- There is compelling evidence in both children and adults with AML that detectable MRD following achievement of remission is associated with an increased risk of relapse [p.65]
- MRD in AML refers to the presence of leukemic cells below the threshold of detection by conventional morphologic methods [p.65]
- Patients whose disease achieved a CR by morphologic assessment alone can still harbor a large number of leukemic cells in the BM [p.65]
- After completion of therapy, "molecular relapses" can predict hematologic relapses within a 3- to 6-month timeframe [p.65]

**Technical Considerations** [p.65]
- There are distinct differences between diagnostic threshold assessments and MRD assessments [p.65]
- Standard NGS diagnostic tests are not designed for MRD assessment due to inferior sensitivity [p.65]
- With contemporary measurement techniques, the role of MRD in treatment decision-making is evolving [p.65]
- The threshold to define MRD+ and MRD- samples depends on the technique and subgroup of AML [p.65]
- There are commercially available tests that can be used for MRD assessments, as well as further testing methods at certain academic centers [p.65]

**Methodology** [p.65]
- The most frequently used methods for MRD assessment include quantitative molecular assays such as real-time quantitative PCR (RQ-PCR) and multicolor flow cytometry (MFC) assays specifically designed to detect abnormal MRD immunophenotypes [p.65]
- The optimal sample for initial MRD assessment is a first, dedicated pull of the BM aspirate [p.65]
- The quality of the sample is of paramount importance to have reliable evaluation [p.65]
- Once MRD-negative remission by BM is achieved, peripheral blood can be utilized for surveillance of MRD for PML::RAR alpha, NPM1, CBFB::MYH11, and RUNX1::RUNX1T1 [p.65]
- Mutations associated with clonal hematopoiesis of indeterminate potential (CHIP) and aging (i.e., DNMT3A, TET2, potentially ASXL1) are not considered reliable markers for MRD and are difficult to differentiate in routine practice [p.65]

### Methods of Testing [p.65]

**Flow Cytometry** [p.65]
- If using flow cytometry to assess MRD, it is recommended that a specific MRD assay that incorporates both different from normal and leukemia-associated immunophenotypes (LAIPs) assessment is utilized [p.65]
- Most importantly, it should be interpreted by an experienced hematopathologist, preferably at the same laboratory as diagnostic flow cytometry for consistency [p.65]
- Utilization of an assay with minimum limit of detection of ≤10⁻³ is recommended [p.65]

**Molecular** [p.65]
- The Panel recommends RQ-PCR for detection of PML::RAR alpha, NPM1, CBFB::MYH11, and RUNX1::RUNX1T1 [p.65]
- Utilization of an assay with minimum limit of detection of ≤10⁻⁴ is recommended [p.65]
- For detection of FLT3-ITD, the Panel recommends a highly sensitive, NGS-based, targeted, deep-sequencing assay with a sensitivity level of ≤10⁻⁵ [p.65]
- The Panel does not routinely recommend other NGS–based assays to detect mutated genes (targeted sequencing, 20–50 genes per panel) for MRD assessment due to inferior sensitivity [p.65]

### Timing of MRD Assessment [p.66]

**Monitoring Frequency** [p.66]
- While there is a paucity of data regarding the optimal frequency of MRD monitoring, the Panel recommends monitoring at the following time points [p.66]:
  - Upon achievement of morphologic remission [p.66]
  - At the time of and following allogeneic HCT [p.66]
  - For NPM1-mutated, CBFB::MYH11, and RUNX1::RUNX1T1 AML: after two cycles of intensive chemotherapy (e.g., one cycle of induction and one cycle of consolidation) and for serial monitoring every 6 to 12 weeks for 24 months [p.66]
  - For PML:RAR alpha: every 3 months for at least 24 months for patients with APL [p.66]
- Additional time points should be guided by the regimen used [p.66]
- Patients receiving venetoclax-based low-intensity therapy may achieve MRD-negative remission at later time points, including a significant minority after 4–6 cycles of therapy; therefore, repeat testing may be obtained [p.66]
- NPM1 molecular MRD is strongly prognostic in patients receiving venetoclax-based low-intensity therapy [p.66]
- The Panel supports monitoring NPM1 by RQ-PCR every 6 to 12 weeks in the BM or peripheral blood in patients receiving venetoclax-based low-intensity therapy [p.66]

**Repeat Testing** [p.66]
- It is recommended that repeat testing should be performed within a short interval (e.g., 1 month) if there is no hematologic relapse and a BM biopsy and aspirate should be performed [p.66]
- Confirmation of MRD positivity in this setting is an indicator of high risk of relapse and consideration should be made for allogeneic HCT, clinical trial, consolidation strategies, targeted therapy where appropriate, or therapy for relapsed/refractory disease as clinically indicated [p.66]

### Management of MRD Positivity [p.66-67]

**Persistent MRD Positivity After Induction/Consolidation** [p.66]
- For NPM1, CBFB::MYH11, and RUNX1::RUNX1T1-mutated AML, if MRD is persistently positive after induction and/or consolidation, consider a clinical trial or alternative therapies, including allogeneic HCT [p.66]

**FLT3-ITD Post-Transplant Management** [p.66]
- The Panel recommends post-transplant maintenance gilteritinib for patients with BM FLT3-ITD MRD ≥10⁻⁶ by a highly sensitive, NGS-based, targeted, deep-sequencing assay with a sensitivity level of ≤10⁻⁵ pre- or post-allogeneic HCT [p.66]

**General Approach to MRD Positivity** [p.66]
- As there is no clear optimal management of MRD positivity, the Panel favors a clinical trial for MRD positivity if available [p.66]
- If a clinical trial, allogeneic HCT, targeted therapy, or other consolidation strategies are not pursued, the Panel notes that venetoclax-based low-intensity therapy has demonstrated high rates of MRD reduction in MRD and oligoblastic AML [p.66]

### Relevant Clinical Trial Results [p.67]

**NPM1-Mutated AML** [p.67]
- In a study of patients with NPM1-mutated AML, proceeding to allogeneic HCT in CR1 led to a significant improvement in overall survival (OS) for patients with MRD-detectable disease but no benefit for patients with MRD-negative disease [p.67]

**FLT3-ITD Maintenance Strategies** [p.67]
- In a planned subgroup analysis of the MORPHO trial, post-transplant maintenance therapy with gilteritinib was found to improve relapse-free survival (RFS) (P = .0105) and overall survival (OS) (P = .0731) for patients with BM FLT3-ITD MRD ≥10⁻⁶ by PCR-NGS pre- or post-allogeneic HCT [p.67]
- The SORMAIN trial revealed that post-transplant sorafenib maintenance had the greatest impact for patients with FLT3-ITD AML who achieved MRD negativity pre-transplant and those with detectable MRD post-transplant [p.67]

**Azacitidine Maintenance** [p.67]
- In a subgroup analysis of the QUAZAR AML-001 trial, oral azacitidine maintenance therapy significantly improved survival among patients who were ineligible for allogeneic HCT with NPM1-mutated AML in CR1 following intensive chemotherapy, with either detectable or undetectable MRD by MFC [p.67]

**Venetoclax + HMA Combination** [p.67]
- In an analysis of patients treated with azacitidine + venetoclax on the VIALE-A trial, those who achieved composite CR (CRc) with MRD <10⁻³ by MFC had improvements in median duration of remission and event-free survival (EFS), as well as a significant improvement in OS compared to patients with MRD ≥10⁻³ [p.67]

## Response Criteria Definitions for Acute Myeloid Leukemia [p.69]

**Morphologic Leukemia-Free State (MLFS)** [p.69]
- BM <5% blasts in an aspirate with spicules; at least 200 cells must be enumerated [p.69]
- No blasts with Auer rods or persistence of extramedullary disease [p.69]
- If there is a question of residual leukemia, a BM aspirate/biopsy should be repeated in one week [p.69]
- A BM biopsy/aspirate should be performed [p.69]

**Complete Remission (CR)** [p.69]
- Morphologic CR [p.69]
  - ANC ≥1 × 10⁹/L (blasts <5%) [p.69]
  - Platelets ≥100 × 10⁹/L (blasts <5%) [p.69]
- CR without MRD (CRMRD-) [p.69]
  - If studied pretreatment, CR with negativity for a genetic marker by reverse transcriptase PCR (RT-PCR) or CR with negativity by MFC [p.69]
  - Sensitivity varies by marker and method used; analyses should be done in experienced laboratories [p.69]
  - Molecular CR – molecular studies negative [p.69]
- CR with partial hematologic recovery (CRh), defined as <5% blasts in the BM, no evidence of disease (NED), and partial recovery of peripheral blood counts (platelets ≥50 × 10⁹/L and ANC ≥0.5 × 10⁹/L) [p.69]
- CRi – All CR criteria and transfusion independence but with persistence of neutropenia (<1 × 10⁹/L) or thrombocytopenia (<100 × 10⁹/L) [p.69]
- Responses less than CR may still be meaningful depending on the therapy [p.69]

**Partial Remission (PR)** [p.69]
- Decrease of at least 50% in the percentage of blasts to 5% to 25% in the BM aspirate and meeting hematologic criteria for CR, as noted above [p.69]

**Relapse Following CR** [p.69]
- Reappearance of leukemic blasts in the peripheral blood or the finding of ≥5% blasts in the BM, not attributable to another cause (e.g., BM regeneration after consolidation therapy) or extramedullary relapse [p.69]

**Primary Refractory** [p.69]
- Inability to attain CR, CRh, or CRi following exposure to at least 2 courses of intensive induction therapy [p.69]

**MRD Relapse** [p.69]
- Conversion from MRD negativity to MRD positivity, independent of method of testing [p.69]

**Note on Response Criteria** [p.69]
- These response criteria were defined in the context of intensive chemotherapy regimens, and may not be predictive of outcomes for patients who receive other therapies [p.69]

## Principles of Venetoclax Use with HMA or LDAC [p.70-73]

### General Recommendations [p.70]

- Recommend consultation with a high-volume tertiary care/academic medical center throughout course of treatment [p.70]
- There are ongoing studies into the addition of a third agent to the combinations described in this section, and participation in clinical trials is encouraged [p.70]
- The maximum number of cycles for these regimens is unknown, and treatment may continue as long as tolerated and effective [p.70]
- Consider antimicrobial prophylaxis per institutional protocol for patients with newly diagnosed disease [p.70]
- Drug-drug interactions occur with strong or moderate CYP3A4 inhibitors and may require dose adjustment; refer to venetoclax prescribing information and consult with a pharmacist for potential drug interactions [p.70]
- Strong or moderate CYP3A4 inducers (e.g., carbamazepine, phenytoin, rifampin) should be avoided [p.70]

### Prognostic Risk Classification for Patients Ineligible for Intensive Therapy Treated with HMA/Venetoclax [p.70]

**Higher Benefit** [p.70]
- Negative for mutations in TP53, KRAS, NRAS, and FLT3-ITD [p.70]

**Intermediate Benefit** [p.70]
- Mutations in KRAS and/or NRAS and/or FLT3-ITD; negative for mutation in TP53 [p.70]

**Lower Benefit** [p.70]
- Mutation in TP53 [p.70]

### Tumor Lysis Syndrome (TLS) Risk/Monitoring During Cycle 1 [p.71]

- To decrease the risk of severe TLS, aim to achieve WBC count of <25 × 10⁹/L with hydroxyurea/leukapheresis prior to starting therapy [p.71]
- Inpatient treatment is strongly recommended during first cycle of treatment, especially through dose escalation [p.71]
- Intrapatient dose escalation for venetoclax with HMA is 100 mg, 200 mg, and 400 mg daily on days 1–3 [p.71]
- Intrapatient dose escalation for venetoclax with LDAC is 100 mg, 200 mg, 400 mg, and 600 mg daily on days 1–4 [p.71]
- Concomitant interacting medications may require changes to these dosages [p.71]
- Recommend treatment with allopurinol or other uric acid–lowering agent until no further risk of TLS [p.71]
- Monitor blood chemistries every 6–8 hours after initiation for a total of 24 hours following maximum dose escalation; if within normal limits, recheck once daily and continue monitoring until no further risk of TLS [p.71]
- Aggressively monitor and manage electrolyte imbalances [p.71]

### Therapy for Patients with Substantial Comorbidities [p.71]

- Consider initiating therapy with a reduced duration of venetoclax (e.g., 7 days) [p.71]

### Therapy for Patients with Relapsed/Refractory Disease [p.71]

- Consider the same TLS and intrapatient dose escalation measures as described for "TLS Risk/Monitoring During Cycle 1" [p.71]
- Consider the same recommendations for early BM biopsy and cytopenia mitigation plan as per AML-J 3 of 4 [p.71]
- Recommend antifungal prophylaxis if indicated [p.71]

### Cycle Management [p.72-73]

**Cycle 1** [p.72]
- Venetoclax + HMA or LDAC [p.72]
- BM aspirate and biopsy days 21–28 [p.72]
- If CR achieved: Delay cycle 2 for up to 14 days to allow recovery of ANC >0.5 × 10⁹/L and platelets >50 × 10⁹/L; G-CSF is encouraged [p.72]
- If MLFS or CRi achieved: Start cycle 2 without delay [p.72]
- If lack of response: Continue therapy up to 4 cycles; if no response, see Therapy for Relapsed/Refractory Disease [p.72]
- Start cycle 2 on day 29 or later if marrow results not available on day 29 [p.72]

**Cycle 2** [p.72]
- Start cycle 2 [p.72]
- Consider reduction of venetoclax duration to 21 days [p.72]
- BM aspirate and biopsy days 21–28 [p.72]
- If MLFS or better response: Continue therapy [p.72]
- If lack of response: See Therapy for Relapsed/Refractory Disease [p.72]

**Cycle 3 and Beyond** [p.72]
- Delay next cycle for up to 14 days to allow recovery of ANC >0.5 × 10⁹/L and platelets >50 × 10⁹/L [p.72]
- G-CSF is encouraged [p.72]
- Consider further reduction in venetoclax duration (e.g., 14 days, 7 days, or 5 days) if cytopenias recur in subsequent cycles [p.72]
- Dose modifications to HMA or LDAC may also be considered in accordance with their label [p.72]
