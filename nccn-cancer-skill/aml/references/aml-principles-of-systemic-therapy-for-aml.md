## APL Classification and Treatment Recommendations

For confirmed APL patients: [p.14]

- **Low-risk disease (WBC count ≤10 x 10⁹/L):** Treatment per APL-2 induction protocol [p.14]
- **High-risk disease (WBC count >10 x 10⁹/L):** [p.14]
  - Without cardiac issues: Treatment per APL-3 induction protocol [p.14]
  - With cardiac issues (low ejection fraction or QTcF prolongation): Treatment per APL-4 induction protocol [p.14]

**Important Notes:** [p.14]
- Therapy-related APL is treated the same as de novo APL. FLT3 inhibitors are not recommended for FLT3-positive APL. [p.14]
- For patients with APL being treated at a community center, collaboration with a center with expertise has been shown to reduce induction mortality. [p.14]
- Refer to Principles of Supportive Care for APL (APL-A) for cardiac issues management. [p.14]

## APL Treatment Induction (Low Risk) - APL-2

### Induction Therapy

**Preferred Regimens:**

- **ATRA + daily arsenic trioxide (Category 1)** [p.15]
  - ATRA 45 mg/m² in 2 divided doses daily
  - Arsenic trioxide 0.15 mg/kg IV daily

- **ATRA + intermittent arsenic trioxide (Category 1)** [p.15]
  - ATRA 45 mg/m² in 2 divided doses daily for 60 days or until achievement of complete remission (CR)
  - Arsenic trioxide 0.3 mg/kg IV on days 1-5 of week 1 and 0.25 mg/kg twice weekly during weeks 2-8

**Useful in Certain Circumstances** (if arsenic is not available or contraindicated):

- **ATRA + idarubicin** [p.15]
  - ATRA 45 mg/m² in 2 divided doses daily
  - Idarubicin 12 mg/m² on days 2, 4, 6, 8

- **ATRA + gemtuzumab ozogamicin** [p.15]
  - ATRA 45 mg/m² in 2 divided doses daily
  - Single dose of gemtuzumab ozogamicin 6 or 9 mg/m² on day 5

### Consolidation Therapy

**ATRA + daily arsenic trioxide (Category 1)** [p.15]
- Arsenic trioxide 0.15 mg/kg IV daily 5 days/week for 4 weeks every 8 weeks for a total of 4 cycles
- ATRA 45 mg/m² for 2 weeks every 4 weeks for a total of 7 cycles

**ATRA + intermittent arsenic trioxide (Category 1)** [p.15]
- First 3 consolidation cycles = 56-day cycles:
  - ATRA 45 mg/m²/day PO in 2 divided doses daily on days 1-14 and 29-42 (2 weeks on followed by 2 weeks off)
  - Arsenic trioxide 0.3 mg/kg on days 1-5 of week 1 followed by 0.25 mg/kg twice weekly during weeks 2-4
- 4th consolidation cycle = 28-day cycle:
  - ATRA 45 mg/m²/day PO in 2 divided doses daily on days 1-14
  - Arsenic trioxide 0.3 mg/kg on days 1-5 of week 1 followed by 0.25 mg/kg twice weekly during weeks 2-4

## APL Treatment Induction (High Risk) - APL-3

### Induction Therapy

**Preferred Regimens:**

- **ATRA + idarubicin + arsenic trioxide** [p.23]
  - ATRA 45 mg/m² (days 1-36, 2 divided doses daily)
  - Age-adjusted idarubicin 6-12 mg/m² on days 2, 4, 6, 8
  - Arsenic trioxide 0.15 mg/kg (days 9-36 as 2 hour IV infusion)

- **ATRA + daily arsenic trioxide + gemtuzumab ozogamicin** [p.23]
  - ATRA 45 mg/m² in 2 divided doses daily
  - Arsenic trioxide 0.15 mg/kg/day IV
  - Single dose of gemtuzumab ozogamicin 6 or 9 mg/m² on day 1, 2, 3, or 4

- **ATRA + intermittent arsenic trioxide + gemtuzumab ozogamicin (Category 1)** [p.23]
  - ATRA 45 mg/m² in 2 divided doses daily for 60 days or until achievement of CR
  - Arsenic trioxide 0.3 mg/kg IV on days 1-5 of week 1 and 0.25 mg/kg twice weekly during weeks 2-8
  - Single dose of gemtuzumab ozogamicin 6 or 9 mg/m² on day 1, 2, 3, or 4

**Useful in Certain Circumstances** (if arsenic is not available or contraindicated during induction):

- **ATRA + daunorubicin x 4 days + cytarabine** [p.24]
  - ATRA 45 mg/m² in 2 divided doses daily
  - Daunorubicin 50 mg/m² x 4 days (IV days 3-6)
  - Cytarabine 200 mg/m² x 7 days (IV days 3-9)

- **ATRA + daunorubicin x 3 days + cytarabine** [p.24]
  - ATRA 45 mg/m² in 2 divided doses daily
  - Daunorubicin 60 mg/m² x 3 days
  - Cytarabine 200 mg/m² x 7 days

- **ATRA + idarubicin** [p.24]
  - ATRA 45 mg/m² in 2 divided doses daily
  - Idarubicin 12 mg/m² on days 2, 4, 6, 8 or on days 2, 4, 6 for those aged >70 years

### Consolidation Therapy

Consolidation regimens vary by initial induction therapy. [p.24-25]

## APL Treatment Induction (High Risk) in Patients with Cardiac Issues - APL-4

### For Patients with Low Ejection Fraction or Prolonged QTcF

**Preferred Regimens:**

- **ATRA + gemtuzumab ozogamicin** [p.25]
  - ATRA 45 mg/m² in 2 divided doses daily
  - Single dose of gemtuzumab ozogamicin 6 or 9 mg/m² on day 1

- **ATRA + intermittent arsenic trioxide + gemtuzumab ozogamicin (Category 1)** [p.25]
  - ATRA 45 mg/m² in 2 divided doses daily for 60 days or until achievement of CR
  - Arsenic trioxide 0.3 mg/kg on days 1-5 of week 1 and 0.25 mg/kg twice weekly in weeks 2-8
  - Single dose of gemtuzumab ozogamicin 6 or 9 mg/m² on day 1

**Other Options:**

- **ATRA + daunorubicin x 3 days + cytarabine** [p.25]
  - ATRA 45 mg/m² in 2 divided doses daily
  - Daunorubicin 60 mg/m² x 3 days
  - Cytarabine 200 mg/m² x 7 days

- **ATRA + idarubicin** [p.25]
  - ATRA 45 mg/m² in 2 divided doses daily
  - Idarubicin 12 mg/m² on days 2, 4, 6, 8 or on days 2, 4, 6 for those aged >70 years

## APL Post-Consolidation Therapy - APL-5

### Monitoring

Perform PCR on blood sample at completion of consolidation to document molecular remission. [p.18]

Prior practice guidelines have recommended monitoring blood by PCR every 3 months for 2 years to detect molecular relapse. For patients with high-risk disease or those who had long interruptions during consolidation, this monitoring is recommended. For patients with low-risk disease in molecular remission at completion of consolidation, risk of relapse is low and monitoring may not be necessary outside the setting of a clinical trial. [p.18]

### If PCR Positive at Confirmation

To confirm PCR positivity, a second blood sample should be done in 2-4 weeks in a reliable laboratory. [p.18]

If molecular relapse is confirmed by a second positive test, treat as first relapse (APL-6). [p.18]

If the second test is negative, frequent monitoring (every 3 months for 2 years) is strongly recommended to confirm that the test remains negative. [p.18]

## APL Therapy for Relapse - APL-6

### First Relapse (Morphologic or Molecular)

**For No Prior Exposure to Arsenic Trioxide or Early Relapse (<6 months) after ATRA + Anthracycline-containing Regimen:** [p.19]

- **Arsenic trioxide ± ATRA ± gemtuzumab ozogamicin** [p.19]

**For Early Relapse (<6 months) after ATRA and Arsenic Trioxide (no anthracycline):** [p.19]

- **Anthracycline-based regimen** (as per APL-3) or **gemtuzumab ozogamicin** [p.19]

### If Second Remission Achieved

**If PCR negative (by BM):** [p.19]
- For transplant candidates: **Matched sibling or alternative donor HCT** [p.19]
- For non-transplant candidates: **Arsenic trioxide consolidation** (total of 6 cycles) [p.19]

**If PCR positive (by BM):** [p.19]
- For transplant candidates: **Matched sibling or alternative donor HCT** [p.19]
- For non-transplant candidates: **Clinical trial** or **Matched sibling or alternative donor HCT** [p.19]

### If No Remission Achieved

Consider CNS prophylaxis with intrathecal (IT) chemotherapy (methotrexate or cytarabine). [p.19]

### For Late Relapse (≥6 months) after Arsenic Trioxide-containing Regimen

- **Arsenic trioxide ± ATRA ± (anthracycline or gemtuzumab ozogamicin)** [p.19]

## Principles of Supportive Care for APL - APL-A

### General Considerations

Early mortality is related to bleeding, differentiation syndrome, or infection. [p.21]

### Clinical Coagulopathy Management

- **Aggressive platelet transfusion support** to maintain platelets ≥50 x 10⁹/L [p.21]
- **Fibrinogen replacement** with cryoprecipitate and fresh frozen plasma to maintain a level >150 mg/dL and PT and PTT close to normal values [p.21]
- Monitor daily until coagulopathy resolves [p.21]
- **Avoid use of tunneled catheter or port-a-cath** [p.21]

### Leukapheresis

Leukapheresis is not routinely recommended in patients with a high WBC count in APL because of the difference in leukemia biology; however, in life-threatening cases with leukostasis that is not responsive to other modalities, leukapheresis can be considered with caution. [p.21]

### Leukocytosis Management

Hydroxyurea can be used to treat leukocytosis in individuals with low-risk disease who experience a rise in WBC count after treatment with an ATRA/arsenic trioxide based regimen. [p.21]

### APL Differentiation Syndrome

Maintain a high index of suspicion of APL differentiation syndrome (fever often associated with increasing WBC count >10 x 10⁹/L, usually at initial diagnosis or relapse; shortness of breath; hypoxemia; pleural or pericardial effusions). [p.21]

Close monitoring of volume overload and pulmonary status is indicated. [p.21]

**At first signs or symptoms of respiratory compromise (hypoxemia, pulmonary infiltrates, pericardial or pleural effusions):** [p.21]
- Initiate **dexamethasone** 10 mg BID for 3-5 days with a taper over 2 weeks
- Consider interrupting ATRA therapy until hypoxia resolves

**For patients at high risk (WBC count >10 x 10⁹/L) or receiving ATRA/arsenic trioxide-based regimen:** [p.21]
- Initiate prophylaxis with corticosteroids:
  - **Prednisone** 0.5 mg/kg day 1
  - OR **Dexamethasone** 10 mg every 12 hours
- Taper the steroid dose over a period of several days

**If patient develops differentiation syndrome:** [p.21]
- Change prednisone to **dexamethasone 10 mg every 12 hours** until count recovery or risk of differentiation has abated
- **Hydroxyurea** can be used to treat leukocytosis associated with differentiation syndrome
- In difficult-to-treat cases, use **anthracycline (daunorubicin or idarubicin) or gemtuzumab ozogamicin**

### Arsenic Trioxide Monitoring

**Prior to initiating therapy:** [p.21]
- Electrocardiogram (ECG) for prolonged QTc interval assessment
- Serum electrolytes and creatinine

**During therapy** (weekly during induction therapy and before each course of post-remission therapy): [p.21-22]
- Minimize use of drugs that may prolong QT interval
- Maintain K and Mg concentrations within middle or upper range of normal
- In patients with prolonged QTc interval >500 milliseconds, correct electrolytes and proceed with caution
- QTcF is recommended; however, in settings where QTcF corrections are unavailable, a cardiology consult may be appropriate for patients with prolonged QTc

### Other Supportive Measures

- **Myeloid growth factors** should not be used during induction. They may be considered during consolidation in selected cases (life-threatening infections, signs/symptoms of sepsis); however, there are no outcomes data regarding the prophylactic use of growth factors in consolidation. [p.22]

- **Antiviral prophylaxis** against varicella-zoster virus for duration of treatment may be appropriate. [p.22]

## General Treatment Principles

### Dosing Considerations

- Data suggest that lower doses of ATRA (25 mg/m²) in divided doses until clinical remission may be used in children and adolescents. [p.15, 24, 25]

- The optimal dosing of arsenic trioxide for patients with obesity is unknown, though retrospective studies suggest there may be a role for dose capping. [p.15, 24, 25]

### Important Clinical Practices

- Begin prophylaxis with prednisone; the optimal duration of steroid prophylaxis is unknown. If differentiation syndrome develops, change to dexamethasone. [p.15, 24, 25]

- Several groups have published large trials with excellent outcomes. However, to achieve the expected results, one needs to use the regimen consistently through all components and not mix induction from one trial with consolidation from another. [p.15, 24, 25]

- BM aspirate and biopsy should be performed to document <5% blasts and no abnormal promyelocytes before proceeding with consolidation. [p.15]

- For regimens using high cumulative doses of cardiotoxic agents, consider reassessing cardiac function prior to each anthracycline/mitoxantrone-containing course. [p.15, 24, 25]

- For all patients with high-risk APL, consider LP before proceeding with consolidation. [p.16]

- For CNS prophylaxis, consider intrathecal (IT) chemotherapy as an option (e.g., 2 doses for each consolidation cycle). [p.25]

## Intensive Induction Eligible - Favorable/Intermediate-Risk Groups

### Treatment Induction

For favorable-risk AML by cytogenetics (core binding factor [CBF]-AML) or favorable-risk AML by molecular mutation profile, with or without FLT3 mutation: [p.29]

**Preferred:** [p.29]
- **Standard 7+3 (daunorubicin or idarubicin) + gemtuzumab ozogamicin (Category 1)** (CD33 positive) [p.29]

**Other Recommended:** [p.29]
- **Standard 7+3 (daunorubicin or idarubicin)** [p.29]
- **FLAG-IDA (fludarabine + cytarabine + G-CSF + idarubicin)** + gemtuzumab ozogamicin (CD33 positive) (Category 2B; use with caution in patients >60 years) [p.29]

**Useful in Certain Circumstances:** [p.29]
- **FLAG** + gemtuzumab ozogamicin (CD33 positive) (for those ineligible for an anthracycline) (Category 2B) [p.29]
- **Standard 7+3 (daunorubicin or idarubicin) + midostaurin** (FLT3-ITD or TKD) (Category 1) [p.29]
- **Standard 7+3 (daunorubicin or idarubicin) + quizartinib** (FLT3-ITD only) (Category 1) [p.29]

### Intermediate-Risk or Poor/Adverse-Risk Groups (Without TP53 Mutation)

For poor-risk AML without TP53 mutation or del(17p) abnormality, including therapy-related AML (other than CBF-AML), antecedent MDS/CMML, or cytogenetic or molecular changes consistent with MDS: [p.30]

**Preferred:** [p.30]
- **CPX-351/dual-drug liposomal cytarabine and daunorubicin (Category 1, preferred for age ≥60 years)** [p.30]
- **Standard 7+3 (daunorubicin or idarubicin) (preferred for age <60 years)** [p.30]

**Other Recommended:** [p.30]
- **CPX-351** (for age <60 years) [p.30]
- **Standard 7+3 (daunorubicin or idarubicin)** (for age ≥60 years) [p.30]
- **Decitabine (days 1-5) + venetoclax** [p.30]
- **Azacitidine + venetoclax** [p.30]
- **CLIA (cladribine + idarubicin + cytarabine) + venetoclax (Category 2B)** [p.30]
- **FLAG-IDA + venetoclax** (Category 2B; use with caution in patients >60 years) [p.30]

### Poor-Risk AML with TP53 Mutation or del(17p) Abnormality

- **Clinical trial (strongly preferred)** [p.30]

**Other Recommended:** [p.30]
- **Standard 7+3 (daunorubicin or idarubicin)** [p.30]
- **CPX-351** (Category 2B) [p.30]
- **FLAG-IDA** (Category 2B; use with caution in patients >60 years) [p.30]
- **Decitabine (days 1-5) + venetoclax** [p.30]
- **Azacitidine + venetoclax** [p.30]
- **CLAG-M (cladribine + cytarabine + G-CSF + mitoxantrone) (Category 2B)** [p.30]
- **CLIA + venetoclax (Category 2B)** [p.30]
- **FLAG-IDA + venetoclax** (Category 2B; use with caution in patients >60 years) [p.30]

## Follow-Up and Re-Induction After Cytarabine-Based Induction - AML-3

Consider follow-up BM aspirate and biopsy 14-21 days after start of therapy. [p.32]

### Response Outcomes

- **If hyoplasia:** Await recovery [p.32]
- **If no response/primary refractory:** [p.32]
  - Allogeneic HCT
  - Cytarabine (if not previously used) ± anthracycline (daunorubicin or idarubicin)
  - Therapy for Relapsed/Refractory Disease (AML-9)
  - Best supportive care

- **If residual disease:** Consider regimens for relapsed/refractory disease (AML-9) [p.32]

### Re-Induction Options (if lack of response)

## Important Clinical Notes

### Patient Eligibility Considerations

Patients with elevated blast counts are at risk for tumor lysis and organ dysfunction secondary to leukostasis. Measures to rapidly reduce WBC count include leukapheresis, hydroxyurea, and/or single dose cytarabine. Prompt institution of definitive therapy is essential. [p.31]

Poor performance status, comorbid medical conditions, and age influence ability to tolerate standard induction therapy. [p.31]

### Venetoclax Use

Venetoclax combination regimens may be continued for patients whose disease demonstrates clinical improvement (CR/CRi), with consideration of subsequent transplant, where appropriate. [p.31]

Patients with cytopenias with disease in remission should take breaks between cycles. [p.31]

### HMA Exposure

Patients whose disease has progressed to AML from MDS after significant exposure to HMAs may be less likely to derive benefit from continued treatment with HMAs compared to HMA-naïve patients. Alternative treatment strategies should be considered. [p.31]

### Cardiac Monitoring

For regimens using high cumulative doses of cardiotoxic agents, consider reassessing cardiac function prior to each anthracycline/mitoxantrone-containing course. [p.33]

### Dosing Considerations

Doses of cytarabine ≥2 g/m² should be used with caution in patients ≥60 years and patients with renal failure due to concern for neurotoxicity. [p.38]

## General Considerations and Supportive Care for Patients with AML Who Prefer Not to Receive Blood Transfusions

There is no established treatment of AML that does not require the use of blood and blood products for supportive care. [p.45]

Key supportive care considerations: [p.45]
- Discuss goals of care and understanding of complications without transfusion
- For Jehovah's Witnesses, contact the Hospital Liaison Committee: https://www.jw.org/en/medical-library/hospital-liaison-committee-hlc-contacts/united-states
- Clarify acceptance of certain blood products (e.g., cryoprecipitate) under certain circumstances
- Minimize blood loss (e.g., use of pediatric collection tubes)
- Minimize risk of bleeding with specific interventions
- Avoid concomitant medicines that increase bleeding or myelosuppression risk
- Consider vitamin K and aminocaproic acid or tranexamic acid for bleeding prevention
- Consider acetaminophen to manage fever
- Consider iron, folate, and vitamin B12 supplementation if deficient
- Consider ESA, G-CSF, and TPO mimetics after thorough discussion
- Consider bed rest and supplemental oxygenation in severe anemia

### Disease-Specific Considerations

- **Test for actionable mutations** and consider targeted agents instead of intensive chemotherapy, particularly in a non-curative setting [p.45]
- May consider less myelosuppressive induction including dose reduction of anthracyclines and non-intensive chemotherapy [p.45]
- Consider referring to centers with experience in bloodless autologous HCT [p.45]

## Important Dosing and Safety Considerations

### Cytarabine Dosing

- Doses of cytarabine ≥2 g/m² should be used with caution in patients ≥60 years and patients with renal failure due to concern for neurotoxicity [p.47, 50, 57]
- For re-induction, no data are available to show superiority with 1-2 g/m² of cytarabine compared to doses ≥2 g/m² [p.50]
- There is no evidence that cytarabine doses ≥2 g/m² are superior to doses 1-2 g/m² in patients with AML with intermediate-risk cytogenetics [p.54]

### Quizartinib Dosing

During cycle 1, quizartinib should be dosed at 26.5 mg PO once daily on days 1-14 if QTcF is ≤450 ms. If QTcF remains ≤450 ms on day 15, the dose should be increased to 53 mg PO daily for the remainder of the 28-day cycle. The 26.5 mg dose should be maintained if QTcF was >500 ms at any point during induction or consolidation. [p.55, 56]

### Cardiac Monitoring

For regimens using high cumulative doses of cardiotoxic agents, consider reassessing cardiac function prior to each anthracycline/mitoxantrone-containing course. [p.47, 50, 54]

### Daunorubicin Dosing

- ECOG reported a significant increase in CR rates and OS using daunorubicin 90 mg/m² x 3 days versus 45 mg/m² x 3 days in patients <60 years of age [p.47]
- CR rates and 2-year OS in patients between 60 and 65 years of age treated with daunorubicin 90 mg/m² is comparable to idarubicin 12 mg/m²; higher-dose daunorubicin did not benefit patients >65 years of age [p.47]
- Daunorubicin 60 mg/m² is preferred over 90 mg/m² [p.47]

### Fludarabine Shortage

In times of fludarabine shortage, cladribine can be substituted for fludarabine. [p.47]
