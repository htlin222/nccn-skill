## Overview of MRD in AML

There is compelling evidence in both children and adults with AML that detectable MRD following achievement of remission is associated with an increased risk of relapse [p.65, ref.1].

**MRD Definition**: MRD in AML refers to the presence of leukemic cells below the threshold of detection by conventional morphologic methods [p.65]. Patients whose disease achieved a CR by morphologic assessment alone can still harbor a large number of leukemic cells in the bone marrow (BM) [p.65, ref.2].

**Molecular Relapses**: After completion of therapy, "molecular relapses" can predict hematologic relapses within a 3- to 6-month timeframe [p.65, ref.1].

### Key Considerations

- There are distinct differences between diagnostic threshold assessments and MRD assessments. Standard NGS diagnostic tests are not designed for MRD assessment due to inferior sensitivity [p.65].

- With contemporary measurement techniques, the role of MRD in treatment decision-making is evolving [p.65].

- The threshold to define MRD+ and MRD- samples depends on the technique and subgroup of AML [p.65].

- There are commercially available tests that can be used for MRD assessments, as well as further testing methods at certain academic centers [p.65].

### MRD Assessment Methods

The most frequently used methods for MRD assessment include [p.65]:
- Quantitative molecular assays such as real-time quantitative PCR (RQ-PCR)
- Multicolor flow cytometry (MFC) assays specifically designed to detect abnormal MRD immunophenotypes [p.65, ref.2]

### Sample Quality and Collection

The optimal sample for initial MRD assessment is a first, dedicated pull of the BM aspirate [p.65]. The quality of the sample is of paramount importance to have reliable evaluation [p.65].

Once MRD-negative remission by BM is achieved, peripheral blood can be utilized for surveillance of MRD for [p.65]:
- PML::RAR alpha
- NPM1 [p.65, ref.3]
- CBFB::MYH11
- RUNX1::RUNX1T1 [p.65, ref.4]

### CHIP and Aging-Associated Mutations

Mutations associated with clonal hematopoiesis of indeterminate potential (CHIP) and aging (i.e., DNMT3A, TET2, potentially ASXL1) are not considered reliable markers for MRD and are difficult to differentiate in routine practice [p.65, ref.5-7].

## Methods of Testing

### Flow Cytometry

If using flow cytometry to assess MRD, it is recommended that [p.65]:
- A specific MRD assay that incorporates both different from normal and leukemia-associated immunophenotypes (LAIPs) assessment is utilized [p.65]
- It is interpreted by an experienced hematopathologist, preferably at the same laboratory as diagnostic flow cytometry for consistency [p.65]
- Utilization of an assay with minimum limit of detection of ≤10⁻³ is recommended [p.65]

### Molecular Assays

**RQ-PCR**: The Panel recommends RQ-PCR for detection of [p.65]:
- PML::RAR alpha
- NPM1 [p.65, ref.3]
- CBFB::MYH11
- RUNX1::RUNX1T1 [p.65, ref.4]
- Utilization of an assay with minimum limit of detection of ≤10⁻⁴ is recommended [p.65]

**FLT3-ITD Detection**: For detection of FLT3-ITD, the Panel recommends a highly sensitive, NGS-based, targeted, deep-sequencing assay with a sensitivity level of ≤10⁻⁵ [p.65].

**Other NGS Assays**: The Panel does not routinely recommend other NGS–based assays to detect mutated genes (targeted sequencing, 20–50 genes per panel) for MRD assessment due to inferior sensitivity [p.66, ref.5-6].

## Timing of MRD Assessment

### Recommended Monitoring Time Points

While there is a paucity of data regarding the optimal frequency of MRD monitoring, the Panel recommends monitoring at the following time points [p.66]:

- Upon achievement of morphologic remission [p.66, ref.5-7]
- At the time of and following allogeneic HCT [p.66, ref.8]
- **For NPM1-mutated, CBFB::MYH11, and RUNX1::RUNX1T1 AML**: after two cycles of intensive chemotherapy (e.g., one cycle of induction and one cycle of consolidation) and for serial monitoring every 6 to 12 weeks for 24 months [p.66, ref.3, 9-11]
- **For PML:RAR alpha**: every 3 months for at least 24 months for patients with APL [p.66]
- Additional time points should be guided by the regimen used [p.66, ref.4]

### Special Considerations for Venetoclax-Based Therapy

Patients receiving venetoclax-based low-intensity therapy may achieve MRD-negative remission at later time points, including a significant minority after 4–6 cycles of therapy. Therefore, repeat testing may be obtained [p.66].

NPM1 molecular MRD is strongly prognostic in patients receiving venetoclax-based low-intensity therapy. The Panel supports monitoring NPM1 by RQ-PCR every 6 to 12 weeks in the BM or peripheral blood in patients receiving venetoclax-based low-intensity therapy [p.66, ref.12].

## Management of MRD Positivity

### Initial Assessment of MRD Positivity

It is recommended that repeat testing should be performed within a short interval (e.g., 1 month) if there is no hematologic relapse and a BM biopsy and aspirate should be performed [p.66].

Confirmation of MRD positivity in this setting is an indicator of high risk of relapse and consideration should be made for [p.66]:
- Allogeneic HCT
- Clinical trial
- Consolidation strategies
- Targeted therapy where appropriate
- Therapy for relapsed/refractory disease as clinically indicated [p.66]

### MRD Positivity After Induction/Consolidation

For NPM1, CBFB::MYH11, and RUNX1::RUNX1T1-mutated AML, if MRD is persistently positive after induction and/or consolidation, consider [p.66]:
- Clinical trial
- Alternative therapies
- Allogeneic HCT [p.66]

### FLT3-ITD Post-Transplant Management

The Panel recommends post-transplant maintenance gilteritinib for patients with BM FLT3-ITD MRD ≥10⁻⁶ by a highly sensitive, NGS-based, targeted, deep-sequencing assay with a sensitivity level of ≤10⁻⁵ pre- or post-allogeneic HCT [p.66, ref.13].

### When Clinical Trial Not Available

As there is no clear optimal management of MRD positivity, the Panel favors a clinical trial for MRD positivity if available [p.66]. If a clinical trial, allogeneic HCT, targeted therapy, or other consolidation strategies are not pursued, the Panel notes that venetoclax-based low-intensity therapy has demonstrated high rates of MRD reduction in MRD and oligoblastic AML [p.66, ref.14].

## Relevant Clinical Trial Results

### NPM1-Mutated AML and Allogeneic HCT

In a study of patients with NPM1-mutated AML, proceeding to allogeneic HCT in CR1 led to a significant improvement in overall survival (OS) for patients with MRD-detectable disease but no benefit for patients with MRD-negative disease [p.67, ref.15].

### FLT3-ITD Maintenance Strategies

In a planned subgroup analysis of the MORPHO trial, post-transplant maintenance therapy with gilteritinib was found to improve relapse-free survival (RFS) (P = .0105) and OS (P = .0731) for patients with BM FLT3-ITD MRD ≥10⁻⁶ by PCR-NGS pre- or post-allogeneic HCT [p.67, ref.13].

The SORMAIN trial revealed that post-transplant sorafenib maintenance had the greatest impact for patients with FLT3-ITD AML who achieved MRD negativity pre-transplant and those with detectable MRD post-transplant [p.67, ref.16].

### NPM1-Mutated AML Maintenance with Oral Azacitidine

In a subgroup analysis of the QUAZAR AML-001 trial, oral azacitidine maintenance therapy significantly improved survival among patients who were ineligible for allogeneic HCT with NPM1-mutated AML in CR1 following intensive chemotherapy, with either detectable or undetectable MRD by MFC [p.67, ref.17].

### Azacitidine + Venetoclax (VIALE-A Trial)

In an analysis of patients treated with azacitidine + venetoclax on the VIALE-A trial, those who achieved composite CR (CRc) with MRD <10⁻³ by MFC had improvements in median duration of remission and event-free survival (EFS), as well as a significant improvement in OS compared to patients with MRD ≥10⁻³ [p.67, ref.18].
