# Sensitive/Inclusive Language Usage -- Evidence Summary

<!-- Source: NCCN Breast Cancer v2.2026, pages 129-129 -->
<!-- Chunk: sensitiveinclusive-language-usage-manuscript -->

## Summary of Key Evidence

- NCCN Guidelines strive to use language that advances the goals of equity, inclusion, and representation [p.129]
- Guidelines endeavor to use person-first, non-stigmatizing language that is anti-racist, anti-classist, anti-misogynist, anti-ageist, anti-ableist, and anti-weight-biased [p.129]
- Guidelines are inclusive of individuals of all sexual orientations and gender identities [p.129]
- NCCN Guidelines incorporate non-gendered language, focusing on organ-specific recommendations, which is both more accurate and more inclusive [p.129]
- Terms such as men, women, female, and male are used when citing statistics, recommendations, or data from organizations or sources that do not use inclusive terms [p.129]
- Most studies do not report how sex and gender data are collected and use these terms interchangeably or inconsistently [p.129]

## Special Considerations

### Data Collection and Representation

If sources do not differentiate gender from sex assigned at birth or organs present, the information is presumed to predominantly represent cisgender individuals [p.129]. NCCN encourages researchers to collect more specific data in future studies and organizations to use more inclusive and accurate language in their future analyses [p.129]
