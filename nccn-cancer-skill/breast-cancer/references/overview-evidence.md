## Summary of Key Evidence

- Breast cancer is the most common malignancy in females in the United States [p.129]
- Lung cancer is the only cancer causing more deaths than breast cancer [p.129]
- The American Cancer Society estimated 313,510 Americans would be diagnosed with breast cancer and 42,780 would die of disease in the United States in 2024 [p.129]
- NCCN Guidelines address clinical management of multiple breast cancer subtypes including carcinoma in situ, invasive breast cancer, Paget disease, Phyllodes tumor, inflammatory breast cancer (IBC), male breast cancer, and breast cancer during pregnancy [p.129]
- Therapeutic options for patients with noninvasive or invasive breast cancer are complex and varied [p.129]

## Guidelines Development and Update Methodology

The NCCN Clinical Practice Guidelines for Breast Cancer are continuously updated by a multidisciplinary panel of representatives from NCCN Member Institutions with breast cancer-focused expertise in medical oncology, surgical oncology, radiation oncology, pathology, reconstructive surgery, and patient advocacy [p.129]

Literature search prior to guideline updates was performed using PubMed database with search terms: Breast Cancer, Breast Neoplasms, DCIS, Inflammatory Breast Cancer, OR Phyllodes [p.129]. Results were limited to studies in humans published in English, confined to article types including Clinical Trial Phase III, Clinical Trial Phase IV, Guideline, Randomized Controlled Trial, Meta-Analysis, Systematic Reviews, and Validation Studies [p.129]

## Special Considerations

### Inclusive Language

NCCN Guidelines strive to use person-first, non-stigmatizing, anti-racist, anti-classist, anti-misogynist, anti-ageist, anti-ableist, and anti-weight-biased language that is inclusive of individuals of all sexual orientations and gender identities [p.129]. Guidelines incorporate non-gendered language, focusing on organ-specific recommendations [p.129]. Terms such as men, women, female, and male are used when citing statistics, recommendations, or data from external organizations or sources that do not use inclusive terms [p.129]
