## Clinical Definition

**Menopause** is the permanent cessation of menses and includes a profound and permanent decrease in ovarian estrogen synthesis. [p.86]

Menopause is usually a clinical diagnosis made after ≥12 months of amenorrhea. Natural menopause is experienced between ages 42–58 years. [p.86]

---

## Menopausal Status Assessment

### Rationale for Determination

Determination of menopausal status may be required to guide selection of endocrine therapy for breast cancer. [p.86]

### Impact of Breast Cancer Treatments on Ovarian Function

Breast cancer treatments may affect ovarian function and menses. [p.86]

**Chemotherapy-induced amenorrhea:** In those who are premenopausal at the beginning of chemotherapy and who develop chemotherapy-induced amenorrhea, ovarian function may still be intact despite amenorrhea or may resume over time. The likelihood of ovarian function resuming after chemotherapy is higher among those aged <40 years. [p.86]

**Tamoxifen:** May cause amenorrhea without inducing menopause in premenopausal individuals. [p.86]

**Ovarian suppression (OFS):** Induces amenorrhea and reduces ovarian estrogen synthesis without causing permanent menopause. [p.86]

---

## Diagnostic Criteria

### Limitations of Amenorrhea Alone

Twelve months of amenorrhea alone is insufficient to diagnose menopause with chemotherapy-induced amenorrhea or with tamoxifen ± ovarian suppression. [p.86]

### Role of Hormonal Testing

FSH and estradiol levels are used to support the diagnosis of menopause; however, clear criteria to guide interpretation of FSH and estradiol in this population is lacking. [p.86]

**Tamoxifen effects on testing:** Tamoxifen may alter FSH levels, limiting its utility in determination of menopausal status. [p.86]

**Serial assessment:** FSH and estradiol should be repeated serially to ensure menopausal status in patients with breast cancer with chemotherapy-induced amenorrhea. [p.86]

---

## Evidence-Based Criteria for Menopause Diagnosis in Breast Cancer Patients

Evidence-based criteria for the diagnosis of menopause in patients with breast cancer are lacking. Clinical trials in breast cancer have utilized a variety of definitions of menopause. [p.86]

### Reasonable Diagnostic Criteria

Reasonable criteria for determining menopause in patients with breast cancer include **any of the following:** [p.86]

1. **Prior bilateral oophorectomy** [p.86]

2. **Age ≥60 years** [p.86]

3. **Age <60 with amenorrhea for ≥12 months** (in the absence of prior chemotherapy, receipt of tamoxifen, toremifene, or ovarian suppression) **and** estradiol and FSH in the postmenopausal range [p.86]

4. **Age <60 years: chemotherapy-induced amenorrhea for ≥12 months** with FSH and estradiol in postmenopausal range on serial assessments [p.86]

5. **Age <60 years: on tamoxifen** with FSH and estradiol level in postmenopausal range [p.86]

---

## Special Considerations

### Patients on Ovarian Suppression

Menopausal status **cannot be determined** in those receiving OFS (ovarian suppression/ablation). [p.86]
