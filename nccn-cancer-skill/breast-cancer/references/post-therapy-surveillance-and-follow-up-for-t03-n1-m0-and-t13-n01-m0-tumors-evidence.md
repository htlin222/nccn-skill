# Post-Therapy Surveillance and Follow-up for T0–3, N1, M0 and T1–3, N0–1, M0 Tumors -- Evidence Summary

<!-- Source: NCCN Breast Cancer v2.2026, pages 179-181 -->

## Summary of Key Evidence

- Post-therapy follow-up should be optimally performed by members of the treatment team with regular history/physical examinations every 4–6 months for the first 5 years after primary therapy and annually thereafter [p.179]
- Annual mammography is the appropriate frequency for surveillance; no clear advantage to shorter interval imaging after breast-conserving surgery (BCS) and radiation [p.179]
- In the absence of clinical signs and symptoms suggestive of recurrent disease, routine laboratory or imaging studies for metastasis screening are not necessary and do not provide advantage in survival [p.179]
- Breast MRI in follow-up may be considered in patients with high lifetime risk (>20%) of developing a second primary breast cancer [p.179]
- Patients on adjuvant aromatase inhibitors (AIs) or with therapy-induced ovarian failure should have bone mineral density monitoring at baseline and periodically [p.180]
- Healthy lifestyle modifications including ideal body weight (BMI 20–25), physical activity, and high fruit/vegetable consumption are associated with improved breast cancer outcomes [p.180–181]

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Physical Examination and Imaging

Regular history and physical examinations should be performed every 4–6 months for the first 5 years after primary therapy and annually thereafter [p.179]. Mammography should be performed annually [p.179].

After BCS followed by radiation, the NCCN Panel agrees with the American Society for Radiation Oncology (ASTRO) "Choosing Wisely" recommendations released in 2014 [p.179]. These recommendations state that "annual mammograms are the appropriate frequency for surveillance of breast cancer patients who have had BCS and RT with no clear advantage to shorter interval imaging" [p.179]. Patients should wait 6–12 months after completion of radiation to begin annual mammogram surveillance; suspicious findings on physical examination or surveillance imaging might warrant shorter intervals [p.179]. Any imaging of reconstructed breast is not indicated [p.179].

### Surveillance for Metastasis

According to the NCCN Panel, in the absence of clinical signs and symptoms suggestive of recurrent disease, laboratory or imaging studies to screen for metastasis are not necessary [p.179]. The routine performance of alkaline phosphatase tests and liver function tests are not included in the guidelines [p.179]. The Panel notes no evidence to support the use of "tumor markers" for breast cancer, and routine bone scans, CT scans, MRI scans, PET scans, or ultrasound examinations in the asymptomatic patient provide no advantage in survival or ability to palliate recurrent disease and are therefore not recommended [p.179].

### Breast MRI for Contralateral Breast Cancer Risk

The use of breast MRI in follow-up of patients with prior breast cancer is undefined and may be considered as an option in patients with high lifetime risk (>20% based on models largely dependent on family history) of developing a second primary breast cancer [p.179]. Rates of contralateral breast cancer after either BCS or mastectomy have been reported to be increased in patients with BRCA1/2 mutations when compared with patients with sporadic breast cancer [p.179].

## Endocrine Therapy Management

### Tamoxifen-Related Monitoring

Patients with intact uteri who are taking adjuvant tamoxifen should have yearly gynecologic assessments and rapid evaluation of any vaginal spotting because of the risk of tamoxifen-associated endometrial carcinoma in postmenopausal patients [p.179]. The performance of routine endometrial biopsy or ultrasonography in the asymptomatic woman is not recommended; neither test has demonstrated utility as a screening test [p.179]. The vast majority of patients with tamoxifen-associated uterine carcinoma have early vaginal spotting [p.179].

### Aromatase Inhibitor Use in Premenopausal Patients

If an adjuvant AI is considered in patients with amenorrhea following treatment, baseline levels of estradiol and gonadotropin followed by serial monitoring of these hormones should be performed if endocrine therapy with an AI is initiated [p.179]. Bilateral oophorectomy assures postmenopausal status in young patients with therapy-induced amenorrhea and may be considered prior to initiating therapy with an AI in a young woman [p.179].

### Management of Hot Flashes and Drug Interactions

Symptom management for patients on adjuvant endocrine therapies often requires treatment of hot flashes and concurrent depression [p.179–180]. Venlafaxine, a serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor (SNRI), has been studied and is an effective intervention in decreasing hot flashes [p.180].

There is evidence suggesting that concomitant use of tamoxifen with certain selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) (e.g., paroxetine, fluoxetine) may decrease plasma levels of endoxifen, an active metabolite of tamoxifen [p.180]. These SSRIs/SNRIs may interfere with the enzymatic conversion of tamoxifen to endoxifen by inhibiting CYP2D6 [p.180]. However, mild CYP2D6 inhibitors such as citalopram, escitalopram, sertraline, and venlafaxine appear to have no or only minimal effect on tamoxifen metabolism [p.180].

### Medication Adherence

Follow-up includes assessment of patient adherence to ongoing medication regimens such as endocrine therapies [p.180]. Predictors of poor adherence include the presence of side effects and incomplete understanding by the patient of the benefits of regular administration [p.180]. The Panel recommends implementation of simple strategies to enhance patient adherence, such as direct questioning during office visits and brief, clear explanations on the value of taking medication regularly and the therapeutic importance of longer durations of endocrine therapy [p.180].

## Special Considerations

### Lymphedema Management

Lymphedema is a common complication after treatment for breast cancer [p.180]. Factors associated with increased risk of lymphedema include extent of axillary surgery, axillary radiation, infection, and patient obesity [p.180]. The Panel recommends educating patients on lymphedema, monitoring for lymphedema, and referring for lymphedema management as needed [p.180].

### Reproductive and Pregnancy Considerations

Many young patients treated for breast cancer maintain or regain premenopausal status following treatment [p.180]. For these patients, the NCCN Panel discourages the use of hormonal birth control methods, regardless of the hormone receptor status of the tumor [p.180]. Alternative birth control methods are recommended, including intrauterine devices, barrier methods, and, for those with no intent of future pregnancy, tubal ligation or vasectomy [p.180].

Breastfeeding during endocrine or chemotherapy treatment is not recommended by the NCCN Panel because of risks to the infant [p.180]. Breastfeeding after BCS for breast cancer is not contraindicated; however, lactation from an irradiated breast may not be possible, or may occur only with diminished capacity [p.180].

### Bone Health Monitoring

Patients on an adjuvant AI or who experience ovarian failure secondary to treatment should have monitoring of bone health with a bone mineral density determination at baseline and periodically thereafter [p.180]. The use of estrogen, progesterone, or selective estrogen receptor modulators to treat osteoporosis or osteopenia in patients with breast cancer is discouraged [p.180]. The use of a bisphosphonate is generally the preferred intervention to improve bone mineral density [p.180].

A single phase 3 study, ABCSG12, demonstrated improved outcomes with the addition of zoledronic acid in premenopausal patients receiving endocrine therapy with ovarian suppression [p.180]. Use of bisphosphonates in such patients and in other subgroups remains controversial [p.180]. Denosumab has been shown to significantly reduce fractures in postmenopausal patients receiving adjuvant AI therapy and improves bone mineral density [p.180].

Optimal duration of bisphosphonate therapy has not been established [p.180]. Factors to consider for duration of anti-osteoporosis therapy include bone mineral density, response to therapy, and risk factors for continued bone loss or fracture [p.180]. Patients treated with a bisphosphonate should undergo a dental examination with preventive dentistry prior to initiating therapy and should take supplemental calcium and vitamin D [p.180].

### Lifestyle and Survivorship

Evidence suggests that a healthy lifestyle may lead to better breast cancer outcomes [p.180]. A nested case-control study of 369 patients with ER-positive tumors who developed a second primary breast cancer compared with 734 matched control patients showed an association between obesity (body mass index [BMI] ≥30), smoking, and alcohol consumption and contralateral breast cancer [p.180].

A prospective study of 1,490 patients diagnosed with stage I–III breast cancer showed an association between high fruit and vegetable consumption, physical activity, and improved survivorship, regardless of obesity [p.180–181]. There is emerging evidence that obesity is associated with poorer outcomes for certain subtypes of breast cancers [p.181].

The Women's Intervention Nutrition group randomized patients with early-stage breast cancer to an intervention group and a control group [p.181]. The intervention consisted of eight one-on-one visits with a registered dietitian trained on a low-fat eating plan [p.181]. Overall survival (OS) analysis showed no significant difference between the two study arms (17% for the intervention vs. 13.6% without); however, subgroup analysis showed that those with ER- and PR-negative disease who were part of the intervention group saw a 54% improvement in OS [p.181].

The NCCN Panel recommends an active lifestyle and ideal body weight (BMI 20–25) for optimal overall health and breast cancer outcomes, as there are reports of proven benefits of exercise and active lifestyle during and after treatment [p.181].

For management of issues related to survivorship including late/long-term effects of cancer and its treatment, refer to the NCCN Guidelines for Survivorship [p.181].
