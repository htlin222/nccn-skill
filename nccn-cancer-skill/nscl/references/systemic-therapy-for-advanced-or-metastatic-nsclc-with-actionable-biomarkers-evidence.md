## Summary of Key Evidence

Multiple classes of therapies are available for the treatment of advanced or metastatic NSCLC, including targeted therapy, immunotherapy, and chemotherapy [p.200]. Real-world retrospective data indicate that the availability of biomarker testing results prior to treatment initiation is associated with longer overall survival in patients with advanced nonsquamous NSCLC [p.200]. Targeted therapies with a first-line indication are generally recommended as initial therapy for patients with advanced or metastatic NSCLC and certain oncogenic drivers, regardless of PD-L1 levels [p.200].

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### EGFR Exon 19 Deletion or L858R Mutation Positive

**Background**: EGFR is altered in a subset of patients with NSCLC. The two most common EGFR mutations are exon 19 deletions and L858R, representing approximately 85% to 90% of all EGFR mutations in NSCLC [p.200].

**FLAURA Trial**: A phase 3 randomized trial assessed first-line therapy with osimertinib compared with either erlotinib or gefitinib in 556 patients with metastatic NSCLC and EGFR exon 19 deletion or L858R mutation [p.201]. The median progression-free survival (PFS) was longer with osimertinib than erlotinib or gefitinib (18.9 vs. 10.2 months). The median duration of response was longer with osimertinib compared with erlotinib or gefitinib (17.2 vs. 8.5 months) [p.201].

**FLAURA2 Trial**: A phase 3 randomized study investigated first-line therapy with osimertinib in combination with chemotherapy (pemetrexed and either cisplatin or carboplatin) versus osimertinib monotherapy in 557 patients with advanced NSCLC with EGFR exon 19 deletion or L858R mutation [p.201]. Adenocarcinoma histology was reported in 99% of the patient population. The median investigator-assessed PFS was longer in the osimertinib plus chemotherapy group than in the osimertinib monotherapy group (25.5 vs. 16.7 months). The median overall survival was 47.5 months among patients who received osimertinib plus chemotherapy and 37.5 months among those who received osimertinib monotherapy (hazard ratio [HR] 0.77; P = .02) [p.201]. The number of grade 3 adverse events (AEs) was higher with osimertinib plus chemotherapy than with osimertinib monotherapy, primarily driven by known chemotherapy-related AEs [p.201].

**MARIPOSA Trial**: A phase 3 randomized trial assessed amivantamab-vmjw (a bispecific antibody against EGFR and HGF receptor) in combination with the third-generation EGFR TKI lazertinib (n = 429) versus single-agent osimertinib (n = 429) as first-line therapy in patients with EGFR-mutated locally advanced or metastatic NSCLC [p.201]. The median PFS was 23.7 months in the lazertinib + amivantamab group compared with 16.6 months for the osimertinib group. The objective response rates between the two groups were similar (86% vs. 85%). The final median overall survival was significantly longer in the lazertinib + amivantamab group than in the osimertinib group (not estimable vs. 36.7 months; HR, 0.75; P = .005) [p.201]. There was a higher rate of discontinuation due to treatment-related AEs with lazertinib + amivantamab compared with osimertinib (10% vs. 3%). Venous thromboembolic AEs were reported more frequently with lazertinib + amivantamab than with osimertinib (37% vs. 9%) [p.201].

A subgroup of the MARIPOSA trial (n = 216) received single-agent lazertinib as first-line treatment, with a median PFS of 18.5 months [p.201].

**MARIPOSA-2 Trial**: This phase 3 randomized trial evaluated amivantamab-vmjw in combination with chemotherapy (carboplatin and pemetrexed) as subsequent therapy compared with chemotherapy in patients with advanced NSCLC and EGFR exon 19 deletion or L858R mutation whose disease progressed on or after osimertinib monotherapy [p.201-202]. The median PFS was longer for the amivantamab-vmjw plus chemotherapy group than the chemotherapy group (6.3 vs. 4.2 months). The objective response rate was 64% with amivantamab-vmjw plus chemotherapy versus 36% with chemotherapy alone. The most common grade 3 or higher AEs were neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, anemia, and leukopenia. The median overall survival was numerically longer with amivantamab + chemotherapy than chemotherapy alone; however, this difference was not statistically significant (17.7 vs. 15.3 months; HR, 0.73; P = .039) [p.202].

**CHRYSALIS Study (EGFR-mutant subset)**: A phase 1 study assessed amivantamab-vmjw in combination with lazertinib as subsequent therapy following progression on osimertinib in a cohort of 45 patients with advanced EGFR exon 19 deletion or L858R mutation-positive NSCLC [p.202]. The overall response rate was 36%, with a median PFS of 4.9 months. In this cohort, 80% of patients experienced rash-related AEs [p.202].

**Datopotamab Deruxtecan (EGFR-mutant subset)**: Efficacy and safety was evaluated in a pooled analysis of the phase 2 TROPION-Lung05 and phase 3 randomized TROPION-Lung01 trials that included 117 patients with EGFR mutation-positive advanced or metastatic NSCLC who received prior targeted therapy and platinum-based chemotherapy [p.202]. The most common mutations were exon 19 deletion (51%) and L858R (32%); T790M was identified in 27% of patients. The confirmed objective response rate was 43% and the median overall survival was 15.6 months. Similar efficacy outcomes were reported between patients previously treated with osimertinib and the overall study population [p.202]. Grade 3 or higher treatment-related AEs were reported in 23% of patients. Oral mucositis and stomatitis were among the most common AEs of special interest (69%). Ocular surface events (any grade) were reported in 32% of patients, while drug-related interstitial lung disease or pneumonitis occurred in 4% [p.202].

### EGFR Exon 20 Insertion Mutation Positive

**Background**: EGFR exon 20 insertion mutations occur in approximately 2% of patients with NSCLC and 4% to 12% of patients with EGFR mutations [p.205]. Treatment with typical EGFR TKIs has generally not been associated with disease response [p.205].

**PAPILLON Trial**: A phase 3 randomized trial assessed first-line amivantamab plus chemotherapy (pemetrexed and carboplatin) versus chemotherapy alone in 308 patients with advanced NSCLC with EGFR exon 20 insertion mutations [p.205]. The median PFS was longer in the amivantamab plus chemotherapy group than in the chemotherapy alone group (11.4 vs. 6.7 months). The objective response rate was higher for those who received amivantamab plus chemotherapy than those treated with chemotherapy only (73% and 47%). Based on the interim overall survival analysis, the hazard ratio for amivantamab plus chemotherapy versus chemotherapy alone was 0.67 (P = .11). The most common AEs reported with amivantamab plus chemotherapy were hematologic effects and skin-related EGFR-related toxic events [p.205].

**CHRYSALIS Trial (exon 20 insertion subset)**: A phase 1 study assessed subsequent therapy with amivantamab in 81 patients with EGFR exon 20 insertion-positive metastatic NSCLC who had received one or more previous lines of therapy [p.205]. The overall response rate was 40%, with 3 complete responses. The median PFS was 8.3 months. Common treatment-related AEs included cutaneous reactions, infusion-related reactions, and paronychia; common grade 3 to 4 AEs included hypokalemia, pulmonary embolism, neutropenia, diarrhea, and rash [p.205].

**Sunvozertinib**: An oral EGFR TKI granted accelerated approval by the FDA for patients with locally advanced or metastatic NSCLC with EGFR exon 20 insertion mutations whose disease has progressed on or after platinum-based chemotherapy [p.205]. Efficacy as subsequent therapy was evaluated in the phase 2 WU-KONG1B trial [p.205-206]. All patients previously received platinum-based chemotherapy; approximately 14% previously received amivantamab. The objective response rate was approximately 46% among the three cohorts evaluated. The disease control rate was approximately 90%. Twelve-month overall survival rate ranged from 62.1% to 73.7% among the three cohorts. Commonly reported treatment-related AEs included diarrhea, skin rash, and increased creatine phosphokinase [p.206].

**Datopotamab Deruxtecan (exon 20 insertion subset)**: Also a subsequent therapy option based on the pooled analysis of the TROPION-Lung05 and TROPION-Lung01 trials [p.206]. The patient population included just 5 patients with EGFR exon 20 insertion mutations in the analysis, and some experienced a treatment response [p.206].

### KRAS G12C Mutation Positive

**Background**: KRAS G12C is an activating mutation present in approximately 25% of patients with adenocarcinomas in a North American population [p.206]. Patients with advanced or metastatic NSCLC and KRAS mutations are likely to derive benefit from immunotherapy-based regimens in the first-line setting [p.206].

**CodeBreaK 200 Trial**: A randomized phase 3 trial compared subsequent therapy with sotorasib to treatment with docetaxel in 345 patients with KRAS G12C mutation-positive advanced NSCLC who had previously received platinum-based chemotherapy plus a PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitor [p.206]. Median PFS was 5.6 months in patients receiving sotorasib compared with 4.5 months in those receiving docetaxel. The median overall survival was 10.6 months in the sotorasib group versus 11.3 months in the docetaxel group; no statistically significant difference was reported. The most common treatment-related AEs associated with sotorasib that were grade 3 or higher included diarrhea and increases in ALT or AST levels [p.206-207].

**KRYSTAL-12 Trial**: A phase 3 trial assessed adagrasib versus docetaxel in 453 patients with locally advanced or metastatic NSCLC with KRAS G12C mutations who previously received platinum-based chemotherapy and a PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitor [p.207]. The median PFS was longer in the adagrasib group than in the docetaxel group (5.5 months vs. 3.8 months; P < .0001). Among patients with baseline brain metastases, the intracranial response rate was 24% with adagrasib compared with 11% with docetaxel. There were four treatment-related deaths among patients who received adagrasib (epilepsy, hepatic failure, hepatic ischemia, and unknown cause), compared with one in the docetaxel group (sepsis) [p.207].

### ALK Gene Fusion Positive

**Background**: An ALK gene fusion is present in about 5% of patients with NSCLC [p.207].

**ALEX Trial**: A phase 3 randomized trial assessed first-line therapy with alectinib versus crizotinib in 303 patients with ALK gene fusion-positive advanced NSCLC, including those with asymptomatic CNS disease [p.207]. Disease progression or death occurred in a lower proportion of patients receiving alectinib compared with crizotinib (41% vs. 68%). Investigator-assessed PFS rate was higher with alectinib than crizotinib (68.4% vs. 48.7%). The objective response rates were 82.9% in the alectinib group versus 75.5% in the crizotinib group. Fewer patients receiving alectinib had CNS progression (12%) versus crizotinib (45%). Patients receiving alectinib had fewer grade 3 or higher AEs than those who received crizotinib (41% vs. 50%, respectively). The final analysis reported that the median overall survival was 81.1 months with alectinib and 54.2 months with crizotinib (HR, 0.78; P = .13) [p.207-208].

**ALTA-1L Trial**: A phase 3 randomized trial assessed brigatinib versus crizotinib in 275 patients with ALK-positive metastatic NSCLC who had not previously received an ALK-targeted therapy [p.208]. At the 3-year analysis, the PFS was higher in the brigatinib group versus the crizotinib group (43% vs. 19%). A pooled analysis found that the 3-year overall survival rate was 74%. The intracranial overall response rate among 47 patients with any brain metastases at baseline was 66%. Approximately 80% of patients experienced a grade 3 treatment-related AE [p.208].

**eXalt3 Trial**: A phase 3 randomized trial assessed the efficacy and safety of ensartinib versus crizotinib in 290 patients with advanced, recurrent, or metastatic ALK-positive NSCLC who had not previously received an ALK inhibitor [p.208]. The median PFS was 25.8 months in the ensartinib group compared with 12.7 months in the crizotinib group. The intracranial response rate was higher with ensartinib than crizotinib (63.6% vs. 21.1%) among those with brain metastases at baseline [p.208].

**CROWN Trial**: A phase 3 randomized trial assessed lorlatinib versus crizotinib as first-line therapy in 296 patients with ALK-positive advanced NSCLC [p.208]. At 12 months, 78% of patients were alive without disease progression in the lorlatinib group versus 39% in the crizotinib group. The objective response rate was 76% for patients receiving lorlatinib and 58% for those receiving crizotinib. For patients with measurable brain metastases, an intracranial response was reported in 82% of those receiving lorlatinib and 23% of those receiving crizotinib; a complete intracranial response was achieved in 71% of those who received lorlatinib [p.208]. More grade 3 or 4 AEs (mainly altered lipid levels) occurred with lorlatinib than with crizotinib (72% vs. 56%). Peripheral neuropathy occurred in 34% of the lorlatinib group and 15% of the crizotinib group. Cognitive effects were reported in 21% of patients treated with lorlatinib (compared with 6% in the crizotinib group) [p.208]. Long-term data demonstrated that the 5-year PFS rate was 60% with lorlatinib and 8% with crizotinib in the first-line setting [p.208].

**Lorlatinib as Subsequent Therapy**: A phase 2 trial assessed lorlatinib in patients with ALK or ROS1 gene fusion-positive advanced NSCLC and disease progression after ALK inhibitor therapy [p.208-209]. In the cohort of ALK-positive patients who had previously received at least one ALK inhibitor, objective responses were achieved in 47% of patients. In those with measurable baseline CNS lesions, an objective intracranial response was observed in 63% of patients. Treatment-related peripheral neuropathy was reported in over a quarter of patients; cognitive effects occurred in over 17% of those treated with lorlatinib [p.209].

Data from this trial also showed that lorlatinib is effective as subsequent therapy in patients with the resistance mutation ALK G1202R [p.209]. The objective response rate with lorlatinib was 62% when using plasma circulating tumor DNA (and 69% when using tissue) for patients with ALK resistance mutations and disease progression on second-generation ALK TKIs compared with 32% (plasma) and 27% (tissue) in patients without ALK mutations [p.209]. However, data from other studies suggest that lorlatinib may not be effective in NSCLC with resistant compound ALK mutations (such as combination of L1196M and G1202R) [p.209].

## Special Considerations

### Biomarker Testing Importance

Real-world data indicate the availability of biomarker testing results prior to treatment initiation is associated with longer overall survival in patients with advanced nonsquamous NSCLC [p.200].

### PD-1/PD-L1 Inhibitor Efficacy in EGFR-Mutant Disease

Several studies have reported that PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor monotherapy in the second-line setting is less effective in EGFR exon 19 deletion or L858R mutation-positive NSCLC, regardless of PD-L1 expression [p.202].

### Central Nervous System Penetration

Targeted therapies are often associated with better efficacy for disease in the central nervous system compared to immunotherapy [p.200]. Multiple ALK inhibitors demonstrate substantial intracranial activity, with complete intracranial response rates ranging from 21-82% depending on the agent [p.208].

### Chemotherapy Toxicity with TKI Combinations

When combining osimertinib with chemotherapy, the number of grade 3 adverse events is higher than osimertinib monotherapy, primarily driven by known chemotherapy-related adverse events [p.201].
## First-Line and Second-Line Therapy Evidence

### ROS1 Gene Fusion Positive

**NCCN Recommendations**: The Panel recommends crizotinib, entrectinib, repotrectinib, or taletrectinib as preferred first-line treatment options for patients with ROS1 gene fusion-positive advanced or metastatic NSCLC [p.211]. All are appropriate for patients with performance status 0–4; however, entrectinib, repotrectinib, or taletrectinib may be better for patients with brain metastases [p.211].

**Lorlatinib for ROS1-Positive Disease**: A phase 1/2 trial assessed lorlatinib in 69 patients with ROS1-positive metastatic NSCLC [p.211]. Many patients (58%) had previously received crizotinib; some patients were TKI-naïve (30%). Objective response rates of 35% and 62% were reported in patients who previously received crizotinib and in those who were TKI-naïve, respectively. An intracranial response was observed in 50% of patients who previously received crizotinib and in 64% of the TKI-naïve group [p.211]. Serious treatment-related adverse events occurred in 7% of patients. Peripheral neuropathy was reported in more than one-third of patients treated with lorlatinib, while cognitive effects occurred in approximately a quarter of the patient population [p.211].

**Progression Strategies**: Upon disease progression, consideration of plasma- or tissue-based testing via multigene panel testing should be considered to identify genomic resistance mechanisms [p.211]. If a resistant mutation (such as ROS1 G2032R) is identified, then repotrectinib or taletrectinib are recommended treatment options [p.211]. For asymptomatic progression on first-line targeted therapy, repotrectinib, taletrectinib, or lorlatinib are recommended as subsequent therapy options [p.211].

### BRAF V600E Mutation Positive

**Clinical Trial Data**: A phase 2 trial assessed dabrafenib in combination with trametinib as first-line therapy in 36 patients with metastatic NSCLC and BRAF V600E mutations [p.212]. The overall response rate with dabrafenib plus trametinib as first-line therapy was 63.9%; the median overall survival was 17.3 months [p.212]. The same trial also assessed dabrafenib plus trametinib as subsequent therapy in 57 patients with advanced NSCLC and BRAF V600E mutations and disease progression on chemotherapy [p.212]. The overall response rate was 68.4% and the median overall survival was 18.2 months with dabrafenib/trametinib as subsequent therapy [p.212]. The most frequent adverse event reported in the trial was pyrexia [p.212].

**PHAROS Study**: A phase 2 PHAROS study evaluated encorafenib in combination with binimetinib in patients with BRAF V600E-positive metastatic NSCLC in both the first-line (n = 59) and subsequent therapy (n = 39) settings [p.212]. The updated analysis reported that the objective response rate was 75% in treatment-naïve patients and 49% in previously treated patients [p.212]. The median overall survival was 47.6 months among the treatment-naïve patients and 22.7 months for the previously treated group [p.212]. The most common treatment-related adverse events were nausea, diarrhea, and fatigue. One grade 5 treatment-related adverse event (intracranial hemorrhage) was reported [p.212].

**NCCN Recommendations**: The Panel recommends dabrafenib/trametinib or binimetinib/encorafenib as preferred first-line therapy options for patients with BRAF V600E mutation-positive advanced or metastatic NSCLC [p.212].

### NTRK1/2/3 Gene Fusion Positive

**Background**: NTRK1/2/3 gene fusions encode TRK fusion proteins that act as oncogenic drivers; estimated to occur in <1% of patients with NSCLC [p.213].

**Entrectinib**: Efficacy was evaluated in an analysis of data pooled from several phase 1 and 2 trials in patients with NTRK gene fusion-positive metastatic NSCLC [p.213]. Updated data in 51 patients with NTRK-positive locally advanced or metastatic NSCLC found that the objective response rate was 62.7%; the median overall survival was 41.5 months [p.213]. Among the patients with CNS metastases at baseline, the intracranial objective response rate was 64.3%. Most treatment-related adverse events were grade 1 or 2 [p.213].

**Larotrectinib**: A study in 55 patients with NTRK gene fusion-positive disease across a range of solid tumors showed that larotrectinib yielded an overall response rate (by independent review) of 75% [p.213]. In an analysis of 15 patients with NTRK gene fusion-positive lung cancer, the objective response rate by investigator assessment was 73% [p.213]. The median overall survival was 40.7 months and the median PFS was 35.4 months. Among those with CNS metastases at baseline, the objective response rate was 63%. Most treatment-related adverse events were grade 1 or 2 [p.213].

**Repotrectinib**: The efficacy and safety of repotrectinib in patients with NTRK gene fusion-positive locally advanced or metastatic solid tumors were investigated as part of the phase 1/2 TRIDENT-1 trial [p.213]. NSCLC was the most common tumor type represented. In the initial analysis, the confirmed objective response rates were 62% and 42% in the TKI-naïve NSCLC and TKI pre-treated NSCLC groups, respectively. The 12-month PFS rate was 64% for the TKI-naïve NSCLC group and 23% for the TKI-pretreated NSCLC group [p.213]. Dizziness was the most common treatment-emergent adverse event. Half of the patients in the study experienced a grade 3 or higher treatment-emergent adverse event [p.213].

**NCCN Recommendations**: The Panel recommends entrectinib, larotrectinib, or repotrectinib as preferred first-line therapy options for patients with NTRK1/2/3 gene fusion-positive advanced or metastatic NSCLC [p.213].

### MET Exon 14 Skipping Mutation Positive

**Background**: MET exon 14 skipping mutations occur in 3% to 4% of patients with lung adenocarcinoma and approximately 2% of patients with other NSCLC histologies [p.214].

**GEOMETRY Study**: The phase 2 GEOMETRY study assessed capmatinib in different cohorts of patients with MET genomic alterations, including those with MET exon 14 skipping mutations [p.214]. First-line capmatinib resulted in an overall response rate of 68% in 28 patients with MET exon 14 skipping mutations; the median PFS was 12.4 months [p.214]. Subsequent therapy with capmatinib yielded an overall response rate of 41% in 69 patients with MET exon 14 skipping mutations; the median PFS was 5.4 months [p.214].

**VISION Trial**: The phase 2 VISION trial assessed tepotinib in patients with MET exon 14 skipping mutation-positive locally advanced or metastatic NSCLC [p.214]. Based on the 18-month analysis, the objective response rate with tepotinib was 57.3% in the treatment-naïve group and 45.0% among previously treated patients [p.214]. The median overall survival was 21.3 months for the treatment-naïve group and 19.3 months for the previously treated group. Peripheral edema was the most common treatment-related adverse event [p.214].

### ERBB2 (HER2) Mutation Positive

**Clinical Trial Data**: The efficacy and safety of sevabertinib in 209 patients with locally advanced or metastatic NSCLC with ERBB2 (HER2) mutations was assessed in the phase 1–2 SOHO-01 trial [p.217]. Over 90% of the patients in the study had an ERBB2 tyrosine kinase domain mutation. Among the 81 patients previously treated with a non–HER2-targeted therapy, the objective response rate with sevabertinib was 64% and the median PFS was 8.3 months [p.217]. Based on an exploratory biomarker analysis of this group, a higher proportion of patients with an ERBB2 TKD mutation experienced an objective response with sevabertinib than those with a non-TKD mutation (70% vs. 14%) [p.217]. Among the 55 patients who previously received a HER2-targeted antibody-drug conjugate, the objective response rate was 38% and the median PFS was 5.5 months. The most common adverse event reported in the study was diarrhea, which was experienced by 84% to 91% of patients across cohorts [p.217].

**NCCN Recommendations**: The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends zongertinib or systemic therapy regimens (such as chemotherapy with or without immunotherapy) for the first-line treatment of advanced or metastatic NSCLC with ERBB2 (HER2) mutation [p.217]. At the time of disease progression, fam-trastuzumab deruxtecan-nxki, zongertinib (if not previously given), or sevabertinib are recommended as preferred subsequent therapy options [p.217].

### NRG1 Gene Fusion Positive

**Background**: NRG1 gene fusions are estimated to occur in <1% of patients diagnosed with NSCLC [p.217]. Invasive mucinous lung adenocarcinomas are associated with higher rates of NRG1 gene fusion [p.217].

**Clinical Trial Data**: Zenocutuzumab is a bispecific HER2- and HER3-directed antibody that has been granted accelerated approval by the FDA for the treatment of advanced, unresectable, or metastatic NSCLC with an NRG1 gene fusion with disease progression on or after prior systemic therapy [p.217]. The accelerated approval was based on data from a phase 2 trial that evaluated zenocutuzumab in patients with advanced or metastatic solid tumors with NRG1 gene fusion [p.217]. Patients with NSCLC represented 58% (94 of 161) of the primary efficacy population. Within the NSCLC group, 87% were previously treated with systemic therapy; 76% received platinum-based chemotherapy and 56% received immunotherapy. The overall response rate based on investigator assessment was 29% in the NSCLC group; the median duration of response was 12.7 months [p.217]. Grade 3 or 4 treatment-related adverse events occurred in 7% of all patients who received zenocutuzumab; diarrhea and anemia were the most common in this group [p.217].

**NCCN Recommendations**: The Panel recommends systemic therapy regimens (such as chemotherapy with or without immunotherapy) for the first-line treatment of advanced or metastatic NSCLC with an NRG1 gene fusion [p.217]. Zenocutuzumab-zbco is recommended as a subsequent therapy option for those with NRG1 gene fusion-positive advanced or metastatic NSCLC [p.218].

### HER2 (IHC 3+) Positive

**Background**: HER2 protein overexpression occurs in many types of solid tumors, including NSCLC, and is often associated with poor outcomes [p.218].

**DESTINY-Lung01 Trial**: Fam-trastuzumab deruxtecan-nxki was granted accelerated approval by the FDA for the treatment of patients with unresectable or metastatic HER2-positive (IHC 3+) solid tumors who have received prior systemic treatment [p.218]. This approval is applicable to patients with NSCLC based on positive data from the phase 2 single-arm DESTINY-Lung01 trial, which included 90 patients with unresectable or metastatic HER2-overexpressing (IHC 3+ or 2+) NSCLC who received fam-trastuzumab deruxtecan-nxki as subsequent therapy [p.218]. The objective response rates were 26.5% and 34.1% in cohort 1 and cohort 1A, respectively. One death was attributed to treatment-emergent pneumonitis. Drug-related interstitial lung disease or pneumonitis (based on independent adjudication) occurred in 20% of patients in cohort 1 (including three grade 5 events) and 5% of cohort 1A (including one grade 5 event) [p.218].

**NCCN Recommendations**: The Panel recommends fam-trastuzumab deruxtecan-nxki as a subsequent therapy option for patients with advanced or metastatic NSCLC with HER2 (IHC 3+) [p.218].

### HGF Receptor (c-Met)-Positive (≥50% IHC 3+ and EGFR wild-type)

**Background**: HGF receptor (c-Met) overexpression in patients with NSCLC is associated with worse prognosis [p.218].

**LUMINOSITY Trial**: The phase 2 LUMINOSITY trial was a two-stage study that evaluated telisotuzumab vedotin-tllv in patients with locally advanced or metastatic NSCLC with HGF receptor (c-Met) overexpression [p.218]. The optimal candidates for telisotuzumab vedotin-tllv were first identified as those with nonsquamous EGFR wild-type NSCLC and HGF receptor (c-Met) overexpression. The overall response rates were 34.6% for 78 patients with HGF receptor (c-Met) ≥50% IHC 3+ and 22.9% among 83 patients ≥25% to <50% IHC 3+ [p.218-219]. The median overall survival was 14.6 months and 14.2 months for the HGF receptor (c-Met) ≥50% IHC 3+ and ≥25% to <50% IHC 3+ groups, respectively. The most common treatment-related adverse events were peripheral sensory neuropathy, peripheral edema, and fatigue. Treatment-related pneumonitis (any grade) was reported in 10.5% of patients with HGF receptor (c-Met) overexpression [p.219]. Adjudicated interstitial lung disease events were reported in 9.9% of patients [p.219].

**NCCN Recommendations**: The Panel recommends telisotuzumab vedotin-tllv as a subsequent therapy option for patients with advanced or metastatic nonsquamous NSCLC with HGF receptor (c-Met) ≥50% IHC 3+ and wild-type EGFR [p.219].

## Special Considerations

### Resistance Mutation Testing

Upon disease progression with ALK or ROS1 fusion-positive disease, consideration of plasma- or tissue-based testing via multigene panel testing is recommended to determine genomic resistance mechanisms and guide selection of subsequent therapy options [p.209].

### Central Nervous System Disease Management

Multiple agents demonstrate substantial intracranial activity. Entrectinib, repotrectinib, and taletrectinib may be better options for patients with brain metastases compared to crizotinib [p.211]. Lorlatinib shows particularly high intracranial response rates in ALK-positive disease and can cross the blood-brain barrier [p.208].

### Toxicity Profiles and Treatment Switching

For BRAF V600E mutations, switching between BRAF inhibitors and MEK inhibitors due to toxicity in the absence of progression may be reasonable, but switching between agents with a similar mechanism of action at the time of progression is not recommended [p.212]. Similarly for HER2 mutations, switching between zongertinib and sevabertinib at the time of progression is not recommended, but switching due to toxicity may be reasonable [p.217].

### Immunotherapy Efficacy in Driver-Positive NSCLC

Data indicate that ICI monotherapy may be less effective in NSCLC with certain driver mutations (EGFR, ERBB2/HER2, and ROS1) [p.173, 1048]. Results from retrospective studies indicate that patients with advanced NSCLC and BRAF mutations may derive benefit from PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors, particularly those with minimal disease burden and/or high PD-L1 levels [p.212].
