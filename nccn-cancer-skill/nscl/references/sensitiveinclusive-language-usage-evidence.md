## Language Principles

**Goals and Values** [p.129]
- NCCN Guidelines strive to use language that advances the goals of:
  - Equity [p.129]
  - Inclusion [p.129]
  - Representation [p.129]

**Language Principles** [p.129]
NCCN Guidelines endeavor to use language that is:
- Person-first [p.129]
- Not stigmatizing [p.129]
- Anti-racist [p.129]
- Anti-classist [p.129]
- Anti-misogynist [p.129]
- Anti-ageist [p.129]
- Anti-ableist [p.129]
- Anti-weight-biased [p.129]
- Inclusive of individuals of all sexual orientations and gender identities [p.129]

## Non-Gendered Language Approach

**Use of Organ-Specific Recommendations** [p.129]
- NCCN Guidelines incorporate non-gendered language, instead focusing on organ-specific recommendations [p.129]
- This language is both more accurate and more inclusive [p.129]
- This approach can help fully address the needs of individuals of all sexual orientations and gender identities [p.129]

## Use of Traditional Gender Terms

**When Gender Terms Are Used** [p.129-130]
- NCCN Guidelines will continue to use the terms men, women, female, and male when citing:
  - Statistics [p.129]
  - Recommendations [p.129]
  - Data from organizations or sources that do not use inclusive terms [p.129]

**Limitations of Available Data** [p.129-130]
- Most studies do not report how sex and gender data are collected [p.129]
- Many studies use the terms men, women, female, and male interchangeably or inconsistently [p.129-130]
- If sources do not differentiate gender from sex assigned at birth or organs present, the information is presumed to predominantly represent cisgender individuals [p.130]

## Future Improvements

**Recommendations for Researchers** [p.130]
- NCCN encourages researchers to collect more specific data in future studies [p.130]
- NCCN encourages organizations to use more inclusive and accurate language in their future analyses [p.130]
