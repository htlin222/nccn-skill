## Overview: Molecular Diagnostic Studies in NSCLC

**Note:** All recommendations are (Category 2A) unless otherwise indicated. [p.99]

Numerous gene alterations have been identified that impact therapy selection. [p.99] Testing of lung cancer specimens for these alterations is important for identification of potentially efficacious targeted therapies, as well as avoidance of therapies unlikely to provide clinical benefit. [p.99]

Some selection approaches for targeted therapy include predictive IHC analyses, which are distinct from IHC studies utilized to identify tumor type and lineage. [p.99] Clinicopathologic features should not be used to select patients for testing. [p.99]

---

## Major Elements of Biomarker Testing

Critical elements for utilization and interpretation of molecular results include: [p.99]

- Use of a laboratory that is properly accredited, with a minimum of Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments (CLIA) accreditation [p.99]
- Understanding the methodologies that are utilized and the major limitations of those methodologies [p.99]
- Understanding the spectrum of alterations tested (and those not tested) by a specific assay [p.99]
- Knowledge of whether a tumor sample is subjected to pathologic review and tumor enrichment (i.e., microdissection, macrodissection) prior to testing [p.99]
- The types of samples accepted by the testing laboratory [p.99]
- Understanding what constitutes an informative versus a null result [p.99]
  - A positive finding for a known oncogenic driver is considered an informative result [p.99]
  - Absence of a known oncogenic driver, including results that show only "passenger" alterations, might be considered null depending on the context of the specimen limitations and clinical situation [p.99]
  - Technical issues related to tumor cellularity in tissue testing or burden of disease in circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) testing are considerations in interpretation of findings [p.99]

---

## Tissue Specimen Acquisition and Management

Although tumor testing has been primarily focused on use of FFPE tissues, increasingly, laboratories accept other specimen types, notably cytopathology preparations not processed by FFPE methods. [p.99-100] Although testing on cell blocks is not included in the FDA approval for multiple companion diagnostic assays, testing on these specimen types is highly recommended when it is the only or best material. [p.100]

A major limitation in obtaining tissue biomarker testing results for NSCLC occurs when minimally invasive techniques are used to obtain samples; the yield may be insufficient for molecular, biomarker, and histologic testing. [p.100] Therefore, bronchoscopists and interventional radiologists should procure sufficient tissue to enable all appropriate testing. [p.100]

When tissue is minimal, laboratories should deploy techniques to maximize tissue for biomarker testing, including dedicated histology protocols for small biopsies, including "up-front" slide sectioning for diagnostic and predictive testing. [p.100] Peripheral blood (plasma ctDNA) can be a surrogate sample. [p.100]

## Testing Methodologies

### General Approaches

Several methodologies are generally considerations for use: [p.100]

**Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS)**
- NGS is used in clinical laboratories [p.100]
- Not all types of alterations are detected by individual NGS assays; it is important to be familiar with the types of alterations identifiable in individual assays or combination(s) of assays [p.100]
- Multi-Gene Panel Testing (MGPT) approaches that efficiently utilize limited biopsy tissue while maximizing diagnostic genomic information are most commonly NGS-based [p.100]
- NCCN acknowledges that many currently available NGS-based assays used to fully genotype NSCLC are larger than the 50-gene limit threshold utilized by Current Procedural Terminology (CPT) coding convention [p.100]
- Utilization of panels >50 genes may be practical to follow these recommendations [p.100]
- Either a single assay or a combination of a limited number of assays may be appropriate [p.100]
- Although MGPT approaches are preferred, in some clinical situations rapid testing may be warranted and should be followed up with MGPT [p.100]

**RNA-Based NGS**
- RNA-based NGS may increase novel fusion detection and can be performed concurrently or sequentially with DNA-based NGS [p.100]
- If no identifiable driver oncogenes with DNA-based molecular profiling are identified, RNA-based testing is recommended [p.100]

**Biomarker Testing Completeness**
- Biomarker testing is considered complete if a minimum of biomarkers are identified in NSCL-20 in either a single assay or a combination of a limited number of assays, and optimally also identifies emerging biomarkers (NSCL-I) [p.100]
- Sequential, single-gene testing is not preferred [p.100]
- Tiered approaches based on low prevalence of co-occurring biomarkers are acceptable [p.100]

**Real-Time Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)**
- Real-time PCR can be used in a highly targeted fashion (specific mutations targeted) [p.100]
- When this technology is deployed, only those specific alterations that are targeted by the assay are assessed [p.100]
- The potential for mutations outside of the testing scope of the specific assay must be considered [p.100]

**Variants of Uncertain Significance (VUS)**
- Any method that interrogates sequences other than a subset of highly specific alterations (e.g., NGS) has the potential to identify VUS [p.100]
- Any variant classified as a VUS, even if in a gene in which other variants are clinically actionable, should not be considered as a basis for targeted therapy selection [p.100]

**Other Methodologies**
- Other methodologies may be utilized, including multiplex approaches not listed above [p.100]

**Fluorescence In Situ Hybridization (FISH)**
- FISH analysis is utilized for many assays examining copy number, amplification, and structural alterations such as gene rearrangements [p.100]
- FISH may have better sensitivity for gene amplification events in some circumstances [p.100]
- FISH testing allows one to identify a chromosomal rearrangement, from which one can infer the presence of a gene fusion event [p.100]

## Specimen Selection

Testing platforms using tissue and peripheral blood are available. [p.100]

### Tissue-Based Testing

Testing using tissue sample requires acquisition of a suitable sample. [p.100]

- Alterations detected using NSCLC tumor tissue can be most directly attributed to the tumor [p.100]
- If an assay has a technical failure related to an insufficient quantity, consideration of an alternate testing modality or procurement of additional tissue is recommended to achieve complete biomarker testing [p.100-101]

### Peripheral Blood (ctDNA) Testing

Testing using peripheral blood (most commonly plasma-based testing of ctDNA) can be utilized in conjunction with tissue-based testing to achieve genotyping for recommended biomarkers. [p.101]

- Many, but not all, ctDNA tests use NGS-based technology [p.101]
- Technical advantages and limitations of ctDNA testing are further elaborated below [p.101]

## Molecular Targets for Analysis

In general, the mutations/alterations described below are seen in a non-overlapping fashion, although between 1%–3% of NSCLC may harbor concurrent alterations. [p.101]

### EGFR (Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor) Gene Mutations

EGFR is a receptor tyrosine kinase normally found on the surface of epithelial cells and is often overexpressed in a variety of human malignancies. [p.101]

- The most commonly described mutations in EGFR (exon 19 deletions, p.L858R point mutation in exon 21) are associated with responsiveness to oral EGFR TKI therapy [p.101]
- Tumors that do not harbor a sensitizing EGFR mutation should not be treated with EGFR TKI in any line of therapy [p.101]
- Biomarker testing for EGFR mutations should be performed when TKI therapy is a consideration for NSCLC stage IB–IIIC [p.101]
  - While the testing process may be technically easier on a resection specimen, initial diagnostic biopsy specimens are also acceptable for testing for this indication [p.101]
- Many of the less commonly observed alterations in EGFR, which cumulatively account for ~10% of EGFR-mutation positive NSCLC (i.e., exon 21 p.L861Q, exon 18 p.G719X, and exon 20 p.S768I mutations), are also associated with responsiveness to certain EGFR TKIs, such as osimertinib and afatinib, and should be considered on a mutation-specific basis, when possible [p.101]

**EGFR p.T790M**
- EGFR p.T790M is most commonly observed as a mutation that arises in response to and as a mechanism of resistance to first- and second-generation EGFR TKI [p.101]
- In patients with progression on first- or second-generation TKI with p.T790M as the primary mechanism of resistance, third-generation TKIs are typically efficacious [p.101]
- If EGFR p.T790M is identified in the absence of prior EGFR TKI therapy, genetic counseling and possible germline MGPT are warranted [p.101]
- Identification of germline EGFR p.T790M confers a high risk for lung cancer regardless of smoking status [p.101]

**EGFR Exon 20 (EGFRex20) Mutations**
- EGFR exon 20 (EGFRex20) mutations (other than EGFR p.T790M) are a heterogeneous group, some of which are responsive to targeted therapy and that require detailed knowledge of the specific alteration [p.101]
- Most EGFRex20 alterations are a diverse group of in-frame duplication or insertions [p.101-102]
  - These are generally associated with lack of response to first-, second-, and third-generation EGFR TKI therapy, with select exceptions [p.102]
  - p.A763_Y764insFQEA is associated with sensitivity to TKI therapy [p.102]
  - p.A763_Y764insLQEA may be associated with sensitivity to first- and third-generation TKI therapy [p.102]
  - EGFRex20 insertions/duplications are associated with responsiveness to specific targeted subsequent therapy agents designed for EGFRex20 events [p.102]
  - The most commonly represented EGFRex20 insertions/duplications in the clinical studies have been insASV, insSVD, and insNPH, although a wide spectrum of other alterations were included [p.102]
  - There is currently no evidence that the specific alteration type impacts the probability of responsiveness to this class of targeted therapy [p.102]
- Because some EGFRex20 mutations are or may be sensitive to first- and third-generation inhibitors, the specific sequence of EGFRex20 insertion mutations remains important [p.102]
- Some assays will identify the presence of an EGFRex20 insertion without specifying the sequence, and additional testing to further clarify the EGFRex20 insertion may be indicated for therapy selection [p.102]
- Targeted PCR-based approaches for detection of EGFR mutations under-detect EGFRex20 insertion events; therefore, NGS-based strategies are preferred [p.102]

**EGFR Testing Methodologies**
- Real-time PCR and NGS are the most commonly deployed methodologies for examining EGFR mutation status [p.101]

### ALK (Anaplastic Lymphoma Kinase) Gene Fusions

ALK is a receptor tyrosine kinase that can be rearranged in NSCLC, resulting in dysregulation and inappropriate signaling through the ALK kinase domain. [p.102]

- The most common fusion partner seen with ALK is echinoderm microtubule-associated protein-like 4 (EML4), although a variety of other fusion partners have been identified [p.102]
- Biomarker testing for ALK gene fusions should be performed when TKI therapy is a consideration for NSCLC stage IB–IIIB [p.102]
  - While the testing process may be technically easier on a resection specimen, initial diagnostic biopsy specimens are also acceptable for testing for this indication [p.102]
- The presence of an ALK gene fusion is associated with responsiveness to oral ALK TKIs [p.102]

**ALK Testing Methodologies**
- FISH break-apart probe methodology was the first methodology deployed widely [p.102]
- IHC can be deployed as an effective screening strategy [p.102]
- FDA-approved IHC can be utilized as a stand-alone test, not requiring confirmation by FISH [p.102]
- Numerous NGS methodologies can detect ALK gene fusions [p.102]
- Targeted real-time PCR assays are used in some settings, although it is unlikely to detect fusions with novel partners [p.102]

### ROS1 (ROS Proto-Oncogene 1) Gene Fusions

ROS1 is a receptor tyrosine kinase that can be rearranged in NSCLC, resulting in dysregulation and inappropriate signaling through the ROS1 kinase domain. [p.102]

- Numerous fusion partners are seen with ROS1, and common fusion partners include: CD74, SLC34A2, CCDC6, and GOPC [p.102]
- The presence of a ROS1 gene fusion is associated with responsiveness to oral ROS1 TKIs [p.102]

**ROS1 Testing Methodologies**
- FISH break-apart probe methodology can be deployed; however, it may under-detect the FIG::ROS1 variant [p.102]
- IHC approaches can be deployed; however, IHC for ROS1 fusions has low specificity, and follow-up confirmatory testing is a necessary component of utilizing ROS1 IHC as a screening modality [p.102]
- Numerous NGS methodologies can detect ROS1 gene fusions, although DNA-based NGS may under-detect ROS1 gene fusions [p.102]
- Targeted real-time PCR assays are utilized in some settings, although they are unlikely to detect fusions with novel partners [p.102]

### BRAF (B-Raf Proto-Oncogene) Point Mutations

BRAF is a serine/threonine kinase that is part of the canonical MAPK pathway. [p.102] Activating mutations in BRAF result in unregulated signaling through the MAPK pathway. [p.102]

- Mutations in BRAF can be seen in NSCLC [p.102]
- The presence of a specific mutation resulting in a change in amino acid position 600 (most commonly p.V600E, but rarely p.V600K, p.V600D, or other changes) has been associated with responsiveness to combined therapy with oral inhibitors of BRAF and MEK [p.102]
- Note that other mutations in BRAF are observed in NSCLC, and the impact of those mutations on therapy selection is not well understood at this time [p.102]

**BRAF Testing Methodologies**
- Real-time PCR and NGS are the most commonly deployed methodologies for examining BRAF mutation status [p.102]
- While an anti-BRAF p.V600E-specific monoclonal antibody is commercially available, and some studies have examined utilizing this approach, it should only be deployed after extensive validation [p.102]

### NRG1 Gene Fusions

NRG1 is an epidermal growth factor (EGF) involved in nervous system and cardiac development and homeostasis. [p.102] NRG1 gene fusions lead to inappropriate signaling via the ERBB family receptor tyrosine kinase pathway. [p.102]

- The presence of an NRG1 gene fusion is associated with responsiveness to a HER2/HER3 bispecific antibody [p.102]

**NRG1 Testing Methodologies**
- NGS-based testing is the primary method for detection of NRG1 gene fusions [p.102]
- RNA-based NGS is preferable to DNA-based NGS as NRG1 gene fusions are often not detected by DNA-based NGS because the large introns in NRG1 are not commonly included in targeted DNA-based NGS panels or whole exome sequencing [p.102]
- IHC is not a suitable method for detection of NRG1 gene fusion [p.102]
- FISH testing requires confirmation by NGS [p.102]

### KRAS (KRAS Proto-Oncogene) Point Mutations

KRAS is a G-protein with intrinsic GTPase activity, and activating mutations result in unregulated signaling through the MAPK pathway. [p.103]

- Mutations in KRAS are most commonly seen at codon 12, although other mutations can be seen in NSCLC [p.103]
- The presence of a KRAS mutation is prognostic of poor survival when compared to patients with tumors without KRAS mutation [p.103]
- Owing to the low probability of overlapping targetable alterations, the presence of a known activating mutation in KRAS identifies patients who are unlikely to benefit from further molecular testing [p.103]
- The presence of KRAS p.G12C is associated with responsiveness to oral KRAS G12C inhibitors used for subsequent therapy, which were designed specifically for this mutation [p.103]
- Responsiveness to this class of inhibitor has not been prospectively evaluated with mutations other than KRAS p.G12C [p.103]

**KRAS Testing Methodologies**
- NGS and real-time PCR are the most commonly deployed methodologies for examining KRAS mutation status [p.103]

### MET (Mesenchymal-Epithelial Transition) Exon 14 (METex14) Skipping Variants

HGF receptor (c-Met) is a receptor tyrosine kinase. [p.103] A mutation that results in loss of exon 14 can occur in NSCLC. [p.103] Loss of METex14 leads to dysregulation and inappropriate signaling. [p.103]

- The presence of METex14 skipping mutation is associated with responsiveness to oral HGF receptor (c-Met) TKIs [p.103]
- A broad range of molecular alterations lead to METex14 skipping [p.103]

**METex14 Testing Methodologies**
- NGS-based testing is the primary method for detection of METex14 skipping events [p.103]
- RNA-based NGS may have improved detection [p.103]
- IHC is not a method for detection of METex14 skipping [p.103]

### RET (Rearranged During Transfection) Gene Fusions

RET is a receptor tyrosine kinase that can be rearranged in NSCLC, resulting in dysregulation and inappropriate signaling through the RET kinase domain. [p.103]

- Common fusion partners are KIF5B, NCOA4, and CCDC6; however, numerous other fusion partners have been identified [p.103]
- The presence of a RET gene fusion is associated with responsiveness to oral RET TKIs regardless of fusion partner [p.103]

**RET Testing Methodologies**
- FISH break-apart probe methodology can be deployed; however, it may under-detect some fusions [p.103]
- Targeted real-time reverse-transcriptase PCR assays are utilized in some settings, although they are unlikely to detect fusions with novel partners [p.103]
- NGS-based methodology has a high specificity, and RNA-based NGS is preferable to DNA-based NGS for fusion detection [p.103]

### ERBB2 (Erb-B2 Receptor Tyrosine Kinase 2)/HER2 Gene Mutations

ERBB2 encodes for HER2, a receptor tyrosine kinase found on the surface of normal epithelial cells that is often overexpressed or mutated in a variety of human malignancies. [p.103]

- A spectrum of genomic alterations involving ERBB2 have been identified in multiple tumor types, including mutations and amplifications, and less commonly, other alterations [p.103]
- Mutations in ERBB2 are most commonly insertion/duplication events in exon 20, and less commonly, other activating mutations are observed [p.103]
- Activating mutations in ERBB2 are associated with responsiveness to anti-HER2 targeted therapy agents [p.103]
- While some mutations are known to be activating, including mutations in the extracellular domain (e.g., p.S310X) and exon 20 insertion/duplication mutations, not all single-nucleotide or double-nucleotide changes in ERBB2 are activating and not all potentially activating mutations were studied in pivotal trials [p.103]
- The definition of an activating mutation is evolving [p.103]
- FDA approved agents may differ in which mutations are considered on-label, with one agent requiring presence of an activating mutation (with individual mutations not specified) while two other agents are approved for tyrosine kinase domain mutations [p.103]
- For oncogenic or likely oncogenic ERBB2 (HER2) mutations, refer to definitions at oncokb.org [p.103]

**ERBB2 Testing Methodologies**
- While targeted PCR techniques can be utilized to examine ERBB2 mutations, the diversity and spectrum of mutations are best surveyed using NGS-based approaches [p.103]

### HER2 Protein Overexpression

HER2, encoded by ERBB2, can be overexpressed in a variety of malignancies. [p.104]

- At this time, there is no recommendation regarding specific IHC antibody clone for HER2 staining in NSCLC [p.104]
- Scoring criteria for anti-HER2 monoclonal antibody/antibody-drug conjugate (ADC) therapy is currently based on criteria initially applied in gastric carcinoma, which utilizes different scoring criteria than that utilized for breast carcinoma and employs different scoring criteria for small biopsy specimens versus resections [p.104]

### HGF (Hepatocyte Growth Factor) Receptor (c-Met) Protein Overexpression

HGF receptor (c-Met), encoded by the MET gene, is a receptor tyrosine kinase found on the surface of normal epithelial cells that can be overexpressed within NSCLC. [p.104]

- High HGF receptor (c-Met) protein expression levels are associated with responsiveness to specific antibody drug conjugate agents [p.104]
- Evaluation for HGF receptor (c-Met) protein expression is distinct from evaluation of MET amplification and MET exon 14 skipping events [p.104]

**HGF Receptor (c-Met) Protein Expression Testing Methodologies**
- IHC is the required method for detection of HGF receptor (c-Met) protein expression [p.104]
- The antibody clone SP44 is currently the only reagent shown to be an effective predictor of response to ADC therapy [p.104]
- Laboratories using modifications to the FDA-approved methodology for SP44 or alternate clones must demonstrate comparability to SP44 with the approved methodology [p.104]
- A positive result is considered 3+ staining in ≥50% of tumor cells [p.104]
- NGS, RT-PCR, and FISH are not methods for detection of HGF receptor (c-Met) protein expression [p.104]

### NTRK1/2/3 (Neurotrophic Tyrosine Receptor Kinase) Gene Fusions

- The presence of NTRK1/2/3 gene fusions is associated with responsiveness to oral TRK inhibitors [p.104]
- NTRK1/2/3 are tyrosine receptor kinases that are rarely rearranged in NSCLC as well as in other tumor types, resulting in dysregulation and inappropriate signaling [p.104-105]
- Numerous fusion partners have been identified [p.105]
- To date, no specific clinicopathologic features, other than absence of other driver alterations, have been identified in association with these fusions [p.105]
- Point mutations in NTRK1/2/3 are generally non-activating and have not been studied in association with targeted therapy [p.105]

**NTRK1/2/3 Testing Methodologies**
- Various methodologies can be used to detect NTRK1/2/3 gene fusions, including: FISH, IHC, PCR, and NGS [p.105]
- False negatives may occur [p.105]
- IHC methods are complicated by baseline expression in some tissues [p.105]
- FISH testing may require at least 3 probe sets for full analysis [p.105]
- NGS testing can detect a broad range of alterations [p.105]
- DNA-based NGS may under-detect NTRK1 and NTRK3 fusions [p.105]

### PD-L1 (Programmed Death Ligand 1)

PD-L1 is a co-regulatory molecule that can be expressed on tumor cells and inhibit T-cell–mediated cell death. [p.105] T cells express PD-1, a negative regulator, which binds to ligands including PD-L1 (CD274) or PD-L2 (CD273). [p.105] In the presence of PD-L1, T-cell activity is suppressed. [p.105]

Checkpoint inhibitor antibodies block the PD-1 and PD-L1 interaction, thereby improving the antitumor effects of endogenous T cells. [p.105]

IHC for PD-L1 can be utilized to identify disease most likely to respond to first-line anti–PD-1/PD-L1. [p.105]

- Various antibody clones have been developed for IHC analysis of PD-L1 expression, and while several are comparable regarding intensity and proportion of cells stained, some are not [p.105]
- The definition of positive and negative testing is dependent on the individual antibody, clone, and platform deployed, which may be unique to each checkpoint inhibitor therapy [p.105]
- The approval of multiple different assays for PD-L1 has raised concern among both pathologists and oncologists [p.105]
- While some clones for PD-L1 IHC are FDA-approved for specific indications, use of multiple IHC tests is not routinely recommended, provided any individual IHC test has been internally validated for comparability for categorical results against the FDA-approved clone [p.105]
- Interpretation of PD-L1 IHC in NSCLC is typically focused on the proportion of tumor cells expressing membranous staining at any level and therefore is a linear variable [p.105]
- Scoring systems may be different in other tumor types [p.105]
- Although PD-L1 expression can be elevated in patients with an oncogenic driver, targeted therapy for certain oncogenic drivers should take precedence over treatment with an ICI [p.105]

## Circulating Tumor DNA (ctDNA) Testing

Some laboratories offer testing for molecular alterations examining nucleic acids in peripheral circulation, most commonly in processed plasma. [p.105]

**Key Recommendations:**
- ctDNA testing should not be used in lieu of a histologic tissue diagnosis [p.105]
- ctDNA is not routinely recommended in settings other than advanced/metastatic disease [p.105]
- For stages I–III, tissue-based testing is preferred [p.105]
- Molecular testing of tissue may have a higher yield in the setting of metastatic disease confined to the thorax [p.105]

**Specificity and Sensitivity:**
- Studies have demonstrated ctDNA and tissue testing to have very high specificity [p.105]
- Both ctDNA and tissue testing have appreciable false-negative rates, supporting the complementarity of these approaches [p.105]
- Data support complementary testing to reduce turnaround time and increase yield of targetable alteration detection [p.105]

**Limitations of ctDNA Testing**
- Low tumor fraction/ctDNA: some assays include a measure of ctDNA fraction, which can aid in identification of situations in which low ctDNA fraction might suggest compromised sensitivity [p.105]
- The presence of mutations from sites other than the target lesion, most commonly clonal hematopoiesis of indeterminate potential (CHIP) or post-chemotherapy marrow clones [p.105]
  - KRAS and TP53 can be seen in either of these circumstances [p.105]
- The inherent ability of the assay to detect fusions or other genomic variation of relevance [p.105]

**Limitations of Tissue Testing**
- Low tumor percent in a sample not sufficiently mitigated by tumor enrichment or high analytic sensitivity methods [p.105-106]
- The inherent ability of the assay to detect fusions or other genomic variation of relevance [p.106]

## Germline Testing

- Consider referral to genetic counseling if a pathogenic/likely pathogenic variant is identified on somatic testing in which there is either molecular or clinical concern for germline predisposition syndrome [p.106]

## Testing in the Setting of Limited Pulmonary Nodules

Testing to distinguish separate primary lung carcinoma versus intrapulmonary metastatic disease should be considered. [p.106]

Studies to explore tumor relatedness by testing tissue from separately sampled lesions using an MGPT approach suggest it may be superior to histopathologic assessment. [p.106]

- Tumor pairs exhibiting entirely non-overlapping, unique mutations are considered clonally unrelated separate primary lung cancers, even if histologically similar [p.106]
- Tumors that share multiple (≥2) mutations are more likely to be clonally related; however, this may depend on the extent to which any individual mutation is extremely common in NSCLC and whether identified alterations are driver or passenger alterations [p.106]
- Results in which no mutations or only one mutation are identified are not informative for this evaluation [p.106]

---

## Testing in the Setting of Progression on Targeted Therapy

For any patient with progression on targeted therapy, histologic transformation (such as small cell) is a possible mechanism of resistance. [p.106]

Tissue biopsy of a progressing lesion should be considered to evaluate morphology and biomarker analysis. [p.106]

PET/CT can be used to identify optimal biopsy targets in the setting of progression. [p.106]

At time of progression, consider ctDNA and tissue-based testing in parallel to identify resistance mechanisms. [p.106]

For many of the above listed analytes, there is growing recognition of the molecular mechanisms of resistance to therapy. [p.106] Re-testing of a sample from a tumor that is actively progressing while exposed to targeted therapy can shed light on appropriate next therapeutic steps. [p.106]

### EGFR Sensitizing Mutation Progression

For patients with an underlying EGFR sensitizing mutation who have been treated with EGFR TKI, minimum appropriate testing includes: [p.106]

**High-Sensitivity Evaluation for p.T790M**
- If patient previously treated with a first- or second-generation EGFR TKI [p.106]
- Assays for the detection of EGFR p.T790M should be designed to have an analytic sensitivity of a minimum of 5% allelic fraction [p.106]
- The original sensitizing mutation can be utilized as an internal control in many assays to determine whether a p.T790M is within the range of detection if present as a subclonal event [p.106]
- The presence of p.T790M can direct patients to third-generation EGFR TKI therapy [p.106]

**Alternate Mechanisms of Resistance**
- Alternate mechanisms of resistance (e.g., MET and/or ERBB2 amplification, gene fusions, new driver mutation [bypass signaling event]) may be used to direct patients for additional therapies [p.106]

### ALK Gene Fusion Progression

For patients with underlying ALK gene fusion who have been treated with ALK TKI, it is unclear whether identification of specific tyrosine kinase domain mutation can identify appropriate next steps in therapy, although some preliminary data suggest that specific kinase domain mutations may impact the next line of therapy. [p.106]

### Multi-Gene Panel Testing Approach for Resistance Mechanisms

MGPT may be the most informative approach to examining potential mechanisms of resistance, which may require more than one instance of such profiling over the course of an individual patient's therapy. [p.106]

Assay methodology selection can impact the ability to identify subclonal events in this setting. [p.106]
