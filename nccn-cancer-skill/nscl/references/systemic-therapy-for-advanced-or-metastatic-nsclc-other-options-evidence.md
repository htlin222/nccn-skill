## Summary of Key Evidence

For patients with metastatic NSCLC and contraindications to pembrolizumab or other immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), chemotherapy options are recommended, including carboplatin plus paclitaxel [p.220]. Chemotherapy with or without bevacizumab is an option if eligibility criteria are met for patients with nonsquamous NSCLC and negative test results for actionable driver mutations, and with PD-L1 expression less than 1% [p.220].

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Platinum-Based Chemotherapy Combinations

**ECOG 4599 Trial**: A phase 2/3 trial randomly assigned 878 patients to either bevacizumab in combination with paclitaxel plus carboplatin or paclitaxel plus carboplatin alone [p.220]. Patients receiving bevacizumab plus paclitaxel plus carboplatin showed an improved median survival (12.3 vs. 10.3 months, P = .003) when compared to patients receiving paclitaxel plus carboplatin alone [p.220]. The overall 1-year survival was 51% versus 44%; 2-year survival was 23% versus 15%, respectively, in favor of the bevacizumab plus paclitaxel plus carboplatin arm [p.220]. More significant toxicities were observed with bevacizumab plus paclitaxel plus carboplatin compared to paclitaxel plus carboplatin (grade 4 neutropenia: 25.5% vs. 16.8%; grade 5 hemoptysis: 1.2% vs. 0%; and grade 3 hypertension: 6.8% vs. 0.5%) [p.220]. Treatment-related deaths were more common with bevacizumab plus paclitaxel plus carboplatin (15 patients) than with paclitaxel plus carboplatin (2 patients) (P = .001) [p.220]. An analysis found that patients with adenocarcinoma histology receiving bevacizumab plus paclitaxel plus carboplatin had improved survival compared with chemotherapy alone (14.2 vs. 10.3 months) [p.220].

**Pemetrexed-Based Regimens**: A noninferiority trial in 1,725 patients with advanced NSCLC assessed cisplatin plus gemcitabine compared with cisplatin plus pemetrexed [p.220]. Patients with either adenocarcinoma or large cell carcinoma (nonsquamous NSCLC) had improved survival with cisplatin plus pemetrexed (adenocarcinoma: 12.6 vs. 10.9 months) [p.220]. Patients with squamous cell carcinoma had improved survival with the cisplatin plus gemcitabine regimen (10.8 vs. 9.4 months) [p.220]. When compared with the cisplatin plus gemcitabine regimen, the cisplatin plus pemetrexed regimen had significantly lower rates of grade 3 or 4 neutropenia, anemia, and thrombocytopenia (P ≤ .001); febrile neutropenia (P = .002); and alopecia (P < .001) [p.220]. Treatment-related deaths were similar for both regimens (cisplatin plus pemetrexed, 9 patients [1.0%]; cisplatin plus gemcitabine, 6 patients [0.7%]) [p.220]. An analysis of three phase 3 trials confirmed that pemetrexed improves survival for patients with nonsquamous NSCLC in first-line, subsequent, and maintenance therapy [p.220].

**Older Patient Populations**: A phase 3 randomized trial in older patients (70–89 years) with advanced NSCLC reported that combined therapy with weekly paclitaxel and monthly carboplatin improved survival when compared with single-agent therapy using either gemcitabine or vinorelbine (10.3 vs. 6.2 months) [p.220].

### Number of Cycles of First-Line Systemic Therapy

**PARAMOUNT Trial**: Data from the PARAMOUNT trial suggest that 4 cycles of platinum-based therapy is not optimal; tumors can shrink between 4 to 6 cycles of chemotherapy [p.221]. However, patients may not be able to tolerate more than 4 cycles of chemotherapy, and most of the maintenance trials used only 4 cycles of chemotherapy [p.221].

**Meta-Analysis Data**: A meta-analysis suggests that continuing the initial regimen beyond 4 to 6 cycles is associated with increased PFS; however, patients have more adverse events [p.221]. A phase 3 randomized trial suggested that continuing chemotherapy beyond 4 to 6 cycles is not beneficial [p.221]. In this phase 3 trial, taxane-based regimens were used and patients had increasing neurotoxicity as more cycles were used [p.221].

**Pemetrexed vs. Taxane Regimens**: Many patients with adenocarcinoma receive pemetrexed-based regimens and not taxane-based regimens [p.221]. Pemetrexed-based regimens are less toxic than taxane-based regimens [p.221]. Studies report that 60% of patients were able to receive 6 cycles of pemetrexed-based chemotherapy (and had a low incidence of toxicity), whereas only 42% were able to receive more than 5 cycles of taxane-based chemotherapy and often stopped therapy because of neurotoxicity [p.221].

### Response Assessment

The NCCN Guidelines recommend that patients receiving first-line systemic therapy for advanced disease should be evaluated for tumor response with a CT scan [p.221]. Response assessment should occur after 2 cycles of initial therapy and then every 2 to 4 cycles using CT of known or high-risk sites of disease [p.221]. Patients with responsive or stable disease can continue to receive a total of 4 to 6 cycles of systemic therapy [p.221]. The NCCN Guidelines do not recommend continuing chemotherapy beyond 4 to 6 cycles [p.221]. Generally, patients with metastatic NSCLC receive 4 cycles of initial systemic chemotherapy before starting maintenance therapy [p.221]. However, if patients are tolerating the therapy, then 6 cycles of systemic therapy can be considered [p.221].

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### Maintenance Therapy

**Switch Maintenance Pemetrexed**: A phase 3 randomized trial in 663 patients with advanced NSCLC assessed the effect of best supportive care with (or without) switch maintenance pemetrexed in patients who had received platinum-based chemotherapy but whose tumors had not progressed [p.222]. Overall survival was 13.4 months (95% CI, 11.9–15.9) with pemetrexed compared with 10.6 months (95% CI, 8.7–12.0) with placebo (HR, 0.50; 95% CI, 0.42–0.61; P < .0001) [p.222].

**Erlotinib as Maintenance**: IUNO, a phase 3 randomized trial, assessed erlotinib as switch maintenance therapy for patients with nonsquamous NSCLC without EGFR mutations [p.222]. Overall survival and PFS were not improved in patients receiving erlotinib when compared with placebo [p.222]. The NCCN NSCLC Panel does not recommend erlotinib as switch maintenance therapy for patients with nonsquamous NSCLC without EGFR mutations [p.222].

**Gemcitabine Continuation Maintenance**: IFCT-GFPC 0502, a phase 3 randomized trial, compared maintenance therapy with either gemcitabine or erlotinib after initial cytotoxic therapy with cisplatin plus gemcitabine in patients with advanced NSCLC [p.222]. Continuation maintenance therapy with single-agent gemcitabine increased PFS to a greater extent (3.8 months) than switch maintenance therapy with erlotinib (2.9 months) compared with observation (1.9 months) [p.222].

**Docetaxel as Maintenance**: A phase 3 trial assessed switch maintenance therapy with docetaxel given either immediately after chemotherapy or delayed until progression in patients with advanced NSCLC [p.222]. Many patients in the delayed chemotherapy arm did not receive docetaxel [p.222].

### Continuation Maintenance Therapy Recommendations

Depending on first-line therapy, the NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends continuation maintenance therapy options for patients with nonsquamous NSCLC with atezolizumab, atezolizumab plus bevacizumab (category 1), bevacizumab (category 1), bevacizumab plus pemetrexed, cemiplimab-rwlc with or without pemetrexed (category 1), durvalumab with or without pemetrexed (category 1), gemcitabine (category 2B), nivolumab plus ipilimumab, pembrolizumab (category 1), pembrolizumab plus pemetrexed (category 1), or pemetrexed (category 1) [p.223].

Switch maintenance therapy with pemetrexed is a recommended option for patients with nonsquamous NSCLC [p.223]. The benefits of continuation maintenance therapy with gemcitabine were very slight; therefore, the recommendation is only category 2B [p.223].

For patients with squamous cell NSCLC and depending on first-line therapy, the panel recommends continuation maintenance therapy with atezolizumab, cemiplimab-rwlc (category 1), durvalumab, gemcitabine (category 2B), nivolumab plus ipilimumab, or pembrolizumab [p.223]. Previously, the NCCN Panel deleted the recommendation for docetaxel (category 2B) as switch maintenance therapy for patients with squamous cell NSCLC because there are better options [p.223].

### Subsequent (Second-Line and Beyond) Systemic Therapy

**ICI Monotherapy**: If patients have not previously received an ICI, the NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends (category 1) atezolizumab, nivolumab, or pembrolizumab as preferred subsequent therapy options in eligible patients with metastatic NSCLC [p.223]. Human ICI antibodies inhibit the PD-1 receptor or PD-L1, which improves antitumor immunity [p.223].

**Subsequent Therapy After ICI Plus Chemotherapy**: For patients with metastatic NSCLC and disease progression after first-line therapy with ICIs plus chemotherapy, subsequent systemic therapy options include docetaxel (± ramucirumab), gemcitabine, albumin-bound paclitaxel, or pemetrexed (for nonsquamous only), depending on which agent was not previously given [p.224].

**Platinum-Based Doublet After ICI Monotherapy**: Platinum-based doublet therapy is recommended (eg, carboplatin plus paclitaxel) as subsequent systemic therapy options for patients with metastatic NSCLC with disease progression after first-line therapy with single-agent ICIs [p.224].

**Chemotherapy Efficacy**: Docetaxel has been proven superior to best supportive care, vinorelbine, or ifosfamide with improved survival and quality of life [p.224]. When compared with docetaxel, pemetrexed has similar median survival but less toxicity [p.224]. Pemetrexed is a recommended subsequent therapy option for eligible patients with metastatic nonsquamous NSCLC [p.224]. Patients often have a limited response to subsequent therapy other than ICIs, although chemotherapy may serve a useful palliative role [p.224].

**Ramucirumab with Docetaxel**: A phase 3 randomized trial assessed ramucirumab plus docetaxel versus docetaxel as subsequent therapy options for all histologic subtypes [p.224].

### Albumin-Bound Paclitaxel Study

**ABOUND Trial**: A phase 2 trial assessed albumin-bound paclitaxel with or without oral 5-azacitidine as subsequent therapy in 161 patients with advanced nonsquamous NSCLC [p.224]. Median overall survival was 8.1 months with combination therapy and 17 months with single-agent albumin-bound paclitaxel (HR, 1.7; 95% CI, 1.08–2.57). Grade 3 or greater adverse events were reported in 41% of patients receiving combination therapy and 32% of single-agent albumin-bound paclitaxel [p.224].

### Subsequent Therapy Recommendations

The NSCLC Panel recommends the following subsequent therapy options for patients with metastatic NSCLC, depending on which agents have not previously been given: 1) atezolizumab, nivolumab, or pembrolizumab if none has been previously given (all are category 1, preferred); 2) docetaxel (± ramucirumab); 3) gemcitabine; 4) albumin-bound paclitaxel; or 5) pemetrexed (nonsquamous only) [p.224]. In patients with performance status of 3 to 4, best supportive care is recommended [p.224].

After second disease progression, subsequent therapy is recommended for certain patients if the following agents have not already been given: 1) atezolizumab, nivolumab, or pembrolizumab if none has been previously given; 2) docetaxel (± ramucirumab; category 2B for both); 3) gemcitabine (category 2B); 4) albumin-bound paclitaxel (category 2B); or 5) pemetrexed (nonsquamous only) (category 2B) [p.224].

### Erlotinib as Subsequent Therapy

**UNO Trial**: A randomized trial showed that overall survival and PFS were not improved in patients receiving erlotinib as a subsequent therapy option when compared with placebo [p.224]. The NCCN NSCLC Panel does not recommend erlotinib as a subsequent therapy option for patients with nonsquamous NSCLC without EGFR mutations [p.224].

### Afatinib vs. Erlotinib in Squamous Cell NSCLC

A phase 3 randomized trial compared afatinib with erlotinib as subsequent therapy options for patients with squamous cell NSCLC [p.225]. Overall survival was 7.9 months (95% CI, 7.2–8.7) for afatinib versus 6.8 months (95% CI, 5.9–7.8) for erlotinib (HR, 0.81; 95% CI, 0.69–0.95; P = .0077) [p.225]. Almost 60% of patients in each arm had grade 3 or higher adverse events. In contrast, the median overall survival was 9.2 months with nivolumab compared with 6.0 months for docetaxel for patients with squamous cell NSCLC [p.225]. In addition, only 7% of patients receiving nivolumab had grade 3 or higher adverse events. The NCCN NSCLC Panel does not recommend erlotinib as a subsequent therapy option for patients with squamous cell NSCLC based on this study comparing afatinib with erlotinib [p.225]. Erlotinib or afatinib are not recommended as subsequent therapy options for patients with squamous cell NSCLC; they are less efficacious and safe compared to other available options [p.225].

## Special Considerations

### Squamous Cell Carcinoma Treatment Options

For patients with metastatic squamous cell NSCLC and negative test results for actionable driver mutations, chemotherapy plus immunotherapy regimens—such as pembrolizumab plus carboplatin with either paclitaxel or albumin-bound paclitaxel—are recommended options (category 1; preferred) [p.220]. For patients with metastatic squamous cell NSCLC who have contraindications to ICIs, recommended options include cisplatin plus gemcitabine (category 1) [p.220].

The NCCN NSCLC Panel does not recommend carboplatin plus etoposide, carboplatin plus vinorelbine, cisplatin plus vinorelbine, cisplatin plus gemcitabine plus necitumumab, etoposide, irinotecan, and vinorelbine for patients with metastatic squamous cell NSCLC and negative test results for actionable driver mutations, because these regimens are rarely used in the United States [p.220]. Regimens containing pemetrexed or bevacizumab are not recommended for squamous cell carcinoma [p.220-221]. Currently, fewer treatment options are available for patients with squamous cell carcinoma compared with nonsquamous NSCLC [p.221].

### Bevacizumab Biosimilars

The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends that bevacizumab biosimilars may be used in any of the systemic therapy regimens containing bevacizumab that are used for eligible patients with metastatic NSCLC [p.220].

### Brain Metastases Considerations

Previously, patients with brain metastases were excluded from receiving bevacizumab because of concerns about CNS hemorrhage; however, data suggest that bevacizumab can be used in patients with treated CNS metastases [p.220].

### Chemotherapy Toxicity and Patient Selection

Systemic therapy for older patients with advanced NSCLC needs to be carefully selected to avoid adverse reactions [p.220]. Pemetrexed-based regimens are less toxic than taxane-based regimens, allowing more patients to tolerate 6 cycles of treatment [p.221].

### Immunotherapy Efficacy Measures

Traditional RECIST response criteria (1.1) are used to assess response for most types of systemic therapy, but different response criteria may be useful for assessing response in patients receiving PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors [p.223].
