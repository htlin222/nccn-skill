## Primary Risk Factor: Tobacco Smoking

**Magnitude of Risk** [p.131]
- The primary risk factor for lung cancer is smoking tobacco, which accounts for most lung cancer-related deaths [p.131]
- Approximately 350 people die every day from lung cancer in the United States; most of these deaths (81%) are caused by cigarette smoking [p.131]
- Approximately 85% to 90% of cases of lung cancer are caused by cigarette smoking [p.131]

**Carcinogenic Exposure** [p.131]
- Cigarette smoke contains many carcinogenic chemicals including:
  - Nitrosamines [p.131]
  - Benzo(a)pyrene diol epoxide [p.131]

**Dose-Response Relationship** [p.131]
- Risk for lung cancer increases with:
  - The number of packs of cigarettes smoked per day [p.131]
  - The number of years spent smoking (i.e., pack-years of smoking history) [p.131]

## Secondary Smoke Exposure

**Risk from Secondhand Smoke** [p.131]
- Individuals who do not smoke have an increased relative risk (RR = 1.24) of developing lung cancer if exposed to tobacco smoke from those who smoke (secondhand smoke) [p.131]
- Other studies have reported a more modest risk (hazard ratio [HR], 1.05) [p.131]
- Those who do not smoke but live with someone who smokes have an increased risk for lung cancer due to secondhand smoke [p.131]

## Other Possible Risk Factors

**Disease History** [p.131]
- COPD [p.131]
- Cancer history [p.131]
- Family history of lung cancer [p.131]

**Environmental Exposures** [p.131]
- The International Agency for Research on Cancer lists several agents known to cause lung cancer:
  - Arsenic [p.131]
  - Asbestos [p.131]
  - Beryllium [p.131]
  - Cadmium [p.131]
  - Chromium [p.131]
  - Coal smoke [p.131]
  - Diesel fumes [p.131]
  - Nickel [p.131]
  - Silica [p.131]
  - Soot [p.131]
  - Uranium [p.131]

**Asbestos Exposure** [p.131]
- Asbestos is a known carcinogen that increases the risk for lung cancer in people exposed to airborne fibers [p.131]
- Risk is especially increased in individuals who smoke cigarettes [p.131]
- About 3% to 4% of lung cancers are caused by asbestos exposure [p.131]
- Asbestos also causes pleural mesothelioma [p.131]

**Radon Exposure** [p.131]
- Radon gas, a radioactive gas produced by the decay of radium 226, seems to cause lung cancer [p.131]

## Hormone Replacement Therapy

**Unclear Association** [p.131]
- It is not clear whether hormone replacement therapy (HRT) affects the risk for lung cancer in women [p.131]
- More than 20 studies have been published, but results have been inconsistent [p.131]

**Estrogen Plus Progestin HRT** [p.131]
- In a large randomized controlled study, no increase in the incidence of lung cancer was found among postmenopausal women treated with estrogen plus progestin HRT [p.131]
- However, the risk of death increased in those with NSCLC [p.131]

**Estrogen-Only HRT** [p.131]
- In women who received estrogen alone, the incidence or risk of death from lung cancer did not increase [p.131]

## Smoking Cessation

### Clinical Evidence

**Patient Demographics with Smoking History** [p.131]
- Of patients aged 20 to 49 years who were recently diagnosed with lung cancer:
  - 81% of men had smoked [p.131]
  - 72% of women had smoked [p.131]

**Smoker Risk Profiles** [p.131]
- Active smoking causes lung cancer [p.131]
- Individuals who formerly smoked are at increased risk for lung cancer compared with those who have never smoked [p.131]

**Broader Health Impacts** [p.131]
- There is a causal relationship between active smoking and lung cancer [p.131]
- There is also a causal relationship between smoking and other cancers:
  - Bladder [p.131]
  - Cervical [p.131]
  - Colorectal [p.131]
  - Esophageal [p.131]
  - Gastric [p.131]
  - Kidney [p.131]
  - Laryngeal [p.131]
  - Oral cavity [p.131]
  - Ovarian [p.131]
  - Pancreatic [p.131]
  - Pharyngeal [p.131]
- Smoking harms nearly every organ in the body [p.131]
- Individuals who smoke have increased mortality compared with those who do not smoke [p.131]

**Nicotine Dependence** [p.131]
- Cigarettes contain nicotine, which is a highly addictive substance [p.131]

### Clinical Recommendations

**Oncologist Role** [p.131]
- Oncologists should encourage smoking cessation, especially in patients with cancer [p.131]
- The 5 A's framework is a useful tool: Ask, Advise, Assess, Assist, Arrange [p.131]

**Patient Benefits** [p.131]
- It is in the best interest of patients to quit smoking tobacco [p.131]
- Persistent smoking is associated with:
  - Second primary cancers [p.131]
  - Treatment complications [p.131]
  - Decreased survival [p.131]

**Surgical Considerations** [p.131]
- Some surgeons will not operate on individuals who currently smoke because active smoking may increase postoperative pulmonary complications [p.131]
- Active smoking should not be used to exclude patients with NSCLC from treatment consideration [p.131]
