## Summary of Key Evidence

This section covers key immune biomarker testing (TMB), and comprehensive treatment approaches including surgery, radiation therapy, and combined modality therapy for non-small cell lung cancer [p.151-160].

## Testing for Immune Biomarkers (Continued)

### Tumor Mutational Burden (TMB)

TMB is an approximate measure of the total number of somatic mutations [p.151]. Theoretically, high TMB levels will correlate with high neoantigen levels that will activate an antitumor immune response [p.151]. TMB levels are typically high in patients with NSCLC who currently or previously smoked cigarettes, while low TMB is more commonly detected in individuals who have never smoked [p.151].

Preliminary data from CHECKMATE 227, a phase 3 randomized trial with complex design, suggested TMB might be a useful immune biomarker for deciding whether to use immunotherapy in metastatic NSCLC [p.151]. However, updated data from CHECKMATE 227 showed that overall survival was improved with nivolumab plus ipilimumab regardless of TMB or PD-L1 expression levels [p.151]. Additionally, combining TMB with PD-L1 expression level did not correlate with overall survival [p.151].

Several trials have shown that high TMB levels do not correlate with PD-L1 expression levels in patients with NSCLC [p.151]. KEYNOTE 158, a phase 2 trial assessing TMB levels in patients with solid tumors who received pembrolizumab as second-line therapy, did not include NSCLC patients [p.151]. TMB does not identify patients who will respond to chemotherapy; therefore, TMB has limited value for assessing combination immunotherapy plus chemotherapy regimens [p.151]. TMB is also not an ideal immune biomarker because some patients with low TMB levels respond to immunotherapy and others with high levels do not respond [p.151].

Technical problems exist with measuring TMB, including: 1) lack of agreement on the definition of a cutoff for designating high TMB levels; and 2) lack of standardization of TMB measurements across laboratories [p.151]. PD-L1 expression level is a more useful immune biomarker than TMB for deciding how to use immunotherapy, because test results are obtained more quickly, less tissue is needed for testing, and data demonstrate relative reproducibility across platforms and individuals [p.151].

In 2020, the NCCN Panel removed TMB as an emerging immune biomarker for patients with metastatic NSCLC based on clinical trial data, concerns about variable TMB measurements, and other issues [p.151]. The NCCN Guidelines do not recommend measurement of TMB levels before deciding whether to use nivolumab plus ipilimumab regimens or other ICIs, such as pembrolizumab [p.151].

## Treatment Approaches

Surgery, radiation therapy (RT), and systemic therapy are the three modalities most commonly used to treat patients with NSCLC and can be used either alone or in combination depending on disease status [p.151]. Older adults may be at risk for treatment-related adverse events [p.151].

## Surgery

### General Principles

In general, surgery provides the best chance for cure in patients with stage I or II disease [p.151]. Thoracic surgical oncology consultation should be part of the evaluation of any patient being considered for curative local therapy [p.151]. It is essential to determine whether patients can tolerate surgery or whether they are medically inoperable; some patients deemed inoperable may be able to tolerate minimally invasive surgery and/or sublobar resection [p.151].

Although frailty is an increasingly recognized predictor of surgical and other treatment morbidity, a preferred frailty assessment system has not been established [p.152].

Determination of resectability, surgical staging, and pulmonary resection should be performed by thoracic surgeons who should participate in multidisciplinary clinics and/or tumor boards for patients with lung cancer [p.152]. Surgery may be appropriate for select patients with uncommon types of lung cancer (e.g., superior sulcus, chest wall involvement) [p.152]. Patients with clinical stage IB or greater disease, or high-risk factors, should be referred to a medical oncologist for evaluation. Patients with stage IIIA NSCLC deemed resectable should be considered for referral to a radiation oncologist [p.152].

The surgical procedure used depends on the extent of disease and cardiopulmonary reserve of the patient [p.152]. A preoperative or intraoperative tissue diagnosis of lung cancer should be established before doing a lobectomy, bilobectomy, or pneumonectomy [p.152]. If a tissue diagnosis appears risky or unreliable, multidisciplinary evaluation is recommended to determine the safest approach, or to provide consensus that a biopsy is too risky or difficult [p.152].

### Surgical Resection Techniques

Lung-sparing anatomic resection (sleeve lobectomy) is preferred over pneumonectomy, if anatomically appropriate and if margin-negative resection can be achieved; lobectomy or pneumonectomy should be done if physiologically feasible [p.152].

Sublobar resection, either segmentectomy (preferred) or wedge resection, is appropriate in select patients: 1) those not eligible for lobectomy; and 2) those with a peripheral nodule 2 cm or less with very low-risk features [p.152]. Segmentectomy (preferred) or wedge resection should achieve parenchymal resection margins that are: 1) 2 cm or more; or 2) the size of the nodule or larger [p.152]. Resection (including wedge resection) is preferred over ablation [p.152]. Wide wedge resection may improve outcomes [p.152].

Patients with medically inoperable early-stage NSCLC may be candidates for definitive RT, preferably SABR, also known as stereotactic body RT (SBRT) [p.152]. If SABR is considered for patients at high risk for surgical morbidity, a multidisciplinary evaluation is recommended [p.152].

### Lymph Node Dissection

ACOSOG Z0030, a randomized phase 3 trial, compared systematic mediastinal lymph node sampling versus complete lymphadenectomy during pulmonary resection in patients with NSCLC who had either N0 or N1 disease [p.152]. In patients with early-stage NSCLC who had negative nodes by systematic lymph node dissection, complete mediastinal lymph node dissection did not improve survival [p.152]. Thus, systematic lymph node sampling is appropriate during pulmonary resection; one or more nodes should be sampled from all mediastinal stations [p.152]. For right-sided cancers, an adequate mediastinal lymphadenectomy should include stations 2R, 4R, 7, 8, and 9. For left-sided cancers, stations 4L, 5, 6, 7, 8, and 9 should be sampled [p.152].

Patients should have N1 and N2 node resection and mapping with a minimum of three N2 stations sampled or a complete lymph node dissection [p.152]. Formal ipsilateral mediastinal lymph node dissection is indicated for patients undergoing resection for stage IIIA (N2) disease [p.152-153]. For patients undergoing sublobar resection, appropriate N1 and N2 lymph node stations should be sampled unless not technically feasible [p.153].

### Thoracoscopic Lobectomy

Video-assisted thoracic surgery (VATS), also known as thoracoscopic lobectomy, is a minimally invasive surgical treatment [p.153]. Published studies suggest that thoracoscopic lobectomy has several advantages over lobectomy by thoracotomy [p.153]. Acute and chronic pain associated with thoracoscopic lobectomy is minimal; thus, this procedure requires shorter hospitalization [p.153]. Thoracoscopic lobectomy is also associated with low postoperative morbidity and mortality, minimal risk of intraoperative bleeding, and minimal locoregional recurrence [p.153].

In patients with stage I NSCLC who had thoracoscopic lobectomy with lymph node dissection, the 5-year survival rate, long-term survival, and local recurrence rate were comparable to those achieved by routine open lung resection [p.153]. Thoracoscopic lobectomy has improved discharge independence in older populations and patients at high risk [p.153]. Data show that thoracoscopic lobectomy improves the ability of patients to complete postoperative chemotherapy regimens [p.153].

Based on favorable effects on postoperative recovery and morbidity, thoracoscopic lobectomy (including robotic-assisted approaches) is recommended as an acceptable approach for surgically resectable patients as long as principles of thoracic surgery are not compromised [p.153]. Robotic VATS seems to be more expensive with longer operating times than conventional VATS [p.153].

### Stage IIIA N2 Disease

Before treatment, it is essential to carefully evaluate for N2 disease using radiologic and invasive staging (EBUS-guided procedures, mediastinoscopy, thoracoscopic procedures) and to discuss whether surgery is appropriate in a multidisciplinary team [p.153]. Randomized controlled trials suggest that surgery does not increase survival in patients with pathologically documented stage IIIA (N2) disease [p.153]. However, one of these trials (EORTC) only enrolled patients with unresectable disease [p.153]. Most clinicians agree that resection is appropriate for patients with negative preoperative mediastinal nodes and with a single positive node (<3 cm) found at thoracotomy [p.153].

Neoadjuvant (preoperative or induction) systemic therapy is recommended for select patients with resectable NSCLC [p.153]. The optimal timing of RT in trimodality therapy (preoperative with chemotherapy or postoperative) is not established and is controversial [p.153-154]. Adding RT to induction regimens for patients with stage IIIA (N2) disease has been associated with higher pathological response but similar overall survival when compared with preoperative chemotherapy [p.154].

For patients with completely resected stage IIIA (N2) NSCLC who received neoadjuvant or adjuvant chemotherapy, data from the LungART and PORT-C trials show that postoperative RT did not improve survival compared with no postoperative RT, although locoregional control was significantly improved [p.154]. Postoperative RT may be considered for select patients with negative margins and high-risk N2 disease [p.154].

It is controversial whether pneumonectomy after preoperative chemoradiotherapy is appropriate [p.154]. Clinicians agree that resection is not appropriate for patients with multiple pathologically proven malignant lymph nodes greater than 3 cm; definitive chemoradiotherapy is recommended for these patients [p.154]. Patients with resectable stage IIIA (N2) disease should not be excluded from surgery, because some may have long-term survival or may be cured [p.154].

The NCCN NSCLC Panel believes that surgery may be appropriate for select patients with N2 disease [p.154]. A 2021 survey of NCCN Member Institutions found that more institutions now use neoadjuvant chemotherapy compared with neoadjuvant chemoradiation in patients with N2 disease. Many (66%) of NCCN Member Institutions use induction chemotherapy, whereas 33% use induction chemoradiation [p.154]. Before surgery, most institutions require at least stable disease after induction therapy but do not require radiologic or pathologic response. All NCCN institutions consider surgery for single-station non-bulky N2 disease, while 50% consider surgery for single-station bulky disease, 39% for multi-station non-bulky disease, and 21% for multi-station bulky disease [p.154].

## Radiation Therapy

### General Principles

Treatment recommendations should be made by a multidisciplinary team [p.154]. Because RT has a potential role in all stages of NSCLC, as either definitive or palliative therapy, input from radiation oncologists who perform lung cancer RT as a prominent part of their practice should be part of the multidisciplinary evaluation [p.154]. RT recommendations include: 1) definitive therapy for locally advanced NSCLC, generally combined with chemotherapy; 2) definitive therapy for early-stage NSCLC in patients with contraindications for surgery; 3) neoadjuvant or adjuvant therapy for selected patients treated with surgery; 4) therapy for limited progression; and/or 5) palliative therapy for incurable NSCLC [p.154].

The goals of RT are to maximize tumor control and minimize treatment toxicity [p.154]. Advanced technologies such as 4D-CT simulation, intensity-modulated RT/volumetric modulated arc therapy (IMRT/VMAT), image-guided RT (IGRT), motion management strategies, and proton therapy have been shown to reduce toxicity and increase survival [p.154].

A secondary analysis of the RTOG 0617 randomized trial reported that 2-year overall survival, PFS, local failure, and distant metastasis-free survival were not significantly different for IMRT when compared with older 3D-conformal RT (3D-CRT) despite higher tumor burden in patients treated with IMRT. However, IMRT yielded lower rates of severe pneumonitis compared with 3D-CRT (3.5% vs. 7.9%; P = .039) [p.155]. CT-planned 3D-CRT is now considered the minimum technological standard, with IMRT preferred [p.155].

### Radiation Simulation, Planning, and Delivery

Simulation should be performed using CT scans obtained in the RT treatment position [p.155]. Intravenous contrast CT scans, with or without oral contrast, are recommended for better target delineation whenever possible, especially in patients with central tumors or nodal involvement [p.155]. FDG PET/CT can significantly improve target delineation accuracy, especially when there is atelectasis or contraindications to intravenous CT contrast [p.155]. Ideally, target delineation should be based on PET/CT obtained no more than 4 weeks before treatment because of potential for rapid NSCLC progression [p.155].

Respiratory motion should be managed [p.155]. The report from the AAPM Task Group 76 is a useful reference for implementing motion management strategies [p.155].

### Target Volumes, Prescription Doses, and Normal Tissue Dose Constraints

Commonly used prescription RT doses and normal tissue dose constraints are summarized in the NSCLC algorithm [p.155]. Reports from the International Commission on Radiation Units and Measurements provide a formalism for defining RT target volumes [p.155]. The ACR Practice Parameters and Technical Standards are also a helpful reference [p.155]. It is essential to evaluate the dose-volume histogram (DVH) of critical structures and to limit doses to organs at risk (such as spinal cord, lungs, heart, esophagus, and brachial plexus) to minimize normal tissue toxicity [p.155].

For patients receiving postoperative RT (PORT), stricter DVH parameters should be considered for the lungs [p.155]. The QUANTEC review provides the most comprehensive estimates from clinical data of dose-response relationships for normal tissue complications [p.155].

The normal tissue dose constraints for conventionally fractionated RT are based on a survey of radiation oncologists at NCCN Member Institutions [p.155]. These constraints are mainly empirical and have not been validated rigorously [p.155]. Therefore, doses and constraints provided are not specific prescriptive recommendations; they are useful reference doses [p.155]. A caveat is that these constraints represent doses that generally should not be exceeded [p.155]. Because risk of toxicity increases progressively with dose to normal tissues, a key principle is to keep normal tissue doses "as low as reasonably achievable" while adequately covering the target [p.155]. After surgery, lung tolerance to RT is much less than for patients with intact lungs; therefore, more conservative constraints should be used for postoperative RT [p.155].

### Definitive RT Dose

For definitive RT, the commonly prescribed dose is 60 to 70 Gy in 2 Gy fractions over 6 to 7 weeks [p.155]. RTOG 0617, a phase 3 randomized trial, suggests that high-dose radiation using 74 Gy with concurrent chemotherapy does not improve survival, and might be harmful, when compared with a dose of 60 Gy [p.155].

In this trial and subsequent retrospective analyses, higher dose to the heart and specific cardiac substructures, such as the coronary arteries, was associated with increased mortality [p.156]. Although optimal RT dose intensification remains a valid question, the NCCN Panel does not currently recommend a high dose of 74 Gy for routine use [p.156].

### General Treatment Information

Definitive RT, preferably SABR, is recommended for patients with early-stage NSCLC (stage I-II, N0) who are medically inoperable or those who refuse surgery [p.156]. SABR is also an option for patients at high surgical risk who cannot tolerate lobectomy because of major medical comorbidity and/or severely limited lung function [p.156]. After definitive RT (preferably SABR), adjuvant chemotherapy may be considered in patients with high-risk factors for recurrence [p.156].

Image-guided thermal ablation (cryotherapy, microwave, radiofrequency ablation [RFA]) is an option for selected patients who are medically inoperable and will not receive SABR or definitive RT [p.156]. Resection is recommended for patients with early-stage NSCLC who are medically fit [p.156]. In patients with clinical stage I or II NSCLC who are upstaged to N2+ after surgery, postoperative chemotherapy can be administered, with concurrent postoperative RT for patients with positive margins, or followed by postoperative RT in selected patients with high-risk features [p.156].

Definitive chemoradiation is recommended for patients with stage II to III disease who are not appropriate surgical candidates [p.156]. For patients with locally advanced NSCLC (stage III), the most commonly prescribed conventionally fractionated doses for definitive RT are 60 to 70 Gy in 2 Gy fractions. Doses of at least 60 Gy should be given [p.156]. Involved-field RT is recommended for treating nodal disease in patients with locally advanced NSCLC [p.156].

### Stage IIIA (N2) Disease

The optimal care of patients with potentially operable stage IIIA (N2) NSCLC is controversial [p.156]. Before surgical resection of stage IIIA NSCLC, induction systemic therapy or induction chemoradiotherapy is recommended to potentially shrink the tumor [p.156]. Preoperative systemic therapy and postoperative RT is an option for patients with resectable stage IIIA NSCLC with minimal N2 disease who can be treated with lobectomy [p.156]. Preoperative concurrent chemoradiation is recommended for resectable superior sulcus tumors and is an option for other resectable stage IIA to IIIA NSCLC [p.156].

A 2021 survey of NCCN Member Institutions found that 66% use induction chemotherapy, whereas 33% use induction chemoradiation before surgery for stage IIIA N2 disease [p.156]. The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends a preoperative RT dose of 45 to 54 Gy in 1.8 to 2 Gy fractions over 5 weeks [p.156]. Definitive RT doses delivered as preoperative chemo/RT can safely be administered and achieve promising nodal clearance and survival rates [p.156-157]. About 50% of NCCN Member Institutions would consider pneumonectomy after preoperative chemotherapy, whereas only 25% do not consider pneumonectomy absolutely contraindicated after induction chemoradiation [p.156].

Surgery is associated with potentially greater risk of complications, particularly stump breakdown and bronchopleural fistula, in fields that have received high-dose RT [p.157]. Thus, surgeons are often wary of resection in areas that have previously received RT doses of more than 45 to 50 Gy, especially in patients who have received definitive doses of preoperative concurrent chemoradiation [p.157]. Soft tissue flap coverage and reduced intraoperative fluid administration and ventilator pressures can reduce these complications [p.157].

When giving preoperative RT to less than definitive doses, one should be prepared to continue to a full definitive dose of RT without interruption if the patient does not proceed to surgery [p.157]. When considering trimodality therapy, the treatment plan—including assessment for resectability and type of resection—should be decided before initiation of therapy [p.157]. In postoperative RT, the clinical target volume (CTV) includes the bronchial stump and high-risk draining lymph node stations [p.157].

Standard RT doses after complete resection are 50 to 54 Gy in 1.8 to 2 Gy fractions over 5 to 6 weeks, but a boost may be considered to high-risk regions [p.157]. Lung dose constraints should be more conservative, because tolerance appears reduced after surgery [p.157]. The LungART and PORT-C trials provide useful guidelines for postoperative RT technique [p.157]. Highly conformal techniques (IMRT or proton therapy) to minimize lung and heart doses are preferred [p.157].

### Advanced/Metastatic Disease and Oligometastatic Disease

For patients with advanced lung cancer (stage IV) with extensive metastases, systemic therapy is recommended; palliative RT can be used for symptom relief and potentially for prophylaxis [p.157]. Shorter courses of palliative RT are preferred for patients with symptomatic chest disease who have poor performance status and/or shorter life expectancy [p.157]. Higher dose and longer course thoracic RT (≥30 Gy in 10 fractions) are associated with modestly improved survival and symptoms, especially in patients with good performance status [p.157].

Oligometastatic disease is heterogenous and refers to limited metastatic sites or disease burden; management is evolving [p.157]. Definitive local therapy to oligometastases (including brain and lung) achieves prolonged survival in a small proportion of well-selected patients [p.157]. Definitive RT to oligometastases, particularly SABR, is an appropriate option if it can be delivered safely [p.157].

In two randomized phase II trials, significantly longer PFS was found for local consolidative therapy (RT or surgery) to primary and oligometastatic lesions versus maintenance systemic therapy or observation for patients not progressing on systemic therapy [p.157]. Updated data from one of the trials show that median overall survival was longer for patients with oligometastatic NSCLC who received local consolidative therapy (median, 41.2 months; 95% CI, 18.9 months–not reached) compared with those receiving maintenance therapy or observation (median, 17.0 months; 95% CI, 10.1–39.8 months; P = .017) [p.157]. A phase 2 trial of consolidative RT for oligometastatic NSCLC (n = 29) reported median overall survival of 28.4 months (95% CI, 14.5–45.8 months) [p.157]. The NCCN Guidelines recommend that local therapy (RT, SABR, or surgery) to primary and oligometastatic lesions should be considered for patients without progression on systemic therapy [p.157].

## Stereotactic Ablative Radiotherapy

SABR (also known as SBRT) uses short courses of highly conformal and dose-intensive (ablative) RT precisely delivered to limited-size targets [p.157]. SABR has achieved good primary tumor control rates and overall survival that is higher than conventionally fractionated RT [p.157].

Studies, including prospective multi-institutional trials, have demonstrated the efficacy of SABR for patients with inoperable stage I NSCLC or for those who refuse surgery [p.158]. With conventionally fractionated RT, 3-year survival is only about 20% to 35% in these patients, with local failure rates of about 40% to 60% [p.158]. In prospective clinical trials, local control and overall survival appear considerably increased with SABR, generally more than 85%, and about 60% at 3 years (median survival, 4 years), respectively, in medically inoperable patients [p.158].

A 7-year follow-up of 65 patients with medically inoperable stage I NSCLC reported overall survival rates of 55.7% at 5 years and 47.5% at 7 years [p.158]. In 12 patients (18.5%), a second primary lung carcinoma developed after SABR at a median of 35 months; 27% (18/65) had disease recurrence at a median of 14.5 months after SABR [p.158].

Although SABR is not proven equivalent to lobectomy for patients with operable early-stage NSCLC, some prospective series have shown similar overall survival and cancer-specific survival [p.158]. A combined analysis of two randomized trials compared SABR to lobectomy, with 3-year overall survival of 95% and 79%, respectively [p.158]. A single-arm SABR expansion cohort with longer follow-up compared non-randomly to a propensity-matched contemporary cohort treated with VATS lobectomy, with 5-year overall survival of 87% and 84%, respectively [p.158]. These analyses do not provide sufficient data to change the standard of care for good surgical candidates but help confirm the indication for SABR in patients with relative contraindications to surgery or those who refuse surgery [p.158]. SABR can also be used for patients with limited lung metastases or limited metastases to other body sites [p.158].

SABR is recommended in the NSCLC algorithm for patients with stage I and II (T1-3,N0,M0) NSCLC who are medically inoperable; SABR is a reasonable alternative to surgery for patients with potentially operable disease who are high risk, older, or refuse surgery [p.158]. If possible, biopsy should confirm NSCLC before use of SABR [p.158]. Decisions about whether to recommend SABR should be based on multidisciplinary discussion [p.158].

Locoregional recurrences can occur after SABR [p.158]. Late recurrences have been reported more than 5 years after SABR, highlighting the need for careful surveillance [p.158]. After SABR, assessment of recurrences by imaging can be challenging because of benign inflammatory/fibrotic changes that can remain FDG-PET avid for 2 or more years after treatment [p.158]. This careful follow-up is particularly relevant, because selected patients with recurrences after SABR may benefit from surgery or re-treatment with SABR [p.158].

SABR fractionation regimens and a limited subset of historically used maximum dose constraints are provided in the NSCLC algorithm; 1 to 5 fractions are generally used [p.159]. In the United States, only regimens of 5 fractions or less meet the arbitrary billing code definition for SBRT; however, slightly more protracted ablative regimens are also appropriate [p.159]. Prescription doses do not completely describe actual delivered doses [p.159]. Maximum dose constraints are for reference and are not intended to be prescriptive [p.159].

The bronchial tree, esophagus, and brachial plexus are critical structures for SABR [p.159]. For centrally located tumors—defined variably as within 2 cm of the proximal bronchial tree and/or abutting mediastinal pleura—regimens of 54 to 60 Gy in 3 fractions are not safe and should be avoided; 4- to 10-fraction SABR regimens appear effective and safe [p.159]. The RTOG 0813 trial evaluated 5-fraction regimens and found no high-grade toxicities at 50 Gy in 5 fractions [p.159].

SRS or SABR for limited oligometastases to the brain or other body sites is recommended for patients with good performance status if their thoracic disease can be treated with definitive therapy [p.159]. SRS or SABR can be considered for select patients with stage M1c disease who have limited metastases amenable to treatment; clinical trials have included up to 3 to 5 metastatic sites [p.159]. For patients with disease progression on targeted therapy for ALK rearrangements, ROS1 rearrangements, or common EGFR mutations, consideration of local therapy is recommended for limited progression [p.159].

Hypofractionated or dose-intensified conventional 3D-CRT is a less preferred option if an established SABR program is not available [p.159]. Nonrandomized clinical data indicate that local tumor control with SABR is higher than with image-guided thermal ablation techniques [p.159]. Image-guided thermal ablation (cryotherapy, microwave, radiofrequency) may be an option for selected patients who will not be receiving SABR or definitive RT [p.159].

## Whole Brain RT and Stereotactic Radiosurgery

Many patients with NSCLC have brain metastases (30%-50%), which substantially affect quality of life [p.159]. Control of brain metastases preserves neurocognitive function [p.159]. However, whole brain RT is itself associated with measurable declines in neurocognitive function in clinical trials, particularly with increasing dose and advanced age [p.159].

For limited brain metastases, randomized trials have found that the addition of whole brain RT to SRS decreases intracranial recurrence but does not improve survival and may increase the risk of cognitive decline [p.159]. Thus, SRS alone is recommended for patients with limited brain metastases [p.159]. A randomized trial assessed cognitive function in 213 patients with 1 to 3 brain metastases who received SRS alone versus SRS with whole brain RT [p.159]. At 3 months after SRS alone, patients had less cognitive deterioration (40/63 patients [63.5%]) than those receiving SRS plus whole brain RT (44/48 patients [91.7%]; difference, -28.2%; 90% CI, -41.9% to -14.4%; P < .001) [p.159].

Resection followed by SRS to the cavity (instead of resection followed by whole brain RT) may decrease the risk of neurocognitive impairment [p.159]. A randomized trial demonstrated that using IMRT to avoid the hippocampus decreases memory impairment after whole brain RT [p.159]. A phase 3 randomized trial assessed optimal supportive care (including dexamethasone) with whole brain RT versus optimal supportive care alone in patients with NSCLC and brain metastases not eligible for brain surgery or SRS [p.159]. Overall survival was similar between the groups (HR, 1.06; 95% CI, 0.90–1.26). Overall quality of life, use of dexamethasone, and reported adverse events were also similar [p.160].

Two retrospective analyses have reported increased survival in patients with brain metastases who received SRS and concurrent ICI therapy [p.160].

Treatment options for limited brain metastases in patients with NSCLC include: 1) SRS alone; and 2) surgical resection for selected patients followed by SRS or whole brain RT [p.160]. Selected patients include those with symptomatic lesions or whose tumor tissue is needed for diagnosis [p.160]. Decisions about whether to recommend SRS alone or brain surgery followed by whole brain RT or SRS should be based on multidisciplinary discussion [p.160]. Treatment should be individualized for patients with recurrent or progressive brain lesions [p.160]. For extensive brain metastases, whole brain RT (with hippocampal avoidance if eligible) and memantine are recommended [p.160].

## Combined Modality Therapy

### Early-Stage Disease

As previously mentioned, surgery provides the best chance for cure for patients with stage I or II disease who are medically fit and can tolerate surgery [p.160]. SABR can be considered for patients with medically inoperable or high-risk stage I or II (T1-3,N0) disease or those who refuse surgery [p.160]. In patients with completely resected NSCLC, adjuvant (postoperative) chemotherapy has been shown to improve survival in patients with early-stage disease [p.160].

Some studies suggest that preoperative chemotherapy (also referred to as neoadjuvant chemotherapy or induction chemotherapy) is as effective as and better tolerated than postoperative chemotherapy [p.160]. A randomized trial found no difference in survival with preoperative versus postoperative chemotherapy [p.160]. The NCCN Guidelines state that patients with stage II or IIIA (T3,N1) disease may be treated with induction systemic therapy before surgery if they would have been candidates for adjuvant chemotherapy after surgery [p.160].

### Concurrent versus Sequential Chemoradiation

Concurrent chemoradiation is more efficacious than sequential chemoradiation for patients with unresectable stage III disease [p.160].

### Advanced Disease Considerations

Cytotoxic chemotherapeutic agents can cause hair loss, which is distressing for patients [p.160]. Data in women with non-metastatic breast cancer suggest that a scalp cooling device may help reduce hair loss in patients receiving cytotoxic chemotherapy regimens [p.160].

For patients with stage IV disease who have a good performance status, platinum-based chemotherapy with or without immunotherapy is beneficial [p.160]. Data show that early palliative care combined with systemic therapy improved quality of life, mood, and survival in patients with metastatic NSCLC, even if these patients had less aggressive end-of-life care [p.160]. Patients should receive treatment for debilitating symptoms [p.160]. A study also suggests that social support, such as being married, is as effective as systemic therapy [p.160]. Data suggest that systematic symptom monitoring during outpatient chemotherapy treatment increases overall survival when compared with usual care [p.160].

Surgery is rarely recommended for patients with stage IV disease [p.160]. However, surgical resection of limited brain progression may improve survival in selected patients with stage IV disease [p.160]. Definitive local therapy with surgical resection or RT is recommended for limited single-organ progression located in sites other than the brain if definitive thoracic therapy is feasible [p.160].
## Summary of Key Evidence

This section covers surgery followed by adjuvant therapy (chemotherapy and targeted therapy), preoperative chemotherapy with or without immunotherapy, chemoradiation, and systemic therapy for metastatic non-small cell lung cancer [p.161-170].

## Surgery Followed by Adjuvant Therapy

### Postoperative Chemotherapy

#### Trial Evidence

The International Adjuvant Lung Cancer Trial (IALT) assessed cisplatin-based postoperative therapy in patients with completely resected stage I, II, or III NSCLC [p.161]. The study included 1867 patients randomly assigned to cisplatin-based postoperative chemotherapy or observation, with median 56-month follow-up [p.161]. The 5-year survival rate was 45% for cisplatin-based therapy versus 40% for observation (HR for death, 0.86; 95% CI, 0.76–0.98; P < .03); disease-free survival rate was 39% versus 34% (HR, 0.83; 95% CI, 0.74–0.94; P < .003) [p.161]. However, after 7.5 years follow-up, there were more deaths in the chemotherapy group and the benefit decreased over time [p.161]. Data show that postoperative chemotherapy prevents recurrences [p.161].

The NCIC CTG JBR.10 trial and ANITA trial compared postoperative vinorelbine plus cisplatin versus observation in early-stage NSCLC [p.161]. In JBR.10, 482 patients (ECOG PS 0-1) with completely resected stage IB or stage II NSCLC were randomly assigned to vinorelbine plus cisplatin or observation [p.161]. Postoperative chemotherapy significantly prolonged overall survival compared with observation (94 vs. 73 months; HR for death, 0.69; P = .04) and relapse-free survival (not reached vs. 47 months; HR for recurrence, 0.60; P < .001) [p.161]. The 5-year survival rates were 69% and 54%, respectively (P = .03) [p.161]. Updated data from JBR.10 after 9 years follow-up showed postoperative chemotherapy was beneficial for stage II disease but not stage IB disease [p.161]. For stage II disease receiving postoperative chemotherapy, median survival was 6.8 versus 3.6 years for observation [p.161]. Patients receiving chemotherapy did not have increased death rate [p.161].

In the ANITA trial, 840 patients with stage IB, II, or IIIA NSCLC were randomly assigned to postoperative vinorelbine plus cisplatin or observation [p.161]. Grade 3 and 4 toxicities were manageable; seven toxic deaths were reported [p.161]. After median 76-month follow-up, median survival was 66 months in the chemotherapy group and 44 months in the observation group [p.161]. Postoperative chemotherapy significantly improved (8.6%) the 5-year overall survival in patients with completely resected stage II and IIIA disease, although no benefit was observed in stage I [p.161].

A meta-analysis of 4584 patients (LACE) found that postoperative cisplatin-based chemotherapy increased 5-year survival (absolute benefit of 5.4%); there was no difference among chemotherapy regimens (vinorelbine, etoposide, and others) [p.161]. A subgroup analysis found that cisplatin plus vinorelbine also increased survival [p.161]. The benefit was greater in stage II and III disease and with good performance status [p.161]. Postoperative chemotherapy benefited older patients up to 80 years of age [p.161].

The CALGB 9633 trial assessed paclitaxel plus carboplatin in patients with stage IB lung cancer [p.161]. In this trial, 344 patients were randomly assigned to paclitaxel plus carboplatin or observation (within 4-8 weeks of resection) with median 74-month follow-up [p.161-162]. Postoperative chemotherapy was well tolerated with no chemotherapy-related toxic deaths [p.162]. Overall survival at 6 years was not significantly different, though subset analysis showed benefit for tumors ≥4 cm; 3-year survival was significant (80% vs. 73%, P=.02) [p.162]. The carboplatin and paclitaxel regimen is only recommended for early-stage disease if patients cannot tolerate cisplatin [p.162].

TREAT, a phase 2 randomized study, assessed cisplatin plus pemetrexed versus cisplatin plus vinorelbine as postoperative therapy for patients with completely resected stages IB to III NSCLC [p.162]. Data showed that cisplatin plus pemetrexed was an effective, less toxic regimen; patients were able to receive more cycles [p.162]. Overall survival at 3 years was similar between arms (75% vs. 77%; P = .858) [p.162].

#### NCCN Recommendations

The NCCN NSCLC Panel does not recommend postoperative chemotherapy for resected stage IA disease [p.162]. Postoperative chemotherapy is recommended for high-risk, margin-negative stage IB disease [p.162].

Recommended chemotherapy regimens for perioperative (neoadjuvant or postoperative) chemotherapy for completely resected stages IB to III NSCLC are provided in NCCN Guidelines with specific dosing [p.162].

The NCCN NSCLC Panel decided that cisplatin plus pemetrexed is the preferred neoadjuvant or adjuvant option for nonsquamous NSCLC if patients are not candidates for ICIs [p.162]. Cisplatin plus gemcitabine or cisplatin plus docetaxel are the preferred perioperative chemotherapy options for squamous cell NSCLC [p.162]. Other recommended options include cisplatin plus vinorelbine or cisplatin plus etoposide [p.162].

Perioperative therapy options for patients not candidates for cisplatin-based therapy (considered "useful in certain circumstances") include: 1) carboplatin plus paclitaxel; 2) carboplatin plus gemcitabine; and 3) carboplatin plus pemetrexed (nonsquamous NSCLC only) [p.162].

### Adjuvant Targeted Therapy

#### ADAURA Trial Evidence

ADAURA, a phase 3 randomized trial, assessed adjuvant osimertinib versus placebo in 682 patients with resected stage IB to IIIA EGFR mutation-positive NSCLC [p.162]. At 24 months, 89% (95% CI, 85%-92%) of the osimertinib group and 52% (95% CI, 46%-58%) of the placebo group were alive and disease-free (overall HR for disease recurrence or death, 0.20; 99.12% CI, 0.14–0.30; P < .001) [p.162]. At 24 months, 98% (95% CI, 95%-99%) of the osimertinib group and 85% (95% CI, 80%-89%) of the placebo group were alive and did not have CNS disease (overall HR, 0.18; 95% CI, 0.10–0.33) [p.162].

For stage IB NSCLC, 88% (95% CI, 78%-94%) of osimertinib group and 71% (95% CI, 60%-80%) of placebo group were alive and disease-free at 24 months (overall HR, 0.39; 95% CI, 0.18–0.76) [p.162]. For stage II NSCLC, 91% (95% CI, 82%-95%) of osimertinib group and 56% (95% CI, 45%-65%) of placebo group were alive and disease-free at 24 months (overall HR, 0.17; 95% CI, 0.08–0.31) [p.162]. For stage IIIA NSCLC, 88% (95% CI, 79%-94%) of osimertinib group and 32% (95% CI, 23%-41%) of placebo group were alive and disease-free at 24 months (overall HR, 0.12; 95% CI, 0.07–0.20) [p.162].

Sixty percent (410/682) of patients received adjuvant chemotherapy [p.162]. Overall survival data are not yet available [p.162]. Serious adverse events were reported in 16% (54/339) of osimertinib patients versus 12% (42/343) receiving placebo [p.162-163]. Ten patients (3%) receiving osimertinib had interstitial lung disease versus no placebo patients; there were no deaths in the osimertinib group versus one in the placebo group [p.163].

#### NCCN Recommendations

The NCCN Panel recommends osimertinib as an adjuvant therapy option for eligible patients with completely resected (R0) stage IB to IIIA, and stage IIIB (only T3,N2) EGFR mutation-positive NSCLC who have previously received adjuvant chemotherapy or are ineligible for platinum-based chemotherapy [p.163]. Osimertinib is recommended for EGFR exon 19 deletions or EGFR exon 21 L858R mutations, which are the most common EGFR mutations [p.163]. Although resected specimen is technically easier, it is acceptable to use initial diagnostic biopsy specimens [p.163]. For the 2023 update, the NCCN Panel expanded criteria to include stage IIIB (only T3,N2) NSCLC based on AJCC staging differences [p.163].

### Adjuvant Immunotherapy

#### Trial Evidence

IMpower010, a phase 3 randomized trial, assessed adjuvant atezolizumab versus best supportive care in 1005 patients with resected early-stage NSCLC and various PD-L1 levels who had received adjuvant chemotherapy [p.163]. In patients with resected stage II to IIIA NSCLC and PD-L1 of 1% or more, disease-free survival was improved with atezolizumab compared with best supportive care (HR, 0.66; 95% CI, 0.5–0.88; P = .0039) [p.163]. Treatment-related grade 3 and 4 adverse events were reported in 11% (53/495); 4 deaths occurred (1%, 4/495) [p.163].

PEARLS/KEYNOTE 091, a phase 3 randomized trial, assessed adjuvant pembrolizumab versus placebo in 1177 patients with completely resected stage IB to IIIA NSCLC [p.163]. Most patients (86%) received adjuvant platinum-based chemotherapy after resection [p.163]. In patients who received adjuvant chemotherapy after resection, median disease-free survival was 53.6 months (95% CI, 39.2–not reached) with pembrolizumab versus 42.0 months (95% CI, 31.3–not reached) with placebo (HR, 0.76; 95% CI, 0.63–0.91) [p.163].

Grade 3 or worse events included hypertension (6%) and pneumonia (2%) [p.163]. In pembrolizumab recipients, four fatal (1%) treatment-related adverse reactions occurred, including myocarditis and cardiogenic shock, septic shock and myocarditis, pneumonia, and sudden death [p.163].

#### NCCN Recommendations

The NCCN Panel recommends atezolizumab as an adjuvant therapy option for eligible patients with completely resected (R0) stage IIB to IIIA, stage IIIB (only T3,N2), or high-risk stage IIA NSCLC with PD-L1 of 1% or more who are negative for EGFR exon 19 deletions, EGFR exon 21 L858R mutations, and ALK rearrangements, and who have previously received adjuvant chemotherapy [p.163].

For 2023 update (Version 2), the NCCN Panel added recommendation for adjuvant pembrolizumab for eligible patients with completely resected stage IIB to IIIA, stage IIIB (only T3,N2), or high-risk stage IIA NSCLC who are negative for EGFR exon 19 deletions, EGFR exon 21 L858R mutations, or ALK fusions and who have previously received adjuvant chemotherapy [p.163]. The panel added a caveat that benefit of adjuvant pembrolizumab is unclear for patients with PD-L1 levels less than 1% [p.163-164].

For 2023 update (Version 1), the NCCN Panel expanded molecular testing criteria to include ALK rearrangements because patients with ALK rearrangements, EGFR exon 19 deletions, or EGFR exon 21 L858R mutations have less benefit from ICIs [p.164]. Clinical trials are assessing whether ALK inhibitors are effective in this setting [p.164]. The NCCN Panel clarified that single-agent osimertinib is recommended if patients have both EGFR mutations and PD-L1 of 1% or more; the panel does not recommend single-agent atezolizumab or combination osimertinib plus atezolizumab [p.164]. The NCCN Panel added recommendation that patients should be evaluated for perioperative therapy before surgery [p.164]. For 2023 update, the NCCN Panel expanded criteria to include stage IIIB (only T3,N2) disease [p.164].

## Preoperative Chemotherapy With or Without Immunotherapy Followed by Surgery

### Trial Data

Data from clinical trials in patients with resected NSCLCs indicate that delivery of chemotherapy is an important problem [p.164]. In the postoperative setting, significant comorbidities often make it difficult for patients to tolerate systemic therapy [p.164].

NATCH, a phase 3 randomized trial, compared surgery alone to preoperative or postoperative chemotherapy with paclitaxel/carboplatin [p.164]. Ninety percent of the preoperative cohort completed 3 cycles of chemotherapy but only 61% of the postoperative cohort completed chemotherapy; however, survival was equivalent among all three arms [p.164].

IFCT 0002, a randomized trial, found no difference in 3-year overall survival (67.4% vs. 67.7%) with preoperative versus postoperative chemotherapy in patients with early-stage NSCLC; response rate and quality of life were similar [p.164].

Several trials suggest that preoperative therapy is beneficial in patients with N2 disease [p.164]. Other trials suggest preoperative therapy is beneficial in earlier stage disease [p.164]. SWOG 9900 evaluated preoperative paclitaxel plus carboplatin in 354 patients with stage IB to IIIA disease versus surgery alone [p.164]. The trial closed prematurely but did show a trend toward improved PFS (33 vs. 20 months) and overall survival (62 vs. 41 months) with preoperative chemotherapy [p.164].

Scagliotti et al published a phase 3 trial of preoperative cisplatin plus gemcitabine versus surgery alone in 270 patients with stage IB to IIIA disease [p.164]. Although the trial closed early, a significant survival benefit was seen in patients with stages IIB and IIIA disease who received preoperative chemotherapy (HR, 0.63) [p.164].

Song et al published a meta-analysis of 13 randomized clinical trials evaluating preoperative chemotherapy followed by surgery versus surgery alone in resectable NSCLCs (overall survival: HR, 0.84; 95% CI, 0.77–0.92; P = .0001) [p.164]. Results are similar to those reported in another meta-analysis (HR, 0.89; 95% CI, 0.81–0.98; P = .02) [p.164]. The benefit from preoperative chemotherapy is similar to that attained with postoperative chemotherapy [p.164].

CheckMate 816, a phase 3 randomized trial, assessed neoadjuvant nivolumab plus platinum-doublet chemotherapy versus chemotherapy alone in 358 patients with resectable NSCLC (stage IB-IIIA) [p.164-165]. Chemotherapy regimens included 1) cisplatin plus either pemetrexed (nonsquamous) or gemcitabine (squamous); or 2) carboplatin plus paclitaxel [p.165]. Patients had good performance status (0-1) and did not have EGFR or ALK alterations [p.165].

The median event-free survival was 31.6 months (95% CI, 30.2–not reached) for nivolumab plus chemotherapy versus 20.8 months (95% CI, 14.0–26.7) for chemotherapy alone (HR, 0.63; 97% CI, 0.43–0.91; P=.005) [p.165]. Pathologic complete response was 24% (95% CI, 18%-31%) with nivolumab plus chemotherapy versus 2.2% (95% CI, 0.6%-5.6%) with chemotherapy alone (odds ratio 13.9; 99% CI, 3.49–55.7; P<.001) [p.165]. Major pathologic response was 36.9% with nivolumab plus chemotherapy versus 8.9% with chemotherapy alone [p.165]. Overall response rate was 53.6% for nivolumab plus chemotherapy versus 37.4% with chemotherapy alone [p.165]. Surgery was done for 83% of patients receiving nivolumab plus chemotherapy versus 75% receiving chemotherapy alone [p.165]. Grade 3 to 4 treatment-related adverse events occurred in 33.5% receiving nivolumab plus chemotherapy versus 36.9% receiving chemotherapy alone [p.165].

NADIM, a single-arm phase 2 study, assessed neoadjuvant nivolumab plus paclitaxel plus carboplatin in 46 patients with surgically resectable stage IIIA NSCLC; 41 patients had resection [p.165]. At 36 months, overall survival was 81.9% (95% CI, 66.8%-90.6%) [p.165]. TMB and PD-L1 levels were not predictive of survival [p.165]. Grade 3 or higher adverse events were reported in 30% (14/46) including increased lipase (3/46, 7%) and febrile neutropenia (3/46, 7%); none were associated with death or surgery delays [p.165].

### NCCN Recommendations

Based on clinical trial data, the NCCN Panel recommends that certain patients likely to receive adjuvant chemotherapy may instead be treated with preoperative systemic therapy after surgical evaluation [p.165]. The NCCN Panel recommends nivolumab plus platinum-doublet chemotherapy as a neoadjuvant systemic therapy option for eligible patients with resectable NSCLC and no contraindications to PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors [p.165].

For 2023 update (Version 1), the panel added recommendation that all patients should be evaluated for neoadjuvant therapy with strong consideration for those with node-positive disease or tumors 4 cm or more [p.165]. However, neoadjuvant therapy should not be used to attempt to induce resectability in patients not meeting resectability criteria [p.165].

Some oncogenic drivers—such as EGFR exon 19 deletions, EGFR exon 21 L858R mutations, or ALK rearrangements—have been shown to be associated with less benefit from PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors [p.165]. Testing for PD-L1 status, EGFR mutations, and ALK rearrangements is recommended before administering neoadjuvant nivolumab plus platinum-doublet chemotherapy [p.165].

For 2023 update (Version 1), the NCCN Panel expanded criteria to include stage IIIB (only T3,N2) NSCLC [p.165]. Chemotherapy regimens that may be used with neoadjuvant nivolumab include: 1) cisplatin plus either pemetrexed (nonsquamous), gemcitabine (squamous), or paclitaxel (any histology); or 2) carboplatin plus either pemetrexed (nonsquamous), gemcitabine (squamous), or paclitaxel (any histology) [p.165].

## Chemoradiation

### Postoperative Radiation Therapy

LungART, a phase 3 randomized trial, assessed postoperative RT versus no postoperative RT in 501 patients with completely resected stage IIIA (N2) NSCLC who had received either neoadjuvant (14%) or adjuvant chemotherapy (75%) [p.166]. Median disease-free survival was 30.5 months (95% CI, 24–49) in postoperative RT group versus 22.8 months (95% CI, 17–37) in control group (HR, 0.86; 95% CI, 0.68–1.08; P = .18) [p.166]. Common grade 3 to 4 adverse events included pneumonitis (5% vs. <1%), lymphopenia (4% vs. 0%), and fatigue (3% vs. <1%) [p.166]. Late-grade 3 to 4 cardiopulmonary toxicity occurred in 11% in postoperative RT group versus 5% in control group [p.166]. Three patients died in postoperative RT group (2 pneumonitis; 1 sepsis) [p.166].

PORT-C, a phase 3 randomized trial, assessed postoperative RT versus no postoperative RT in 394 patients with completely resected stage IIIA (N2) NSCLC who had received adjuvant chemotherapy [p.166]. Three-year disease-free survival rates were 40.5% with postoperative RT versus 32.7% with no RT (median, 22.1 vs. 18.6 months) (HR, 0.84; 95% CI, 0.65–1.09; P = .20) [p.166]. Three-year overall survival rates were 78.3% in postoperative RT versus 82.8% with no RT (HR, 1.02; P = .93) [p.166]. Three-year local recurrence only rates were 9.5% and 18.3%, respectively (Fine-Gray HR, 0.55; Gray test P = .04) [p.166]. No radiotherapy-related grade 4 or 5 adverse events were reported [p.166].

Data from LungART and PORT-C trials show that postoperative RT did not improve survival compared with no postoperative RT, although locoregional control was significantly improved [p.166]. For 2023 update (Version 1), the NCCN Panel clarified that postoperative RT may be considered for select patients with high-risk N2 disease such as extracapsular extension, multi-station involvement, inadequate lymph node dissection or sampling, and/or refusal or intolerance to adjuvant systemic therapy [p.166]. To minimize lung and heart toxicities, highly conformal RT techniques such as IMRT or proton therapy are preferred [p.166].

### Concurrent Chemoradiation

For patients with unresectable stage IIIA or stage IIIB disease, combined modality therapy (chemoradiation) is more efficacious than RT alone [p.166]. Concurrent chemoradiation is more efficacious than sequential chemoradiation [p.166]. However, concurrent chemoradiation has a higher rate of grade 3 or 4 esophagitis [p.166]. Selection of patients should be based on anticipated response and tolerance to therapy [p.166]. Accelerated RT regimens may be useful if concurrent chemoradiation would not be tolerated [p.166]. Sequential chemoradiation or RT alone is recommended for frail patients who cannot tolerate concurrent chemoradiation [p.166].

JCOG0301, a phase 3 randomized trial, assessed chemoradiation using low-dose carboplatin versus RT alone in patients (>70 years) with unresectable NSCLC [p.166]. Median overall survival was 22.4 months (95% CI, 16.5–33.6) for chemoradiotherapy with carboplatin and 16.9 months (95% CI, 13.4–20.3) for RT alone (HR, 0.68; 95.4% CI, 0.47–0.98; P=.0179) [p.166]. In the chemoradiation group, 3% (3/100) of patients died, whereas 4% (4/100) in the RT group [p.166]. Grade 3 to 4 hematologic effects occurred at greater rate in chemoradiation arm than RT alone, including leucopenia (63.5% vs. none), neutropenia (57.3% vs. none), and thrombocytopenia (29.2% vs. 2%) [p.166].

Long-term follow-up data in older patients receiving chemoradiation versus RT alone continue to support using chemoradiation (HR, 0.74; 95% CI, 0.55–0.99; P = .024) [p.167]. Although optimal sequence is not established, postoperative RT is generally administered after adjuvant chemotherapy or concurrently for positive resection margins [p.167].

### Concurrent Chemoradiation Regimens

Concurrent chemoradiation regimens recommended for all histologies for initial treatment include 1) cisplatin plus etoposide; and 2) carboplatin plus paclitaxel [p.167]. For nonsquamous NSCLC, additional recommended regimens include pemetrexed plus either carboplatin or cisplatin [p.167]. A weekly paclitaxel plus carboplatin regimen is another chemoradiation option [p.167].

The NCCN NSCLC Panel decided that the following concurrent chemoradiation options are preferred: 1) pemetrexed plus either carboplatin or cisplatin for nonsquamous NSCLC only; 2) carboplatin plus paclitaxel for all histologies; and 3) cisplatin plus etoposide for all histologies [p.167]. The cisplatin plus vinblastine concurrent regimen is rarely used in the United States; therefore, it is not recommended [p.167].

### Sequential Chemoradiation Regimens

The recommended sequential chemoradiation options include cisplatin combined with pemetrexed (nonsquamous only), docetaxel, etoposide, gemcitabine, or vinorelbine [p.167]. Carboplatin regimens are recommended for patients with comorbidities or those unable to tolerate cisplatin, including 1) carboplatin plus gemcitabine; 2) carboplatin plus paclitaxel; and 3) carboplatin plus pemetrexed (nonsquamous only) [p.167].

The NCCN NSCLC Panel decided that the following sequential chemoradiation options are preferred: 1) pemetrexed plus cisplatin for nonsquamous NSCLC only; 2) cisplatin plus gemcitabine for squamous histology; and 3) cisplatin plus docetaxel for squamous histology [p.167].

### Durvalumab Consolidation Immunotherapy

Durvalumab is a human ICI antibody that inhibits PD-L1 [p.167]. PACIFIC, a phase 3 randomized trial, compared adjuvant durvalumab (consolidation immunotherapy) versus placebo in eligible patients with unresectable stage III NSCLC (PS 0-1) without disease progression after 2 or more cycles of definitive concurrent platinum-based chemoradiation [p.167]. Eligible patients received adjuvant durvalumab after concurrent chemoradiation (1-42 days) [p.167]. Most patients currently or previously smoked cigarettes and did not have EGFR mutations; PD-L1 status was typically less than 25% or unknown [p.167].

Grade 3 or 4 adverse events occurred at similar rate in both groups (durvalumab, 30.5% vs. placebo, 26.1%) [p.167]. Pneumonia was the most common grade 3 or 4 adverse event (durvalumab, 4.4% vs. placebo, 3.8%) [p.167]. Durvalumab did not compromise patient-reported outcomes [p.167].

An updated analysis reported that overall survival was increased after durvalumab consolidation (47.5 months; 95% CI, 38.4–52.6) compared with placebo (29.1 months; 95% CI, 22.1–35.1) (stratified HR for death, 0.72; 95% CI, 0.45–0.68) [p.167]. After 5 years, 42.9% of patients who received durvalumab were alive versus 33.4% of those who received placebo [p.167]. Additionally, 42.9% were alive and free of disease progression after 5 years if they received durvalumab and 33% were alive and free of disease progression compared with 19% receiving placebo [p.167]. Patients receiving durvalumab received less subsequent immunotherapy (11.6%) compared with those receiving placebo (28.3%) [p.167].

#### NCCN Recommendations

The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends durvalumab (category 1) as a consolidation immunotherapy option (regardless of PD-L1 status) for eligible patients (PS 0-1) with unresectable stage III NSCLC and without disease progression after treatment with definitive concurrent platinum-based chemoradiation [p.167]. Durvalumab is used as an adjuvant treatment option, not as second-line therapy [p.167]. Durvalumab may be used after any of the concurrent chemoradiation regimens [p.167].

Ten patients with stage II NSCLC were included in PACIFIC using older AJCC 7th edition staging [p.167]. Therefore, the NCCN NSCLC Panel also recommends durvalumab as a consolidation immunotherapy option (regardless of PD-L1 status) for eligible patients (PS 0-1) with unresectable stage II NSCLC and without disease progression after treatment with definitive concurrent platinum-based chemoradiation [p.167].

A few recommended concurrent chemoradiation regimens have an option for additional chemotherapy cycles (2 or 4) after radiation, termed consolidation chemotherapy [p.168]. If patients will be receiving durvalumab, the NCCN NSCLC Panel does not recommend consolidation chemotherapy due to concerns about increased pneumonitis risk [p.168]. Durvalumab should be discontinued for severe or life-threatening pneumonitis and should be withheld or discontinued for other severe or life-threatening immune-mediated adverse events [p.168]. If patients will not be receiving durvalumab because of medical contraindications, consolidation chemotherapy is an option [p.168].

## Systemic Therapy

### Chemotherapy for Metastatic Disease

Patients with metastatic (stage IV) NSCLC who have good performance status benefit from chemotherapy, usually with a platinum-based regimen [p.168]. If patients are not eligible for targeted therapy or immunotherapy regimens, then chemotherapy regimens are recommended [p.168]. Combination chemotherapy regimens produce 1-year survival rates of 30% to 40% and are more efficacious than single agents [p.168]. However, survival rates are higher for patients with stage IV NSCLC eligible for newer targeted therapy or immunotherapy regimens [p.168].

Phase 3 randomized trials have shown that many platinum-doublet combinations yield similar objective response rates and survival [p.168]. Platinum-doublet regimens differ slightly for toxicity, convenience, and cost [p.168]. Non-platinum-based regimens are also options, such as 1) gemcitabine plus vinorelbine, or 2) gemcitabine plus docetaxel [p.168]. The prognosis for stage IV inoperable lung cancer remains poor if patients are not candidates for targeted therapy or immunotherapy regimens [p.168].

In the United States, frequently used initial cytotoxic regimens for stage IV nonsquamous NSCLC include: 1) cisplatin (or carboplatin) plus pemetrexed; or 2) carboplatin plus paclitaxel with (or without) bevacizumab [p.168]. Gemcitabine plus cisplatin (or carboplatin) is often used for stage IV squamous cell NSCLC [p.168]. These chemotherapy regimens are recommended based on phase 3 randomized trials [p.168].

A phase 3 randomized trial suggests that conventional cytotoxic agents should not be continued beyond 4 to 6 cycles of therapy [p.169].

### Specific Chemotherapy Trials

A phase 3 randomized trial assessed cisplatin plus pemetrexed versus cisplatin plus gemcitabine as first-line therapy in patients with stage IIIB or IV NSCLC [p.169]. For patients with adenocarcinoma receiving cisplatin plus pemetrexed, median overall survival was 12.6 months compared with 10.9 months for cisplatin plus gemcitabine (HR, 0.84; 95% CI, 0.71–0.99; P = .03) [p.169]. For patients with squamous cell NSCLC receiving cisplatin plus pemetrexed, overall survival was 9.4 versus 10.8 months (HR, 1.23; 95% CI, 1.00–1.51; P = .05) [p.169]. Patients with nonsquamous NSCLC receiving cisplatin plus pemetrexed had less toxicity [p.169]. Median overall survival was similar for both regimens when histologies were combined (8.6 vs. 9.2 months; HR, 1.08; 95% CI, 0.81–1.45; P = .586) [p.169].

TAX 326, a phase 3 randomized trial, assessed docetaxel plus cisplatin (or carboplatin) versus vinorelbine plus cisplatin as first-line therapy for stage IIIB or IV NSCLC [p.169]. Docetaxel plus cisplatin was associated with similar overall survival (11.3 vs. 10.1 months; P = .044; HR, 1.183; 97.2% CI, 0.989–1.416) and better response rate (31.6%) compared with cisplatin plus vinorelbine (24.5%; P = .029); docetaxel plus cisplatin was associated with better quality of life and better tolerance [p.169].

Many oncologists use pemetrexed-based regimens for stage IV adenocarcinomas (if patients not candidates for targeted therapy or PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors), because taxane-based regimens are associated with more toxicity [p.169]. There are no agents for prevention of peripheral neuropathy and few agents useful for treatment [p.169].

The POINTBREAK trial showed that carboplatin plus pemetrexed plus bevacizumab is a reasonable option for patients with metastatic NSCLC and confirmed that taxane-based regimens are more toxic than pemetrexed-based regimens [p.169]. The POINTBREAK trial also showed that both regimens are similar in overall survival rates [p.169]. A retrospective cohort study suggests that the addition of bevacizumab to carboplatin plus paclitaxel does not increase survival in patients ≥65 years with advanced nonsquamous NSCLC [p.169]. However, another retrospective cohort study reported increased survival in older patients [p.169]. A combined analysis of ECOG 4599 and POINTBREAK trials found a survival benefit with bevacizumab addition in patients <75 years but no benefit in those >75 years [p.169].

### Albumin-Bound Paclitaxel

Albumin-bound paclitaxel (nab-paclitaxel) can be substituted for paclitaxel or docetaxel for patients: 1) who experienced hypersensitivity reactions despite premedication; or 2) in whom premedications are contraindicated [p.169]. A phase 3 randomized trial in patients with advanced NSCLC reported that albumin-bound paclitaxel plus carboplatin is associated with less neurotoxicity and improved response rate compared with paclitaxel plus carboplatin [p.169]. Based on the trial and FDA approval, the NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends albumin-bound paclitaxel plus carboplatin regimen as initial cytotoxic therapy for patients with advanced NSCLC and good performance status [p.169].

### NCCN Recommendations for Chemotherapy

Chemotherapy is recommended for patients with stage IV NSCLC and negative test results for actionable driver mutations (ALK, BRAF p.V600E, ERBB2, EGFR, KRAS, METex14 skipping, NTRK1/2/3, RET, and ROS1), PD-L1 expression less than 1%, and contraindications to PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors [p.169-170]. Chemotherapy regimens may be used as first-line or subsequent therapy options in eligible patients with actionable driver mutations, although generally not preferred [p.170]. Targeted therapy is a preferred first-line therapy option for patients with actionable driver mutations, except for EGFR exon 20 mutations or KRAS mutations [p.170].

Recommended chemotherapy regimens are based on performance status and include platinum agents, taxanes, vinorelbine, etoposide, pemetrexed, and gemcitabine [p.170]. The NCCN Guidelines list all combination systemic therapy regimens and single agents recommended for metastatic NSCLC based on histology and performance status [p.170].

For patients with advanced NSCLC who have performance status of 2, platinum-based combinations and a few single-agent chemotherapy agents are recommended; cisplatin-based regimens are not recommended [p.170]. For nonsquamous NSCLC or NSCLC NOS, single-agent chemotherapy includes gemcitabine, pemetrexed, or taxanes; combination regimens include carboplatin plus paclitaxel or carboplatin plus pemetrexed [p.170]. Patients with performance status of 2 are often treated with single-agent chemotherapy due to toxicity concerns [p.170].

Treatment with carboplatin plus pemetrexed increased median overall survival compared with pemetrexed alone (9.3 vs. 5.3 months, P = .001) in patients with performance status of 2; however, 4 treatment-related deaths occurred in the carboplatin plus pemetrexed arm [p.170].

The NCCN NSCLC Panel has preference stratified all systemic therapy regimens for patients with NSCLC [p.170]. Newer chemotherapy plus pembrolizumab regimens are preferred for eligible patients with metastatic NSCLC who do not have contraindications to immunotherapy and are not candidates for targeted therapy [p.170]. For patients with metastatic nonsquamous NSCLC and performance status 0 to 1 who have contraindications to immunotherapy, the panel decided that following chemotherapy regimens are "useful in certain circumstances": 1) carboplatin with paclitaxel, docetaxel, etoposide, gemcitabine, or pemetrexed (all category 1); 2) cisplatin with paclitaxel, docetaxel, etoposide, gemcitabine, or pemetrexed (all category 1); 3) bevacizumab with carboplatin and paclitaxel or pemetrexed; and 4) gemcitabine with docetaxel or vinorelbine [p.170].

The panel also preference stratified regimens for patients with metastatic nonsquamous NSCLC and performance status 2; carboplatin plus pemetrexed is preferred for adenocarcinoma [p.170]. Regimens for squamous cell NSCLC have also been preference stratified [p.170].

The initial cytotoxic systemic therapy regimens do not include options that are less effective, more toxic, and/or infrequently used in the United States based on panel member experience and NCCN survey data [p.170]. For patients with metastatic nonsquamous NSCLC and NSCLC NOS, the following regimens are not recommended: carboplatin plus vinorelbine, cisplatin plus vinorelbine, etoposide, irinotecan, and vinorelbine [p.170]. For patients with metastatic squamous cell NSCLC, the following regimens are not recommended: carboplatin plus vinorelbine, cisplatin plus gemcitabine plus necitumumab, cisplatin plus vinorelbine, etoposide, irinotecan, and vinorelbine [p.170].
## Summary of Key Evidence

This section covers immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) therapy, including the use of atezolizumab, cemiplimab-rwlc, nivolumab, and pembrolizumab for metastatic non-small cell lung cancer [p.171-180].

## Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors

### Background

Human ICI antibodies inhibit the PD-1 receptor or PD-L1, which improves antitumor immunity; PD-1 receptors are expressed on activated cytotoxic T cells [p.171]. Cemiplimab-rwlc, nivolumab, and pembrolizumab inhibit PD-1 receptors [p.171]; atezolizumab and durvalumab inhibit PD-L1 [p.171].

Single-agent immunotherapy or combination immunotherapy plus chemotherapy regimens are not recommended if patients have contraindications to immunotherapy, which may include active or previously documented autoimmune disease and/or current use of immunosuppressive agents [p.171]. PD-L1 inhibitors are less beneficial in patients with some oncogenic drivers (EGFR exon 19 deletions, EGFR exon 21 L858R mutations, ALK rearrangements) [p.171].

Monitoring is recommended during initial therapy with response assessment via CT (with or without contrast) of known or high-risk sites after 2 cycles and then every 2 to 4 cycles [p.171]. During maintenance or subsequent therapy, monitoring is recommended every 6 to 12 weeks [p.171]. ICIs (also known as immunotherapy or IO agents) are associated with a delay in benefit when compared with targeted therapy or cytotoxic chemotherapy [p.171].

### PD-L1 Testing Recommendations

The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends IHC testing for PD-L1 expression (category 1) before first-line treatment in all patients with metastatic NSCLC, regardless of histology, based on the efficacy of pembrolizumab with or without chemotherapy [p.172]. Ideally, PD-L1 expression levels are assessed before first-line therapy, if clinically feasible [p.172]. Every effort needs to be made to assess for oncogenic driver variants for which targeted therapies are available [p.172].

Plasma-based ctDNA testing can be used to evaluate for genomic alterations if the assay has been validated and if the patient is unfit for invasive tissue sampling or if there is insufficient tissue for molecular analyses; however, plasma-based ctDNA testing is less sensitive than tissue assays [p.172].

### Treatment Selection

It is important to note that targeted therapies are recommended for patients with metastatic NSCLC and specific oncogenic drivers, independent of PD-L1 levels [p.172]. Patients with metastatic NSCLC and PD-L1 expression levels of 1% or more—but who also have a targetable driver oncogene molecular variant (e.g., EGFR, ALK, ROS1)—should receive first-line targeted therapy and not first-line ICIs, because targeted therapies yield higher response rates (e.g., osimertinib, 80%) than ICIs, targeted therapy is better tolerated, and these patients are less likely to respond to single-agent ICIs [p.172].

For patients receiving first-line ICIs with or without chemotherapy, oncologists should be aware of the long half-life of the ICIs and potential adverse effects when using osimertinib in combination with or following ICIs [p.172]. A few case reports suggest that patients treated with ICIs who have acquired resistance may have transformation to SCLC [p.172].

### TMB Status

In 2020, the NCCN Panel removed TMB as an emerging immune biomarker for patients with metastatic NSCLC based on clinical trial data, concerns about variable TMB measurements, and other issues [p.172]. The NCCN Guidelines do not recommend measurement of TMB levels before deciding whether to use nivolumab plus ipilimumab regimens or other ICIs, such as pembrolizumab [p.172].

### Adverse Events and Management

ICIs are associated with unique immune-mediated adverse events, such as endocrine disorders, that are not seen with traditional cytotoxic chemotherapy [p.173]. Health care providers should be aware of the spectrum of potential immune-mediated adverse events, know how to manage them, and educate patients [p.173].

Atezolizumab, cemiplimab-rwlc, durvalumab, nivolumab, or pembrolizumab should be discontinued for patients with severe or life-threatening pneumonitis or myocarditis and should be withheld or discontinued for other severe or life-threatening immune-mediated adverse events when indicated [p.173].

Pseudoprogression has been reported; therefore, traditional RECIST criteria may not be applicable [p.173].

## Atezolizumab

Atezolizumab is a humanized monoclonal immunoglobulin G1 antibody that inhibits PD-L1, which improves antitumor immune response [p.173]. Immune-mediated adverse events may occur with atezolizumab [p.173]. For patients with immune-mediated adverse events, intravenous high-dose corticosteroids should be administered based on severity [p.173].

Atezolizumab should be permanently discontinued for patients with severe or life-threatening pneumonitis or myocarditis and discontinued for other severe or life-threatening immune-mediated adverse events [p.173].

### First-Line Therapy Evidence

IMpower110, a phase 3 randomized trial, compared first-line atezolizumab monotherapy versus platinum-based chemotherapy in different subgroups of patients with metastatic NSCLC with high PD-L1 expression [p.173-174]. Patients were wild type for EGFR or ALK variants and most either currently or previously smoked cigarettes [p.174]. Patients receiving first-line atezolizumab monotherapy also received maintenance therapy with atezolizumab [p.174]. Chemotherapy regimens for nonsquamous NSCLC included cisplatin (or carboplatin) plus pemetrexed and maintenance with pemetrexed; patients with squamous cell NSCLC received cisplatin plus gemcitabine [p.174].

Atezolizumab monotherapy was associated with fatal adverse reactions in 3.8% of all patients (11/286) [p.174]; 4.2% of chemotherapy patients (11/263) also died [p.174]. Grade 3 to 4 treatment-related adverse events occurred in 12.9% receiving atezolizumab monotherapy versus 44.1% with chemotherapy [p.174]. Most frequent serious adverse reactions with atezolizumab monotherapy were pneumonia (2.8%), COPD (2.1%), and pneumonitis (2.1%); 28% of patients had serious adverse reactions [p.174].

A different IHC assay was used in IMpower110 (SP142 PD-L1 IHC assay) compared with assays for pembrolizumab in KEYNOTE-024 (22C3 pharmDx assay); however, results were similar [p.174]. Data suggest that different methods of testing for PD-L1 levels are not equivalent [p.174]. Based on analysis using SP142 assay, median OS was 20.2 months (95% CI, 16.5–not estimable) with atezolizumab monotherapy (n = 107) versus 13.1 months (95% CI, 7.4–16.5 months) with chemotherapy (n = 98) (HR, 0.59; 95% CI, 0.40–0.89; P=.0106) in patients with high PD-L1 expression [p.174]. Based on 22C3 assay analysis, median OS was 20.2 months with atezolizumab (n = 134) versus 11.0 months with chemotherapy (n = 126) (HR, 0.60; 95% CI, 0.41–0.86) [p.174]. There was no survival advantage in other patient subgroups with lower PD-L1 expression [p.174].

#### First-Line NCCN Recommendations

The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends atezolizumab monotherapy (category 1; preferred) as a first-line therapy option for eligible patients with metastatic NSCLC based on clinical trial data and FDA approval [p.174]. Atezolizumab monotherapy is recommended as a first-line therapy option for patients with metastatic NSCLC, PD-L1 levels of 50% or more, and negative test results for actionable driver mutations, regardless of histology; maintenance therapy with atezolizumab is also recommended [p.174]. Cemiplimab-rwlc and pembrolizumab are also recommended first-line therapy options (category 1; preferred) [p.174]. The NCCN NSCLC Panel has preference stratified the ICI regimens and decided that atezolizumab, cemiplimab-rwlc, and pembrolizumab (all category 1) are preferred first-line therapy options [p.174].

### ABCP Combination Therapy (Atezolizumab, Bevacizumab, Carboplatin, Paclitaxel)

IMpower150, a phase 3 randomized trial, compared first-line ABCP regimen versus bevacizumab plus chemotherapy for patients with metastatic nonsquamous NSCLC [p.174]. Median overall survival was 19.2 months (95% CI, 17.0–23.8) in the ABCP arm versus 14.7 months (95% CI, 13.3–16.9) in the carboplatin plus paclitaxel plus bevacizumab arm; HR for death was 0.78 (95% CI, 0.64–0.96; P = .02) [p.174]. PFS was longer in the ABCP arm versus chemotherapy plus bevacizumab (8.3 vs. 6.8 months; HR, 0.62; 95% CI, 0.52–0.74; P < .001) [p.174].

Some patients with EGFR mutations or ALK rearrangements (n = 108) who had disease progression on or were intolerant of prior TKI were enrolled, although most patients (87%) did not have these genetic variants [p.174]. In these patients with EGFR mutations or ALK rearrangements, PFS was also increased with ABCP compared with chemotherapy plus bevacizumab (9.7 vs. 6.1 months; HR, 0.59; 95% CI, 0.37–0.94) [p.174]. A subgroup analysis reported that subsequent therapy with ABCP increased median overall survival in a few patients with EGFR mutation-positive metastatic NSCLC (n = 34) compared with those receiving carboplatin plus paclitaxel plus bevacizumab (n = 45) [p.174]. Therefore, ABCP regimen may be an option for patients with EGFR mutations or ALK rearrangements and disease progression after initial TKI therapy [p.174].

#### ABCP NCCN Recommendations

The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends the ABCP regimen (category 1; other recommended intervention) as a first-line therapy option for eligible patients with metastatic nonsquamous NSCLC based on clinical trial data and FDA approval [p.174-175]. The ABCP regimen is recommended as a first-line therapy option for patients with negative test results for actionable driver mutations (ALK, BRAF p.V600E, EGFR, METex14 skipping, NTRK1/2/3, RET, and ROS1), regardless of PD-L1 expression levels [p.175]. ABCP regimen may be used as first-line or subsequent therapy option, although it is not a preferred option, in eligible patients with actionable driver mutations [p.175]. Targeted therapy is a preferred first-line therapy option for patients with actionable driver mutations, except for EGFR exon 20 mutations or KRAS mutations [p.175].

Maintenance therapy with atezolizumab and bevacizumab is also recommended (category 1) [p.175]. The NCCN NSCLC Panel has preference stratified systemic therapy regimens and decided that ABCP regimen is an other recommended intervention, because the panel prefers pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy regimens based on tolerability and experience [p.175]. The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends that bevacizumab biosimilars may be used [p.175].

### Atezolizumab Plus Chemotherapy

IMpower130, a phase 3 randomized trial, compared atezolizumab plus carboplatin plus albumin-bound paclitaxel versus chemotherapy alone as first-line therapy in patients with metastatic nonsquamous NSCLC with no EGFR mutations or ALK rearrangements [p.176]. Median overall survival was 18.6 months (95% CI, 16.0–21.2) in the atezolizumab plus chemotherapy arm versus 13.9 months (95% CI, 12.0–18.7) in the carboplatin plus albumin-bound paclitaxel arm (HR, 0.79; 95% CI, 0.64–0.98; P = .033) [p.176]. Treatment-related deaths were reported in 2% (8/473) in atezolizumab plus chemotherapy arm and less than 1% (1/232) in chemotherapy-only arm [p.176].

#### Atezolizumab Plus Chemotherapy NCCN Recommendations

The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends atezolizumab plus carboplatin plus albumin-bound paclitaxel (other recommended intervention) as a first-line therapy option for eligible patients with metastatic nonsquamous NSCLC based on clinical trial data [p.176]. This regimen is recommended for patients with metastatic NSCLC and negative test results for actionable driver mutations, regardless of PD-L1 levels [p.176]. The regimen may be used as first-line or subsequent therapy option, although not a preferred option, in eligible patients with actionable driver mutations [p.176]. Maintenance therapy with atezolizumab is also recommended [p.176]. Targeted therapy is a preferred first-line therapy option for patients with actionable driver mutations, except for EGFR exon 20 mutations or KRAS mutations [p.176].

### Atezolizumab as Subsequent Therapy

OAK, a phase 3 randomized trial, compared atezolizumab versus docetaxel in patients with metastatic NSCLC and disease progression during or after systemic therapy [p.176]. Most patients either currently or previously smoked cigarettes and had received platinum-based chemotherapy; 10% had EGFR mutations and ALK rearrangements [p.176]. Patients with nonsquamous NSCLC receiving atezolizumab had longer overall survival (15.6 months; 95% CI, 13.3–17.6) compared with those receiving docetaxel (11.2 months; 95% CI, 9.3–12.6; HR, 0.73; 95% CI, 0.6–0.89; P = .0015) [p.176]. In patients with squamous cell NSCLC, overall survival was 8.9 months (95% CI, 7.4–12.8) with atezolizumab versus 7.7 months (95% CI, 6.3–8.9) with docetaxel (HR, 0.73; 95% CI, 0.54–0.98; P = .038) [p.176]. Fewer treatment-related severe adverse events (grades 3-4) were reported for atezolizumab versus docetaxel (15% vs. 43%) [p.176].

#### Subsequent Therapy NCCN Recommendations

If patients have not previously received a PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor, the NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends atezolizumab (category 1; preferred) as a subsequent therapy option for patients with metastatic nonsquamous or squamous cell NSCLC based on clinical trial data and FDA approval [p.176]. Nivolumab and pembrolizumab are also recommended subsequent therapy options (category 1; preferred) [p.176]. Testing for PD-L1 expression levels is not required for prescribing subsequent therapy with atezolizumab or nivolumab but may provide useful information [p.176]. If patients have progressed on a PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor, then using a PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor is not recommended [p.176].

## Cemiplimab-rwlc

Cemiplimab-rwlc is a recombinant human immunoglobulin G4 monoclonal antibody that inhibits PD-1 receptors, which improves antitumor immune response [p.176]. Immune-mediated adverse events may occur with cemiplimab-rwlc [p.176]. For patients with immune-mediated adverse events, intravenous high-dose corticosteroids should be administered based on severity [p.176]. Cemiplimab-rwlc should be permanently discontinued for patients with grades 2, 3, or 4 myocarditis and discontinued for other severe or life-threatening immune-mediated adverse events [p.176].

### EMPOWER-Lung 1 Trial

EMPOWER-Lung 1, a phase 3 randomized trial, compared first-line cemiplimab monotherapy versus platinum-based chemotherapy for patients with metastatic squamous or nonsquamous NSCLC, PD-L1 levels of 50% or more, and negative test results for EGFR mutations, ALK rearrangements, or ROS1 rearrangements [p.176]. Patients receiving first-line cemiplimab also received maintenance therapy with cemiplimab [p.176]. Median overall survival was not reached for patients receiving cemiplimab (95% CI, 17.9–not evaluable) versus 14.2 months (95% CI, 11.2–17.5) for chemotherapy (HR, 0.57; 95% CI, 0.42–0.77; P=.0002) [p.176]. Response rate was 39% (95% CI, 34%-45%) for cemiplimab versus 20% (95% CI, 16%-26%) for chemotherapy [p.176].

Of patients treated with cemiplimab, 28% (98/355) had grade 3 to 4 adverse events compared with 39% (135/342) for chemotherapy [p.176]. Treatment-related deaths occurred in 2.5% (9/355) of patients receiving cemiplimab versus 2.0% (7/342) with chemotherapy [p.176]. Cemiplimab-related deaths were due to autoimmune myocarditis, cardiac failure, cardiopulmonary failure, cardiorespiratory arrest, nephritis, respiratory failure, septic shock, tumor hyperprogression, and unknown reasons [p.176].

### Cemiplimab NCCN Recommendations

The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends cemiplimab-rwlc monotherapy (category 1; preferred) as a first-line therapy option for eligible patients with metastatic NSCLC regardless of histology, PD-L1 levels of 50% or more, and negative test results for actionable driver mutations based on clinical trial data and FDA approval; maintenance therapy with cemiplimab-rwlc is also recommended [p.176]. Atezolizumab and pembrolizumab are also recommended first-line therapy options (category 1; preferred) [p.176]. The NCCN NSCLC Panel has preference stratified the regimens [p.176].

## Single-Agent Pembrolizumab and Pembrolizumab Plus Chemotherapy

Single-agent pembrolizumab, atezolizumab, or cemiplimab-rwlc are recommended (category 1; preferred) as first-line therapy options for eligible patients with metastatic NSCLC regardless of histology, PD-L1 expression levels of 50% or more, and negative test results for actionable driver mutations that have recommended first-line targeted therapies (ALK, BRAF p.V600E, EGFR, METex14 skipping, NTRK1/2/3, RET, ROS1) [p.172]. The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends single-agent pembrolizumab as a first-line therapy option in eligible patients with metastatic NSCLC regardless of histology, PD-L1 levels of 1% to 49% (category 2B; useful in certain circumstances), and negative test results for actionable driver mutations [p.172].

Combination therapy with pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy is recommended (category 1; preferred) as a first-line therapy option in eligible patients with metastatic NSCLC and negative test results for actionable driver mutations, regardless of PD-L1 expression levels [p.172].

Combination therapy with ABCP regimen is recommended (category 1; other recommended intervention) as a first-line therapy option for eligible patients with metastatic NSCLC and negative test results for actionable driver mutations, regardless of PD-L1 expression levels [p.172].

Combination therapy with pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy or with ABCP may be used as first-line or subsequent therapy options, although they are not preferred options, in eligible patients with actionable driver mutations [p.172]. Targeted therapy is a preferred first-line therapy option for patients with actionable driver mutations, except for EGFR exon 20 mutations or KRAS mutations [p.172]. Continuation maintenance immunotherapy is recommended for 2 years, if tolerated, for all first-line immunotherapy (±chemotherapy) regimens [p.172].

### Subsequent Pembrolizumab Therapy

If patients have disease progression on PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor therapy (±chemotherapy), then using a PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor is not recommended for subsequent therapy [p.172]. Single-agent pembrolizumab is recommended (category 1; preferred) as a subsequent therapy option for select patients with metastatic NSCLC and PD-L1 levels greater than 1%; nivolumab or atezolizumab is recommended (category 1; preferred) as a subsequent monotherapy option for select patients with metastatic NSCLC regardless of PD-L1 levels [p.172].

Based on data in the second-line setting, PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitor monotherapy appears less effective in patients with EGFR exon 19 deletions, EGFR exon 21 L858R mutations, or ALK rearrangements, regardless of PD-L1 expression levels [p.172]. A small study suggests that single-agent pembrolizumab is not effective as first-line therapy in patients with metastatic NSCLC and EGFR mutations, even those with PD-L1 levels more than 50% [p.173]. Patients with ALK-positive NSCLC and very high PD-L1 expression levels do not respond to pembrolizumab [p.173]. In trials assessing efficacy of first-line therapy with pembrolizumab with (or without) chemotherapy, most patients were wild type for EGFR or ALK variants [p.173]. Maintenance immunotherapy is recommended, if tolerated, until progression for all subsequent therapy regimens [p.173].

## Additional Considerations

The necitumumab plus cisplatin plus gemcitabine regimen is not recommended for patients with metastatic squamous cell NSCLC [p.171]. The NCCN NSCLC Panel voted unanimously against this regimen based on toxicity, cost, and limited improvement in efficacy when compared with cisplatin plus gemcitabine [p.171]. A phase 3 randomized trial showed a slight improvement in overall survival (11.5 months; 95% CI, 10.4–12.6) versus 9.9 months (95% CI, 8.9–11.1) (stratified HR, 0.84; 95% CI, 0.74–0.96; P = .01) [p.171]. There were more grade 3 or higher adverse events in patients receiving the necitumumab regimen (388 [72%] of 538 patients) than in patients receiving only gemcitabine plus cisplatin (333 [62%] of 541) [p.171].
## Summary of Key Evidence

This section covers pembrolizumab monotherapy and combination therapy for metastatic non-small cell lung cancer, as well as tremelimumab plus durvalumab combination therapy and maintenance therapy strategies [p.181-186].

## Pembrolizumab

### Background

Although not optimal, PD-L1 expression is currently the best available biomarker to assess whether patients are candidates for pembrolizumab [p.181]. PD-L1 expression is continuously variable and dynamic; thus, a cutoff value for a positive result is artificial [p.181]. Patients with PD-L1 expression levels just below and just above 50% will probably have similar responses [p.181]. Unique anti-PD-L1 IHC assays have been developed for each different ICI currently available [p.181]. The definition of a positive PD-L1 test result varies depending on which biomarker assay is used [p.181].

Immune-mediated adverse events may occur with pembrolizumab [p.181]. For patients with immune-mediated adverse events, intravenous high-dose corticosteroids should be administered based on severity [p.181]. Pembrolizumab should be discontinued for patients with severe or life-threatening pneumonitis or myocarditis and withheld or discontinued for other severe or life-threatening immune-mediated adverse events [p.181].

### First-Line Monotherapy

#### KEYNOTE-024 Trial

KEYNOTE-024, a phase 3 randomized trial, compared single-agent pembrolizumab versus platinum-based chemotherapy as first-line therapy for patients with advanced nonsquamous or squamous NSCLC and PD-L1 expression levels of 50% or more, but without EGFR mutations or ALK rearrangements [p.181]. At 6 months, the rate of overall survival was 80.2% with pembrolizumab monotherapy versus 72.4% with chemotherapy (HR for death, 0.60; 95% CI, 0.41–0.89; P = .005) [p.181]. Responses were higher for pembrolizumab than for chemotherapy (44.8% vs. 27.8%) [p.181].

An updated analysis of KEYNOTE-024 showed that median overall survival was 26.3 months (95% CI, 18.3 months–40.4) with pembrolizumab monotherapy compared with 13.4 months (95% CI, 9.4–18.3 months) with chemotherapy (HR, 0.62; 95% CI, 0.48–0.81) [p.181]. The 5-year overall survival rate was 31.9% for pembrolizumab and 16.3% for chemotherapy [p.181]. Fewer severe treatment-related adverse events (grades 3-5) were reported in patients receiving pembrolizumab monotherapy compared with chemotherapy (31.2% vs. 53.3%) [p.181]. Treatment-related deaths occurred in 1.3% (2/154) of patients receiving pembrolizumab monotherapy versus 2% (3/150) of patients receiving chemotherapy [p.181].

#### KEYNOTE-042 Trial

KEYNOTE-042, a phase 3 randomized trial, compared single-agent pembrolizumab versus platinum-based chemotherapy as first-line therapy for patients with advanced nonsquamous or squamous NSCLC and PD-L1 expression levels of 1% or more, but without EGFR mutations or ALK rearrangements [p.181]. Overall survival was 20.0 months (95% CI, 15.4–24.9) in patients with PD-L1 levels of 50% or more who received single-agent pembrolizumab compared with 12.2 months (95% CI, 10.4–14.2) with chemotherapy (HR, 0.69; 95% CI, 0.56–0.85; P = .0003) [p.181]. In a subgroup analysis, overall survival was similar in patients with PD-L1 levels of 1% to 49% who received single-agent pembrolizumab (13.4 months; 95% CI, 10.7–18.2) compared with chemotherapy (12.1 months; 95% CI, 11.0–14.0) (HR, 0.92; 95% CI, 0.77–1.11) [p.181].

#### Long-Term Survival Data

Long-term data from KEYNOTE-001 show that 5-year survival for patients with metastatic NSCLC is approximately 23% for patients who received first-line pembrolizumab monotherapy; for patients with PD-L1 levels of 50% or more, 5-year overall survival is about 29.6% [p.181-182]. Median overall survival was 22.3 months (95% CI, 17.1–32.3) for treatment-naïve patients [p.182]. For patients with metastatic NSCLC receiving chemotherapy alone, 5-year overall survival is approximately 6% [p.182].

#### NCCN Recommendations for Monotherapy

The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends IHC testing for PD-L1 expression (category 1) before first-line treatment in all patients with metastatic NSCLC based on the efficacy of pembrolizumab [p.182]. The panel recommends single-agent pembrolizumab (category 1; preferred) as a first-line therapy option for eligible patients with advanced nonsquamous or squamous NSCLC, PD-L1 expression levels of 50% or more, no contraindications to PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors, and negative test results for actionable driver mutations based on clinical trial data and FDA approval [p.182]. Maintenance therapy with pembrolizumab is also a recommended option (category 1) [p.182]. The NCCN NSCLC Panel has preference stratified the ICI regimens and decided that pembrolizumab, atezolizumab, and cemiplimab-rwlc (all category 1) are all preferred single-agent options [p.182].

For patients who have disease progression on first-line therapy with single-agent pembrolizumab, subsequent therapy with initial cytotoxic systemic therapy regimens (e.g., carboplatin plus paclitaxel) is recommended [p.182].

The NCCN NSCLC Panel also recommends single-agent pembrolizumab as a first-line therapy option (category 2B; useful in certain circumstances) for eligible patients with metastatic NSCLC, PD-L1 expression levels of 1% to 49%, no contraindications to PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors, and negative test results for actionable driver mutations [p.182]. The Panel decided that single-agent pembrolizumab is a useful intervention in patients with PD-L1 levels of 1% to 49% when there are contraindications to combination chemotherapy (category 2B; useful in certain circumstances) [p.182]. In patients with PD-L1 levels of 1% to 49%, the HR of 0.92 is not statistically or clinically significant for pembrolizumab monotherapy versus chemotherapy; therefore, pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy is recommended (category 1; preferred) if patients can tolerate the therapy [p.182].

### First-Line Combination Therapy

#### KEYNOTE-189 Trial (Nonsquamous NSCLC)

KEYNOTE-189, a phase 3 randomized trial, compared pembrolizumab added to carboplatin (or cisplatin) plus pemetrexed versus chemotherapy in patients with metastatic nonsquamous NSCLC [p.182]. Most patients received pembrolizumab plus carboplatin plus pemetrexed (72% [445/616]); some received pembrolizumab plus cisplatin plus pemetrexed (28% [171/616]); patients did not have EGFR mutations or ALK rearrangements [p.182].

The estimated rate of overall survival at one year was 69.2% (95% CI, 64.1%-73.8%) in patients receiving pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy versus 49.4% (95% CI, 42.1%-56.2%) for chemotherapy alone (HR for death, 0.49; 95% CI, 0.38–0.64; P < .001) after median 10.5-month follow-up [p.182]. Overall survival was improved regardless of PD-L1 expression levels; TMB did not predict for response [p.182]. For the pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy group, median PFS was 8.8 months (95% CI, 7.6–9.2) compared with 4.9 months (95% CI, 4.7–5.5) for chemotherapy alone (HR for disease progression or death, 0.52; 95% CI, 0.43–0.64; P < .001) [p.182]. Grade 3 or higher adverse events occurred at similar rate in both arms (pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy, 67.2% vs. chemotherapy, 65.8%) [p.182].

#### KEYNOTE-189 NCCN Recommendations

The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends pembrolizumab plus pemetrexed and either carboplatin or cisplatin (category 1; preferred) as a first-line therapy option for eligible patients with metastatic nonsquamous NSCLC (adenocarcinoma, large cell carcinoma) or NSCLC NOS based on clinical trial data and FDA approval [p.182]. The NCCN NSCLC Panel has preference stratified the systemic therapy regimens and decided that these pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy regimens are preferred first-line options for eligible patients with metastatic nonsquamous NSCLC [p.182-183]. These regimens are recommended (category 1; preferred) for patients with metastatic nonsquamous NSCLC, no contraindications to PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors, and negative test results for actionable driver mutations, regardless of PD-L1 expression levels [p.183]. Maintenance therapy with pembrolizumab plus pemetrexed is also a recommended option (category 1) [p.183].

Combination therapy with pembrolizumab plus pemetrexed and either carboplatin or cisplatin may be used as first-line or subsequent therapy option in eligible patients with actionable driver mutations [p.183]. However, targeted therapy is a preferred first-line therapy option for patients with actionable driver mutations, except for EGFR exon 20 mutations or KRAS mutations [p.183]. For patients with metastatic NSCLC and disease progression on combination therapy with PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors plus chemotherapy, subsequent therapy with docetaxel (with or without ramucirumab), pemetrexed (nonsquamous only), albumin-bound paclitaxel, or gemcitabine is recommended if not previously given [p.183].

#### KEYNOTE-407 Trial (Squamous NSCLC)

KEYNOTE-407, a phase 3 randomized trial, compared pembrolizumab added to carboplatin and either paclitaxel or albumin-bound paclitaxel in patients with metastatic squamous cell NSCLC; 32% of patients received albumin-bound paclitaxel [p.183]. Median overall survival was 15.9 months (95% CI, 13.2–not reached) with pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy versus 11.3 months (95% CI, 9.5–14.8) with chemotherapy alone (HR for death, 0.64; 95% CI, 0.49–0.85; P < .001) [p.183]. Patients receiving pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy had an overall response rate of 57.9% compared to 38.4% for those receiving chemotherapy alone [p.183].

Only 38% of patients had a PD-L1 tumor proportion score (TPS) less than 1% [p.183]. Grade 3 or higher adverse events were similar in both groups (pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy, 69.8% vs. chemotherapy alone, 68.2%) [p.183]. Because of adverse events, more patients discontinued treatment with pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy than with chemotherapy (13.3% vs. 6.4%, respectively) [p.183]. A pooled analysis of three randomized trials (KEYNOTE-189, KEYNOTE-407, KEYNOTE-021) in patients with metastatic NSCLC and PD-L1 levels less than 1% showed that overall survival was improved in those receiving pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy versus chemotherapy alone (HR, 0.63; 95% CI, 0.50–0.79) [p.183].

#### KEYNOTE-407 NCCN Recommendations

The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends pembrolizumab plus carboplatin and either paclitaxel or albumin-bound paclitaxel (category 1; preferred) as a first-line therapy option for patients with metastatic squamous cell NSCLC based on clinical trial data and FDA approval [p.183]. Maintenance therapy with pembrolizumab is also a recommended option (category 1) [p.183]. The NCCN NSCLC Panel has preference stratified the systemic therapy regimens and decided that these pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy regimens are preferred for eligible patients with metastatic squamous cell NSCLC [p.183]. These regimens are recommended (category 1; preferred) as first-line therapy options for patients with metastatic squamous cell NSCLC, no contraindications to PD-1 or PD-L1 inhibitors, and negative test results for actionable driver mutations, regardless of PD-L1 expression levels [p.183].

The panel does not recommend pembrolizumab plus cisplatin with either paclitaxel or albumin-bound paclitaxel, because there are fewer data for this regimen [p.183]. Combination therapy with pembrolizumab plus carboplatin and either paclitaxel or albumin-bound paclitaxel may be used as first-line or subsequent therapy option in eligible patients with actionable driver mutations [p.183]. However, targeted therapy is a preferred first-line therapy option for patients with actionable driver mutations, except for EGFR exon 20 mutations or KRAS mutations [p.183].

### Subsequent Therapy

#### KEYNOTE-010 Trial

KEYNOTE-010, a phase 3 randomized trial, assessed single-agent pembrolizumab in patients with previously treated advanced nonsquamous and squamous NSCLC who were PD-L1 positive (≥1%); most patients either currently or previously smoked cigarettes [p.183-184]. There were three arms: pembrolizumab at 2 mg/kg, pembrolizumab at 10 mg/kg, and docetaxel at 75 mg/m² every 3 weeks [p.184]. Median overall survival was 10.4 months for the lower dose of pembrolizumab, 12.7 months for the higher dose, and 8.5 months for docetaxel [p.184].

Overall survival was significantly longer for both doses of pembrolizumab versus docetaxel (pembrolizumab 2 mg/kg: HR, 0.71; 95% CI, 0.58–0.88; P = .0008; pembrolizumab 10 mg/kg: HR, 0.61; 95% CI, 0.49–0.75; P < .0001) [p.184]. For those patients with at least 50% PD-L1 expression in tumor cells, overall survival was also significantly longer at either dose of pembrolizumab versus docetaxel (pembrolizumab 2 mg/kg: 14.9 vs. 8.2 months; HR, 0.54; 95% CI, 0.38–0.77; P = .0002; pembrolizumab 10 mg/kg: 17.3 vs. 8.2 months; HR, 0.50; 95% CI, 0.36–0.70; P < .0001) [p.184].

When compared with docetaxel, there were fewer grade 3 to 5 treatment-related adverse events at either dose of pembrolizumab (pembrolizumab 2 mg/kg: 13% [43/339]; pembrolizumab 10 mg/kg: 16% [55/343]; docetaxel: 35% [109/309]) [p.184]. A total of six treatment-related deaths occurred in patients receiving pembrolizumab (three at each dose) and five treatment-related deaths occurred in the docetaxel arm [p.184].

Long-term data from KEYNOTE-010 show that 5-year survival for patients with metastatic NSCLC is approximately 15.6% for patients who received subsequent pembrolizumab monotherapy versus 6.5% for those receiving docetaxel [p.184]. For patients with PD-L1 levels of 50% or more, 5-year overall survival was about 25% for patients receiving pembrolizumab versus 8.2% for those receiving docetaxel [p.184].

#### Subsequent Therapy NCCN Recommendations

If patients have not previously received a PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor, the NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends single-agent pembrolizumab (category 1; preferred) as a subsequent therapy option for patients with metastatic nonsquamous or squamous NSCLC and PD-L1 expression levels of 1% or more based on clinical trial data and FDA approval [p.184]. Nivolumab and atezolizumab are also recommended subsequent therapy options (category 1; preferred) [p.184]. Testing for PD-L1 expression levels is recommended before prescribing pembrolizumab monotherapy [p.184].

## Tremelimumab-actl and Durvalumab

### Background

Tremelimumab-actl is a monoclonal IgG2 antibody that enhances immune function by inhibiting CTLA-4 and preventing interactions with CD80/CD86; CTLA-4 inhibits T-cell activation [p.184]. Durvalumab is a human immunoglobulin G1 monoclonal antibody that inhibits PD-L1 binding to PD-1 and CD80, thus increasing immune function [p.184]. Immune-mediated adverse events may occur with these agents [p.184]. For patients with immune-mediated adverse events, intravenous high-dose corticosteroids should be administered based on severity [p.184]. These agents should be permanently discontinued for patients with grades 2, 3, or 4 myocarditis and discontinued for other severe or life-threatening immune-mediated adverse events [p.184].

### POSEIDON Trial

POSEIDON, a phase 3 randomized trial, assessed three different first-line treatment arms in 1013 patients with metastatic NSCLC who did not have EGFR or ALK mutations [p.184]. The treatment arms included 1) tremelimumab plus durvalumab plus platinum-based chemotherapy; 2) durvalumab plus platinum-based chemotherapy; and 3) platinum-based chemotherapy alone; there were 675 patients in arms 1 and 3 [p.184-185]. Chemotherapy regimens included 1) tremelimumab plus durvalumab plus albumin-bound paclitaxel plus carboplatin for either nonsquamous or squamous cell histology; 2) tremelimumab plus durvalumab plus pemetrexed plus either cisplatin or carboplatin for nonsquamous NSCLC; or 3) tremelimumab plus durvalumab plus gemcitabine plus either cisplatin or carboplatin for squamous cell histology [p.185]. Most patients received the pemetrexed regimen [p.185]. Most patients either currently smoked (25%) or had previously smoked (57%) [p.185].

Median overall survival was 14 months (95% CI, 11.7–16.1) in patients receiving tremelimumab plus durvalumab plus chemotherapy versus 11.7 months (95% CI, 10.5–13.1) in patients receiving chemotherapy alone (HR, 0.77; 95% CI, 0.65–0.92; P=.003) [p.185]. Subgroup analysis showed that overall survival was similar between the groups for squamous cell histology and other subgroups [p.185].

In patients receiving tremelimumab plus durvalumab plus chemotherapy, grade 3 or 4 adverse events included neutropenia, anemia, leukopenia, lymphocytopenia, increased lipase levels, hyponatremia, and thrombocytopenia [p.185]. Common serious adverse events included pneumonia (11%), anemia (5%), diarrhea (2.4%), thrombocytopenia (2.4%), pyrexia (2.4%), and febrile neutropenia (2.1%) [p.185]. Grade 3 to 4 immune-related adverse events occurred in 10% of patients receiving tremelimumab plus durvalumab plus chemotherapy versus 1.5% receiving chemotherapy alone [p.185]. Treatment-related deaths occurred in 3.3% (11/330) of patients receiving tremelimumab plus durvalumab plus chemotherapy and 2.4% (8/333) receiving chemotherapy alone [p.185].

An exploratory analysis suggests that tremelimumab plus durvalumab plus chemotherapy may be beneficial in patients with metastatic NSCLC who have STK11, KEAP1, or KRAS mutations; however, the sample size was too small to yield definitive conclusions [p.185].

### POSEIDON NCCN Recommendations

The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends tremelimumab-actl plus durvalumab plus platinum-based chemotherapy regimens as first-line therapy options for eligible patients with metastatic NSCLC—regardless of histology or PD-L1 levels, and with negative test results for actionable driver mutations—based on clinical trial data and FDA approval; maintenance therapy with durvalumab with or without pemetrexed is also recommended [p.185]. Combination therapy with tremelimumab-actl plus durvalumab plus platinum-based chemotherapy may be used as first-line or subsequent therapy option, although it is not a preferred option, in eligible patients with actionable driver mutations [p.185]. Targeted therapy is a preferred first-line therapy option for patients with actionable driver mutations, except for EGFR exon 20 mutations or KRAS mutations [p.185].

The category assignment for the tremelimumab-actl plus durvalumab plus platinum-based chemotherapy regimens varies depending on the histology, chemotherapy, and PD-L1 levels [p.185]. The NCCN Panel has preference stratified these regimens and decided that they are other recommended first-line therapy options for eligible patients with metastatic NSCLC based on clinical trial data [p.185].

## Maintenance Therapy

### General Principles

Maintenance therapy refers to therapy given for patients with advanced NSCLC after 4 to 6 cycles of first-line therapy [p.185]. Patients are only candidates for maintenance therapy if their tumors have responded to previous treatment or they have stable disease and their tumors have not progressed [p.185]. Continuation maintenance therapy refers to the use of at least one agent that was given in the first-line regimen [p.185]. Switch maintenance therapy refers to initiation of a different agent not included in the first-line regimen [p.185].

Selection of appropriate maintenance therapy depends on several factors, such as histologic type, presence of mutations or gene fusions, and performance status [p.185]. Maintenance therapy is recommended for select patients with tumor response or stable disease and is not recommended for all patients; it is not recommended for patients with performance status 3 to 4 or those with progression [p.185]. Monitoring is recommended during maintenance therapy with response assessment via CT (with or without contrast) of known or high-risk sites every 6 to 12 weeks [p.186].

### Continuation Maintenance Therapy

For continuation maintenance therapy, select agents (which were initially given with first-line therapy) may be continued until evidence of disease progression or unacceptable toxicity based on clinical trial designs [p.186]. This section mainly discusses continuation maintenance with chemotherapy; continuation maintenance with ICIs is discussed in another section [p.186]. Use of continuation maintenance therapy depends on whether the patient had minimal toxicity during treatment [p.186]. A drug vacation may be more appropriate for some patients [p.186].

Some clinicians feel that continuation maintenance therapy is only appropriate for select patients, because it has only been shown to improve overall survival or quality of life for a few agents and not all agents, although it has been shown to improve PFS [p.186]. In addition, maintenance therapy has not been shown to be superior to subsequent therapy initiated at disease progression [p.186]. A phase 3 randomized trial suggests that conventional cytotoxic agents should not be continued beyond 4 to 6 cycles of therapy; however, many patients assigned to longer duration of therapy did not receive the planned number of cycles [p.186].

#### Pemetrexed Continuation Maintenance

PARAMOUNT, a phase 3 randomized trial, reported that continuation maintenance therapy with pemetrexed slightly increased PFS when compared with placebo (4.1 vs. 2.8 months) [p.186]. Updated results from PARAMOUNT reported that continuation maintenance therapy with pemetrexed also improves overall survival (13.9 vs. 11.0 months) [p.186]. The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends single-agent pemetrexed as continuation maintenance therapy (category 1) in patients with nonsquamous NSCLC based on clinical trial data and FDA approval [p.186].

#### Bevacizumab Continuation Maintenance

POINTBREAK, a phase 3 randomized trial, assessed bevacizumab plus carboplatin plus pemetrexed compared with bevacizumab plus carboplatin plus paclitaxel in patients with metastatic NSCLC; patients received maintenance therapy with either bevacizumab plus pemetrexed or bevacizumab alone [p.186]. PFS was 6 months with pemetrexed plus carboplatin plus bevacizumab versus 5.6 months with paclitaxel plus carboplatin plus bevacizumab [p.186]. The pemetrexed-based arm was associated with less toxicity (less neurotoxicity, less neutropenia, less hair loss) than the paclitaxel-based arm [p.186].

AVAPERL, a phase 3 randomized trial, assessed maintenance therapy with bevacizumab plus pemetrexed versus bevacizumab alone in patients with advanced nonsquamous NSCLC; the initial regimen was bevacizumab plus cisplatin plus pemetrexed [p.186]. An updated analysis reported that overall survival was 17.1 months with bevacizumab plus pemetrexed maintenance versus 13.2 months with bevacizumab alone (HR, 0.87; 95% CI, 0.63–1.21; P = .29) [p.186].

The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends continuation maintenance therapy with bevacizumab plus pemetrexed in patients with nonsquamous NSCLC who initially received bevacizumab plus pemetrexed plus platinum regimen based on clinical trial data [p.186]. Single-agent bevacizumab (category 1) may be continued beyond 4 to 6 cycles of initial therapy (platinum-doublet chemotherapy given with bevacizumab) in patients with nonsquamous NSCLC [p.186]. The Panel recommends that bevacizumab biosimilars may be used in any systemic therapy regimens containing bevacizumab for eligible patients with metastatic NSCLC based on clinical data and FDA approvals [p.186]. Therefore, if a bevacizumab biosimilar was initially used as part of first-line combination therapy, the biosimilar should be continued as maintenance therapy in eligible patients [p.186].
