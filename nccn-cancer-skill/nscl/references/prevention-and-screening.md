## Overview

Lung cancer prevention and screening is a multifaceted approach addressing both disease prevention and early detection. [p.19]

## Key Clinical Principles

### Smoking and Etiology

- Approximately 85% to 90% of lung cancer cases are caused by voluntary or involuntary (second-hand) cigarette smoking. [p.19]
- Lung cancer is unique in that the major etiologic agent is an addictive product made and promoted by an industry. [p.19]
- Reduction of lung cancer mortality requires effective public health policies to prevent initiation of smoking, FDA oversight of tobacco products, and other tobacco control measures. [p.19]

### Non-Smokers and Emerging Risk Factors

- The number of lung cancers in individuals who do not smoke is increasing worldwide. [p.19]
- More research is necessary to identify relevant risk factors including:
  - Environmental exposures (e.g., indoor and outdoor air pollution, radon, asbestos) [p.19]
  - Chronic lung diseases [p.19]
  - Family history/genetic predisposition [p.19]
- These factors may augment lung cancer screening in the future. [p.19]

### Persistent Smoking Consequences

- Persistent smoking is associated with:
  - Second primary cancers [p.19]
  - Treatment complications [p.19]
  - Drug interactions [p.19]
  - Other tobacco-related medical conditions [p.19]
  - Diminished quality of life [p.19]
  - Reduced survival [p.19]

### Second-Hand Smoke Evidence

- Reports from the Surgeon General show that both active smoking and second-hand smoke cause lung cancer. [p.19]
- Evidence shows a 20% to 30% increase in the risk for lung cancer from second-hand smoke exposure associated with living with a person who smokes. [p.19]
- Every person should be informed of:
  - Health consequences of tobacco consumption and exposure [p.19]
  - Addictive nature of tobacco [p.19]
  - Mortal threat posed by tobacco consumption and smoke exposure [p.19]
- Effective legislative, executive, administrative, or other measures should be contemplated at the appropriate governmental level to protect all persons from exposure to tobacco smoke. [p.19]

### Nicotine Addiction and Chemoprevention

- The delivery system of lung carcinogens contains the highly addictive substance, nicotine. [p.19]
- Reduction of lung cancer mortality requires widespread implementation of AHRQ Guidelines to identify, counsel, and treat people with nicotine habituation. [p.19]
- People who smoke or who formerly smoked have significant risk for lung cancer development. [p.19]
- Chemoprevention agents are not yet established for smokers and former smokers. [p.19]
- When possible, these individuals should be encouraged to enroll in chemoprevention trials. [p.19]

- Lung cancer screening using low-dose CT (LDCT) is recommended in select individuals at high risk for lung cancer who smoke or formerly smoked. [p.19]
