# Smoking Cessation -- Evidence Summary

<!-- Source: NCCN NSCLC v3.2026, pages 131-131 -->
<!-- Chunk: smoking-cessation-manuscript -->

## Summary of Key Evidence

Smoking is the primary risk factor for lung cancer, accounting for approximately 81% of lung cancer-related deaths in the United States [p.131]. Approximately 85-90% of lung cancer cases are caused by cigarette smoking [p.131]. Cigarette smoke contains many carcinogenic chemicals including nitrosamines and benzo(a)pyrene diol epoxide [p.131]. The risk for lung cancer increases with the number of packs of cigarettes smoked per day and the number of years spent smoking (pack-years) [p.131].

Individuals who do not smoke but are exposed to secondhand smoke have an increased relative risk (RR = 1.24) of developing lung cancer [p.131]. Active smoking causes not only lung cancer but also other cancers (bladder, cervical, colorectal, esophageal, gastric, kidney, laryngeal, oral cavity, ovarian, pancreatic, pharyngeal) and other diseases [p.131]. Smoking harms nearly every organ in the body, and smokers have increased mortality compared to non-smokers [p.131].

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Smoking Cessation Counseling and Pharmacotherapy

Oncologists should encourage smoking cessation, especially in patients with cancer [p.131]. The 5 A's framework is a useful tool: Ask, Advise, Assess, Assist, Arrange [p.131]. It is in the best interest of patients to quit smoking tobacco, as persistent smoking is associated with second primary cancers, treatment complications, and decreased survival [p.131]. Some surgeons will not operate on current smokers due to increased postoperative pulmonary complications, but active smoking should not exclude patients from early-stage lung cancer surgical treatment that will prolong survival [p.131].

Programs combining behavioral counseling with FDA-approved medications for smoking cessation can be very useful [p.132]. Agents that promote smoking cessation include:

- Nicotine replacement (gum, inhaler, lozenge, nasal spray, patch) [p.132]
- Bupropion sustained release [p.132]
- Varenicline [p.132]

A study suggests cytisine is more efficacious than nicotine replacement therapy, although more side effects were reported with cytisine such as nausea, vomiting, and sleep disorders [p.132].

Studies have shown that varenicline is better than bupropion or nicotine patch for smoking cessation [p.132]. However, the FDA has issued an alert for varenicline regarding neuropsychiatric symptoms. Varenicline has been associated with visual disturbances, movement disorders, unconsciousness, and cardiovascular disorders; therefore, it is banned in truck and bus drivers, pilots, and air traffic controllers [p.132]. Other side effects of varenicline include nausea, abnormal dreams, insomnia, and headache [p.132].

Bupropion may also be associated with similar serious neuropsychiatric symptoms. Nicotine replacement has fewer adverse effects than varenicline or bupropion [p.132]. Despite potential adverse effects, it is probably more beneficial for motivated patients to use agents to promote smoking cessation [p.132].

## Special Considerations

The effectiveness of varenicline for preventing relapse has not been clearly established [p.132]. The American Cancer Society (ACS) has resources on How to Quit Using Tobacco.
