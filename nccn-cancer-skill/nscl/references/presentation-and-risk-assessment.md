## Diagnosis

### Clinical Presentation and Risk Assessment

Incidental finding of nodule suspicious for lung cancer requires [p.20]:
- **Multidisciplinary evaluation** [p.20] including thoracic surgeons, thoracic radiologists, and pulmonologists to determine the likelihood of a cancer diagnosis and the optimal diagnostic or follow-up strategy [p.20]
- **Smoking cessation counseling** [p.20]

Risk calculators can be used to quantify individual patient and radiologic factors but do not replace evaluation by a multidisciplinary diagnostic team with substantial experience in the diagnosis of lung cancer [p.20].

### Patient Risk Factors [p.20]

- Age [p.20]
- Smoking history [p.20]
- Previous cancer history [p.20]
- Family history [p.20]
- Occupational exposures [p.20]
- Other lung disease (chronic obstructive pulmonary disease [COPD], pulmonary fibrosis) [p.20]
- Exposure to infectious agents (e.g., endemic areas of fungal infections, tuberculosis) or risk factors or history suggestive of infection (e.g., immune suppression, aspiration, infectious respiratory symptoms) [p.20]

### Radiologic Factors [p.20]

- Size, shape, and density of the pulmonary nodule [p.20]
- Associated parenchymal abnormalities (e.g., scarring or suspicion of inflammatory changes) [p.20]
- Fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG) avidity on FDG-PET/CT imaging [p.20]

The most important radiologic factor is change or stability compared with a previous imaging study [p.20].

## Workup

### Risk Stratification and Follow-up Strategy

#### Solid Nodules (DIAG-2) [p.21]

**Incidental finding: solid nodule(s) on chest CT** [p.21]

**High-risk patients** (history of smoking or other known risk factors; known risk factors include history of lung cancer in a first-degree relative or exposure to asbestos, radon, or uranium) [p.21]:

- <6 mm: No routine follow-up [p.21]
- 6–8 mm: CT at 6–12 mo [p.21]
  - If stable: Consider CT at 18–24 mo [p.21]
- >8 mm: Consider CT at 3 mo, FDG-PET/CT, or biopsy [p.21]

**Low-risk patients** (minimal or absent history of smoking or other known risk factors) [p.21]:

- <6 mm: No routine follow-up [p.21]
- 6–8 mm: CT at 12 mo (optional) [p.21]
  - If stable: CT at 6–12 mo [p.21]
- >8 mm: Stable → Repeat CT at 18–24 mo [p.21]
  - Unstable or concerning: Consider CT at 3 mo, FDG-PET/CT, or biopsy [p.21]

#### Subsolid Nodules (DIAG-3) [p.22]

**Incidental finding: subsolid nodule(s) on chest CT** [p.22]

Non-solid (ground-glass) nodules may require longer follow-up to exclude indolent adenocarcinoma [p.22].

**Solitary pure ground-glass nodules** [p.22]:

- <6 mm: No routine follow-up [p.22]
- ≥6 mm: CT at 6–12 mo to confirm no growth or development of a solid component, then CT every 2 y until 5 y [p.22]

Consider follow-up imaging at 12 months for individuals at high risk of lung cancer [p.22].

**Solitary part-solid nodules** [p.22]:

- <6 mm: CT at 3–6 mo. If stable, consider CT at 2 and 4 y [p.22]
- ≥6 mm: CT at 3–6 mo to confirm no growth or change in solid component, then annual CT for 5 y [p.22]
  - If solid component ≥6 mm, consider FDG-PET/CT or biopsy [p.22]

**Multiple subsolid nodules** [p.22]:

- <6 mm: No routine follow-up [p.22]
- ≥6 mm: CT at 3–6 mo. Subsequent management based on most suspicious nodule(s) [p.22]

### FDG-PET/CT Imaging [p.21, p.22]

FDG-PET/CT performed skull base to mid-thigh [p.21].

A positive FDG-PET/CT result is defined as a standardized uptake value (SUV) in the lung nodule greater than the baseline mediastinal blood pool [p.21].

**False-positive FDG-PET/CT findings** can be caused by infection or inflammation, including [p.21]:
- Absence of lung cancer with localized infection [p.21]
- Presence of lung cancer with associated (e.g., postobstructive) infection [p.21]
- Presence of lung cancer with related inflammation (e.g., nodal, parenchymal, pleural) [p.21]

**False-negative FDG-PET/CT** can be caused by [p.21]:
- Small nodule [p.21]
- Low cellular density (nonsolid nodule or ground-glass opacity [GGO]) [p.21]
- Low tumor avidity for FDG (e.g., adenocarcinoma in situ [AIS; previously known as bronchoalveolar carcinoma], carcinoid tumor) [p.21]

If a false-negative FDG-PET/CT is due to low tumor avidity and/or low cellularity is suspected, follow-up CT or biopsy are reasonable options [p.21].

## Staging

### Principles of Diagnostic Evaluation (DIAG-A) [p.23-25]

#### Preoperative Biopsy Decision [p.23]

The decision to pursue preoperative biopsy of a potential stage I lung cancer should be informed by the pre-test probability of malignancy [p.23].

Factors that might be considered in pre-test probability assessment include [p.23]:
- Risk factors [p.23]
- Radiologic appearance (including comparison to prior chest imaging if available or FDG-PET/CT if performed) [p.23]
- Current or prior residence in regions with prevalent endemic infectious lung disease (i.e., fungal, mycobacterial) [p.23]
- Other potential factors [p.23]

**Patients with very high pre-test probability of stage IA lung cancer do not require a biopsy before surgery** [p.23]. A biopsy adds time, costs, and procedural risk and may not be needed for treatment decisions [p.23].

If a preoperative tissue diagnosis has not been obtained, then an intraoperative diagnosis (i.e., wedge resection, needle biopsy) is necessary before lobectomy, bilobectomy, or pneumonectomy [p.23].

#### Situations for Preoperative Biopsy [p.23]

- A non-lung cancer diagnosis that can be diagnosed by minimally invasive biopsy is at least moderately likely (e.g., granulomatous nodule due to endemic fungus) [p.23]
- Suspected stage IB or higher lung cancer in patients who may be candidates for systemic therapy prior to surgery [p.23]
- An intraoperative diagnosis appears difficult or very risky [p.23]
- To establish the diagnosis prior to stereotactic ablative radiotherapy (SABR) [p.23]

Patients require tissue confirmation of non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) before a lobectomy, bilobectomy, or pneumonectomy [p.23]. If a preoperative or intraoperative tissue diagnosis appears risky or unreliable, multidisciplinary evaluation that at least includes interventional radiology, thoracic surgery, and interventional pulmonology is recommended to determine the safest and most efficient approach for biopsy [p.23].

#### Bronchoscopy [p.24]

A preoperative bronchoscopy may be preferred for tissue diagnosis and/or mediastinal staging (endobronchial ultrasound [EBUS]) [p.24].

If a bronchoscopy has not been previously performed for diagnosis or staging, bronchoscopy should be performed during the planned surgical resection, rather than as a separate procedure [p.24].

**Bronchoscopy is required before surgical resection** [p.24]. A separate bronchoscopy may not be needed for treatment decisions before the time of surgery and adds time, costs, and procedural risk [p.24].

A preoperative bronchoscopy may be appropriate if [p.24]:
- A central tumor requires pre-resection evaluation for biopsy [p.24]
- Surgical planning is needed (e.g., potential sleeve resection) [p.24]
- Preoperative airway preparation is needed (e.g., coring out an obstructive lesion) [p.24]

#### Invasive Mediastinal Staging [p.24]

Invasive mediastinal staging is recommended before surgical resection for most patients with clinical stage I or II lung cancer except for peripheral clinical stage I tumors with no suspicious nodes on chest CT or FDG-PET/CT [p.24].

For patients undergoing EBUS/endoscopic ultrasound (EUS) staging, this most commonly should be a separate procedure to allow for pathologic evaluation [p.24].

Patients having mediastinoscopy should preferably undergo invasive mediastinal staging (mediastinoscopy) as the initial step before the planned resection (during the same anesthetic procedure), rather than as a separate procedure [p.24].

Preoperative invasive mediastinal staging may be appropriate for [p.24]:
- Strong clinical suspicion of N2 or N3 nodal disease [p.24]
- When intraoperative cytology or frozen section analysis is not available [p.24]

#### Diagnostic Tools Available [p.24-25]

**Diagnostic tools that should be routinely available** [p.24]:
- Sputum cytology [p.24]
- Bronchoscopy with biopsy and transbronchial needle aspiration (TBNA) [p.24]
- Image-guided transthoracic needle core biopsy (preferred) or fine-needle aspiration (FNA) [p.24]
- Thoracentesis [p.24]
- Mediastinoscopy [p.24]
- Video-assisted thoracic surgery (VATS) and open surgical biopsy [p.24]
- Left anterior mediastinotomy/Chamberlain [p.24]

**Diagnostic tools that provide important additional strategies for biopsy** [p.24]:
- EBUS–guided biopsy [p.24]
- EUS–guided biopsy [p.24]
- Navigational bronchoscopy [p.24]
- Robotic bronchoscopy [p.24]

#### Optimal Diagnostic Strategy [p.25]

The preferred diagnostic strategy for an individual patient depends on [p.25]:
- Size and location of the tumor [p.25]
- Presence of mediastinal or distant disease [p.25]
- Patient characteristics (such as pulmonary pathology and/or other significant comorbidities) [p.25]
- Local experience and expertise [p.25]

**Factors to be considered in choosing the optimal diagnostic step** [p.25]:
- Anticipated diagnostic yield (sensitivity) [p.25]
- Diagnostic accuracy including specificity and particularly the reliability of a negative diagnostic study (i.e., true negative) [p.25]
- Adequate volume of tissue specimen for diagnosis and molecular testing [p.25]
- Invasiveness and risk of procedure [p.25]
- Efficiency of evaluation [p.25]
  - Access and timeliness of procedure [p.25]
  - Concomitant staging is beneficial, because it avoids additional biopsies or procedures [p.25]
  - FDG-PET/CT imaging is frequently best performed before a diagnostic biopsy site is chosen in cases of high clinical suspicion for aggressive, advanced-stage tumors [p.25]
- Technologies and expertise available [p.25]
- Tumor viability at proposed biopsy site from FDG-PET/CT imaging [p.25]

**Decisions about the optimal diagnostic steps** for suspected stage I to III lung cancer should be made by thoracic radiologists, interventional radiologists, thoracic surgeons, and pulmonologists who devote a significant portion of their practice to thoracic oncology [p.25].

Multidisciplinary evaluation should also include a pulmonologist or thoracic surgeon with expertise in advanced bronchoscopic techniques for diagnosis [p.25].

The least invasive biopsy with the highest yield is preferred as the first diagnostic study [p.25].

#### Specific Diagnostic Approaches [p.25]

**Central masses with suspected endobronchial involvement** [p.25]:
- Patients should undergo bronchoscopy [p.25]

**Pulmonary nodules** [p.25]:
- May benefit from navigational bronchoscopy (including robotic), radial EBUS, or transthoracic needle aspiration (TTNA) [p.25]

**Suspected nodal disease** [p.25]:
- Should be biopsied by EBUS, EUS, navigational bronchoscopy, or mediastinoscopy [p.25]
- EBUS provides access to nodal stations 2R/2L, 3P, 4R/4L, 7, 10R/10L, 11–13, and other hilar nodal stations if necessary [p.25]
- An EBUS-TBNA negative for malignancy in a clinically (FDG-PET/CT and/or CT) positive mediastinum should undergo subsequent mediastinoscopy prior to surgical resection [p.25]
- EUS–guided biopsy provides additional access to stations 3P, 5, 7, 8, and 9 lymph nodes if these are clinically suspicious [p.25]
- TTNA and anterior mediastinotomy (i.e., Chamberlain procedure) provide additional access to anterior mediastinal (stations 3a, 5, and 6) lymph nodes [p.25]
- If TTNA is not possible due to proximity to aorta, VATS biopsy is also an option [p.25]

**Adrenal gland access** [p.25]:
- EUS provides reliable access to the left adrenal gland [p.25]

**On-site evaluation** [p.25]:
- Rapid on-site evaluation (ROSE), when available, helps to increase diagnostic and molecular yield [p.25]

**Pleural effusion** [p.25]:
- Patients with lung cancer with an associated pleural effusion should undergo thoracentesis and cytology [p.25]
- A negative pleural cytology result does not exclude pleural involvement [p.25]
- Thoracoscopic evaluation of the pleura should be considered before starting curative intent therapy if pleural fluid is a lymphocytic exudate with negative pleural fluid cytology [p.25]

**Metastatic disease** [p.25]:
- Patients suspected of having a solitary site of metastatic disease should have tissue confirmation of that site if feasible [p.25]
- Patients suspected of having metastatic disease should have confirmation from one of the metastatic sites if feasible [p.25]
- Patients who may have multiple sites of metastatic disease—based on a strong clinical suspicion—should have biopsy of the primary lung lesion or mediastinal lymph nodes if it is technically difficult or very risky to biopsy a metastatic site [p.25]

## Follow-Up

Follow-up strategies are detailed in DIAG-2 (solid nodules) and DIAG-3 (subsolid nodules) sections above, with recommendations stratified by nodule size, radiologic characteristics, and patient risk factors. All follow-up recommendations are category 2A unless otherwise indicated [p.20].
