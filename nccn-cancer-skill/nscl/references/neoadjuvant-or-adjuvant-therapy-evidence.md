# Neoadjuvant or Adjuvant Therapy -- Evidence Summary

<!-- Source: NCCN NSCLC v3.2026, pages 194-196 -->
<!-- Chunk: neoadjuvant-or-adjuvant-therapy-manuscript -->

## Summary of Key Evidence

Chemotherapy is recommended for patients with locally advanced NSCLC as neoadjuvant or adjuvant therapy (perioperative therapy) [p.194]. Multiple phase 3 trials have demonstrated survival advantages with preoperative chemotherapy compared to surgery alone [p.195]. Data suggest chemotherapy may eradicate micrometastatic disease in patients with stage III disease who have both local and distant failure patterns [p.194].

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Neoadjuvant and Adjuvant Chemotherapy

**Cisplatin-Based Regimens**: The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends cisplatin combined with docetaxel, etoposide, gemcitabine, or vinorelbine as neoadjuvant or adjuvant therapy options for all histologies in eligible patients with locally advanced disease [p.194]. For nonsquamous NSCLC, cisplatin plus pemetrexed is preferred; for squamous cell NSCLC, cisplatin plus either gemcitabine or docetaxel is preferred [p.194]. Cisplatin combined with vinorelbine or etoposide are "other recommended" options [p.194].

**Carboplatin-Based Regimens**: For patients with comorbidities or those who cannot tolerate cisplatin, carboplatin may be combined with pemetrexed (nonsquamous only), paclitaxel, or gemcitabine [p.194].

**SWOG S9900 Trial**: One of the largest randomized trials examining preoperative chemotherapy in early-stage NSCLC assessed surgery alone compared with surgery plus preoperative paclitaxel plus carboplatin in patients with stage IB/IIA and stage IIB/IIIA NSCLC (excluding superior sulcus tumors). Progression-free survival and overall survival were improved with preoperative chemotherapy [p.195]. The trial was stopped early because of positive results [p.195].

### Stage-Specific Adjuvant Approaches

**Stage IA Disease**: Post-surgical treatment options for patients with stage IA tumors (T1abc, N0) and positive surgical margins (R1, R2) include re-resection (preferred) or radiotherapy (category 2B); observation is recommended for patients with negative surgical margins (R0) [p.195].

**Stage IIA High-Risk Disease**: Postoperative chemotherapy (category 1) is recommended for patients with T2ab, N0 tumors and negative surgical margins who have high-risk features, including poorly differentiated tumors, vascular invasion, wedge resection, visceral pleural involvement, and unknown lymph node status (Nx) [p.195].

### Adjuvant Immunotherapy

**Atezolizumab**: The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends atezolizumab as an adjuvant therapy option for eligible patients with completely resected (R0) stage IIB to IIIA, stage IIIB (only T3, N2), or high-risk stage IIA NSCLC and PD-L1 of 1% or more, and negative for EGFR exon 19 deletions, EGFR L858R mutations, or ALK rearrangements, who have previously received adjuvant chemotherapy [p.195].

**Pembrolizumab**: The NCCN Panel recommends pembrolizumab as an adjuvant therapy option following adjuvant chemotherapy for eligible patients with completely resected early-stage NSCLC based on clinical trial data and FDA approval [p.195].

**Osimertinib**: The panel recommends osimertinib as an adjuvant therapy option for eligible patients with completely resected (R0) stage IB to IIIA or stage IIIB (only T3, N2) NSCLC and EGFR exon 19 deletions or EGFR L858R mutations who have previously received adjuvant chemotherapy or are ineligible to receive platinum-based chemotherapy [p.195]. For patients with these EGFR mutations and PD-L1 levels of 1% or more, the panel only recommends osimertinib; the panel does not recommend single-agent atezolizumab or pembrolizumab or combination therapy with osimertinib plus atezolizumab or pembrolizumab [p.195].

**Stage IIB Adjuvant Therapy**: The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends adjuvant chemotherapy (category 1) followed by atezolizumab, pembrolizumab, or osimertinib for eligible patients with appropriate biomarkers, negative surgical margins, and stage IIB disease [p.195].

### Radiation Therapy

**Postoperative RT in Early-Stage Disease**: After complete resection of clinical early-stage NSCLC, postoperative radiotherapy (PORT) was found to be detrimental for pathologic N0 or N1 stage disease in meta-analysis data from SEER of small randomized trials using older techniques [p.196].

**LungART and PORT-C Trials**: Data from two randomized phase 3 trials, the LungART and PORT-C trials, show that postoperative radiotherapy did not improve survival compared with no postoperative RT, although locoregional control was significantly improved in patients with completely resected stage IIIA (N2) NSCLC who received neoadjuvant or adjuvant chemotherapy [p.196]. Postoperative RT may be considered for select patients with negative margins and high-risk N2 disease [p.196].

## Special Considerations

### Superior Sulcus Tumors

For stage IIIA superior sulcus tumors (T4 extension, N0–1) that become resectable after preoperative concurrent chemoradiation, resection followed by chemotherapy is recommended and then atezolizumab, pembrolizumab, or osimertinib, depending on biomarker status [p.196]. If the lesion remains unresectable after preoperative concurrent chemoradiation, completion of definitive dose chemoradiation without interruption, followed by consolidation immunotherapy with durvalumab (category 1) is recommended for eligible patients [p.196].

### Positive Surgical Margins

For patients with positive margins in stage IB/IIA disease, management depends on resection type. Most NCCN Member Institutions favor concurrent chemoradiation for positive margins, but sequential chemoradiation is reasonable in frailer patients [p.195-196].

### N2 Disease

For patients with stage III disease and positive mediastinal nodes (T1–3, N2) with no apparent disease progression after initial treatment with induction systemic therapy with or without RT, surgery is recommended [p.196].

### Oligometastatic Disease

After progression on tyrosine kinase inhibitors, patients with EGFR mutation–positive metastatic NSCLC may be able to continue with their current TKIs; local therapy can be considered to treat limited progression (such as stereotactic radiosurgery to brain metastases or other sites, stereotactic ablative body radiotherapy for limited thoracic or other metastatic disease, or surgery) [p.194].
