# Surveillance -- Evidence Summary

<!-- Source: NCCN NSCLC v3.2026, pages 197-197 -->
<!-- Chunk: surveillance-manuscript -->

## Summary of Key Evidence

Surveillance is recommended for eligible patients with NSCLC who have no evidence of clinical or radiographic disease after definitive therapy [p.197]. Recurrence is common after treatment for NSCLC, and surveillance recommendations are based on practice patterns rather than randomized trial data [p.197].

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Surveillance Schedules

Because randomized phase 3 trial data are not available to clarify surveillance recommendations, surveillance recommendations are controversial [p.197]. The surveillance recommendations were compiled by polling the NCCN NSCLC Panel regarding their practice patterns [p.197]. Details regarding specific surveillance schedules are outlined in the algorithm based on stage and the definitive treatment [p.197].

**Imaging Protocols**: The NCCN Guidelines recommend a chest CT scan with (or without) contrast and a history and physical examination for initial surveillance schedules (2–5 years after definitive treatment) followed by annual low-dose non-contrast-enhanced CT and a history and physical examination [p.198].

**Advanced Imaging**: Fluorodeoxyglucose positron emission tomography/CT or brain MRI is not routinely recommended for routine surveillance in patients without symptoms [p.198]. However, PET may be useful for assessing CT scans that appear to show malignant neoplasms but may be radiation fibrosis, atelectasis, or other benign conditions [p.198].

**Radiation-Induced Changes**: Areas previously treated with radiotherapy may remain fluorodeoxyglucose avid for up to 2 years; therefore, histologic confirmation of suspicious areas with apparent "recurrent" disease is needed [p.198].

**Recurrence Assessment**: The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends assessing patients with recurrences using PET/CT and brain MRI with contrast; if brain MRI is not possible, then CT with contrast of the head is recommended [p.198].

### Symptom Management

Information about smoking cessation (such as advice, counseling, therapy) should be provided for patients undergoing surveillance to improve their quality of life [p.198].

## Special Considerations

### Lung Cancer Screening vs. Surveillance

The surveillance recommendations for patients who have been treated for NSCLC are different from the screening recommendations for individuals at high risk for lung cancer [p.198].

**NLST Study**: A large randomized trial (NLST) assessed lung screening with low-dose CT screening versus chest radiography in individuals at high risk for lung cancer. Low-dose CT screening decreased mortality from lung cancer (mainly adenocarcinoma) compared with chest radiography (247 vs. 309 deaths, respectively; 20% relative reduction in mortality; 95% CI, 6.8–26.7; P = .004) [p.198]. The NCCN NSCLC Panel feels that low-dose CT is beneficial for identifying recurrences in patients previously treated for NSCLC [p.198].

### Metastatic Disease Surveillance

Surveillance schedules for most patients with metastatic disease are individualized for each patient, although the NCCN Guidelines provide a surveillance schedule for certain patients with stage IV oligometastatic disease [p.197].

### Cancer Survivorship Care

The NCCN Guidelines include information about the long-term follow-up care of NSCLC survivors, including guidelines for routine cancer surveillance, immunizations, health monitoring, counseling for wellness and health promotion, and cancer screening [p.198].
