## Summary of Key Evidence

Clinical evaluation of NSCLC requires a systematic approach to staging and patient workup. The NCCN NSCLC Panel provides evidence-based recommendations for initial assessment, mediastinal evaluation, and treatment planning that emphasize the importance of multidisciplinary evaluation before treatment initiation. [p.187-188]

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Maintenance Therapy Approaches

**Continuation Maintenance with Gemcitabine**

The IFCT-GFPC 0502 phase 3 randomized trial compared continuation maintenance therapy with gemcitabine or erlotinib after first-line cisplatin-gemcitabine therapy in patients with advanced NSCLC. [p.187] Continuation maintenance with single-agent gemcitabine increased PFS to 3.8 months compared to 2.9 months with switch maintenance erlotinib and 1.9 months with observation. [p.187, refs 731,886]

A CECOG phase 3 randomized trial assessed continuation maintenance with gemcitabine versus best supportive care after cisplatin-gemcitabine. [p.187] Results showed a slight PFS difference but no difference in overall survival (13 vs. 11 months; P = .195). [p.187, ref 887]

**NCCN Recommendation:** The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends gemcitabine (category 2B) as continuation maintenance therapy regardless of histology in patients with metastatic NSCLC, negative test results for actionable driver mutations, and PD-L1 expression less than 1%. [p.187]

### Switch Maintenance Therapy

**Pemetrexed Switch Maintenance**

Two phase 3 randomized trials reported benefits in PFS and overall survival with pemetrexed initiation after first-line chemotherapy (4-6 cycles) in patients with nonsquamous NSCLC and no apparent disease progression. [p.187, refs 889,890]

**NCCN Recommendation:** The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends switch maintenance therapy with pemetrexed in eligible patients with nonsquamous cell carcinoma, negative test results for actionable driver mutations, and PD-L1 expression less than 1% based on clinical trial data and FDA approval. [p.187, refs 890,891]

**Erlotinib - Not Recommended**

The NCCN NSCLC Panel does not recommend erlotinib as switch maintenance therapy for patients with nonsquamous NSCLC, good PS, and negative test results for actionable driver mutations based on results from the IUNO randomized trial and revised FDA indication. [p.187, ref 892]

The NCCN NSCLC Panel also does not recommend switch maintenance therapy with erlotinib in patients with squamous cell NSCLC, as overall survival and quality of life were not improved. [p.187-188, refs 731,893]

**Docetaxel - Not Recommended**

A phase 3 trial assessed switch maintenance therapy with docetaxel given either immediately after gemcitabine-carboplatin or delayed until progression in advanced NSCLC; however, many patients in the delayed chemotherapy arm did not receive docetaxel. [p.188, ref 894] The panel deleted the recommendation for switch maintenance therapy with docetaxel in squamous cell NSCLC because better options are available. [p.188, refs 894,895]

### General Considerations on Maintenance Therapy

Issues have been raised about switch maintenance therapy, including trial design, modest survival benefits, quality of life impacts, and toxicity. [p.187, refs 807,888]

## Clinical Evaluation and Staging

### Initial Workup

After patients are confirmed to have NSCLC based on pathologic diagnosis, clinical evaluation must be performed. [p.188] In patients with symptoms, clinical stage is initially determined from disease history (cough, dyspnea, chest pain, weight loss) and physical examination together with a limited battery of tests. [p.188]

**Smoking Cessation:** The NCCN NSCLC Panel recommends that smoking cessation advice, counseling, and pharmacotherapy be provided to patients. [p.188, refs 29,898-900]

**Multidisciplinary Evaluation:** After clinical stage determination, the patient is assigned to pathways defined by stage, specific stage subdivision, and tumor location. [p.188] A multidisciplinary evaluation should be done before treatment, and in some patients, diagnosis, staging, and surgical resection are done during the same operative procedure. [p.188]

### Mediastinal Node Evaluation

**Imaging Modalities**

FDG PET/CT scans can be used as initial assessment of hilar and mediastinal nodes to determine whether N1, N2, or N3 nodes are positive, which is key for determining stage II and III disease. [p.188] However, CT scans have known limitations for evaluating lymph node involvement extent in lung cancer. [p.188, refs 89,901-903]

When compared with noninvasive staging methods (EBUS, EUS), surgical staging with mediastinoscopy is more appropriate for certain settings when evaluating mediastinal nodes; however, clinicians use both methods. [p.188, ref 89]

**Mediastinoscopy Recommendations**

Mediastinoscopy is encouraged as part of initial evaluation, particularly if imaging results are not conclusive and the probability of mediastinal involvement is high (based on tumor size and location). [p.188] Mediastinoscopy is appropriate for patients with T2 to T3 lesions even if FDG PET/CT scan does not suggest mediastinal node involvement. [p.188]

Mediastinoscopy may also be appropriate to confirm mediastinal node involvement in patients with positive FDG PET/CT scan. [p.188] Although mediastinal biopsy is generally preferred, the risks in selected patients may outweigh the benefits, such as in medically inoperable patients. [p.188]

**Peripheral vs. Central Tumors**

In patients with peripheral tumors (outer one third of lung) less than 3 cm, pathologic mediastinal lymph node evaluation is optional if nodes are FDG PET/CT negative due to low likelihood of positive mediastinal nodes. [p.188, ref 904]

Invasive mediastinal staging is recommended for central tumors. [p.188] Mediastinal evaluation can be considered in patients with clinical stage IA disease (T1abc, N0). [p.188]

In patients with peripheral T2a, central T1abc, or T2a lesions with negative FDG PET/CT scans, the risk for mediastinal lymph node involvement is higher and mediastinoscopy and/or EUS-FNA and EBUS-TBNA are recommended. [p.188]

**Selective Mediastinoscopy Strategy**

Dillemans et al reported a selective mediastinoscopy strategy, proceeding straight to thoracotomy without mediastinoscopy for T1 peripheral tumors without enlarged mediastinal lymph nodes on preoperative CT. [p.188, ref 905] This strategy resulted in a 16% incidence of positive N2 nodes discovered only at the time of thoracotomy. [p.188]

### Accuracy of Staging Methods

Chest CT scans for identifying N2 disease had sensitivity and specificity rates of 69% and 71%, respectively. [p.188] Using chest CT scan plus mediastinoscopy was significantly more accurate (89% vs. 71%) than using CT scan alone for identifying N2 disease. [p.188]

When using CT scans, node positivity is based on lymph node size, so CT scans will miss small metastases that do not result in node enlargement. [p.188] Arita et al examined lung cancer metastases in normal-size mediastinal lymph nodes in 90 patients and found a 16% incidence (14/90) of false-negative chest CT scans. [p.188]

## Special Considerations

### Targeted Therapy Overview

Targeted therapies are available for eligible patients with NSCLC. [p.187, refs 171,896,897] For information about targeted therapy use in early-stage resectable NSCLC, see the Surgery Followed by Adjuvant Therapy and Neoadjuvant and Adjuvant Therapy sections. [p.187] For targeted therapies in advanced or metastatic NSCLC with clinically actionable biomarkers, see the Systemic Therapy for Advanced or Metastatic NSCLC section. [p.187]

### Lung Nodule Screening and Incidental Findings

The workup and evaluation of incidental lung nodules detected on imaging for other conditions are described in the NSCLC algorithm. [p.187] The cutoff threshold is 6 mm for a positive scan result for incidental solid and subsolid lung nodules detected on chest CT based on the Fleischner criteria. [p.187, refs 71-75]

Low-dose CT screening is recommended for asymptomatic select patients at high risk for lung cancer, and management of nodules detected in these patients is described in the NCCN Guidelines for Lung Cancer Screening. [p.187-188]
