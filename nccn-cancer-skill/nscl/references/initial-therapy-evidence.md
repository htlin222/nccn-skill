## Summary of Key Evidence

Accurate pathologic assessment and staging are essential before treatment for NSCLC, because management varies depending on the stage, histology, presence of genetic variants, and performance status [p.190]. Determination of tumor resectability should be made by thoracic surgeons who perform lung cancer surgery as a prominent part of their practice [p.190].

### Staging and Imaging Performance Data

CT scans have known limitations for evaluating the extent of lymph node involvement in lung cancer [p.189]. For mediastinal lymph node staging, CT demonstrates sensitivity of 40-65% and specificity of 45-90% depending on clinical scenario [p.189].

PET scans may be more sensitive than CT scans as they detect tumor physiology rather than anatomy [p.189]. Key performance data:
- Chin et al: PET staging of mediastinal nodes showed 78% sensitivity, 81% specificity, 89% negative predictive value [p.189]
- Kernstine et al: PET more sensitive than CT in identifying mediastinal node disease (70% vs. 65%) [p.189]

FDG PET/CT has demonstrated utility in restaging patients after adjuvant therapy and is recommended by the NCCN panel for evaluation and staging of stage I-IV NSCLC [p.189]. When patients with early-stage disease are accurately staged using FDG PET/CT, inappropriate surgery is avoided [p.189]. EBUS-TBNA demonstrates high sensitivity and specificity for staging mediastinal and hilar lymph nodes and can clarify positive CT/PET findings [p.189].

### Imaging Recommendations

- Brain MRI with contrast recommended for stage II, III, and IV disease to rule out asymptomatic brain metastases if aggressive combined-modality therapy being considered [p.189-190]
- Brain MRI optional for stage IB disease; consider for high-risk patients (tumors >5 cm, central location) [p.190]
- Routine bone scans NOT recommended [p.190]

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Targeted and Immunotherapy Indications

**First-line targeted therapy** recommended for metastatic NSCLC with actionable mutations: ALK, BRAF p.V600E, EGFR, METex14 skipping, NTRK1/2/3, RET, ROS1 [p.190]

**Second-line targeted therapy** recommended for metastatic NSCLC with EGFR exon 20 insertions or KRAS p.G12C mutations [p.190]

**First-line immunotherapy** recommended for metastatic NSCLC with negative actionable driver mutation testing; may be used in patients with actionable mutations though generally not preferred [p.190]

### Stage I, II, and IIIA Disease

#### Early-Stage Surgical Management
- Patients with stage I or subset of stage II (T1-2,N1) tumors candidates for surgical resection and mediastinal lymph node dissection [p.190]
- Positive mediastinal nodes (N2) discovered at surgery require additional staging and modified treatment [p.190]

#### Radiation Therapy Options
- Definitive RT, preferably SABR, recommended for medically inoperable or refusing patients [p.190]
- RT can be considered alternative to surgery in high-risk patients [p.190]
- Image-guided thermal ablation (cryotherapy, microwave, RFA) option for selected medically inoperable patients [p.190]

#### Superior Sulcus Tumors (T3-4, N0-1)

**Resectable (T3, N0-1) tumors:**
- Preoperative concurrent chemoradiation followed by surgical resection shows 2-year survival 50-70% range; 5-year survival approximately 40% [p.190-191]
- NCCN recommendation: Preoperative concurrent chemoradiation → surgical resection → adjuvant chemotherapy + atezolizumab, pembrolizumab, or osimertinib (if EGFR mutations present or PD-L1 ≥1%) [p.191]

**Unresectable (T4, N0-1) tumors:**
- Definitive concurrent chemoradiation followed by durvalumab (category 1) [p.191]

#### Chest Wall, Proximal Airway, or Mediastinal Tumors (T3-4, N0-1)

**Resectable tumors:** Surgical resection is preferred treatment; preoperative systemic therapy or concurrent chemoradiation before surgery are alternative options [p.191]

**Unresectable (T4, N0-1, without pleural effusion):**
- Definitive concurrent chemoradiation (category 1) followed by consolidation immunotherapy with durvalumab (category 1) [p.191]
- Consolidation chemotherapy option if durvalumab not used [p.191]
- NOTE: Consolidation chemotherapy NOT recommended if receiving durvalumab due to increased pneumonitis risk [p.191]

#### Stage IIIA with Positive Mediastinal Nodes (T1-2, N2)

Multimodality therapy recommended for most stage III NSCLC patients [p.191]. Treatment based on pathologic mediastinal lymph node evaluation:

**Negative mediastinal biopsy findings:** Candidates for surgery with mediastinal lymph node dissection or sampling [p.191]

**Node-positive disease:**
- Brain MRI with contrast and FDG PET/CT scan (if not previously done) recommended to search for distant metastases [p.191]
- When distant metastases absent: definitive concurrent chemoradiation therapy recommended [p.191]

**Post-operative management by nodal status:**
- N2 nodes, R2 resection or positive margins: Concurrent chemoradiation [p.192]
- N2 nodes, R1 resection: Sequential or concurrent chemoradiation (most NCCN institutions favor concurrent) [p.192]
- N2 nodes, negative margins: Adjuvant chemotherapy (category 1) or sequential chemotherapy with RT [p.192]
- N0-1 disease: Adjuvant chemotherapy followed by adjuvant atezolizumab, pembrolizumab, or osimertinib depending on EGFR and PD-L1 levels [p.192]

#### Lung Metastases

When lung metastasis present with systemic metastases: prognosis poor, many not surgical candidates but systemic therapy recommended [p.191]

**Potentially curable scenarios (T3-4, N0-1, no systemic metastases):** 5-year survival approximately 30% [p.191]

### Stage IIIB and IIIC NSCLC

Stage composition: Stage IIIB includes T1-2,N3 and T3-4,N2 (unresectable); Stage IIIC includes T3,N3 and T4,N3 (unresectable) [p.294-297]

#### T1-2, N3 Disease
- Surgical resection NOT recommended [p.298]
- Pathologic confirmation of nodal status required [p.298-300]
- FDG PET/CT and brain MRI with contrast required in pretreatment evaluation [p.302]
- Definitive concurrent chemoradiation (category 1) followed by durvalumab (category 1) if N3 confirmed [p.306-307]

#### T3-4, N2-3 Disease (Stages IIIB and IIIC)
- Surgical resection NOT recommended [p.322]
- Initial workup includes biopsies of N3 and N2 nodes [p.323]
  - **If negative biopsies:** Use same treatment options as stage IIIA (T4, N0-1) disease [p.324-325]
  - **If mediastinal node positive:** Definitive concurrent chemoradiation (category 1) followed by durvalumab [p.326-328]

#### Durvalumab Consolidation Immunotherapy
- Durvalumab (category 1) recommended as consolidation immunotherapy for eligible patients (PS 0-1) with unresectable stage III NSCLC without disease progression after definitive concurrent chemoradiation [p.164-165, 308-309]
- Recommended regardless of PD-L1 status for stage II NSCLC patients receiving concurrent platinum-based chemoradiation [p.171]
- Consolidation chemotherapy NOT recommended if receiving durvalumab due to pneumonitis risk [p.185-186, 313-315]
- Consolidation chemotherapy option for eligible patients if not receiving durvalumab [p.317-318, 333-334]

## Special Considerations

### Multiple Lung Cancers (Synchronous and Metachronous)

#### Diagnostic Differentiation

Most multiple lung tumors are metastases, not separate primaries [p.235]. Diagnostic approach:
- Lesions with different cell types (squamous vs. adenocarcinoma) usually different primaries [p.241]
- Lesions of same cell type may not be metastases; histologic determination essential [p.243-244]
- Rule out infection and benign diseases (e.g., inflammatory granulomas) [p.245-246]

**Martini and Melamed diagnostic criteria:** Separate primaries if (1) different histologies, OR (2) same histology but without lymph node involvement and no extrathoracic metastases [p.250-251]

**NGS testing:** May help determine clonal relationship; non-overlapping, unique mutations indicate separate primary lung cancers even if histologically similar [p.252-258]

#### Treatment Strategy

- Treatment depends on lymph node status (N0-1) and symptomatic status [p.260-262]
- Multidisciplinary evaluation required (thoracic surgeons, pulmonary medicine, radiation oncologists, medical oncologists) [p.263-264]
- Parenchymal-sparing resection preferred for eligible patients [p.266]
- VATS or SABR reasonable options depending on number/distribution of tumors [p.267-268]
- Multiple lung nodules may be observed with imaging or require biopsy/excision [p.269-272]

#### 2023 Panel Updates
- Added multidisciplinary evaluation recommendation for suspected/confirmed multiple lung cancers [p.275-276]
- Goal to assess which nodules can be observed vs. erroneously staging as stage IV NSCLC [p.278-280]
- **Observation appropriate for:** Small subsolid nodules with slow growth [p.290]
- **Treatment indicated for:** Lesions showing accelerating growth, increasing solid component, or increasing FDG uptake, even if small [p.291-292]

#### Synchronous Solitary Nodules
- Contralateral lung nodules should be treated as two primary lung tumors if both curable, even with similar histology [p.231-233]

### Limited Metastatic Disease

- Systemic therapy is recommended foundation for metastatic NSCLC [p.337]
- Palliative treatment including RT may be needed for localized symptoms, diffuse brain metastases, or bone metastases [p.340-341]
- Biomarker testing recommended for stage IVA disease [p.346-348]
