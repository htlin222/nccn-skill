## Summary of Key Evidence

The AJCC Cancer Staging Manual (8th edition) is effective for all cancer cases recorded on or after January 1, 2018 [p.138]. The lung cancer staging system was revised by the International Association for the Study of Lung Cancer (IASLC) and was adopted by the AJCC [p.138].

The definitions for TNM and the stage grouping for the eighth edition are summarized in the staging tables. The descriptors of the TNM classification scheme are also summarized [p.138].

### Stage Definitions

Early-stage disease is stages I and II with negative nodes (N0), whereas locally advanced disease is stages II and III with positive nodes (N+) [p.138]. Advanced or metastatic disease is stage IV [p.138].

Pathologic staging uses both clinical staging information (which is noninvasive and includes medical history and physical [H&P] examination, and imaging) and other invasive staging procedures (such as thoracotomy, examination of lymph nodes using mediastinoscopy) [p.138].

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Survival Data

From 2012 to 2018, the overall 5-year relative survival rate for adenocarcinoma was 29.6% in the United States; the 5-year survival rate for squamous cell carcinoma was 23% [p.138].

The corresponding 5-year relative survival rates for adenocarcinoma were:
- 70.1% for localized disease [p.138]
- 44.7% for regional disease [p.138]
- 9.6% for distant disease [p.138]
- 20% for unstaged disease [p.138]

### Stage I Disease

Five-year survival after lobectomy for pathologic stage I NSCLC ranges from 45% to 65%, depending on whether the patient has stage 1A or 1B disease and on the location of the tumor [p.138].

Another study in patients with stage I disease (n = 19,702) found that 82% had surgical resection and their 5-year overall survival was 54%; for untreated stage I NSCLC, 5-year overall survival was only 6% [p.138].

Of patients with stage I disease who refused surgery (although it was recommended), 78% died of lung cancer within 5 years [p.138].
