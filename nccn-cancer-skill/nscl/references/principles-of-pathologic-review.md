## Pathologic Evaluation

### Purpose by Specimen Type

The purpose of the pathologic evaluation of NSCLC will vary depending on whether the sample: [p.66]

1. **Biopsy or cytology specimen for initial diagnosis** in a case of suspected NSCLC:
   - Primary purpose (a): Make an accurate diagnosis using the 2021 WHO classification [p.66]
   - Primary purpose (b): Preserve the tissue for molecular studies, especially if the patient has advanced-stage disease [p.66]

2. **Resection specimen**: [p.66-67]
   - Primary purpose (a): Classify the histologic type [p.67]
   - Primary purpose (b): Determine all staging parameters, as recommended by the American Joint Committee on Cancer (AJCC), including:
     - Tumor size [p.67]
     - Extent of invasion [p.67]
     - Adequacy of surgical margins [p.67]
     - Presence or absence of lymph node metastases [p.67]
   - The number of involved lymph node stations should be documented since it has prognostic significance (AJCC 9th ed.) [p.66]
   - Direct extension of the primary tumor into an adjacent lymph node is considered as nodal involvement [p.66]
   - All lobectomy specimens should be extensively dissected to search for involved lymph nodes [p.66]

3. **Small biopsies or cytology specimens for molecular testing** (in the context of an established diagnosis after progression on targeted therapies): [p.66]
   - Primary purpose (a): Confirm the original pathologic type with minimal use of tissue for IHC only in suspected small cell carcinoma transformation or a different histology [p.66]
   - Primary purpose (b): Preserve material for molecular analysis [p.66]

### Classification and Reporting

#### NSCC and NSCC-NOS Terminology

In small biopsies of poorly differentiated carcinomas: [p.66]
- The terms "non-small cell carcinoma (NSCC)" or "non-small cell carcinoma not otherwise specified (NSCC-NOS)" should be used as little as possible and only when a more specific diagnosis is not possible by morphology and/or special staining [p.66]
- The following terms are acceptable: "NSCC favor adenocarcinoma" and "NSCC favor squamous cell carcinoma" [p.66]
- "NSCC-NOS" should be reserved only for cases in which IHC testing is uninformative or ambiguous [p.66]

#### Tissue Preservation

- Preservation of material for molecular testing is critical [p.66]
- Efforts should be undertaken to minimize block reorientation and the number of IHC stains for cases that cannot be classified on histologic examination alone [p.66]

#### Material Suitability for Molecular Analysis

- Formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded (FFPE) material is suitable for most molecular analyses, except bone biopsies that were previously treated with acid decalcifying solutions [p.66]
- Non-acid decalcification approaches may be successful for subsequent molecular testing [p.66]
- While many molecular pathology laboratories currently also accept cytopathology specimens such as cell blocks, direct smears, or touch preparations, laboratories that do not currently do so are strongly encouraged to identify approaches to testing on non-FFPE cytopathology specimens [p.66]

## NSCLC Classification

### Types of NSCLC

The types of NSCLC are: adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, adenosquamous carcinoma, large cell carcinoma, and sarcomatoid carcinoma. [p.67]

### Squamous Cell Carcinoma

A malignant epithelial tumor that either shows keratinization and/or intercellular bridges, or a morphologically undifferentiated NSCC that expresses IHC markers of squamous cell differentiation. [p.67]

### Adenocarcinoma

For small (<3 cm), resected lesions, determining extent of invasion is critical. [p.67]

#### Adenocarcinoma in Situ (AIS)

- Formerly called BAC
- A small (≤3 cm) localized nodule with lepidic growth, mostly non-mucinous, although mucinous types can occur [p.67]
- Multiple synchronous AIS tumors can also occur [p.67]

#### Minimally Invasive Adenocarcinoma (MIA)

- A small (≤3 cm) solitary adenocarcinoma with a predominantly lepidic pattern and ≤5 mm invasion in greatest dimension [p.67]
- MIA is usually non-mucinous, but rarely may be mucinous [p.67]
- MIA is, by definition, solitary and discrete [p.67]

#### Invasive Adenocarcinoma

- A malignant epithelial tumor with glandular differentiation, mucin production, or pneumocyte marker expression [p.67]
- Tumors show an acinar, papillary, micropapillary, lepidic, or solid growth pattern, with either mucin or pneumocyte marker expression [p.67]
- The invasive adenocarcinoma component should be present in at least one focus measuring >5 mm in greatest dimension [p.67]

#### Invasive Adenocarcinoma Variants

- Invasive mucinous adenocarcinoma [p.67]
- Colloid adenocarcinoma [p.67]
- Fetal adenocarcinoma [p.67]
- Enteric adenocarcinoma [p.67]

*Refer to College of American Pathologists Protocols for additional information.* [p.67]

### Adenosquamous Carcinoma

- A carcinoma showing components of both squamous cell carcinoma and adenocarcinoma, with each component constituting at least 10% of the tumor [p.67]
- Definitive diagnosis requires a resection specimen, although it may be suggested based on findings in small biopsies, cytology, or excisional biopsies [p.67]
- Presence of any adenocarcinoma component in a biopsy specimen that is otherwise squamous should trigger molecular testing [p.67]

### Large Cell Carcinoma

- Undifferentiated NSCC that lacks the cytologic, architectural, and histochemical features of small cell carcinoma, adenocarcinoma, or squamous cell carcinoma [p.67]
- Diagnosis requires a thoroughly sampled resected tumor with IHC stains that exclude:
  - Adenocarcinoma (thyroid transcription factor-1 [TTF-1], napsin A) [p.67]
  - Squamous cell carcinoma (p40, p63) [p.67]
- This diagnosis cannot be made on non-resection or cytology specimens [p.67]

### Sarcomatoid Carcinoma

A general term that includes pleomorphic carcinoma, carcinosarcoma, and pulmonary blastoma. It is best to use the specific term for these entities whenever possible rather than the general term. [p.67]

#### Pleomorphic Carcinoma

- A poorly differentiated NSCC that contains at least 10% spindle and/or giant cells or a carcinoma consisting only of spindle and giant cells [p.67]
- Spindle cell carcinoma consists of an almost pure population of epithelial spindle cells [p.67]
- Giant cell carcinoma consists almost entirely of tumor giant cells [p.67]

#### Carcinosarcoma

- A malignant tumor that consists of a mixture of NSCC and sarcoma-containing heterologous elements (e.g., rhabdomyosarcoma, chondrosarcoma, osteosarcoma) [p.67]

#### Pulmonary Blastoma

- A biphasic tumor that consists of fetal adenocarcinoma (typically low grade) and primitive mesenchymal stroma [p.67]

## Immunohistochemistry

### General Principles

- Judicious use of IHC is strongly recommended to preserve tissue for biomarker testing, most notably in small specimens [p.68]
- When adenocarcinoma or squamous cell carcinomas are poorly differentiated, the defining morphologic criteria that would allow for specific diagnosis may be inconspicuous or absent [p.68]
- In this case, IHC or mucin staining may be necessary to determine a specific diagnosis [p.68]

### Small Specimen Strategy

- A limited number of immunostains with one lung adenocarcinoma marker (TTF-1, napsin A) and one squamous carcinoma marker (p40, p63) should suffice for most diagnostic problems [p.68]
- Virtually all tumors that lack squamous cell morphology and show co-expression of p63 and TTF-1 are preferably classified as adenocarcinoma [p.68]
- A simple panel of TTF-1 and p40 may be sufficient to classify most NSCC-NOS cases [p.68]

### NUT Carcinoma Screening

- Testing for nuclear protein in testis (NUT) expression by IHC should be considered in all poorly differentiated carcinomas that lack glandular differentiation or specific etiology, particularly in patients who do not smoke or in patients presenting at a young age, for consideration of a pulmonary NUT carcinoma [p.68]

### Differential Diagnostic Applications

- IHC should be used to differentiate primary lung adenocarcinoma from squamous cell carcinoma, large cell carcinoma, metastatic carcinoma, and primary pleural mesothelioma (particularly for pleural specimens) [p.68]

### Primary Pulmonary Adenocarcinoma

In patients for whom the primary origin of the carcinoma is uncertain, an appropriate panel of IHC stains is recommended to assess for metastatic carcinoma to the lung. [p.68]

#### TTF-1 (Thyroid Transcription Factor-1)

- A homeodomain-containing nuclear transcription protein of the NKX2 gene family that is expressed in epithelial cells of the embryonal and mature lung and thyroid [p.68]
- TTF-1 immunoreactivity is seen in primary pulmonary adenocarcinoma in the majority (70%–90%) of non-mucinous adenocarcinoma subtypes [p.68]
- Metastatic adenocarcinoma to the lung is nearly always negative for TTF-1 except in metastatic thyroid malignancies, in which case thyroglobulin and PAX8 are also positive [p.68]
- Rare cases of TTF-1 positivity in tumors of other organs (gynecologic tract, pancreatobiliary) have been noted, and may be dependent on the specific TTF-1 clone utilized, stressing the importance of correlation with clinical and radiologic features [p.68]

#### Napsin A

- An aspartic proteinase expressed in normal type II pneumocytes and in proximal and distal renal tubules [p.68]
- Appears to be expressed in >80% of lung adenocarcinomas and may be a useful adjunct to TTF-1 [p.68]

#### Combined Panel Strategy

- The panel of TTF-1 (or alternatively napsin A) and p40 (or alternatively p63) may be useful in refining the diagnosis to either adenocarcinoma or squamous cell carcinoma in small biopsy specimens previously classified as NSCC-NOS [p.68]

### Neuroendocrine Differentiation

- IHC should be used to confirm neuroendocrine differentiation when there is morphologic evidence of neuroendocrine morphology (e.g., speckled chromatin pattern, nuclear molding, peripheral palisading) [p.69]
- Markers used to identify neuroendocrine tumors: [p.69]
  - NCAM (CD56) [p.69]
  - Chromogranin [p.69]
  - Synaptophysin [p.69]
  - INSM1 [p.69]
- A panel of markers is useful, but one positive marker is enough if the staining is unambiguous in >10% of the tumor cells [p.69]

### Pleural Mesothelioma versus Pulmonary Adenocarcinoma

The distinction between pulmonary adenocarcinoma and pleural mesothelioma (epithelioid type) can be made by correlation of the histology with the clinical impression, imaging studies, and a panel of immunomarkers. [p.69]

#### Markers for Pleural Mesothelioma

Immunostains sensitive and specific for pleural mesothelioma (usually negative in adenocarcinoma): [p.69]
- WT-1 [p.69]
- Calretinin [p.69]
- CK5/6 [p.69]
- D2-40 [p.69]

#### Markers for Adenocarcinoma

Immunostains sensitive and specific for adenocarcinoma (negative in pleural mesothelioma): [p.69]
- pCEA [p.69]
- Claudin-4 [p.69]
- TTF-1 [p.69]
- Napsin A [p.69]

#### Additional Markers

Other potentially useful markers that can be considered include: [p.69]
- B72.3 [p.69]
- Ber-EP4 [p.69]
- MOC-31 [p.69]
- CD15 [p.69]

However, these generally do not have the sensitivity and specificity of the above markers. [p.69]

#### Pancytokeratin

- A pancytokeratin such as AE1/AE3 is also useful, as a negative result suggests the possibility of other tumors [p.69]

#### Markers for Specific Tumor Origins

Other markers can be helpful in the differential diagnosis between pleural mesothelioma and metastatic carcinoma, and will also help determine the tumor origin. Examples include: [p.69]

- Lung adenocarcinoma: TTF-1, napsin A [p.69]
- Breast carcinoma: ERα, PR, GCDFP-15, mammaglobin, GATA-3 [p.69]
- Renal cell carcinoma: PAX8 [p.69]
- Serous carcinoma: PAX8, PAX2, ER [p.69]
- Adenocarcinomas of the gastrointestinal tract: CDX2 [p.69]
- Prostate cancer: NKX3.1 [p.69]

#### Pseudosquamous Mesothelioma

- p40 (or p63) is helpful for distinguishing epithelioid pleural mesotheliomas with pseudosquamous morphology from squamous cell carcinomas [p.69]
