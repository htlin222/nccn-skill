# Treatment of Recurrences and Distant Metastases -- Evidence Summary

<!-- Source: NCCN NSCLC v3.2026, pages 198-199 -->
<!-- Chunk: treatment-of-recurrences-and-distant-metastases-manuscript -->

## Summary of Key Evidence

Recurrences are subdivided into locoregional recurrences and distant metastases, with distinct management approaches [p.198]. While recurrent and metastatic disease have historically been regarded as incurable, select limited locoregional recurrences and oligometastatic disease may be treated with curative intent therapy [p.199].

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Locoregional Recurrences

Management of locoregional recurrences or symptomatic local disease—including endobronchial obstruction, mediastinal lymph node recurrence, superior vena cava (SVC) obstruction, and severe hemoptysis—is described in the NCCN Guidelines [p.198].

**SVC Obstruction**: An SVC stent may be used with either concurrent chemoradiation or radiotherapy to treat SVC obstruction [p.198].

**Endobronchial Obstruction**: For patients with endobronchial obstruction, relieving airway obstruction may increase survival, especially in patients who are severely compromised, and may improve their quality of life [p.198].

**Post-Recurrence Therapy**: After treatment for locoregional recurrence, observation or systemic therapy (category 2B for systemic therapy) is recommended if disseminated disease is not evident [p.198]. Systemic therapy is recommended for disseminated disease [p.198]. The type of systemic therapy depends on the histologic type, whether somatic genomic alterations are present that can be treated with targeted therapy, and performance status [p.198].

### Distant Metastases

Management of distant metastases—including localized symptoms, bone metastases, and limited, diffuse brain, or disseminated metastases—is described in the NCCN Guidelines [p.199].

**Palliative Radiation**: Palliation of symptoms throughout the disease course can be achieved with external-beam radiotherapy for distant metastases with localized symptoms, diffuse brain metastases, or bone metastases [p.199].

**Bone-Directed Therapy**: Bisphosphonate or denosumab therapy can be considered for bone metastases [p.199]. For patients at risk of fracture in weight-bearing bone, orthopedic stabilization and palliative radiotherapy are recommended [p.199].

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### Oligometastatic Disease

Select limited locoregional recurrences may be treated with curative intent therapy using re-resection (preferred), radiotherapy, or stereotactic ablative body radiotherapy (SABR) [p.199].

Patients with limited-site oligometastatic disease and good performance status may benefit from aggressive local therapies to the metastatic and primary sites, with clinical data suggesting the possibility of long-term survival [p.199].

### Reirradiation

Emerging clinical data suggest the feasibility of definitive reirradiation of local recurrences within prior radiotherapy fields using highly conformal techniques, although this should be limited to highly selected cases in specialty centers with appropriate expertise because of the potential for severe toxicity with high cumulative radiation doses to critical structures [p.199].

## Special Considerations

### Bone Metastases Therapies

**Denosumab vs. Zoledronic Acid**: In patients with NSCLC who have bone metastases, data suggest that denosumab increases median overall survival when compared with zoledronic acid (9.5 vs. 8 months) [p.199].

**FDA Approvals**: The FDA has approved the use of zoledronic acid and denosumab in patients with bone metastases from solid tumors [p.199].

**Adverse Effects**: Denosumab and bisphosphonate therapy can be associated with severe hypocalcemia; patients with hypoparathyroidism and vitamin D deficiency are at increased risk for hypocalcemia [p.199].

**Clinical Recommendations**: Denosumab or intravenous bisphosphonate therapy can be considered in patients with bone metastases to decrease bone complications (such as decrease pain, delay skeletal-related events) based on clinical trial data [p.199].
