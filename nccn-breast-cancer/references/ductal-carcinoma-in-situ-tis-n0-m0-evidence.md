## Summary of Key Evidence

- Over 50,000 cases of DCIS of the female breast are diagnosed annually in the United States, with increased incidence following widespread mammography screening [p.130]
- Whole breast radiation therapy (WBRT) after breast-conserving surgery (BCS) reduces ipsilateral breast events by approximately 50% (HR, 0.49; 95% CI, 0.41–0.58, P < .00001) but does not improve overall survival [p.131]
- At 15-year follow-up of NSABP B-17, RT reduced ipsilateral invasive recurrence by 52% (HR, 0.48; 95% CI, 0.33–0.69) compared to excision alone, without OS benefit [p.131]
- Radiation therapy boost provides statistically significant additional reduction in ipsilateral breast tumor recurrence (IBTR) across all age groups, with greater absolute benefit in younger patients (HR, 0.73; 95% CI, 0.57–0.94, P = .01) [p.131]
- Tamoxifen reduces ipsilateral in-breast recurrence by 3.4% absolute risk reduction and contralateral breast cancers by 3.2% in ER-positive DCIS patients treated with BCT [p.134]
- Anastrozole shows non-inferior efficacy to tamoxifen for ER-positive DCIS with improved breast cancer-free interval, particularly in postmenopausal patients <60 years [p.135-136]

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Workup for DCIS

Complete workup includes history and physical examination, bilateral diagnostic mammography, pathology review, tumor estrogen receptor (ER) status determination, and MRI as indicated [p.130].

**Pathology and Receptor Testing:** The NCCN Panel endorses the College of American Pathologists (CAP) Protocol for reporting. ER status testing is recommended to determine benefit of adjuvant endocrine therapy or risk reduction, per ASCO/CAP guidelines [p.130]. PR testing is considered optional [p.130]. Although HER2 status has prognostic significance in invasive cancer, studies have found unclear or weak evidence of HER2 prognostic value in DCIS, and no statistically significant benefit to trastuzumab concurrently with radiation in HER2-amplified DCIS [p.130]. Therefore, the NCCN Panel concludes HER2 status does not alter DCIS management and is not recommended [p.130].

**Genetic Counseling:** Recommended if the patient meets criteria for high-risk hereditary breast cancer as defined by NCCN Guidelines for Genetic/Familial High-Risk Assessment [p.130].

**MRI Imaging:** MRI demonstrates sensitivity up to 98% for high-grade DCIS [p.130]. In a prospective observational study of 193 pure DCIS patients, MRI detected 92% of lesions versus 56% by mammography (P < .0001); of 89 high-grade DCIS cases, MRI alone detected 48% missed by mammography [p.130]. However, MRI may overestimate disease extent [p.130]. Surgical decisions for mastectomy should not be based solely on MRI findings [p.130]. Studies on whether MRI reduces re-excision rates show conflicting results; some demonstrate reduction in re-excision rates, while others show no benefit for pure DCIS undergoing BCS [p.130]. One study reported 6.2% additional cancer detection with preoperative MRI [p.130]. The NCCN Panel recommends MRI only in select circumstances where additional information is warranted, noting that MRI has not been shown to increase likelihood of negative margins or decrease conversion to mastectomy [p.130].

### Primary Treatment Options

**BCS plus WBRT (Category 1):** Multiple prospective randomized trials demonstrate that WBRT after BCS decreases in-breast disease recurrence but not distant metastasis-free survival [p.131]. A meta-analysis of four large multicenter trials confirmed statistically and clinically significant reduction in ipsilateral breast events (HR, 0.49; 95% CI, 0.41–0.58, P < .00001) but no OS benefit [p.131]. Long-term follow-up of NSABP B-17 at 15 years showed RT resulted in 52% reduction of ipsilateral invasive recurrence (HR, 0.48; 95% CI, 0.33–0.69, P < .001) with similar OS and cumulative all-cause mortality (HR for death, 1.08; 95% CI, 0.79–1.48) [p.131].

A large observational study of 108,196 DCIS patients in the SEER database reported 50% reduction in ipsilateral recurrence at 10 years (adjusted HR, 0.47 [95% CI, 0.42–0.53]; P < .001) with WBRT addition, though breast cancer-specific mortality was similar (HR, 0.86 [95% CI, 0.67–1.10]; P = .22) [p.131]. Population-based studies suggest beneficial effects of WBRT in higher-risk DCIS (higher nuclear grade, younger age, larger tumor size) with modest but statistically significant improvement in OS [p.131].

In another SEER database study of 140,366 DCIS patients, 15-year breast cancer mortality was 1.7% for breast-conserving therapy versus 2.3% for BCS alone (HR, 0.77; 95% CI, 0.67–0.88; P < .001) [p.131]. The NCCN Panel recommends WBRT following BCS reduces IBTR rates by approximately 50% to 70% [p.133]. The Panel recommends an individualized approach to RT boost consideration based on patient preference and factors such as longevity [p.133].

**RT Boost:** A pooled analysis of 4,131 pure DCIS patients treated with BCS and WBRT compared RT boost (median dose 14 Gy, n=2,661) versus no boost (n=1,470) at median 9-year follow-up [p.131-132]. IBTR rates decreased with boost across all timepoints: 97.1% vs. 96.3% at 5 years, 94.1% vs. 92.5% at 10 years, and 91.6% vs. 88.0% at 15 years (P = .0389) [p.132]. Boost was associated with significantly decreased IBTR (HR, 0.73; 95% CI, 0.57–0.94; P = .01), remaining statistically significant in multivariate analysis accounting for grade, ER status, tamoxifen use, margin status, and age (HR, 0.69; 95% CI, 0.53–0.91; P < .010) [p.132]. Benefit persisted even in very low-risk patients with negative margins [p.132]. Similar to invasive cancers, absolute benefit magnitude was greatest in younger patients [p.132].

Two randomized phase III trials (NCT00470236 and NCT00907868) are studying RT boost efficacy in DCIS, with completed accrual and active follow-up ongoing [p.132]. Health-related quality of life analysis from BIG 3-07/TROG 07.01 trial (NCT00470236) showed cosmetic status was impacted negatively with boost versus no boost at 2 years [p.132]. Five-year data from this trial showed 93% of patients without boost were free from local recurrence versus 97% with RT boost (HR, 0.47; 95% CI, 0.31–0.72; P < .001) [p.132].

**BCS Alone Without WBRT:** Retrospective series suggest selected low-risk patients have low in-breast recurrence risk with excision alone [p.132]. One retrospective review of 186 DCIS patients treated with BCS alone reported 10-year disease-free survival rates of 94% for low-risk DCIS and 83% for intermediate/high-risk DCIS [p.132]. In another retrospective study of 215 patients without RT or systemic therapy, 8-year recurrence rates were 0% for low-risk, 21.5% for intermediate-risk, and 32.1% for high-risk DCIS, stratified using modified Van Nuys Prognostic Index based on tumor grade, size, comedo necrosis absence, margin width, and age [p.132].

A multi-institutional prospective study evaluated selected low-risk DCIS without radiation: low/intermediate-grade disease ≤2.5 cm (n=561) or high-grade disease ≤1 cm (n=104) with minimum 3-mm negative margins [p.132]. Only 30% received tamoxifen; margins substantially exceeded protocol requirement (≥5 mm in 62% of low/intermediate-risk group, >10 mm or no tumor on re-excision in 48%) [p.132]. Although 5-year IBTR was acceptably low for low/intermediate-grade group, at median 12.3-year follow-up, IBTR rates were 14.4% for low/intermediate-grade and 24.6% for high-grade DCIS (P = .003), suggesting delayed rather than prevented events [p.132].

The RTOG 9804 trial randomized 636 low-risk DCIS patients (low/intermediate-grade, <2.5 cm, ≥3 mm margins) to RT or observation [p.132-133]. At 7-year median follow-up, local recurrence was reduced with RT (0.9% vs. 6.7%; HR, 0.11; 95% CI, 0.03–0.47), with no DFS or OS difference [p.133]. At 15-year follow-up, local recurrence was reduced by 50% with RT versus without RT (7.1% vs. 15.1%; HR, 0.36; 95% CI, 0.20–0.66) [p.133].

For patients undergoing BCS and WBRT, the NCCN Panel notes that irrespective of margin width, IBTR risk without radiation is substantially higher than with excision followed by WBRT, even for predefined low-risk DCIS subsets [p.133].

**Accelerated Partial Breast Irradiation (APBI):** Four randomized trials (NSABP B-39/RTOG 0413, OCOG-RAPID, University of Florence, GEC-ESTRO) demonstrated APBI with multi-catheter brachytherapy is noninferior in local control compared to WBRT with similar toxicity and cosmetic outcomes [p.133]. DCIS patients comprised 25%, 18%, 8.8%, and 6% of patients in these respective trials [p.133]. Per ASTRO guidelines, screen-detected DCIS measuring <2.5 cm with grade I or II disease and ≥3 mm negative margins are "suitable" candidates for APBI [p.133]. The NCCN Panel recommends BCS plus APBI in carefully selected patients (category 2A), specifically those meeting RTOG 9804 low-risk DCIS or ASTRO "suitable" definitions [p.133].

**Margin Status:** Prospective randomized trials have not analyzed whether wider margins can replace RT need for DCIS [p.133]. A retrospective study of 445 pure DCIS patients showed margin width was the most important independent predictor of local recurrence, with decreasing risk apparent with margins <1 mm compared to ≥10 mm [p.133]. In a meta-analysis of 4,660 DCIS patients treated with BCS and radiation, <2 mm margins associated with increased IBTR compared to 2 mm, though no significant differences were observed with >2 mm to 5 mm or >5 mm margins [p.133].

A retrospective database study of 2,996 DCIS patients showed wider margins significantly associated with lower recurrence only in patients not receiving RT (P < .0001), not in radiation-treated patients (P = .95) [p.133]. According to the SSO/ASTRO/ASCO Consensus Guideline on Margins, ≥2 mm margins in DCIS treated with WBRT are associated with low IBTR rates [p.133]. Additional factors to consider include residual calcifications presence, which margin is close (anterior/posterior vs. medial/superior/inferior/lateral), and patient life expectancy [p.133-134]. When DCIS is admixed with invasive carcinoma, SSO/ASTRO/ASCO invasive breast cancer margin guidelines apply, supporting "no tumor on ink" as adequate margin [p.134].

For DCIS treated with excision alone (no WBRT), there is substantially higher IBTR regardless of margin width, even in low-risk patients [p.134]. Although optimal margin width is unknown, it should be ≥2 mm, with evidence suggesting improved IBTR with widths >2 mm [p.134].

For DCIS with microinvasion (DCIS-M, invasive focus ≤1 mm), optimal margin width should follow DCIS definition (≥2 mm), as majority of DCIS-M is DCIS with treatment patterns closer to DCIS than invasive carcinoma [p.134-135].

**Mastectomy:** Patients with DCIS and widespread disease (involving ≥2 quadrants) on diagnostic imaging, physical examination, or biopsy may require mastectomy [p.134]. For mastectomy or local excision in anatomically sensitive locations (e.g., breast tail), sentinel lymph node biopsy (SLNB) should strongly be considered to avoid full axillary lymph node dissection (ALND) [p.134]. Only about 25% of pure DCIS on initial biopsy has invasive cancer at definitive surgery [p.134]; therefore, ALND is not recommended unless pathologically documented invasive cancer or ALN metastatic disease exists [p.134].

**NCCN Primary Treatment Recommendations:**

1. **BCS plus WBRT with or without boost (Category 1)** [p.133]
2. **Total mastectomy, with or without SLNB with optional reconstruction (Category 2A)** [p.133]
3. **BCS plus APBI in carefully selected patients (Category 2A)** [p.133]
4. **BCS alone (Category 2B)** -- Consider only if patient/physician assess low recurrence risk; for ER-positive, fully resected disease with negative margins, absolute recurrence reduction may not justify RT risks, particularly with endocrine therapy [p.133]

Contraindications to breast conservation therapy are listed in the algorithm. Complete resection should be documented by margin analysis and specimen radiography [p.134]. Post-excision mammography may be considered for uncertainty about excision adequacy [p.134]. Clips may delineate tumor bed for radiation coverage and boost field design [p.134]. For BCS and WBRT, quantitative description of tumor close to margin is helpful; 2 mm margins associated with reduced IBTR versus narrower margins [p.134]. Routine pursuit of >2 mm margins is not supported by evidence [p.134]. For minimal/focal DCIS near margin, clinical judgment should weigh re-excision risks against recurrence [p.134].

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### Endocrine Therapy for Risk Reduction

**Tamoxifen:** DCIS falls within the spectrum of breast proliferative abnormalities between atypical ductal hyperplasia (ADH) and invasive ductal carcinoma [p.134]. The NSABP Breast Cancer Prevention Trial showed 75% reduction in invasive breast cancer occurrence in ADH patients treated with tamoxifen, with substantial reduction in invasive breast disease risk [p.134]. EBCTCG overview analysis showed 5 years of tamoxifen therapy resulted in 39% reduction in annual odds of recurrence of invasive breast cancer in ER-positive or receptor-unknown invasive tumors [p.134].

The NSABP B-24 trial examined tamoxifen benefit in DCIS after BCT. Patients randomized to placebo or tamoxifen; at median 13.6-year follow-up, tamoxifen resulted in 3.4% absolute reduction in ipsilateral in-breast tumor recurrence risk (HR, 0.30; 95% CI, 0.21–0.42; P < .001) and 3.2% absolute reduction in contralateral breast cancers (HR, 0.68; 95% CI, 0.48–0.95; P = .023) [p.134]. Ten-year cumulative rates: tamoxifen group had 4.6% invasive and 5.6% noninvasive ipsilateral recurrence versus 7.3% invasive and 7.2% noninvasive for placebo; contralateral 6.9% and 4.7% invasive/noninvasive in placebo and tamoxifen groups [p.134]. No OS differences noted [p.134]. Retrospective ER analysis suggested increased ER expression predicts tamoxifen benefit for risk reduction [p.134].

A phase III trial randomized excised DCIS patients to receive WBRT or no WBRT and tamoxifen or no tamoxifen with independent randomization for each treatment [p.134-135]. At 12.7-year median follow-up, tamoxifen decreased all new breast events (HR, 0.71; 95% CI, 0.58–0.88; P = .002) [p.135]. Tamoxifen decreased ipsilateral and contralateral breast events in subjects not receiving WBRT (ipsilateral HR, 0.77; 95% CI, 0.59–0.98; contralateral HR, 0.27; 95% CI, 0.12–0.59) but not in those receiving WBRT (ipsilateral HR, 0.93; 95% CI, 0.50–1.75; P = .80; contralateral HR, 0.99; 95% CI, 0.39–2.49; P = 1.0) [p.135].

Standard tamoxifen dose is 20 mg/day for 5 years [p.135]. The TAM-01 phase III trial studied lower-dose tamoxifen (5 mg for 3 years) in 501 patients with breast intraepithelial neoplasia including DCIS, LCIS, and ADH [p.135]. Recurrence rate of intraepithelial neoplasia or invasive breast cancer was 5.7% with 5-mg tamoxifen versus 11.9% with placebo (HR, 0.48; 95% CI, 0.25–0.89) at 5.1-year median follow-up [p.135]. Relative risk reduction was consistent with higher-dose trials but with less severe toxicity [p.135].

**Anastrozole:** In ER-positive and/or PR-positive DCIS patients treated by wide local excision with or without RT, the IBIS-II trial (randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled) compared anastrozole (n=1,471) with tamoxifen (n=1,509) [p.135]. Results demonstrated anastrozole non-inferiority to tamoxifen [p.135]. After 7.2-year median follow-up, 67 recurrences occurred with anastrozole versus 77 with tamoxifen (HR, 0.89; 95% CI, 0.64–1.23); 33 deaths with anastrozole versus 36 with tamoxifen (HR, 0.9393; 95% CI, 0.58–1.50; P = .78) [p.135-136]. Adverse event rates were similar (anastrozole 91%, tamoxifen 93%), but side-effect profiles differed; anastrozole had more fractures, musculoskeletal events, hypercholesterolemia, and strokes; tamoxifen had more muscle spasms, gynecologic cancers/symptoms, vasomotor symptoms, and deep vein thromboses [p.136].

The NSABP B-35 trial randomly assigned 3,104 postmenopausal hormone-positive DCIS patients treated with lumpectomy and radiation to tamoxifen or anastrozole for 5 years, stratified by age (<60 or >60 years) [p.136]. Primary endpoint was breast cancer-free interval [p.136]. Anastrozole resulted in statistically significant decrease in breast cancer-free interval events versus tamoxifen (HR, 0.73; 95% CI, 0.56–0.96; P = .0234) [p.136]. Significant difference appeared only after 5 years of follow-up [p.136]. Ten-year breast cancer-free interval: 89.1% for tamoxifen versus 93.1% for anastrozole [p.136]. Anastrozole showed further improvement in younger postmenopausal patients (<60 years) [p.136]. Adverse effects: tamoxifen group had higher thrombosis/embolism incidence; anastrozole group had more arthralgia and myalgia [p.136].

Results from IBIS-II and NSABP B-35 indicate anastrozole provides comparable or superior benefit as adjuvant treatment for postmenopausal hormone receptor-positive DCIS treated with BCS and RT, with different toxicity profile [p.136].

### Follow-Up and Surveillance

Surveillance after DCIS treatment facilitates early recognition of disease recurrences (DCIS or invasive disease) and evaluation/management of therapy-related complications [p.136]. Most DCIS recurrences after BCT are in-breast, occurring in close proximity to prior disease location [p.136]. Approximately one-half of local recurrences after initial pure DCIS treatment are invasive in nature; remainder recur as pure DCIS [p.136].

**NCCN Follow-Up Recommendations:**

In ER-positive DCIS patients treated with BCT, endocrine therapy with tamoxifen (premenopausal and postmenopausal) or aromatase inhibitor (postmenopausal, especially <60 years or those with embolism concerns) may reduce ipsilateral breast cancer recurrence risk [p.136]:
- **Category 1** for those undergoing BCT followed by RT
- **Category 2A** for those undergoing excision alone

For ER-negative DCIS, endocrine therapy benefit is unknown [p.136]. Low-dose tamoxifen (5 mg/day for 3 years) is an option only if 20-mg standard dose is not tolerated [p.136].

Follow-up includes interval history and physical examination every 6-12 months for 5 years, then annually, plus yearly diagnostic mammography [p.136]. For BCT patients, first follow-up mammogram should be 6-12 months after RT completion (category 2B) [p.136]. Patients receiving endocrine therapy for risk reduction should be monitored per NCCN Guidelines for Breast Cancer Risk Reduction [p.136].

## Special Considerations

### Ongoing Trials for De-escalation

Trials are ongoing to determine if a selected favorable biology DCIS subgroup may not require surgical excision [p.133]. Until definitive evidence regarding non-surgical approach safety is demonstrated, the NCCN Panel continues to recommend surgical excision for all DCIS [p.133].

### Key Management Principles

The goal of primary DCIS therapy is to prevent progression to invasive breast carcinoma [p.131]. Management strategies include surgery (mastectomy or BCS) and/or radiation therapy (RT), followed by adjuvant endocrine therapy in eligible patients to reduce recurrence risk [p.131]. Choice of local treatment does not impact overall disease-related survival; therefore, individual preferences for risk reduction must be considered [p.131]. RT adds to treatment cost and has adverse effects; therefore, several trials examined RT omission in carefully selected low-risk patients to de-escalate treatment and limit morbidity [p.132].
