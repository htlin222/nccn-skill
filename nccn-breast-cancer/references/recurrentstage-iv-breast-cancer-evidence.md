## Summary of Key Evidence

- Metastatic disease at presentation or first recurrence should be biopsied to ensure accurate determination of metastatic/recurrent disease, tumor histology, biomarker status, and appropriate treatment selection [p.182]
- ER, PR, and HER2 status should be re-tested in all cases when diagnostic tissue is obtained, as discordance rates between primary and metastatic tumors range from 3.4%-60% for ER and 0.7%-11% for HER2 [p.182]
- Germline BRCA1/2 mutations should be assessed in all patients with recurrent or metastatic breast cancer to identify candidates for PARP inhibitor therapy [p.182]
- CDK 4/6 inhibitors combined with aromatase inhibitors or fulvestrant are category 1 preferred first-line options for HR-positive, HER2-negative disease, with improved PFS compared to endocrine monotherapy [p.187-188]
- Bone-modifying agents (zoledronic acid, pamidronate, or denosumab) are recommended for patients with bone metastases (category 1) with expected survival ≥3 months [p.185]
- For HER2-positive disease, HER2-targeted therapy in combination with chemotherapy is the treatment approach, with continuation of HER2-targeted therapy until progression [p.192]

## Staging and Workup for Recurrent and Stage IV Breast Cancer

Staging evaluation includes history and physical examination, CBC, LFTs, chest diagnostic CT, bone scan, radiographs of painful or abnormal weight-bearing bones, diagnostic CT of the abdomen (with or without pelvis), or MRI scan of the abdomen [p.182]. Biopsy documentation of first recurrence is preferred when possible [p.182].

The NCCN Panel generally discourages use of sodium fluoride PET or PET/CT scans for evaluation of recurrent disease [p.182]. There is limited evidence (mostly from retrospective studies) to support PET/CT scanning to guide treatment planning through determination of disease extent in select patients [p.182]. Non-diagnostic CT scans used for PET under-evaluate the lungs and liver compared with contrast-enhanced diagnostic CT [p.182]. The Panel considers biopsy of equivocal or suspicious sites more likely to provide accurate staging information [p.182].

FDG-PET/CT is optional and most helpful when standard imaging results are equivocal or suspicious [p.182]. The NCCN Panel recommends bone scan or sodium fluoride PET/CT (category 2B) to detect bone metastases; however, if FDG-PET results clearly indicate bone metastases, these scans can be omitted [p.182].

Metastatic disease at presentation or first recurrence should be biopsied as part of workup for patients with recurrent or stage IV disease [p.182]. This ensures accurate determination of metastatic/recurrent disease, tumor histology, biomarker determination, and selection of appropriate treatment [p.182]. Soft tissue tumor biopsy is preferred over bone sites unless a portion can be protected from harsh decalcification solution to preserve biomarker assessment [p.182].

HR status (ER and PR) and HER2 status should be repeated in all cases when diagnostic tissue is obtained [p.182]. ER and PR assays may be falsely negative or falsely positive with discordance between primary and metastatic tumors [p.182]. Discordance rates are: 3.4%-60% for ER-negative to ER-positive; 7.2%-31% for ER-positive to ER-negative; and 0.7%-11% for HER2 [p.182]. Reasons for discordance may relate to change in disease biology, differential effects of prior treatment on clonal subsets, tumor heterogeneity, or imperfect accuracy and reproducibility of assays [p.182].

The NCCN Panel recommends re-testing receptor status of recurrent disease, especially when previously unknown, originally negative, or not overexpressed [p.182]. For patients with clinical courses consistent with HR-positive breast cancer or with prior positive HR results, endocrine therapy is reasonable regardless of whether receptor assay is repeated or results of most recent HR assay [p.182].

Genetic counseling is recommended if the patient is at high risk for hereditary breast cancer, as defined by NCCN Guidelines for Genetic/Familial High-Risk Assessment [p.182]. Germline BRCA1/2 mutations should be assessed in all patients with recurrent or metastatic breast cancer to identify candidates for PARP inhibitor therapy [p.182].

## Management of Locally Recurrent Disease

Patients with local recurrence only are divided into three groups: 1) those treated initially by mastectomy alone; 2) those treated initially by mastectomy plus RT; and 3) those who received breast-conserving therapy plus RT [p.183].

In one retrospective study of local recurrence patterns in patients who had undergone mastectomy and adjuvant chemotherapy without RT, the most common sites of local recurrence were the chest wall and supraclavicular lymph nodes [p.183].

Recommendations for treatment are supported by analyses of a combined database from the EORTC 10801 and Danish Breast Cancer Cooperative Group 82TM trials [p.183]. Among 133 patients (approximately 8%) experiencing local recurrence as an initial event, approximately 50% of both mastectomy and breast-conserving therapy groups were alive at 10-year follow-up with no difference in survival [p.183].

Mastectomy-treated patients should undergo surgical resection of local recurrence (if accomplished with limited morbidity) and involved-field RT to the chest wall and supraclavicular area (if chest wall was not previously treated or if additional RT may be safely administered) [p.183]. Unresectable chest wall recurrent disease should be treated with RT if no prior radiation has been given [p.183].

For patients with local breast recurrence after BCS and RT who had prior sentinel lymph node (SLN) biopsy, repeat SLNB may be considered although accuracy of repeat SLNB is unproven; prognostic significance after mastectomy is unknown and use is discouraged [p.183]. The consensus recommendation for most patients with local recurrence following breast-conserving therapy and SLNB is mastectomy and level I/II axillary dissection [p.183].

The CALOR trial results found that after complete resection in patients with isolated locoregional recurrence, adjuvant chemotherapy improves both DFS and OS [p.183]. After median follow-up of 4.9 years, overall DFS was 69% in the chemotherapy group versus 57% without chemotherapy (HR, 0.59; P = .046) [p.183]. Five-year OS was significantly improved with chemotherapy (88% vs. 76%, P = .024) [p.183]. Benefit was mostly seen in ER-negative disease: 5-year DFS was 67% versus 35% (HR, 0.32; 95% CI, 0.14-0.73); in ER-positive disease, 5-year DFS was 70% versus 69% (HR, 0.94; 95% CI, 0.47-1.89) [p.183].

After local treatment, patients with local recurrences only should be considered for limited duration systemic chemotherapy or endocrine therapy similar to adjuvant chemotherapy [p.183]. The Panel emphasized importance of individualizing treatment strategies in patients with recurrence limited to a local site [p.183].

## Surgery for Recurrent or Stage IV Disease

The primary treatment approach for patients with metastatic breast cancer and an intact primary tumor is systemic therapy, with consideration of surgery after initial systemic treatment for those requiring symptom palliation or with impending complications (skin ulceration, bleeding, fungation, pain) [p.184]. Such surgery should be undertaken only if complete local clearance may be obtained and other disease sites are not immediately life-threatening [p.184]. Alternatively, RT may be considered instead of surgery [p.184].

Retrospective studies suggest potential survival benefit from complete excision of in-breast tumor in select patients with metastatic breast cancer [p.184]. However, substantial selection biases exist in these studies and are likely to confound results [p.184].

**Prospective Randomized Trial 1 (n=350):** Patients with de novo metastatic breast cancer who achieved partial or complete response to anthracycline-based chemotherapy were randomly assigned to surgery of primary tumor plus adjuvant radiation or no locoregional treatment [p.184]. There was no difference in OS between groups (19.2 vs. 20.5 months; HR, 1.04; 95% CI, 0.81-1.34) [p.184].

**Prospective Multicenter Registry Study:** Patients whose disease responded to first-line systemic therapy were randomized to primary tumor management by surgery or not [p.184]. Preliminary data showed no difference in OS between groups [p.184].

**Turkish Federation Trial (MF07-01, n=274):** Patients with de novo metastatic breast cancer were randomized to local management (mastectomy or BCS with radiation) followed by systemic therapy versus systemic therapy only [p.184]. While no difference in survival was seen at 36 months, at 40 months, patients treated with local management showed improved survival (46.4% vs. 26.4%; HR, 0.66; 95% CI, 0.49-0.88) [p.184]. Notable differences in this trial: patients receiving surgery had lower rates of triple-negative disease (7% vs. 17%), visceral metastases (29% vs. 45%), and many had solitary bone metastases only (33% vs. 20%) [p.184].

In unplanned subgroup analysis, patients deriving greatest OS benefit from local management included: HR-positive disease (HR, 0.63; 95% CI, 0.44-0.89; P = .008); HER2-negative disease (HR, 0.64; 95% CI, 0.45-0.91; P = .01); those <55 years (HR, 0.57; 95% CI, 0.38-0.86; P = .007); and those with solitary bone metastases (HR, 0.47; 95% CI, 0.23-0.98; P = .04) [p.185].

The Panel recognizes the need for more data from randomized clinical trials addressing risks and benefits of local therapy for stage IV disease while eliminating selection biases [p.185]. Though available data do not support broadly considering local therapy with surgery and/or RT, this may be reasonable in select patients with disease responding to initial systemic therapy [p.185]. Patient engagement in decision-making is encouraged [p.185].

## Bone-Modifying Agents for Bone Metastases

Complications from bone metastases include pain, decreased performance status, decreased QOL, and skeletal-related events (SREs): need for radiation or surgery to bone, pathologic fractures, spinal cord compression, and hypercalcemia [p.185].

The NCCN Panel recommends treatment with bone-modifying agents such as zoledronic acid, pamidronate, or denosumab (category 1) in addition to chemotherapy or endocrine therapy if bone metastasis is present and expected survival is ≥3 months [p.185]. Patients should undergo dental examination with preventive dentistry prior to initiation [p.185].

Bisphosphonates and denosumab are associated with risk of osteonecrosis of the jaw (ONJ) [p.185]. Poor baseline dental health or dental procedures during treatment are known risk factors [p.185]. Additional ONJ risk factors include chemotherapy or corticosteroids administration and poor oral hygiene with periodontal disease and dental abscess [p.185].

### Bisphosphonates

Extensive data from randomized trials support use of bisphosphonates for metastatic bone disease [p.185]. Treatments include zoledronic acid and pamidronate in the United States [p.185]. Bisphosphonate treatment is associated with fewer SREs, fewer pathologic fractures, and less need for RT and surgery for bone pain [p.185]. No impact on OS has been observed with bisphosphonate treatment [p.185].

Zoledronic acid and pamidronate may be given on a 3- to 4-week schedule or every 12 weeks [p.185]. Three randomized trials have compared zoledronic acid dosed every 4 weeks versus every 12 weeks [p.185].

**ZOOM Trial:** Skeletal morbidity rate was 0.22 (95% CI, 0.14-0.29) with every 4 weeks versus 0.26 (95% CI, 0.15-0.37) with every 12 weeks [p.186].

**CALGB 70604 Trial:** SRE rate was 29.5% in the 4-week arm versus 28.6% in the 12-week arm [p.186].

**OPTIMIZE-2 Trial:** SRE rate was 22% in the 4-week arm and 23.2% in the 12-week arm [p.186].

The NCCN Panel recommends optimal dosing every 12 weeks [p.186].

Bisphosphonates should be accompanied by calcium supplementation (1200-1500 mg daily) and vitamin D3 (400-800 IU) [p.186]. Recommended agents are pamidronate 90 mg intravenously over 2 hours or zoledronic acid 4 mg intravenously over 15 minutes [p.186]. Original studies continued treatment for up to 24 months; limited long-term safety data indicates treatment can continue beyond that time [p.186]. Renal toxicity risk necessitates serum creatinine monitoring prior to each dose with dose reduction or discontinuation if renal function is reduced [p.186]. Current clinical trial results support bisphosphonate use for up to 2 years [p.186].

### Denosumab

Patients with metastatic breast cancer to bone who are candidates for bisphosphonate therapy may be considered for denosumab [p.186]. This is based on a single randomized trial comparing denosumab to zoledronic acid [p.186]. All trial patients were recommended to supplement with vitamin D and calcium [p.186].

Patients received either 120 mg denosumab subcutaneously every 4 weeks plus intravenous placebo or 4 mg zoledronic acid intravenously every 4 weeks and subcutaneous placebo [p.186]. Denosumab significantly delayed time to first SRE by 18% compared with zoledronic acid (HR, 0.82; 95% CI, 0.71-0.95; P < .001 for non-inferiority; P = .01 for superiority) and time to first and subsequent SREs (rate ratio, 0.77; 95% CI, 0.66-0.89; P = .001) [p.186]. No difference in time to progression or OS was observed [p.186]. Dosing of denosumab outside of every 3 to 6 weeks has not been studied [p.186].

## First-Line Therapy Evidence for HR-Positive, HER2-Negative Breast Cancer

From diagnosis of recurrent/stage IV metastatic disease, patients should be offered appropriate supportive care and symptom-related interventions [p.184]. NCCN recognizes importance of clinical trials and encourages participation [p.184].

Systemic treatment prolongs survival and enhances QOL but is not curative [p.185]. Therefore, treatments associated with minimal toxicity are preferred; minimally toxic endocrine therapies are preferred to cytotoxic therapy whenever reasonable [p.185].

Patients with stage IV or recurrent disease characterized by HR-positive, HER2-negative tumors with no visceral crisis are treated with endocrine therapy alone or endocrine therapy in combination with targeted agents [p.186]. Patients whose disease progresses after a year from adjuvant endocrine-based therapy and those presenting with de novo stage IV/metastatic breast cancer are eligible for first-line endocrine therapy [p.186].

Many premenopausal and postmenopausal patients with HR-positive breast cancer benefit from sequential endocrine therapies at disease progression [p.186]. Patients whose disease responds to endocrine-based therapy with shrinkage or long-term disease stabilization should receive additional endocrine therapy at progression [p.186]. For disease progression on or within 12 months of adjuvant endocrine therapy or on first-line metastatic endocrine therapy, patients are eligible for second-line endocrine therapy as monotherapy or in combination with targeted agents [p.186].

The NCCN Panel recommends premenopausal patients with HR-positive disease have adequate ovarian suppression/ablation and then be treated same as postmenopausal patients [p.186].

### AI in Combination with CDK 4/6 Inhibitor

In postmenopausal patients or premenopausal patients receiving ovarian ablation/suppression with LHRH agonist, combinations of AIs with CDK 4/6 inhibitors (palbociclib, ribociclib, or abemaciclib) have demonstrated improved PFS relative to AI alone [p.187].

**Palbociclib + Letrozole (Phase III, n=666):** Postmenopausal patients with metastatic, HR-positive, HER2-negative breast cancer who had not received prior advanced disease treatment [p.187]. Improved PFS (24.8 vs. 14.5 months; HR, 0.58; 95% CI, 0.46-0.72) and ORR (42% vs. 35%) with combination versus letrozole alone [p.187]. Grade 3 and 4 adverse effects with combination: neutropenia (66.5% vs. 1.4%), leukopenia (24.8% vs. 0%), anemia (5.4% vs. 1.8%), fatigue (1.8% vs. 0.5%) [p.187].

**Ribociclib + Letrozole (Phase III, n=668):** Postmenopausal patients with HR-positive, HER2-negative recurrent/stage IV breast cancer [p.187]. At median 26.4-month follow-up, improved PFS (25.3 vs. 16.0 months; HR, 0.56; 95% CI, 0.45-0.70) and ORR (43% vs. 29%) with combination versus letrozole alone [p.187]. Grade 3 or 4 adverse events more common with combination: neutropenia (62% vs. 1.2%), leukopenia (21.3% vs. 0.9%), abnormal LFTs (10.2% vs. 2.4%) [p.187].

**Abemaciclib + AI (MONARCH Trial, Phase III):** Combination of abemaciclib with AI (letrozole or anastrozole) as first-line treatment for advanced HR-positive, HER2-negative breast cancer [p.187]. Combination improved PFS versus AI alone (median not reached vs. 14.7 months; HR, 0.54; 95% CI, 0.41-0.72) [p.187]. ORR higher with combination (59% vs. 44%) [p.187]. Most frequent grade 3 or higher adverse events: diarrhea (9.5% vs. 1.2%), neutropenia (21.1% vs. 1.2%), leukopenia (8% vs. 0.6%), fatigue (2% vs. 0%) [p.187].

**Ribociclib + Goserelin + AI/Tamoxifen (MONALEESA-7, Phase III, n=672):** Pre- or perimenopausal patients with HR-positive, HER2-negative advanced breast cancer [p.187]. Improved PFS with ribociclib (median 24 vs. 13 months; HR, 0.55; 95% CI, 0.4-0.69) [p.187]. At 3.5 years, improved OS with ribociclib (70% vs. 46%; HR, 0.71; 95% CI, 0.54-0.95) [p.188]. Grade 3 and 4 adverse events >10%: neutropenia (61% vs. 4%), leukopenia (14% vs. 1%) [p.188].

The NCCN Panel has included AI in combination with CDK 4/6 inhibitors as category 1 first-line option for postmenopausal and premenopausal patients with ovarian ablation/suppression with HR-positive, HER2-negative recurrent/stage IV breast cancer [p.188].

### Fulvestrant Monotherapy

Fulvestrant is an ER antagonist originally approved as monthly intramuscular injection (250 mg per month); higher doses have proven more effective in subsequent trials [p.187].

**First-Line Study (n>200):** Fulvestrant was as effective as anastrozole in ORR (36.0% vs. 35.5%; OR, 1.02; 95% CI, 0.56-1.87) [p.188]. Improved time to progression with fulvestrant versus anastrozole (median 23.4 vs. 13.1 months; HR, 0.63; 95% CI, 0.39-1.00; P = .0496) [p.188]. Study used higher 500 mg loading dose every 2 weeks for 3 doses then 500 mg monthly [p.188]. Median OS longer in fulvestrant group (54.1 vs. 48.4 months; HR, 0.70; P = .041) [p.188].

**Dose Comparison Study:** Fulvestrant 500 mg every 2 weeks for 3 doses followed by 500 mg monthly versus 250 mg monthly [p.188]. PFS superior with 500 mg regimen (HR, 0.80; 95% CI, 0.68-0.94; P = .006) [p.188]. Final analyses showed increase in median OS (4.1 months) and reduced death risk (19%) with 500 mg versus 250 mg: median OS 26.4 versus 22.3 months (HR, 0.81; 95% CI, 0.69-0.96; P = .02) [p.188].

**FALCON Trial (Phase III):** First-line fulvestrant (500 mg) versus anastrozole in endocrine therapy-naive patients with metastatic ER-positive breast cancer [p.188]. Improved PFS with fulvestrant over anastrozole at 25.0-month median follow-up (16.6 vs. 13.8 months; HR, 0.797; 95% CI, 0.637-0.999) [p.188]. Similar QOL outcomes with most common adverse effects: arthralgia (17% vs. 10%) and hot flashes (11% vs. 10%) for fulvestrant and anastrozole [p.188].

### Fulvestrant + CDK 4/6 Inhibitor

**MONALEESA-3 (Phase III, n=726):** Advanced HR-positive breast cancer patients with no prior endocrine therapy or disease progression on prior therapy [p.188]. Combination of ribociclib with fulvestrant showed improved PFS versus fulvestrant alone (21 vs. 13 months; HR, 0.59; 95% CI, 0.48-0.73) [p.188]. PFS benefits consistent across patients with and without prior endocrine treatment [p.188]. Significant improvement in OS observed: at 42 months estimated OS was 57.8% (95% CI, 52.0-63.2) in ribociclib group versus 45.9% (95% CI, 36.9-54.5) in placebo group [p.188].

Multiple trial comparisons including second-line studies of fulvestrant with palbociclib or abemaciclib have shown statistically significant PFS improvement [p.188]. The NCCN Panel has included fulvestrant in combination with CDK 4/6 inhibitors as category 1 first-line option for postmenopausal and premenopausal patients with ovarian ablation/suppression with HR-positive, HER2-negative recurrent/stage IV breast cancer [p.188].

### Fulvestrant + Nonsteroidal AI

**FACT Trial:** Combination of fulvestrant with anastrozole was not superior to single-agent anastrozole (time to progression HR, 0.99; 95% CI, 0.81-1.20; P = .91) [p.188].

**SoFEA Trial (Phase III):** Effect of fulvestrant alone or combined with anastrozole or exemestane in patients with advanced breast cancer with acquired resistance to nonsteroidal AI [p.188]. Median PFS was 4.8 months with fulvestrant alone, 4.4 months with anastrozole plus fulvestrant, and 3.4 months with fulvestrant plus exemestane [p.188]. No differences in ORR, clinical benefit rate, and OS [p.188-189].

**SWOG S0226 Trial:** PFS (HR, 0.80; 95% CI, 0.68-0.94; stratified log-rank P = .007) and OS (HR, 0.81; 95% CI, 0.65-1.00; stratified P = .049) superior with combination anastrozole plus fulvestrant [p.189]. Subgroup analysis showed patients without prior adjuvant tamoxifen experienced greatest OS benefit with combination (median 52.2 vs. 40.3 months; HR, 0.73; 95% CI, 0.58-0.92) [p.189].

Divergent outcomes in these trials may relate to different patient populations - SWOG S0226 had more de novo stage IV disease versus FACT [p.189]. FACT included more heterogeneous postmenopausal/premenopausal patients with locally advanced/metastatic disease [p.189]. SoFEA enrolled only patients with acquired endocrine resistance [p.189].

The NCCN Panel has included AI and fulvestrant as first-line therapy (category 1) for postmenopausal patients [p.189].

### Monotherapy with Endocrine Agents

Evidence supports use of an AI as first-line therapy for recurrent disease in postmenopausal patients [p.189]. Prospective randomized trials comparing AIs head-to-head have demonstrated all AIs are equivalent [p.189]. Tamoxifen is commonly used for premenopausal patients [p.189]. In postmenopausal patients, AI monotherapy has superior outcome compared with tamoxifen, although differences are modest [p.189]. A phase III trial comparing tamoxifen with exemestane as first-line for postmenopausal metastatic breast cancer showed no significant differences in PFS or OS [p.189].

**NCCN First-Line Recommendations (Postmenopausal):** Category 1 preferred regimens include: CDK 4/6 inhibitor with AI; fulvestrant with or without CDK 4/6 inhibitor; fulvestrant with nonsteroidal AI [p.189]. Category 2A preferred regimens include: nonsteroidal AI (anastrozole, letrozole); steroidal AI (exemestane); SERM (tamoxifen or toremifene) [p.189]. For premenopausal patients, first-line includes ovarian suppression/ablation and endocrine therapy listed above or alternately with SERM alone [p.189].

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence for HR-Positive, HER2-Negative Breast Cancer

### Fulvestrant + CDK 4/6 Inhibitor

Fulvestrant in combination with CDK 4/6 inhibitor may be offered to patients who experienced disease progression during prior AI treatment with or without one line of prior chemotherapy (category 1) [p.189]. PFS was improved compared with fulvestrant alone in Phase III PALOMA-3 trial [p.189]. Treatment should be limited to those without prior CDK 4/6 inhibitor exposure [p.189].

**PALOMA-3:** Compared palbociclib and fulvestrant to fulvestrant in pre- or postmenopausal patients with HR-positive, HER2-negative advanced breast cancer whose disease progressed on prior endocrine therapy [p.189]. Pre- or perimenopausal patients also received goserelin [p.189]. Median PFS was 9.5 months for combination versus 4.6 months for fulvestrant (HR, 0.46; P < .000001) [p.190]. Grade 3/4 adverse events mainly neutropenia (65% of patients) [p.190].

**MONARCH 2 (Phase III):** Patients with disease progression on endocrine therapy randomly assigned to fulvestrant with or without abemaciclib [p.190]. Combination therapy had improved PFS versus fulvestrant alone (16.4 vs. 9.3 months; HR, 0.55; 95% CI, 0.45-0.68) [p.190]. ORR higher with abemaciclib and fulvestrant (48% vs. 21%) [p.190]. Improvement in OS with abemaciclib plus fulvestrant versus fulvestrant alone (46.7 vs. 37.3 months; HR, 0.757; 95% CI, 0.606-0.945) [p.190].

Based on above data showing addition of CDK 4/6 inhibitor to fulvestrant in patients with prior endocrine therapy provides significant PFS improvement, the NCCN Panel has included fulvestrant in combination with CDK 4/6 inhibitor as category 1 option for postmenopausal and premenopausal patients with ovarian ablation/suppression with HR-positive, HER2-negative recurrent/stage IV breast cancer [p.190]. The Panel notes limited data to support additional line with another CDK 4/6 inhibitor-containing regimen if disease progression occurs [p.190].

### Fulvestrant Monotherapy

Fulvestrant monotherapy appears at least as effective as anastrozole in patients whose disease progressed on previous tamoxifen [p.190].

**Phase II Study (n>200):** Anastrozole versus fulvestrant in advanced breast cancer [p.190]. Initial analysis showed fulvestrant as effective as anastrozole in ORR (36.0% vs. 35.5%; OR, 1.02; 95% CI, 0.56-1.87; P = .947) [p.190]. Improved time to progression with fulvestrant versus anastrozole (median 23.4 vs. 13.1 months; HR, 0.63; 95% CI, 0.39-1.00; P = .0496) [p.190]. Study used 500 mg loading dose every 2 weeks for 3 doses then 500 mg monthly [p.190]. Median OS longer in fulvestrant group (54.1 vs. 48.4 months; HR, 0.70; P = .041) [p.190].

**Phase II Study (Postmenopausal):** Fulvestrant in postmenopausal patients with advanced breast cancer and disease progression following AI therapy [p.190]. Partial response rate of 14.3% with additional 20.8% achieving stable disease for ≥6 months [p.190].

**Phase III Trial:** Clinical benefit rates of exemestane versus fulvestrant in postmenopausal patients with HR-positive advanced breast cancer who experienced disease progression on prior nonsteroidal AI were comparable (32.2% vs. 31.5%; P = .853) [p.190]. Fulvestrant administered as 500 mg loading dose followed by 250 mg doses on day 14, day 28, and then monthly [p.191].

### Fulvestrant + Alpelisib (PI3K Inhibitor)

**Phase III Randomized Trial (n=572):** Advanced HR-positive breast cancer patients with confirmed PIK3CA status who had received prior AI [p.191]. Patients enrolled in PIK3CA mutant (n=341) or PIK3CA non-mutant cohorts, each randomized to fulvestrant plus alpelisib versus fulvestrant plus placebo [p.191].

Patients with PIK3CA mutation receiving alpelisib showed improved PFS versus fulvestrant alone [p.191]. At 20-month median follow-up, PFS was 11.0 months (95% CI, 7.5-14.5) with alpelisib versus 5.7 months (95% CI, 3.7-7.4) with fulvestrant (HR, 0.65; 95% CI, 0.50-0.85; P < .001) [p.191]. In non-mutant cohort, HR was 0.85 (95% CI, 0.58-1.25) [p.191]. Most frequent grade 3 or 4 adverse events with alpelisib plus fulvestrant versus fulvestrant alone: hyperglycemia (36.6% vs. 0.7%), rash (9.9% vs. 0.3%), diarrhea (6.7% vs. 0.3%) (grade 3); no grade 4 diarrhea or rash reported [p.191].

### Everolimus + Endocrine Therapy

Resistance to endocrine therapy in HR-positive patients is frequent [p.191]. One mechanism of resistance is activation of mTOR signal transduction pathway [p.191].

**Phase II Randomized Study:** Tamoxifen alone versus tamoxifen combined with everolimus (mTOR inhibitor) in HR-positive, HER2-negative metastatic breast cancer previously treated with AI [p.191]. At 13-month median follow-up, clinical benefit was 42.1% (95% CI, 29.1-55.9) with tamoxifen alone and 61.1% (95% CI, 46.9-74.1) with tamoxifen plus everolimus [p.191]. Improvement in median time to progression: 4.5 months (95% CI, 3.7-8.7) with tamoxifen alone versus 8.5 months (95% CI, 6.01-13.9) with everolimus and tamoxifen [p.191].

**Phase III Trial (Temsirolimus + Letrozole):** Postmenopausal patients with advanced, HR-positive breast cancer with no prior endocrine therapy for advanced disease, randomized to letrozole with or without mTOR inhibitor temsirolimus [p.191]. PFS was not different between arms (HR, 0.89; 95% CI, 0.75-1.05; log-rank P = .18) [p.191]. Trial outcomes differ from BOLERO-2, possibly due to patient selection and extent of prior endocrine therapy [p.191].

**BOLERO-2 Trial (Phase III):** Postmenopausal patients with HR-positive advanced breast cancer with disease progression on nonsteroidal AI, randomized to exemestane with or without mTOR inhibitor everolimus [p.191]. Final results at 18-month median follow-up showed median PFS significantly longer with everolimus plus exemestane versus placebo plus exemestane (11.0 vs. 4.1 months; HR, 0.38; 95% CI, 0.31-0.48; P < .0001) [p.191]. Adverse events occurring more frequently with everolimus: stomatitis, infections, rash, pneumonitis, hyperglycemia [p.191]. Safety and efficacy analysis in older patients showed similar adverse event incidences; younger patients had more on-treatment deaths [p.191].

Based on BOLERO-2 evidence, the NCCN Panel has included everolimus plus exemestane as option for patients fulfilling entry criteria [p.191]. Tamoxifen or fulvestrant in combination with everolimus also included as options [p.191]. The Panel notes limited data to support additional line with another everolimus regimen if disease progression occurs [p.191].

### Aromatase Inhibitors Monotherapy

AIs as monotherapy are options as subsequent-line therapy [p.191]. The three AIs (anastrozole, letrozole, exemestane) have shown similar efficacy in second-line setting [p.191]. AI monotherapy may be useful in patients desiring single-agent treatment, if they have not received prior AI or may not be suitable for combination therapy [p.191]. Patients who received prior nonsteroidal AI may benefit from steroidal AI as subsequent therapy or vice versa [p.191].

### Selective ER Modulator

Analysis of two randomized studies comparing first-line anastrozole followed by second-line tamoxifen and vice versa showed tamoxifen is effective as second-line option [p.192].

### NCCN Recommendations for Second-Line Therapy

For postmenopausal patients with HR-positive, HER2-negative recurrent/stage IV breast cancer, preferred options include: fulvestrant with CDK 4/6 inhibitor (palbociclib, ribociclib, abemaciclib) (category 1); for tumors with PIK3CA mutations, fulvestrant with alpelisib; everolimus with AI, tamoxifen, or fulvestrant; monotherapy with fulvestrant; nonsteroidal or steroidal AI; or SERM [p.192].

ESR1 activating mutations are frequently detected in patients with prior AI exposure [p.192]. Tumors with these mutations are generally resistant to both AIs and tamoxifen [p.192]. Certain tumors with these mutations retain sensitivity to fulvestrant [p.192]. All may benefit by adding to fulvestrant: a CDK 4/6 inhibitor, or an mTOR inhibitor, or alpelisib if tumor has PIK3CA mutation [p.192].

### Regimens for Certain Circumstances

Megestrol acetate, estradiol, androgens (fluoxymesterone), and single-agent abemaciclib listed as options useful in certain circumstances [p.192].

**MONARCH 1 Trial (Phase II):** Evaluated abemaciclib as single agent in patients (n=132) with refractory HR-positive, HER2-negative metastatic breast cancer with disease progression on endocrine therapy after multiple prior systemic therapies (average three prior regimens) [p.192]. Ninety percent had visceral disease and 50.8% had >3 metastatic sites [p.192]. Single-agent abemaciclib induced partial response in 26 patients (19.7%) with ORR of 19.7% (95% CI, 13.3-27.5) [p.192]. Median PFS was 6 months (95% CI, 4.2-7.5) [p.192]. At final analysis, median OS at 18 months was 22.3 months (95% CI, 17.7-not reached) [p.192]. Most frequent adverse event was diarrhea (90.2%) [p.192]. Other common adverse events: fatigue (65.2%), nausea (64.4%), decreased appetite (45.5%) [p.192]. Grade 3 and 4 neutropenia occurred in 26.9% [p.192].

The NCCN Panel has included single-agent abemaciclib as option for disease progression on prior endocrine therapy and prior chemotherapy in metastatic setting [p.192].

## HER2-Positive, HR-Negative Breast Cancer

For patients with HER2-positive, HR-negative recurrent/stage IV breast cancer, the treatment approach is HER2-targeted therapy in combination with systemic chemotherapy [p.192]. An FDA-approved biosimilar is an appropriate substitute for trastuzumab [p.192]. Trastuzumab and hyaluronidase-oysk injection for subcutaneous use may be substituted for trastuzumab with different dosage and administration instructions [p.192].

Patients with disease progression on HER2-targeted therapy should be offered additional line of treatment with HER2-targeted therapy since continued suppression of HER2 pathway is beneficial [p.192]. Choice of HER2-targeted therapy depends on previously administered therapy, relapse-free interval, and patient preference and access [p.192].

The optimal sequence of available HER2-targeted therapies and optimal duration for recurrent/stage IV breast cancer is unknown [p.192]. The NCCN Panel recommends continuing HER2-targeted therapy until progression/unacceptable toxicity [p.192].

Preoperative Systemic Therapy
The NCCN Panel has outlined the rationale, appropriate patient selection,
and response assessment for preoperative systemic therapy in a new
section titled, Principles of Preoperative Chemotherapy.
Randomized clinical trials have found no significant differences in
long-term outcomes when systemic chemotherapy is given before or after
surgery.251,252 Historically, a primary advantage of administering
preoperative systemic therapy has been to improve surgical outcomes.
Preoperative systemic therapy can convert inoperable tumors to operable
and also downstage a significant number of patients with operable breast
cancer to allow for more limited breast conservation procedures. 253
Results from large clinical trials and retrospective reviews indicate that
breast conservation rates are improved with preoperative systemic
therapy.252,254 Clinicians need to carefully consider the extent of disease in
the breast, tumor biology and likelihood of adequate tumor response
before recommending preoperative systemic therapy to improve the
likelihood of successful breast conservation.
In addition, use of preoperative systemic therapy may provide important
prognostic information based on response to therapy. Achieving a
pathologic complete response (pCR) to neoadjuvant therapy is associated
with favorable disease-free and OS in early-stage breast cancer.  The
correlation between pathologic response and long-term outcomes in
patients with early-stage breast cancer is strongest for patients with
triple-negative breast cancer, less so for HER2-positive disease, and least
for hormone-positive disease.255-257
Other benefits of preoperative systemic therapy include allowing time for
appropriate genetic testing and for planning potential breast reconstruction
in patients proceeding with mastectomy. For those with significant residual
disease after standard preoperative systemic therapy, it may provide an
opportunity to identify patients who may benefit from further adjuvant
therapy after surgery. It many allow sentinel lymph node biopsy (SLNB)
alone or allow for limited radiation fields if clinically node positive disease
becomes clinically node negative after preoperative systemic therapy. In
addition, preoperative systemic therapy also serves as an excellent
research platform to test novel therapies and predictive biomarkers by
providing tumor specimens and blood samples prior to and during
systemic treatment.
Selection of Patients for Preoperative Therapy
Not all patients are appropriate candidates for preoperative systemic
therapy. According to the NCCN Panel, among those with inoperable
breast tumors, preoperative systemic therapy is indicated in patients with
locally advanced or inoperable breast cancer including those with
inflammatory breast cancer; those with bulky or matted cN2 axillary nodes;
cN3 regional lymph node nodal disease; and cT4 tumors.
In patients with operable tumors, preoperative systemic therapy is the
preferred approach for the following scenarios: for patients with TNBC and
HER2+ breast cancer that is clinical stage T2N0 and higher or is clinically
node positive; if the patient’s breast cancer subtype is associated with a
high likelihood of response; or if a patient desires BCS and the size of the
tumor is large relative to that of the breast.
