# Principles of Monitoring Metastatic Disease (BINV-S) -- Algorithm Reference

<!-- Source: NCCN Breast Cancer v2.2026, pages 105-105 -->
<!-- Chunk: principles-of-monitoring-metastatic-disease-algorithm -->

## Overview

Monitoring of patient symptoms and cancer burden during treatment of metastatic breast cancer is important to determine whether the treatment is providing benefit and that the patient does not have toxicity from an ineffective therapy. [p.105]

## Components of Monitoring

Monitoring includes periodic assessment of varied combinations of symptoms, physical examination, routine laboratory tests, imaging studies, and blood biomarkers where appropriate. [p.105]

Results of monitoring are classified as: [p.105]
- Response/continued response to treatment
- Stable disease
- Uncertainty regarding disease status
- Progression of disease

The clinician typically must assess and balance multiple different forms of information to make a determination regarding whether disease is being controlled and the toxicity of treatment is acceptable. [p.105] Sometimes, this information may be contradictory. Clinicians should take into account patient preferences through a shared decision-making process. [p.105]

## Definition of Disease Progression

Unequivocal evidence of progression of disease by one or more of the following factors is required to establish progression of disease, either because of ineffective therapy or acquired resistance of disease to an applied therapy. [p.105]

Progression of disease may be identified through evidence of:
- Growth or worsening of disease at previously known sites of disease [p.105]
- Occurrence of new sites of metastatic disease [p.105]

### Findings Concerning for Progression of Disease

- Worsening symptoms such as pain or dyspnea [p.105]
- Evidence of worsening or new disease on physical examination [p.105]
- Declining performance status [p.105]
- Unexplained weight loss [p.105]
- Increasing alkaline phosphatase, alanine aminotransferase (ALT), aspartate aminotransferase (AST), or bilirubin [p.105]
- Hypercalcemia [p.105]
- New radiographic abnormality or increase in the size of pre-existing radiographic abnormality [p.105]
- New areas of abnormality on functional imaging (eg, bone scan, PET/CT) [p.105]
- Increasing tumor markers (eg, carcinoembryonic antigen [CEA], CA 15-3, CA 27.29) [p.105]

**Note:** Rising tumor markers (eg, CEA, CA 15-3, CA 27.29) are concerning for tumor progression, but may also be seen in the setting of responding disease. An isolated increase in tumor markers should rarely be used to declare progression of disease. Changes in bone lesions are often difficult to assess on plain or cross-sectional radiology or on bone scan. For these reasons, patient symptoms and serum tumor markers may be more helpful in patients with bone-dominant metastatic disease. [p.105]
