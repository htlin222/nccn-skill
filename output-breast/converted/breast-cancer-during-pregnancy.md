# Breast Cancer During Pregnancy (PREG-1) -- Algorithm Reference

## Diagnosis

Clinical presentation of confirmed breast cancer in pregnant patient [p.111]

## Workup

**Imaging and Assessment** [p.111]

- Chest x-ray (with abdominal shielding) [p.111]
- Abdominal ultrasound to assess liver metastases [p.111]
- Consider non-contrast MRI of spine if indicated to assess for bone metastases [p.111]
- **Contraindicated**: CT scans, nuclear imaging (bone scan or PET/CT), and contrast-enhanced MRI during pregnancy [p.111]

**Psychosocial Assessment** [p.111]

- Assess for distress using NCCN Distress Thermometer and Problem List, which includes social determinants of health (refer to NCCN Guidelines for Distress Management DIS-A) [p.111]

## Staging / Risk Stratification

Determine presence or absence of distant metastases on staging [p.111]

## First-Line Therapy

Treatment approach depends on trimester of pregnancy at diagnosis [p.111]:

### First Trimester [p.111]

**Initial Discussion** [p.111]
- Discuss pregnancy termination (non-therapeutic) vs. continuing pregnancy

**If Continuing Pregnancy - Treatment Pathway** [p.111]
- Preoperative chemotherapy, mastectomy, or breast-conserving surgery (BCS) + axillary staging (see PREG-2) [p.111]
- Begin adjuvant chemotherapy in second trimester [p.111]
- ± Radiation therapy (RT) postpartum [p.111]
- ± Adjuvant endocrine therapy postpartum (see PREG-2) [p.111]

### Second Trimester / Early Third Trimester [p.111]

- Mastectomy + axillary staging (see PREG-2) [p.111]
- **OR** Mastectomy or BCS + axillary staging (see PREG-2) [p.111]
- Adjuvant chemotherapy [p.111]
- ± RT postpartum [p.111]
- ± Adjuvant endocrine therapy postpartum (see PREG-2) [p.111]

### Late Third Trimester [p.111]

- Mastectomy or BCS + axillary staging (see PREG-2) [p.111]
- Adjuvant chemotherapy [p.111]
- ± RT postpartum [p.111]
- ± Adjuvant endocrine therapy postpartum (see PREG-2) [p.111]

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy

See PREG-2 guidelines for details on systemic therapy considerations and contraindicated agents [p.112]

## Follow-Up / Surveillance

Postpartum considerations: Considerations for postpartum chemotherapy are the same as for non-pregnancy-associated breast cancer [p.112]

## Important Clinical Notes [p.111-112]

**Therapeutic Approach** [p.111]

- Survival outcomes of breast-conservation therapy are equivalent to mastectomy in both non-pregnancy and pregnancy-associated breast cancers [p.111]
- Therapeutic RT is generally avoided during pregnancy due to potential risks to the fetus [p.111]
- Mastectomy may be preferred, particularly for early (first trimester) gestational diagnosis, as early BCS may preclude timely administration of RT [p.111]
- Generally, intervals of 12–16 weeks between treatment modalities (surgery, RT, and chemotherapy) are considered acceptable [p.111]

**General Considerations for Management** [p.112]

- Considerations and selection of optimal local therapy and systemic therapy are similar to that recommended in non-pregnancy-associated breast cancer [p.112]
- The selection and timing of chemotherapy, endocrine therapy, and RT are different for pregnancy-associated breast cancer compared to non-pregnancy-associated breast cancer [p.112]
- BCS during the first trimester of pregnancy can be considered in those who will require adjuvant chemotherapy and can have adjuvant RT delayed until after delivery [p.112]
- Chemotherapy should not be administered during the first trimester of pregnancy [p.112]
- RT should not be administered during any trimester of pregnancy [p.112]
- Coordination is recommended between the oncology and obstetrics teams to plan the optimal timing of systemic therapy administration during pregnancy [p.112]

## Considerations for Systemic Therapy [p.112]

**Pre-Treatment Assessment** [p.112]

- A fetal examination with ultrasound should be performed prior to systemic therapy administration to exclude pre-existing abnormalities [p.112]
- Standard-dose chemotherapy should be used based on actual weight [p.112]

**Safe Chemotherapy Agents** [p.112]

- Anthracycline, cyclophosphamide, and taxanes appear to be safe during the second and third trimesters [p.112]
- Doxorubicin/cyclophosphamide every 3 weeks is preferred [p.112]
- Limited data suggest that dose-dense (dd) AC with granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF) use during pregnancy appears to be safe [p.112]
- Weekly paclitaxel is preferred [p.112]
- Avoid chemotherapy after 35 weeks to allow maternal bone marrow to recover before delivery [p.112]

**Contraindicated Agents** [p.112]

Anti-HER2 therapies: [p.112]
- Trastuzumab
- Pertuzumab
- Ado-trastuzumab emtansine
- Fam-trastuzumab-deruxtecan-nxki
- Neratinib
- Lapatinib
- Tucatinib

Other contraindicated agents: [p.112]
- CDK4/6 inhibitors (no safety data)
- Antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs): fam-trastuzumab-deruxtecan-nxki, datopotamab deruxtecan-dlnk, sacituzumab govitecan-hziy
- Endocrine therapy: tamoxifen, aromatase inhibitors, gonadotropin-releasing hormone agonists/antagonists
- PARP inhibitors
- Immune checkpoint inhibitors

## Supportive Care Considerations [p.112]

**Antiemetics** [p.112]
- Ondansetron and metoclopramide are considered safe

**NK1 Inhibitors** [p.112]
- Avoid using these, as data from human studies are lacking

**Steroids** [p.112]
- Methylprednisolone, prednisolone, or hydrocortisone are preferred
- Avoid dexamethasone or betamethasone if possible

**Granulocyte Colony-Stimulating Factor (G-CSF)** [p.112]
- Use with caution. There are insufficient data to support G-CSF use, but it appears to be safe during pregnancy
