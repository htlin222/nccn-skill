## Summary of Key Evidence

- Paget disease of the breast is rare, characterized by neoplastic cells in the epidermis of the nipple-areolar complex, with an associated cancer elsewhere in the breast in 80-90% of cases [p.204]
- Phyllodes tumors are rare, comprised of stromal and epithelial elements, with benign, borderline, and malignant subtypes; local recurrence is the most common site of recurrence [p.206]
- Breast cancer during pregnancy is infrequent (1.3 per 10,000 live births), typically presents with ALN-positive disease, larger primary tumor size, and approximately 30% HER2-positive [p.207]
- Inflammatory breast cancer (IBC) is a rare, aggressive form accounting for 1-6% of breast cancers, classified as T4d by definition, and is usually HR-negative but more frequently HER2-positive [p.209]
- Axillary breast cancer (occult primary with axillary metastases) can be successfully treated with axillary nodal dissection followed by radiation, with breast MRI identifying the primary lesion in 70% of cases [p.212]

## Paget Disease

### Clinical Presentation and Diagnosis
Paget disease of the breast is a rare manifestation characterized by neoplastic cells in the epidermis of the nipple-areolar complex (NAC) [p.204]. It most commonly presents with eczema of the areola, bleeding, ulceration, and itching of the nipple [p.204]. Diagnosis is often delayed due to the rare nature of the condition and confusion with other dermatologic conditions [p.204].

Associated cancer elsewhere in the breast occurs in 80-90% of cases [p.204], and these associated cancers may be either ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) or invasive cancer, not necessarily located adjacent to the NAC [p.204].

### Diagnostic Evaluation
Patients with clinical signs raising suspicion for Paget disease require complete history and physical examination and diagnostic breast imaging [p.204]. The skin of the NAC should undergo surgical biopsy, including full thickness of the epidermis and at least a portion of any clinically involved NAC [p.204]. When biopsy of the NAC is positive for Paget disease, breast MRI is recommended to define the extent of disease and identify additional disease [p.204].

### Surgical Management
There are no category 1 data that specifically address local management of Paget disease [p.204]. Management has traditionally been total mastectomy with axillary dissection [p.204]. Total mastectomy remains a reasonable option regardless of the absence or presence of associated breast cancer [p.204].

Satisfactory local control may be achieved with breast-conserving surgery (BCS) including excision with negative margins of any underlying breast cancer along with resection of the NAC followed by whole breast radiation therapy (WBRT) [p.204]. The risk of ipsilateral breast recurrence after breast-conserving NAC resection and RT with or without associated cancer is similar to that with BCS and RT with typical invasive or in situ cancer [p.204-205].

For Paget disease without associated cancer (no palpable mass or imaging abnormality), BCS should consist of removal of the entire NAC with negative margin of underlying breast tissue [p.205]. In cases with associated cancer elsewhere in the breast, surgery includes removal of the NAC with negative margin and removal of the peripheral cancer using standard breast-conserving technique [p.205]. It is not necessary to remove the NAC and peripheral cancer in continuity through a single surgical specimen or incision [p.205]. Mastectomy remains an appropriate treatment option [p.205].

### Axillary Staging
Axillary lymph node (ALN) staging is not necessary when breast-conserving therapy is used to treat Paget disease with underlying DCIS without evidence of invasive cancer following clinical examination, imaging evaluation, and full-thickness skin biopsy of the involved NAC [p.205]. In the presence of underlying invasive breast cancer treated with BCS, axillary surgery should be performed according to standard surgical axillary staging guidelines [p.205].

In cases treated by total mastectomy, axillary staging is recommended for patients with invasive disease and should be considered for patients with underlying DCIS without evidence of invasive disease, as final pathology may reveal invasive cancer in the mastectomy specimen [p.205]. Retrospective studies provide evidence for high accuracy in identification of sentinel node(s) in patients with Paget disease [p.205].

### Radiation Therapy
Patients treated with breast conservation should receive whole breast radiation [p.205]. Extended-field radiation to regional lymph nodes should be used in cases of associated invasive breast cancer with involved lymph nodes, as for any breast cancer [p.205]. A radiation boost should be considered for the site of the resected NAC and any associated resected cancer site, if applicable [p.205].

### Systemic Therapy
Patients with associated invasive cancer have substantial risk of developing metastases [p.205]. Adjuvant systemic therapy should be administered according to the stage of the cancer [p.205]. Patients with Paget disease treated with breast conservation and without associated cancer or those with associated ER-positive DCIS should consider tamoxifen for risk reduction [p.205]. Those with associated invasive cancer should receive adjuvant systemic therapy based on stage and hormone receptor (HR) status [p.205].

## Phyllodes Tumors of the Breast

### Clinical Characteristics
Phyllodes tumors of the breast (also known as cystosarcoma phyllodes) are rare tumors comprised of both stromal and epithelial elements [p.206]. They exist in benign, borderline, and malignant subtypes, although there is not uniform agreement on criteria for assigning subtype or predicting biological behavior [p.206]. The subtype of phyllodes tumor appears less important for risk of recurrence than the margin of tumor-free resection achieved by surgical treatment [p.206].

Diagnosis of phyllodes tumors prior to excisional biopsy/lumpectomy is uncommon [p.206]. Phyllodes tumors occur in an older age distribution than fibroadenoma with a mean age of 40 years [p.206]. Phyllodes tumors often enlarge rapidly and are usually painless [p.206]. On ultrasound and mammography, phyllodes tumors often appear as fibroadenomas, and fine needle aspiration cytology and even core needle biopsy are inadequate to reliably distinguish phyllodes tumors from fibroadenomas [p.206]. In the setting of a large or rapidly enlarging clinical fibroadenoma, excisional biopsy should be considered to pathologically exclude a phyllodes tumor [p.206].

Patients with Li-Fraumeni syndrome (germline TP53 mutation) have an increased risk for phyllodes tumors [p.206]. Local recurrences of phyllodes tumors are the most common site of recurrence [p.206]. Most distant recurrences occur in the lung, appearing as solid nodules or thin-walled cavities [p.206].

### Surgical Management
Treatment of phyllodes tumors (benign, borderline, and malignant subtypes) is with local surgical excision with tumor-free margins of ≥1 cm [p.206]. Lumpectomy or partial mastectomy is the preferred surgical therapy [p.206]. Total mastectomy is necessary only if negative margins cannot be obtained by lumpectomy or partial mastectomy [p.206-207]. Surgical axillary staging or axillary lymph node dissection (ALND) is not necessary unless the lymph nodes are pathologic on clinical examination, as phyllodes tumors rarely metastasize to the ALNs [p.207].

In patients who experience a local recurrence, resection of the recurrence with wide, tumor-free surgical margins should be performed [p.207]. Some Panel members recommend local RT of the remaining breast or chest wall following resection of a local recurrence, but this recommendation is controversial (category 2B) [p.207].

### Systemic Therapy
While the epithelial component of most phyllodes tumors contains estrogen receptor (ER; 58%) and/or progesterone receptor (PR; 75%), endocrine therapy has no proven role in the treatment of phyllodes tumors [p.207]. There is no evidence that adjuvant cytotoxic chemotherapy provides benefit in reduction of recurrences or death [p.207].

In the rare patient who experiences a systemic recurrence (usually in the lung), treatment should be as recommended in the NCCN Guidelines for Soft Tissue Sarcoma [p.207].

## Breast Cancer During Pregnancy

### Epidemiology and Presentation
Breast cancer occurring concurrently with pregnancy is infrequent, with a California registry study showing 1.3 breast cancers per 10,000 live births [p.207]. Breast cancer during pregnancy is most often ALN-positive and with larger primary tumor size [p.207]. Histologically, tumors are poorly differentiated, more frequently ER/PR-negative, and approximately 30% are HER2-positive [p.207-208]. The diagnosis is often delayed because neither the patient nor the physician suspects malignancy [p.208].

### Diagnostic Evaluation
Evaluation of the pregnant patient with suspected breast cancer should include physical examination with particular attention to the breast and regional lymph nodes [p.208]. Mammogram of the breast with shielding can be done safely with reported accuracy >80% [p.208]. Ultrasound of the breast and regional lymph nodes can be used to assess disease extent and guide biopsy [p.208]. Ultrasound has been reported to be abnormal in up to 100% of breast cancers occurring during pregnancy [p.208].

Fine needle aspiration of the breast and suspicious lymph nodes may be performed for cytologic evaluation; however, the preferred technique is core needle biopsy, which provides tissue for histologic confirmation of invasive disease and adequate tissue for HR and HER2 analyses [p.208].

### Staging
Staging assessment should be guided by clinical disease stage and tailored to minimize fetal exposure to radiation [p.208]. For clinically node-negative T1-T2 tumors, appropriate studies include chest x-ray (with shielding), liver function and renal function assessment, and complete blood count (CBC) with differential [p.208]. In patients with clinically node-positive or T3 breast lesions, additional studies include ultrasound of the liver and consideration of screening MRI of the thoracic and lumbar spine without contrast [p.208].

Assessment of the pregnancy should include maternal-fetal medicine consultation and review of antecedent maternal risks such as hypertension, diabetes, and complications with prior pregnancies [p.208]. Documentation of fetal growth, development, and fetal age by ultrasonographic assessment is appropriate [p.208]. Estimation of delivery date will help with systemic chemotherapy planning [p.208].

Counseling of the pregnant patient should include review of treatment options, which include mastectomy or BCS as well as systemic therapy [p.208].

### Surgical Management
The most common surgical procedure has been modified radical mastectomy [p.208]. However, BCS is possible if RT can be delayed to the postpartum period, and breast-conserving therapy during pregnancy does not appear to have a negative impact on survival [p.208]. When surgery is performed at 25 weeks of gestation or later, obstetrical and prenatal specialists must be onsite and immediately available [p.208].

Regarding sentinel lymph node biopsy (SLNB), there are limited case reports and small retrospective studies evaluating its use in pregnant patients, and the sensitivity and specificity have not been established in this setting [p.208]. There are insufficient data on which to base recommendations for its use in pregnant patients, and decisions should be individualized [p.208]. Sentinel node biopsy should not be offered to pregnant patients less than 30 weeks gestation [p.208]. Isosulfan blue or methylene blue dye for sentinel node biopsy procedures is discouraged during pregnancy [p.208].

### Systemic Therapy
The indications for systemic chemotherapy are the same in the pregnant patient as in the non-pregnant patient, although chemotherapy should not be administered at any point during the first trimester of pregnancy [p.208]. The largest experience in pregnancy has been with anthracycline and alkylating agent chemotherapy [p.208-209]. The first trimester has the greatest risk of fetal malformation [p.209]. Fetal malformation risks in the second and third trimester are approximately 1.3%, not different than that of fetuses not exposed to chemotherapy during pregnancy [p.209].

If systemic therapy is initiated, fetal monitoring prior to each chemotherapy cycle is appropriate [p.209]. Chemotherapy during pregnancy should not be given after week 35 of pregnancy or within 3 weeks of planned delivery to avoid potential hematologic complications during delivery [p.209].

Data from a single-institution prospective study indicate that FAC chemotherapy (5-fluorouracil 500 mg/m² IV days 1 and 4, doxorubicin 50 mg/m² by IV infusion over 72 hours, and cyclophosphamide 500 mg/m² IV day 1) may be given with relative safety during the second and third trimesters of pregnancy [p.209]. In one report, the median gestational age at delivery was 38 weeks, more than 50% of patients had vaginal delivery, and there were no fetal deaths [p.209]. An update of experience with 57 patients treated with FAC in the adjuvant or neoadjuvant setting resulted in 57 live births, with reported healthy progression in school [p.209].

Ondansetron, lorazepam, and dexamethasone can be used as part of the pre-chemotherapy antiemetic regimen [p.209].

There are limited data on the use of taxanes during pregnancy [p.209]. If used, weekly administration of paclitaxel is recommended after the first trimester if clinically indicated by disease status [p.209].

There are only case reports of trastuzumab use during pregnancy [p.209]. The majority indicated oligohydramnios or anhydramnios with trastuzumab administration; fetal renal failure occurred in one case [p.209]. If trastuzumab is otherwise indicated, it should be administered in the postpartum period, and the Panel recommends against its use during pregnancy [p.209].

A single case report of first-trimester exposure to lapatinib during treatment for breast cancer reported an uncomplicated delivery of a healthy female neonate [p.209].

Endocrine therapy and RT are contraindicated during pregnancy [p.209]. If indicated, they should not be initiated until the postpartum period [p.209].

Communication between the oncologist and maternal-fetal medicine specialist is essential at every visit and for every treatment decision point [p.209].

## Inflammatory Breast Cancer

### Epidemiology and Diagnosis
Inflammatory breast cancer (IBC) is a rare, aggressive form of breast cancer estimated to account for 1-6% of breast cancer cases in the United States [p.209]. IBC is a clinical diagnosis that requires erythema and dermal edema (peau d'orange) of a third or more of the skin of the breast [p.209].

IBC is usually HR-negative and is more frequently HER2-positive than the usual ductal breast cancers [p.209]. Gene expression profiling studies of IBC have demonstrated that all subtypes of IBC exist, but basal and HER2 overexpressed are more frequent [p.209]. According to the 7th edition of the AJCC Cancer Staging Manual, IBC is classified as stage IIIB, stage IIIC, or stage IV depending on the degree of nodal involvement and whether distant metastases are present [p.209]. The primary tumor is classified as T4d by definition, even when no mass is specifically apparent in the breast [p.209].

On radiographic imaging, findings of skin thickening and, in some cases, an underlying mass are observed [p.209]. The characteristic clinical features of IBC are due to blockage of dermal lymphatics by tumor emboli [p.209]. Although a biopsy is required to evaluate for cancer in breast tissue and dermal lymphatics, a diagnosis of IBC is based on clinical findings [p.209]. Dermal lymphatic involvement is neither required nor sufficient by itself to assign a diagnosis of IBC [p.209].

When compared with patients with noninflammatory forms of locally advanced breast cancer, patients with IBC are more likely to have a less favorable prognosis and be younger at the time of disease presentation [p.209].

### Workup and Staging
Patients with clinical/pathologic diagnosis of IBC without distant metastasis (stage T4d, N0-N3, M0) should undergo thorough staging evaluation by a multidisciplinary team [p.209].

Recommendations for workup include complete history and physical examination involving a CBC and platelet count [p.209]. Pathology review and pre-chemotherapy determinations of tumor HR and HER2 status should be performed [p.209-210]. HER2 has a predictive role in determining which patients with IBC will benefit from HER2-targeted therapy [p.210]. The NCCN Panel endorses the CAP protocol for pathology reporting and the ASCO CAP recommendations for quality control performance of HER2 testing [p.210].

Diagnostic bilateral mammogram with the addition of ultrasound as necessary is required for all patients suspected with IBC [p.210]. A breast MRI scan is optional [p.210]. Evaluation for distant metastasis in the asymptomatic patient includes liver function tests (LFTs), bone scan or sodium fluoride PET/CT (category 2B), and diagnostic CT imaging of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis (category 2B; category 2A for diagnostic CT of the chest when pulmonary symptoms are present) [p.210].

FDG-PET/CT may be most helpful in situations where standard imaging results are equivocal or suspicious [p.210]. There is limited evidence suggesting that PET/CT may be a useful adjunct to standard imaging of IBC due to the increased risk of regional lymph node involvement and distant spread in this group of patients [p.210]. Equivocal or suspicious sites identified by FDG-PET/CT scanning or other imaging methods should be biopsied for confirmation of stage IV disease whenever possible [p.210]. FDG-PET/CT is a category 2B recommendation [p.210]. If FDG-PET and diagnostic CT are performed and both clearly indicate bone metastases, bone scan or sodium fluoride PET/CT may not be needed [p.210].

Genetic counseling is recommended if the patient is considered to be at high risk for hereditary breast cancer [p.210].

### Treatment Approach
The treatment of patients with IBC should involve a combined modality approach comprising preoperative systemic therapy followed by surgery (mastectomy) and radiotherapy [p.210].

### Preoperative Chemotherapy
There are no large randomized trials evaluating optimal systemic treatment of IBC due to its rare nature [p.210]. The systemic therapy recommendations are based on data from retrospective analyses, small prospective studies, and data from non-IBC locally advanced breast cancer [p.210].

The benefit of preoperative systemic therapy followed by mastectomy over preoperative systemic therapy alone in patients with IBC was shown in a retrospective analysis demonstrating lower local recurrence rates and longer disease-specific survival with the combined modality approach [p.210]. Results from a large retrospective study at The University of Texas MD Anderson Cancer Center demonstrated that initial treatment with doxorubicin-based chemotherapy followed by local therapy (RT or mastectomy, or both) and additional postoperative chemotherapy resulted in a 15-year disease-free survival (DFS) rate of 28% [p.210].

A retrospective study demonstrated that the addition of a taxane to an anthracycline-based regimen improved progression-free survival (PFS) and overall survival (OS) in patients with ER-negative IBC [p.210]. A systematic review found evidence for an association between the intensity of preoperative therapy and the likelihood of a pathologic complete response (pCR) [p.210]. A study of IBC patients with cytologically confirmed ALN metastases treated with anthracycline-based chemotherapy with or without taxane indicated that more patients receiving the anthracycline-taxane combination achieved a pCR compared with those receiving only anthracycline-based therapy [p.210]. Patients who had a pCR in the ALNs had superior OS and DFS compared with those with residual axillary disease [p.210].

The NCCN Panel recommends preoperative systemic therapy with an anthracycline-based regimen with or without taxanes for the initial treatment of patients with IBC [p.210]. The Panel recommends completing the planned chemotherapy prior to mastectomy [p.210]. If chemotherapy was not completed preoperatively, it should be completed postoperatively [p.210].

### Targeted Therapy
All patients with HR-positive IBC are recommended to receive endocrine therapy sequentially after completing the planned preoperative systemic therapy [p.210].

HER2-positive IBC is associated with a poor prognosis [p.210]. For patients with HER2-positive disease, the addition of trastuzumab to primary systemic chemotherapy is associated with better response rates [p.210-211]. A prospective study that randomized patients with locally advanced breast cancers, including those with IBC, to neoadjuvant anthracycline-based chemotherapy with or without trastuzumab for 1 year demonstrated that the addition of trastuzumab significantly improved the response rate and event-free survival [p.211]. The NCCN Panel recommends inclusion of trastuzumab in the chemotherapy regimen for patients with HER2-positive disease [p.211]. There are no available data to indicate the optimal duration of trastuzumab specifically among patients with IBC; however, based on available data, the Panel recommends continuing trastuzumab therapy for up to 1 year [p.211].

Results of small phase II trials indicate that other HER2-targeting agents such as lapatinib and pertuzumab have clinical benefit in IBC [p.211]. The results of the NEOSPHERE trial that included patients with IBC showed increased pCR with pertuzumab-containing regimens [p.211]. The NCCN Panel has included in a footnote that a pertuzumab-containing regimen may be administered preoperatively in patients with HER2-positive IBC [p.211].

Determination of response to neoadjuvant chemotherapy in IBC should include a combination of physical examination and radiologic assessment [p.211].

### Surgery
Patients with clinical/pathologic diagnosis of IBC should always be treated with chemotherapy before surgery [p.211]. Surgical treatment as primary treatment of patients with IBC is associated with poor outcomes [p.211]. Sentinel lymph node dissection is not a reliable method of assessing ALNs among patients with IBC [p.211].

Breast-conserving surgery in patients with IBC has been associated with poor cosmesis, and limited data suggest that rates of local recurrence may be higher when compared with mastectomy [p.211]. Breast-conserving therapy is not recommended for patients with IBC [p.211].

Mastectomy with level I/II ALND is the recommended surgical procedure for patients whose tumor responds to neoadjuvant chemotherapy [p.211]. The NCCN Panel has listed delayed breast reconstruction as an option that can be recommended to patients with IBC who have undergone a modified radical mastectomy [p.211]. Reconstruction of the breasts soon after mastectomy may compromise post-mastectomy RT outcomes [p.211].

For patients with IBC who do not respond to preoperative systemic therapy, mastectomy is not generally recommended [p.211]. Additional systemic chemotherapy and/or preoperative radiation should be considered for these patients [p.211]. Patients with tumors responding to this secondary therapy should undergo mastectomy and subsequent treatment as described above [p.211].

### Radiation Therapy
After mastectomy, RT is recommended after the completion of the planned chemotherapy [p.211]. The probability of locoregional lymph node involvement is high for patients with IBC [p.211]. To reduce the risk of local recurrence, the Panel recommends RT to the chest wall and the supraclavicular region [p.211].

If the internal mammary lymph node(s) is clinically or pathologically involved, RT should include the internal mammary nodes [p.211]. If the internal mammary nodes are not clinically or pathologically involved, then including them in the RT field is at the discretion of the treating radiation oncologist (category 3) [p.211]. For HER2-positive disease, trastuzumab may be administered concomitantly with RT [p.211].

### Stage IV or Recurrent IBC
Patients with stage IV or recurrent IBC should be treated according to the guidelines for recurrence/stage IV breast cancer [p.211].

## Axillary Breast Cancer (Occult Primary with Axillary Metastases)

### Clinical Presentation and Diagnosis
Occult breast cancer presenting with axillary metastases is an unusual presentation that can be a diagnostic and therapeutic challenge [p.212]. Evidence to support recommendations for patients presenting with axillary breast cancer comes from a limited number of retrospective studies involving small numbers of patients [p.212]. Although treatment of patients with axillary metastases from an unknown primary tumor has typically involved mastectomy and axillary nodal dissection, some patients have been successfully treated with axillary nodal dissection followed by RT [p.212].

Patients with suspected occult primary breast cancer will typically present to the oncologist after undergoing initial biopsy: core needle biopsy (preferred), and/or fine needle aspiration [p.212]. Accurate pathologic assessment of the biopsied material is most important; therefore, the pathologist must be consulted to determine whether available biopsy material is adequate or if additional biopsy material is necessary to provide an accurate and complete diagnosis [p.212].

### Workup for Possible Primary Breast Cancer
Breast MRI can facilitate identification of occult breast cancer and help select patients most likely to benefit from mastectomy [p.212]. In a study of 40 patients with biopsy-proven breast cancer in the axilla and a negative or indeterminate mammogram, MRI identified the primary breast lesion in 70% of patients [p.212]. In addition, of the 7 patients with a negative MRI who subsequently underwent ALND and RT to the whole breast, no evidence of local recurrence was evident at a median follow-up of 19 months [p.212].

Testing for immunohistochemical markers including ER/PR and HER2 is recommended [p.212]. Elevated ER/PR levels provide strong evidence for a breast cancer diagnosis [p.212]. MRI of the breast should be considered for a patient with histopathologic evidence of breast cancer when mammography and ultrasound are not adequate to assess disease extent [p.212]. MRI may be especially helpful in patients with dense breast tissue, positive axillary nodes, suspected occult primary breast tumor, or to evaluate the chest wall [p.212].

Breast MRI has been shown to be useful in identifying the primary site in patients with occult primary breast cancer and may facilitate breast conservation in selected patients by allowing lumpectomy instead of mastectomy [p.212]. In one report, the primary site was identified using MRI in about half of the patients presenting with axillary metastases, irrespective of breast density [p.212].

For patients diagnosed with adenocarcinoma or carcinoma not otherwise specified of the axillary nodes without evidence of a primary breast lesion, additional workup should include chest and abdominal CT to evaluate for evidence of distant metastases [p.212]. Breast MRI and ultrasound are recommended, and axillary ultrasound should also be performed [p.212].
