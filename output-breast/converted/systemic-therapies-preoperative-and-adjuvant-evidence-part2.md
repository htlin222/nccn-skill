# Systemic Therapies (Preoperative and Adjuvant) -- Evidence Summary

<!-- Source: NCCN Breast Cancer v2.2026, pages 168-178 -->
<!-- Chunk: systemic-therapies-preoperative-and-adjuvant-manuscript-part2 -->

## Summary of Key Evidence

- Genomic risk assessment using multigene assays (70-gene, 21-gene RT-PCR, 50-gene PAM50, 12-gene EndoPredict, and Breast Cancer Index) helps guide chemotherapy decisions in HR-positive, HER2-negative disease [p.168-170]
- CDK 4/6 inhibitors, particularly abemaciclib for 2 years, reduce recurrence risk by 6.4% in high-risk HR-positive/HER2-negative disease [p.171]
- Adjuvant olaparib for 1 year improves disease-free survival by 7.3% in germline BRCA1/2-mutated, HER2-negative tumors [p.171]
- Bisphosphonate therapy, particularly zoledronic acid, reduces bone recurrence risk in postmenopausal patients [p.171-172]
- HER2-targeted therapy (trastuzumab with or without pertuzumab) combined with chemotherapy is standard for HER2-positive tumors, with dual HER2-targeted therapy preferred for node-positive disease [p.175-177]
- T-DM1 (trastuzumab emtansine) offers improved disease-free survival in patients with residual disease after preoperative therapy [p.176]

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Genomic Assays for HR-Positive, HER2-Negative Axillary Lymph Node-Negative Tumors

#### 70-Gene Assay (MammaPrint)

In the prospective MINDACT trial, 79% of patients had lymph node-negative disease and 21% had 1-3 positive lymph nodes [p.168]. Among patients with high clinical risk/low genomic risk, the 5-year rate of survival without distant metastasis in those who did not receive adjuvant chemotherapy was 94.7% (95% CI, 92.5 to 96.2) [p.168]. In the intention-to-treat population, among high clinical risk/low genomic risk patients, the 5-year distant metastasis-free survival was 95.9% (95% CI, 94.0 to 97.2) with chemotherapy versus 94.4% (95% CI, 92.3 to 95.9) without chemotherapy (adjusted HR 0.78; 95% CI, 0.50 to 1.21) [p.168].

Among patients with 1-3 positive lymph nodes and high clinical risk/low genomic risk, the 5-year distant metastasis-free survival was 96.3% (95% CI, 93.1 to 98.1) with adjuvant chemotherapy versus 95.6% (95% CI, 92.7 to 97.4) without chemotherapy, suggesting additional benefit of chemotherapy is likely small [p.168].

For node-negative patients with high clinical risk/low genomic risk, the 5-year distant metastasis-free survival was 95.7% (95% CI, 93.0 to 97.4) with chemotherapy versus 93.2% (95% CI, 90.1 to 95.4) without chemotherapy [p.168]. The 70-gene assay does not provide evidence for chemotherapy recommendations in patients with low clinical risk [p.168].

#### 50-Gene Assay (PAM50)

The PAM50 risk of recurrence (ROR) score stratifies HR-positive patients into risk groups [p.169]. In the Danish Breast Cancer Cooperative Group database, node-negative lymph node patients with low ROR had a distant recurrence risk of 5.0% (95% CI, 2.9% to 8.0%), whereas high ROR tumors had 17.8% (95% CI, 14.0% to 22.0%) distant recurrence risk [p.169]. Patients with T1 and T2, HR-positive, HER2-negative, node-negative tumors with low ROR score are in the same prognostic category as T1a-T1b, N0, M0 tumors [p.169].

In patients with 1-3 lymph node-positive, HR-positive, HER2-negative disease with low recurrence score, distant recurrence risk was less than 3.5% at 10 years with endocrine therapy alone, with no distant recurrence seen at 10 years in TransATAC [p.169].

#### 12-Gene Assay (EndoPredict)

This assay identifies subgroups with very low recurrence risk without adjuvant chemotherapy [p.169]. Based on ABCSG-6 and ABCSG-8 trials, HR-positive, HER2-negative, node-negative patients with low-risk score had 4% distant recurrence risk at 10 years [p.169]. Patients with T1 and T2, HR-positive, HER2-negative, node-negative tumors with low-risk 12-gene score are in the same prognostic category as T1a-T1b, N0, M0 [p.169].

In TransATAC, patients with 1-3 positive nodes in the low-risk group had 5.6% distant recurrence risk at 10 years, suggesting chemotherapy would be of limited benefit [p.169].

#### Breast Cancer Index (BCI)

The BCI combines the HOXB13-to-IL17BR expression ratio (H:I ratio) and Molecular Grade Index (MGI) [p.169]. BCI was prognostic in node-negative breast cancer for both early (years 0-5) and late (years 5-10) distant recurrence in ATAC trial analysis [p.169]. For T1 and T2, HR-positive, HER2-negative, node-negative tumors with low-risk BCI, the tumor is in the same prognostic category as T1a-T1b, N0, M0 [p.169].

Secondary analyses of MA.17, Trans-aTTom, and IDEAL trials showed high BCI (H/I) patients demonstrated significant DFS improvements when adjuvant endocrine therapy was extended [p.169-170].

#### NCCN Recommendations for Node-Negative Tumors

For small tumors up to 0.5 cm, adjuvant chemotherapy is not recommended [p.169]. For invasive ductal or lobular tumors greater than 0.5 cm with no lymph node involvement, the NCCN panel strongly recommends the 21-gene RT-PCR assay (Category 1) [p.169].

The 21-gene RT-PCR assay is validated to predict chemotherapy benefit [p.169]. From exploratory TAILORx analysis, adjuvant chemotherapy may be considered in patients 50 years or younger with 21-gene RS of 16-25 [p.169]. Patients with T1b tumors with low grade histology should be considered for endocrine monotherapy [p.169].

The 70-gene assay is listed as a category 1 option based on MINDACT results [p.169-170]. The 70-gene panel is not useful for guiding chemotherapy decisions in the discordant low clinical risk/high genomic risk group [p.170].

### Multigene Assays for HR-Positive, HER2-Negative Axillary Lymph Node-Positive Tumors

For patients with four or more involved nodes, the panel recommends systemic adjuvant chemotherapy followed by endocrine therapy (Category 1) [p.170].

For patients with less than four involved nodes or pN1mi with ≤2 mm axillary node metastasis, clinical decision-making for chemotherapy should be based on clinical risk stratification, tumor stage, pathology, and comorbidities [p.170]. If not a chemotherapy candidate, adjuvant endocrine therapy alone is recommended (Category 2A) [p.170].

In those with N1mi and N1 tumors, multigene assays are prognostic and can identify low-risk patients likely to derive little benefit from adjuvant chemotherapy [p.170]. A secondary analysis of SWOG 8814 with 21-gene assay demonstrated no chemotherapy benefit for patients with 1-3 involved nodes and low RS, with significant benefit for high-RS (≥31) [p.170].

The RxPONDER trial demonstrated that premenopausal patients with HR-positive, HER2-negative, node-positive disease with 21-gene assay RS up to 25 had additional benefit of adjuvant chemotherapy for invasive disease-free survival [p.170]. In MINDACT, among 1-3 positive node patients with high clinical risk/low 70-gene risk, survival rates were similar between those receiving chemotherapy plus endocrine therapy versus endocrine therapy alone [p.170].

### Adjuvant Targeted Therapies for HR-Positive, HER2-Negative Tumors

#### Adjuvant CDK 4/6 Inhibitors

In the MonarchE study, 2 years of abemaciclib added to endocrine therapy reduced absolute recurrence risk at 4 years by 6.4% (HR 0.664, 95% CI 0.578-0.762, P < .0001) in high-risk HR-positive/HER2-negative breast cancer [p.171]. High-risk was defined as ≥4 pathologically involved lymph nodes or 1-3 involved nodes with grade 3 or size ≥5 cm [p.171].

Two palbociclib trials as adjuvant therapy in HR-positive, HER2-negative early breast cancer did not show invasive disease-free survival benefit [p.171]. The NATALEE trial showed 3.3% improvement in invasive disease-free survival with ribociclib plus adjuvant endocrine therapy (HR 0.75, 95% CI 0.62-0.91, P = .003) for stage II and III disease [p.171].

Two years of adjuvant CDK 4/6 therapy with abemaciclib is a Category 1, preferred option with endocrine therapy in high-risk HR-positive/HER2-negative disease [p.171].

#### Adjuvant Olaparib

In the OlympiA trial for patients with germline BRCA1/2 mutations and high-risk HER2-negative tumors, 4-year OS was 89.8% with olaparib versus 86.4% with placebo (95% CI -0.1% -6.8%) [p.171]. Four-year invasive DFS was 82.7% (olaparib) versus 75.4% (placebo) (95% CI 3.0% -11.5%), and 4-year distant DFS was 86.5% versus 79.1% (95% CI 3.6% -11.3%) [p.171].

Adjuvant olaparib for 1 year may be considered in germline BRCA1/2-mutated patients with HR-positive, HER2-negative tumors with ≥4 positive lymph nodes after adjuvant chemotherapy, or residual disease after preoperative therapy with CPS+EG score ≥3 (Category 2A) [p.171]. Olaparib may be used concurrently with endocrine therapy [p.171].

### Adjuvant Bisphosphonate Therapy

In ABCSG-12, for patients over 40 years, zoledronic acid significantly reduced recurrence risk by 34% (HR 0.66; P=.014) and death risk by 49% (HR 0.51; P=.020) [p.171]. No improvement in DFS or OS was seen in post hoc analysis for patients under 40 [p.171].

In AZURE, zoledronic acid improved DFS in patients more than 5 years postmenopausal [p.171]. A meta-analysis of seven adjuvant bisphosphonate trials (AZURE, ABCSG-12, ZO-FAST, Z-FAST, EZO-FAST, NSABP-B34, GAIN) showed significant benefit in postmenopausal, low-estrogen state patients [p.171-172].

The EBTCG meta-analysis of 26 bisphosphonate studies reported convincing evidence of benefit in postmenopausal patients [p.172]. Greatest improvements were seen in bone recurrence (RR=0.83, P = .004) and bone fractures (RR=0.85, P = .02), with no effect on distant recurrence outside bone [p.172].

In postmenopausal patients, zoledronic acid significantly reduced bone recurrence (3.4% vs. 4.5%, RR=0.73, 99% CI 0.53 to 1.00), though breast cancer mortality difference was not statistically significant [p.172].

#### Denosumab

The ABCSG-18 trial in postmenopausal patients on adjuvant aromatase inhibitors showed denosumab reduced clinical fractures (HR 0.5, P < .0001) [p.172]. After 8-year follow-up, denosumab improved bone metastasis-free survival (88.9% vs. 86.4%; HR 0.81; 95% CI 0.65 to 1.00) and OS (90.9% vs. 89.9%; HR 0.80; 95% CI 0.64 to 1.01) [p.172-173].

However, the phase III D-Care trial failed to demonstrate bone metastasis-free survival difference [p.173]. Due to conflicting results, denosumab is currently not recommended in the adjuvant setting [p.173]. The panel recommends considering adjuvant bisphosphonate therapy for risk reduction of distant metastasis for 3-5 years in high-risk node-negative or node-positive tumors [p.173].

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### Adjuvant Therapy for HER2-Negative Tumors

#### Chemotherapy Regimens

Meta-analysis from EBCTCG shows anthracycline and taxane-based combination chemotherapy reduces breast cancer mortality compared with no chemotherapy, with dose-dense schedules further reducing recurrence or death risk [p.173].

Randomized trials comparing AC with or without sequential paclitaxel showed improved disease-free rates and OS improvement with paclitaxel addition [p.173]. Apparent advantage appears greater in ER-negative breast cancers [p.173].

A randomized trial comparing concurrent versus sequential chemotherapy showed dose-dense regimens achieved 26% reduction in recurrence hazard (P = .01) and 31% reduction in death hazard (P = .013) [p.173].

The ECOG E1199 trial (n=4950) randomized patients to AC followed by paclitaxel or docetaxel, weekly or every 3-weeks [p.173]. Weekly paclitaxel was superior to every-3-week paclitaxel in DFS (HR 1.27; 95% CI 1.03-1.57; P = .006) and OS (HR 1.32; 95% CI 1.02-1.72; P = .01) [p.173]. Every-3-week docetaxel was superior to every-3-week paclitaxel in DFS (HR 1.23; 95% CI 1.00-1.52; P = .02) but not OS [p.173].

TC chemotherapy compared with AC in 1016 patients at 7-year median follow-up showed improved overall DFS (81% vs. 75%; HR 0.74; 95% CI 0.56-0.98; P = .033) and OS (87% vs. 82%; HR 0.69; 95% CI 0.50-0.97; P = .032) [p.173]. TC may be preferred in patients for whom anthracyclines are contraindicated [p.173].

#### Residual Disease After Preoperative Therapy

Residual disease after preoperative systemic therapy indicates 20-30% disease relapse risk [p.173]. The CREATE-X trial evaluated adjuvant capecitabine in HER2-negative breast cancer patients with residual disease [p.173]. Results showed improved DFS (HR 0.70, 95% CI 0.53-0.92, P = .01) and OS (HR 0.59, 95% CI 0.39-0.90, P = .01) [p.173]. OS was higher in TNBC (HR 0.52) [p.173].

For germline BRCA1/2-mutated TNBC, adjuvant olaparib for 1 year may be considered if tumors ≥pT2 or ≥pN1 disease after adjuvant chemotherapy, or with residual disease after preoperative chemotherapy (Category 1) [p.173]. No data exists on sequencing with capecitabine [p.173].

#### Other Recommended Regimens

AC and EC are recommended [p.173]. TAC versus FAC showed TAC superior with 5-year DFS of 75% versus 68% (HR 0.72; 95% CI 0.59-0.88; P = .001) and OS 87% versus 81% (HR 0.70; 95% CI 0.53-0.91; P = .008) [p.174].

NSABP B-30 trial comparing TAC versus AT versus AC followed by docetaxel showed AC followed by docetaxel had DFS advantage (HR 0.83; P = .006) and OS advantage versus AT (HR 0.83; P = .034) [p.174].

#### Useful in Certain Circumstances

The E1199 trial 10-year results showed weekly paclitaxel and every-3-week docetaxel associated with significant DFS improvements versus every-3-week paclitaxel [p.174]. For triple-negative disease, 10-year DFS with weekly paclitaxel was 69% and OS was 75% [p.174].

AC for four cycles showed relapse-free survival and OS equivalent to CMF [p.174]. In TNBC (n=434), maintenance low-dose capecitabine (650 mg/m² twice daily for 1 year) improved 5-year DFS to 85.8% versus 75.8% without (HR 0.60; 95% CI 0.38-0.92; P = .02) [p.174]. Five-year OS was 85.5% versus 81.3% (HR 0.75; 95% CI 0.47-1.19; P = .22) [p.174].

### Adjuvant Therapy for HER2-Positive Tumors

The NCCN Panel suggests trastuzumab and chemotherapy for HER2-positive, node-negative T1b (0.6-1.0 cm) tumors and for smaller tumors with ≤2 mm axillary node metastases (pN1mi) [p.175].

Studies show higher recurrence risk for HER2-positive, node-negative tumors ≤1 cm compared to HER2-negative of same size [p.175]. Ten-year breast cancer-specific survival and recurrence-free survival in HER2-positive, ER-positive tumors was 85% and 75%, respectively [p.175]. HER2-positive, ER-negative tumors showed 70% and 61%, respectively [p.175].

Retrospective series report 5-year recurrence-free survival of 77.1% in HER2-positive versus 93.7% in HER2-negative T1a-bN0M0 tumors (P < .001) [p.175].

#### Preferred Regimens

Paclitaxel and trastuzumab was evaluated in 406 patients with small, node-negative, HER2-positive tumors [p.175]. Three-year DFS rate was 98.7% (95% CI 97.6-99.8) with low serious toxic effects (0.5% heart failure incidence) [p.175]. Long-term follow-up reported 10-year invasive DFS of 91.3%, breast cancer-specific survival of 98.8%, and OS of 94.3% [p.175]. This is a preferred option for low-risk T1, N0, M0, HER2-positive disease, particularly those not eligible for other regimens [p.175].

The BCIRG 006 study (n=3222) randomized HER2-positive patients to AC followed by docetaxel, AC followed by docetaxel plus trastuzumab (AC-TH), or carboplatin, docetaxel, trastuzumab (TCH) [p.175]. At 65-month follow-up, AC-TH had HR for DFS of 0.64 (P < .001) versus control (AC-T) [p.175]. TCH versus AC-T had HR for DFS of 0.75 (P = .04) [p.175]. OS advantage was reported for both trastuzumab-containing arms (AC-TH vs. AC-T HR 0.63, P = .001; TCH vs. AC-T HR 0.77, P = .04) [p.175].

Cardiac toxicity was significantly lower in TCH (9.4% with >10% LVEF decline) versus AC-TH (18.6%; P < .0001) [p.175]. CHF was more frequent with AC-TH than TCH (2% vs. 0.4%; P < .001) [p.175]. More distant recurrences occurred with TCH (144 vs. 124) but fewer cardiac events (4 vs. 21) [p.175].

The FinHer trial (n=1010) randomized to vinorelbine or docetaxel followed by FEC [p.175-176]. HER2-positive node-positive or node-negative ≥2 cm, PR-negative patients (n=232) were further randomized for trastuzumab during taxane/FEC [p.176]. At median 3-year follow-up, trastuzumab addition reduced recurrence risk (HR 0.42; 95% CI 0.21-0.83; P = .01) [p.176]. No statistically significant OS or cardiac toxicity differences were observed [p.176]. At 5-year follow-up, distant DFS and OS HRs were higher than 3-year results [p.176]. TCH is a preferred regimen, especially for those with cardiac toxicity risk [p.176].

The APHINITY trial compared adjuvant trastuzumab plus pertuzumab versus trastuzumab-placebo, both with chemotherapy, in node-positive or high-risk node-negative HER2-positive tumors [p.176]. The study demonstrated trastuzumab plus pertuzumab significantly improved 3-year iDFS (HR 0.81, 95% CI 0.66-1.00, P = .045) [p.176].

With 8-year follow-up, the node-positive subgroup maintained clear invasive DFS benefit with 8-year iDFS of 86% versus 81% (HR 0.72, 95% CI 0.60-0.87) with no OS difference [p.176]. No benefit was seen in node-negative subgroup [p.176]. These results confirm long-term benefit of adding pertuzumab to trastuzumab plus chemotherapy for node-positive disease [p.176].

Trastuzumab with chemotherapy is Category 1 for all HER2-positive tumors >1 cm [p.176]. Chemotherapy plus trastuzumab and pertuzumab is Category 1, preferred regimen for HER2-positive, node-positive disease [p.176].

#### Residual Disease Therapy

The KATHERINE trial evaluated adjuvant T-DM1 (trastuzumab emtansine) in patients with residual invasive cancer [p.176]. Invasive DFS at 3 years was 88.3% with T-DM1 versus 77.0% with trastuzumab [p.176]. T-DM1 significantly decreased invasive breast cancer recurrence or death (HR 0.50, 95% CI 0.39-0.64, P < .001) [p.176].

The ATEMPT trial compared long-term outcomes of T-DM1 (n=383) versus trastuzumab/paclitaxel (n=114) [p.176]. Five-year invasive disease-free survival with T-DM1 was 97.0% (95% CI 95.2%-98.7%), recurrence-free interval was 98.3% (95% CI 96.3%-99.0%), and OS was 97.8% (95% CI 96.3%-99.3%) [p.176].

Reported 5-year invasive disease-free survival with paclitaxel/trastuzumab was 91.3% (95% CI 86.0-96.9%), recurrence-free interval was 93.3% (95% CI 88.6-98.2%), and OS was 97.9% (95% CI 95.2-100%) [p.176]. T-DM1 may be considered an alternative for patients ineligible for paclitaxel/trastuzumab [p.176].

#### Therapeutic Duration and Cardiac Considerations

Trastuzumab administration length in adjuvant trials is 12 months [p.177]. HERA demonstrated no additional benefit extending trastuzumab to 2 years versus 1 year [p.177].

PERSEPHONE trial showed non-inferiority for 6 months versus 12 months of trastuzumab [p.177]. However, PHARE study observed more events in 6-month cohort, and non-inferiority was not established [p.177-178]. Adverse events remained similar in both arms [p.178].

The NCCN panel recommends up to one year of HER2-targeted therapy with trastuzumab [p.178]. Based on updated APHINITY data, pertuzumab may be considered with trastuzumab in node-positive disease [p.178].

Increased cardiac toxicity has been observed with trastuzumab [p.178]. Anthracycline and taxane-based regimens combined with HER2-targeted agents are associated with further increased cardiac toxicity [p.178]. The panel recommends LVEF evaluation prior to and during treatment [p.178]. FDA label recommends LVEF measurements prior to trastuzumab initiation and every 3 months during therapy [p.178].

FDA-approved biosimilars are appropriate substitutes for trastuzumab [p.178]. Trastuzumab and hyaluronidase-oysk injection for subcutaneous use may substitute for intravenous trastuzumab with different dosage and administration [p.178].

#### Other Regimens

The TRAIN-2 trial showed high pCR rates with anthracycline plus trastuzumab and pertuzumab (67%) and without anthracycline plus trastuzumab and pertuzumab (68%) [p.177]. Anthracycline-containing regimens experienced more febrile neutropenia, hypokalemia, and LVEF decline to grade 2+ [p.177].

Follow-up TRAIN-2 analysis showed similar 3-year EFS and OS with or without anthracyclines in stage II and III HER2-positive disease [p.177]. Given added toxicity, non-anthracycline containing regimens with trastuzumab and pertuzumab were added as treatment options [p.177].

Other recommended regimens include: AC followed by docetaxel + trastuzumab; AC followed by docetaxel + trastuzumab + pertuzumab; and paclitaxel/carboplatin + trastuzumab + pertuzumab [p.177].

#### Extended Therapy

One year of extended neratinib after 1 year of adjuvant trastuzumab (without pertuzumab) was evaluated in ExteNET phase III trial [p.177]. Neratinib improved invasive DFS (HR 0.73, 95% CI 0.57-0.92, P = .0083), primarily in HR-positive subgroup (HR 0.60, 95% CI 0.43-0.83, P = .063) [p.177]. Neratinib is associated with moderate to severe diarrhea [p.177].

Regimens listed as useful in certain circumstances include: docetaxel + cyclophosphamide + trastuzumab; AC followed by paclitaxel + trastuzumab (various schedules); AC followed by paclitaxel + trastuzumab + pertuzumab; paclitaxel + trastuzumab + pertuzumab; adjuvant neratinib; and adjuvant T-DM1 [p.177].

## Special Considerations

### HER2-Positive Therapy Mechanism

All adjuvant trastuzumab trials demonstrated clinically significant DFS improvements [p.177]. HERA and combined NSABP B31/NCCTG N9831 analysis showed significant OS improvement with trastuzumab [p.177]. A meta-analysis (excluding BCIRG 006) showed trastuzumab addition resulted in average 10-year absolute recurrence risk reduction of 9.0% (95% CI 7.4-10.7; P < .0001) [p.177].

Trastuzumab reduced 10-year breast cancer mortality by 6.4% (95% CI 4.9-7.8; P < .0001) and all-cause mortality by 6.5% (95% CI 5.0-8.0; P < .0001) [p.177]. Trastuzumab benefits are independent of ER status [p.177].

The NCCN Panel considers it reasonable to incorporate pertuzumab into adjuvant regimens [p.177].

### Favorable Histologies

The guidelines provide systemic treatment recommendations for favorable-histology invasive breast cancers (pure tubular, pure mucinous, pure cribriform, adenoid cystic secretory carcinoma, rare low-grade metaplastic carcinoma) based on ER/PR status, tumor size, and ALN status [p.178].

Endocrine therapy, chemotherapy, and sequencing options for favorable histologies are similar to usual histology breast cancers [p.178]. Rare metaplastic subtypes (low-grade adenosquamous, low-grade fibromatosis-like carcinoma) have favorable prognosis even without adjuvant systemic therapies [p.178].

The vast majority of pure tubular, mucinous, and cribriform cancers are ER-positive and HER2-negative [p.178]. To be associated with favorable prognosis, the histologic type should not be high grade, should be pure (>90%), and should be HER2-negative [p.178]. If atypical features are present, consider treating as ductal/NST [p.178].

If a tumor is ER-negative and/or HER2-positive, or ER- and PR-negative with grade 1, pathology evaluation accuracy should be reviewed [p.178]. ER-negative pure tubular or mucinous cancers should be treated per usual histology, ER-negative guideline [p.178]. Prospective data on adjuvant therapy for pure tubular and mucinous histologies is lacking [p.178].
