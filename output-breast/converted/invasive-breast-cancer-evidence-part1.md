# Invasive Breast Cancer -- Evidence Summary

## Summary of Key Evidence

- Routine CBC and liver function tests do not add benefit in detecting metastatic disease in asymptomatic early-stage breast cancer [p.137]
- Breast MRI is optional for staging and not universally recommended; no prospective randomized studies demonstrate improvement in re-excision rates after initial BCS [p.137]
- Mastectomy and breast conserving therapy (BCS) with whole breast radiation therapy (WBRT) are equivalent with respect to overall survival for stage I and II breast cancers [p.140]
- Sentinel lymph node biopsy (SLNB) alone is sufficient for axillary staging in patients with negative sentinel nodes, eliminating need for axillary lymph node dissection (ALND) [p.141-143]
- For patients with positive sentinel nodes undergoing BCT with WBRT and cT1-2 tumors with ≤2 positive nodes, completion ALND is not needed; locoregional recurrence risk is similar [p.143]
- Hypofractionated whole breast radiation (40-42.5 Gy in 15-16 fractions) is equivalent to standard fractionation in local control and has favorable late effects [p.146]
- Regional nodal irradiation reduces distant recurrences and improves disease-free survival when added to breast/chest wall irradiation [p.147]

## Diagnostic Workup

### Imaging for Non-Metastatic (M0) Disease [p.137]

Bilateral diagnostic mammography is recommended. Breast ultrasound is recommended only if necessary.

**Breast MRI Considerations:** Advocates note high sensitivity for extent of disease evaluation, particularly in dense breasts. However, MRI has high false-positive rates and overestimates disease extent, resulting in increased mastectomy frequency. Two prospective randomized studies examining preoperative MRI utility showed no improvement in re-excision rates after initial BCS [p.137]. One systematic review documented that MRI staging altered surgical treatment in 7.8% to 33.3% of patients, but no differences in local recurrence or survival were demonstrated [p.137]. No evidence supports that MRI increases margin-negative resection rates [p.137].

**MRI may be used for:** staging evaluation to define cancer extent; detecting multifocal or multicentric cancer in ipsilateral breast; screening contralateral breast; clinical axillary metastasis with occult primary cancer; Paget disease of nipple when primary not identified by other modalities; follow-up screening of patients with prior mammographically undetected cancers; and those with lifetime risk >20% of second primary breast cancer based on family history models [p.137].

### Pathology Assessment [p.137-138]

Specimens should be oriented for the pathologist with specific requests for biomarker determination (ER, PR, HER2 status). Ki-67 testing is recommended if HR-positive, HER2-negative, and considering adjuvant abemaciclib [p.138]. CAP protocols are endorsed for standardized reporting. Data show approximately 50% of breast cancer pathology reports are missing critical disease management elements [p.138].

### Additional Diagnostic Workup [p.139-140]

Routine systemic imaging is not indicated for early-stage breast cancer in absence of signs/symptoms of metastatic disease [p.139]. Metastases were identified in 5.1% of stage I, 5.6% of stage II, and 14% of stage III patients by bone scan; no metastases detected by liver ultrasound or chest x-ray in stage I/II [p.139].

**Laboratory and Imaging Recommendations:**
- CBC, metabolic panel, and liver function tests should be considered only if candidate for preoperative or adjuvant systemic therapy [p.140]
- Bone scan or sodium fluoride PET/CT indicated for localized bone pain or elevated alkaline phosphatase [p.140]
- Diagnostic chest CT indicated only if pulmonary symptoms present [p.140]
- Abdominal/pelvic imaging indicated if elevated alkaline phosphatase, abnormal LFTs, abdominal symptoms, or abnormal physical examination [p.140]
- Routine FDG-PET/CT not recommended for staging clinical stage I, II, or operable III disease due to high false-negative rate for small (<1 cm) or low-grade lesions [p.140]

### Genetic Counseling and Distress Assessment [p.138-139]

Genetic counseling is recommended for patients at high risk for hereditary breast cancer per NCCN Guidelines for Genetic/Familial High-Risk Assessment. Distress assessment is recommended for newly diagnosed patients, particularly younger patients who have higher rates of psychosocial distress [p.138].

### Fertility and Sexual Health [p.138-139]

Childbearing after treatment for invasive breast cancer does not increase recurrence or death rates, and offspring do not have increased birth defect rates [p.138]. However, cytotoxic chemotherapy may impair fertility. While menstrual function may resume within 2 years of completing chemotherapy, particularly in those <35 years, this does not correlate with fertility [p.138].

All premenopausal patients should be informed about potential impact of chemotherapy on fertility and offered fertility preservation options if future childbearing desired [p.139]. GnRH agonists (such as goserelin) administered prior to and concurrently with adjuvant chemotherapy protect against ovarian failure and reduce early menopause risk [p.139]. In one trial, goserelin improved pregnancy probability from 11% to 21% in HR-negative early-stage breast cancer patients [p.139].

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Surgery: Breast Conserving Surgery vs Mastectomy [p.140-141]

**Equivalence of Surgery Options:** Mastectomy is equivalent to BCT (BCS with WBRT) with respect to overall survival as primary treatment for majority of stage I and II breast cancers (category 1) [p.140]. Optimal surgery choice is based on shared decision between patient and clinician discussing benefits and risks regarding long-term survival, local recurrence risk, cosmetic outcome, and QOL [p.140].

**BCS Contraindications:**
- Pregnancy requiring radiation during pregnancy
- Diffuse suspicious or malignant-appearing microcalcifications
- Widespread disease not incorporated by local excision with satisfactory cosmesis
- Diffusely positive pathologic margins
- Homozygous (biallelic) inactivation for ATM mutation (category 2B) [p.140]

**BCS Relative Contraindications:** Previous RT to breast/chest wall; active connective tissue disease involving skin (scleroderma, lupus); persistently positive pathologic margins; known or suspected genetic predisposition to breast cancer with increased IBTR risk; known or suspected Li-Fraumeni syndrome (category 2B) [p.140-141]

**Young Age and BCS:** Young age is a significant predictor of increased likelihood of IBTR after BCT, though family history and genetic predisposition factors are more common in young populations, confounding independent contributions of age [p.140-141]. For young patients, BCT or mastectomy outcomes are similar with respect to overall survival [p.141]. Some studies showed improved survival and fewer post-surgical complications with BCS [p.141].

### Mastectomy Considerations [p.141-142]

**Contralateral Risk-Reducing Mastectomy:** SEER database analysis (1998-2003) showed contralateral risk-reducing mastectomy associated with reduced breast cancer-specific mortality only in young patients (18-49 years) with stage I/II, ER-negative disease (HR 0.68; 95% CI 0.53-0.88; P=.004); 5-year survival improvement was modest (88.5% vs 83.7%, difference=4.8%) [p.141]. Statistical simulation found absolute 20-year survival benefit from risk-reducing mastectomy was <1% [p.141]. Meta-analysis found no absolute reduction in distant metastasis risk with risk-reducing mastectomy [p.142].

**NCCN Recommendations:** Patients ≤35 years or premenopausal who are BRCA1/2 mutation carriers should consider additional risk reduction strategies following appropriate counseling with multidisciplinary consultation [p.142]. Except as outlined in Genetic/Familial High-Risk guidelines, risk reduction mastectomy of contralateral breast for unilateral cancer is discouraged [p.142].

### Margin Assessment [p.142-143]

Benefit of BCS is predicated on achieving pathologically negative margins after resection. For stage I/II invasive cancers after BCS, positive margin is defined as "ink on tumor" (any invasive cancer or DCIS cells on ink) [p.142]. Patients with positive margins require further surgery—re-excision to achieve negative margin or mastectomy [p.142].

For invasive cancers with DCIS component, "no ink on tumor" definition should be used based on SSO/ASTRO guidelines, except for DCIS-M which behaves like pure DCIS and requires 2-mm margins [p.142]. Surgical specimens should be directionally oriented with pathologist descriptions of margin status, distance, orientation, and tumor type [p.142]. Tumor bed marking with clips facilitates accurate RT boost field planning [p.142].

For APBI patients, individualized clinical judgment should be utilized case-by-case based on postoperative mammography and clinical-pathologic factors (quantitative extent near margin, EIC, young age, multiple close margins) [p.142].

### Surgical Axillary Staging [p.143-151]

**Traditional ALND Standards:** Axillary status important for systemic adjuvant treatment and RT planning. Traditional ALND of levels I and II requires ≥10 lymph nodes for pathologic evaluation [p.143].

**SLNB vs ALND - Landmark Trials:**

**Milan Trial (1998-1999):** 516 patients with tumors ≤2 cm randomized to ALND vs SLNB with ALND only if SLN involved. After 79 months, no difference in OS and DFS [p.143].

**NSABP B-32 (1999-2004):** 5,611 patients with invasive breast cancer ≤2 cm randomized to ALND vs SLNB alone with ALND only if SLN positive. After 95.6 months, OS and DFS similar; ALND group had significantly higher arm morbidity and restricted work/social activity and impaired QOL [p.143].

**ALMANAC Trial (n=1031):** After 12 months, lymphedema and sensory loss higher in ALND group. Operative time, drainage use, hospitalization, and resumption of normal life much longer with ALND vs SLNB [p.143].

**SNAC and DBCCG Trials:** Both showed less morbidity with SLNB compared with ALND [p.143].

**Finding:** For negative sentinel nodes, ALND is not needed [p.143].

**ACOSOG Z0011 Trial - Positive SLN with BCT:** Randomized patients ≥18 years with clinical T1/T2 tumors, <3 positive SLNs, undergoing BCS and WBRT to SLNB alone (n=436) or completion ALND (n=420). No difference in local recurrence, DFS, or OS between groups [p.143]. At 6.3-year median follow-up, locoregional recurrences were 4.1% in ALND group and 2.8% in SLNB group (P=.11); median OS approximately 92% in each group [p.143-144]. Long-term follow-up (9.25 years) showed no statistically significant difference in local recurrence-free survival (P=.13); 10-year cumulative ipsilateral axillary recurrences were 0.5% with ALND and 1.5% with SLNB alone (P=.28); 10-year locoregional recurrences were 6.2% with ALND and 5.3% with SLNB alone (P=.36) [p.144].

**IBCSG 23-01 Trial - Micrometastases:** Compared ALND vs no ALND in patients with sentinel micrometastases (≤2 mm), including mastectomy patients (9%). Between SLNB+ALND vs SLNB alone, no differences in 5-year DFS (84.4%; 95% CI 80.7-88.1% vs 87.8%; 95% CI 84.4-91.2%), cumulative incidence of breast cancer events (10.8%; 95% CI 7.6-14.0 vs 10.6%; 95% CI 7.5-13.8%), or OS (97.6%; 95% CI 96.0-99.2% vs 97.5%; 95% CI 95.8-99.1%) [p.144]. Regional recurrence <1% for both groups [p.144]. ALND not needed for micrometastases [p.144].

**AMAROS Trial - Axillary RT vs ALND:** 4,823 patients with T1/T2 breast cancer with positive SLNs randomized to ALND or axillary RT (1,425 with positive SLNs, including 248 mastectomy patients 17%). No difference in 5-year OS or DFS: 5-year DFS 86.9% (95% CI 84.1-89.3) with ALND vs 82.7% (79.3-85.5) with axillary RT; 5-year OS 93.3% (95% CI 91.0-95.0) with ALND vs 92.5% (90.0-94.4) with axillary RT [p.144]. At 5 years, lymphedema less frequent with axillary RT vs ALND (11% vs 23%) [p.144]. 10-year follow-up results showed no significant differences in OS (84.6% with ALND vs 81.4% with axillary RT), distant metastasis-free survival (81.7% with ALND vs 78.2% with axillary RT), or locoregional recurrence (3.59% with ALND vs 4.07% with axillary RT); axillary recurrence 1.8% with axillary RT vs 0.93% with ALND [p.145].

**OTOASAR Trial:** 2,100 patients with tumors ≤3.0 cm, clinically node negative, randomized to ALND or axillary RT if 1-2 positive SLNs. No difference in axillary recurrence between groups [p.145].

**Cytokeratin IHC Assessment:** Clinical significance of lymph nodes negative by H&E but positive by cytokeratin IHC is not clear. Current treatment decisions should be based solely on H&E staining [p.141]. ACOSOG Z0010 trial in H&E negative node patients showed cytokeratin IHC not associated with improved OS over 6.3-year median follow-up [p.141-142].

**Preoperative Chemotherapy Setting:** SENTINA study reported false-negative rate of 14.2% for SLNB performed after preoperative systemic therapy in node-positive patients [p.145]. ACOSOG-Z1071 trial reported 12.6% false-negative rate [p.145]. SN FNAC trial reported 13.3% false-negative rate [p.145]. Subgroup analyses showed dual-agent lymphatic mapping, identifying ≥3 SLNs, and marking metastatic lymph node with clip before neoadjuvant therapy reduces false-negative rates to <10% [p.145].

Targeted axillary dissection (TAD) with selective localization and removal of clipped nodes showed false-negative rates reduced to approximately 2% compared with 4% with clipped node removal alone [p.145].

**NCCN Axillary Staging Recommendations:**

If ALNs clinically negative, ≤2 suspicious nodes on imaging, or ≤2 positive nodes by needle biopsy, Panel recommends SLNB [p.151]. If SLN negative, no further surgery needed [p.151].

If SLN positive, no further surgery recommended only if ALL criteria met: cT1-2, N0 tumors; no preoperative systemic therapy; only 1-2 positive SLNs; BCT (BCS+WBRT) [p.151]. If any criteria not met, Panel recommends level I and II axillary dissection [p.151].

Based on AMAROS and OTOASAR data, no further surgery recommended if ALL criteria met: cT1-2, N0 tumors; no preoperative systemic therapy; 1-2 positive SLNs; lumpectomy or mastectomy with adjuvant RT with intentional axillary inclusion [p.151]. If any criteria not met, Panel recommends level I and II dissection [p.151].

For mastectomy with clinically negative axillae but 1-2 positive SLNs, axillary radiation may replace ALND for regional control [p.151]. No ALND recommended for positive SLNs limited to only micrometastatic (>0.2 mm but ≤2.0 mm per AJCC) [p.151].

For clinically suspicious (palpable) nodes or ≥3 suspicious nodes on imaging, or if preoperative systemic therapy planned, Panel recommends pathologic confirmation with ultrasound-guided FNA or core biopsy with clip placement [p.151].

ALND levels I and II recommended only for biopsy-proven axillary metastases (without preoperative systemic therapy) or residual disease after preoperative chemotherapy [p.151]. Highly selected patients with biopsy-proven metastases converted to clinically node negative after preoperative systemic therapy may undergo SLNB with removal of clipped node (category 2B, high false-negative rate) [p.151].

When sentinel nodes not successfully identified, Panel recommends level I and II axillary dissection for staging [p.151].

## Radiation Therapy Evidence

### Whole Breast Radiation Therapy (WBRT) [p.146-147]

WBRT reduces local recurrence risk and shows beneficial effect on survival [p.146]. Randomized trials demonstrate decreased in-breast recurrences with additional boost dose to tumor bed (by photons, brachytherapy, or electron beam) [p.146].

**Hypofractionated WBRT Trials:**

Four randomized trials compared hypofractionated schedules (39-42.9 Gy in 2.6-3.3 Gy fractions) vs standard 50 Gy in 2-Gy fractions [p.146]. 10-year START trial follow-up data consistent with Canadian trial showing similar local tumor control and breast cosmesis with 42.5 Gy in 16 fractions over 3.2 weeks vs standard 50 Gy in 25 fractions over 5 weeks [p.146]. Radiation-related effects to normal breast tissue (shrinkage, telangiectasia, edema) less common with hypofractionated regimen [p.146].

Randomized trial (n=1854) comparing hypofractionated schedule (40 Gy in 15 fractions) with standard fractionation (50 Gy in 25 fractions) in node-negative breast cancer (n=1608) or DCIS (n=246) showed similar outcomes [p.146]. 9-year locoregional recurrence risk was 3.3% in 50-Gy group and 3.0% in 40-Gy group; 9-year OS was 93.4% in both groups; radiation-associated cardiac and lung disease comparable [p.146].

**FAST Trial:** Patients >50 years with low-risk disease (pT1-2, pN0) randomized to standard 50 Gy in 25 fractions or 30 Gy or 28.5 Gy in 5 weekly fractions. After 10-year follow-up, no significant normal tissue differences for standard vs once-weekly 28.5 Gy, but normal tissue effects higher with weekly 30 Gy [p.147].

**FAST Forward Trial:** 4,096 patients after BCS or mastectomy randomized to 40 Gy in 15 fractions over 3 weeks; 27 Gy in 5 fractions over 1 week (5.4 Gy/fraction); or 26 Gy in 5 fractions over 1 week (5.2 Gy/fraction) to whole breast or chest wall [p.147]. 5-year ipsilateral breast tumor relapse incidence was 2.1% with standard 40 Gy vs 1.7% with 27 Gy (HR 0.86; 95% CI 0.51-1.44) and 1.4% with 26 Gy (HR 0.67; 95% CI 0.38-1.16) [p.147]. Moderate or marked tissue effects in breast/chest wall were 15% with 27 Gy, 12% with 26 Gy, and 10% with 40 Gy; differences between 40 Gy and 26 Gy groups not statistically different [p.147].

**RT Boost to Tumor Bed:** In higher-risk patients (age <50 years, high-grade disease, focally positive margins), RT boost reduces local relapse [p.147]. Boost delivered using enface electrons, photons, or brachytherapy [p.147].

**NCCN WBRT Recommendations:** Panel defines target as breast tissue at risk [p.147]. Dose of 40-42.5 Gy in 15-16 fractions recommended for all patients getting whole breast radiation without regional nodal radiation, based on equivalence in efficacy and toxicity demonstrated in moderately hypofractionated trials [p.147]. Conventionally fractionated regimen of 46-50 Gy in 23-25 fractions may be utilized in selected patients [p.147]. RT boost doses to decrease local recurrence are 10-16 Gy in 4-8 fractions [p.147].

Ultra-hypofractionated WBRT of 28.5 Gy as 5 once-weekly fractions may be considered in select patients with pTis/T1/T2/N0 aged >50 years after BCS, though optimal boost fractionation unknown [p.147]. Alternatively, 26 Gy in 5 daily fractions over 1 week may be considered, though data beyond 5 years for local relapse or toxicity not yet available [p.147]. When using ultra-hypofractionated dosing, 3D planning essential to minimize inhomogeneity and heart/lung exposure [p.147].

### Chest Wall Radiation [p.147]

Target includes ipsilateral chest wall, mastectomy scar, and drain sites when indicated. Several techniques using photons/electrons appropriate depending on breast reconstruction status [p.147].

**NCCN Chest Wall Recommendations:** Panel recommends 45-50.4 Gy in 25-28 fractions to chest wall [p.147]. Scar boost of 1.8-2 Gy per fraction to total dose approximately 60-66 Gy may be considered based on risk [p.147]. Special consideration for bolus material to ensure adequate skin dose, particularly in IBC [p.147].

### Regional Nodal Irradiation (RNI) [p.147]

**MA.20 Trial:** Evaluated addition of RNI to internal mammary nodes and upper axillary nodes including supraclavicular region to WBRT or chest wall irradiation after BCS or mastectomy. Regional recurrences reduced from 2.7% with breast irradiation only to 0.7% with nodal irradiation [p.147]. Distant recurrences reduced from 17.3% to 13.4% [p.147]. DFS improved from 77% to 82% at 10 years with RNI [p.147].

**EORTC 22922/10925 Trial:** Regional RT reduced regional recurrence incidence from 4.2% to 2.7% and decreased distant metastases from 19.6% to 15.9% at median 10.9-year follow-up [p.147]. 15.7-year follow-up showed breast cancer mortality reduced (19.8% vs 16%; 95% CI 0.70-0.94) and breast cancer recurrence reduced (27.1% vs 24.5%; 95% CI 0.77-0.98%) with internal mammary and medial supraclavicular RT [p.147].

**Internal Mammary Node RT Debate:** Independent contribution of internal mammary nodal RT continues to be debated as associated with higher cardiac and lung toxicity risk, and data regarding benefits conflicting [p.147].

### Radiation Therapy Principles [p.146]

Individualize RT planning and delivery. CT-based treatment planning encouraged to delineate target volumes and adjacent organs at risk. Greater target dose homogeneity and normal tissue sparing accomplished using compensators (wedges), forward planning, and IMRT [p.146]. Respiratory control techniques (deep inspiration breath-hold, prone positioning) may reduce dose to adjacent tissues, particularly heart and lung [p.146]. Weekly imaging verification of treatment setup consistency recommended; daily imaging not standard [p.146]. DVHs should evaluate dose constraints, dose to normal tissues, and ensure adequate PTV coverage including breast/chest wall, supraclavicular fossa, axillary levels I-III, and internal mammary nodes [p.146].
