# Breast Reconstruction -- Evidence Summary

<!-- Source: NCCN Breast Cancer v2.2026, pages 153-156 -->
<!-- Chunk: breast-reconstruction-manuscript -->

## Summary of Key Evidence

- All patients undergoing breast cancer treatment should be educated about breast reconstructive options and offered consultation with a reconstructive plastic surgeon [p.153]
- Breast reconstruction does not impact the probability of recurrence or death but is associated with improved quality of life for many patients [p.153]
- Smoking and obesity increase the risk of complications for all types of breast reconstruction (implant or flap) and are considered relative contraindications [p.153] [218-223]
- Plans for post-mastectomy radiation significantly impact reconstruction decisions due to increased risk of implant capsular contracture and potential negative impact on cosmesis [p.154] [228,229]
- Skin-sparing mastectomy with immediate reconstruction can improve cosmetic outcomes without increased risk of local recurrence compared to standard procedures [p.155] [242-246]
- Oncoplastic volume displacement techniques permit wider surgical margins while better preserving breast shape and appearance compared to standard resections [p.155] [250]

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Reconstruction After Mastectomy

**Immediate vs. Delayed Reconstruction**

Reconstruction can be performed either immediately at the time of mastectomy under the same anesthetic or in a delayed fashion [p.154]. Many cases involve a staged approach requiring multiple procedures, including surgery on the contralateral breast for symmetry, revision surgery, and/or nipple and areola reconstruction [p.154].

**Implant-Based Reconstruction**

Reconstruction with implants can be performed by:
- Immediate placement of a permanent subpectoral implant [p.153]
- Initial placement of a subpectoral expander implant followed by gradual expansion with stretching of the pectoralis major muscle and skin, then replacement with a permanent implant [p.153]

A wide variety of implants are available containing saline, silicone gel, or a combination with a solid silicone envelope [p.153].

**Autogenous Tissue Reconstruction**

Autogenous tissue methods use various combinations of fat, muscle, skin, and vasculature from donor sites (abdomen, buttock, back) [p.153]. Tissues may be brought to the chest wall with their original blood supply (pedicle flap) or as free flaps with microvascular anastomoses [p.153]. Available procedures include transverse rectus abdominis myocutaneous flap, latissimus dorsi flap, and gluteus maximus myocutaneous flap reconstruction [p.153].

**Composite Reconstruction**

Composite reconstruction techniques combine implants with autogenous tissue reconstruction to provide volume and symmetry [p.154].

### Factors Influencing Reconstruction Decisions

The decision regarding reconstruction type includes patient preference, body habitus, smoking history, comorbidities, plans for irradiation, and the expertise and experience of the reconstruction team [p.153].

### Skin-Sparing Mastectomy

Skin-sparing mastectomy procedures remove the breast parenchyma including the nipple-areolar complex while preserving the majority of the original skin envelope, followed by immediate reconstruction [p.154]. These procedures may be followed by autogenous tissue, prosthetic implant, or composite reconstruction [p.154].

**NAC-Sparing Procedures**

Nipple-areolar complex (NAC)-sparing mastectomy may be an option in patients carefully selected by experienced multidisciplinary teams [p.154]. Assessment of nipple margins is mandatory [p.154]. Retrospective data support NAC-sparing procedures for patients with:
- Early-stage, biologically favorable invasive cancers (Nottingham grade 1 or 2, node-negative, HER2-negative, no lymphovascular invasion) [p.155]
- Ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) peripherally located in the breast (>2 cm from nipple) [p.155]
- Low rates of nipple involvement and low rates of local recurrence [p.155] [239,240]

**Contraindications to NAC-Sparing**

Contraindications include evidence of nipple involvement such as Paget disease, nipple discharge associated with malignancy, or imaging findings suggesting malignant involvement of nipple and subareolar tissues [p.155].

**Cosmetic Outcomes**

Advantages of skin-sparing mastectomy include improved cosmetic outcome with reduced mastectomy scar size and more natural breast shape, especially with autologous tissue [p.155] [241]. Results of retrospective studies indicate the risk of local recurrence is not increased with skin-sparing compared to non-skin-sparing procedures [p.155] [242-246].

Skin-sparing mastectomy should be performed by an experienced breast surgery team working in a coordinated, multidisciplinary fashion [p.155].

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### Radiation Therapy Considerations

**Impact on Implant-Based Reconstruction**

Post-mastectomy radiation therapy can significantly impact breast reconstruction decisions. There is a significantly increased risk of implant capsular contracture following irradiation of an implant [p.154] [228,229]. Immediate placement of an implant in patients requiring postoperative radiation has an increased rate of capsular contracture, malposition, poor cosmesis, and implant exposure [p.154].

**Preferred Approach for Irradiated Patients**

For post-mastectomy patients requiring radiation therapy with implant reconstruction, the NCCN Panel prefers a staged approach with immediate tissue expander placement followed by implant placement (category 2B) [p.154]. Surgery to exchange tissue expanders with permanent implants can be performed prior to radiation or after completion of radiation therapy [p.154].

**Autologous Tissue and Radiation**

Post-mastectomy irradiation may have a negative impact on breast cosmesis when autologous tissue is used in immediate breast reconstruction and may interfere with targeted delivery of radiation [p.154] [228,229]. However, some studies have not found significant compromise in reconstruction cosmesis after radiation therapy [p.154] [230]. Generally, radiation therapy is preferred to precede autologous reconstruction due to reported loss in reconstruction cosmesis [p.154].

**Previously Radiated Patients**

In a previously radiated patient, the use of tissue expanders/implants is relatively contraindicated [p.154] [231]. Tissue expansion of irradiated skin can result in significantly increased risk of capsular contracture, malposition, poor cosmesis, implant exposure, and failed reconstruction [p.154] [232,233]. For patients who have previously received radiation therapy to the breast, autologous tissue reconstruction is the preferred method [p.154].

### Special Considerations for Comorbidities

Patients with underlying diabetes or who smoke tobacco have increased rates of complications following autogenous tissue breast cancer reconstruction, presumably due to underlying microvascular disease [p.154].

### Reconstruction After Lumpectomy

**Oncoplastic Volume Displacement Techniques**

For patients with large surgical defects or expected cosmetically unsatisfactory outcomes from lumpectomy, oncoplastic techniques for breast conservation can extend breast-conserving surgical options [p.155]. The evolving field of oncoplastic surgery includes "volume displacement" techniques performed in conjunction with large partial mastectomy [p.155].

Oncoplastic volume displacement procedures combine removal of generous regions of breast tissue with "mastopexy" techniques where remaining breast tissues are shifted together within the breast envelope to fill the surgical defect and avoid significant breast deformity [p.155]. These procedures are generally performed during the same operative setting as the breast-conserving lumpectomy by the same surgeon performing the cancer resection [p.155] [248,249].

**Advantages and Limitations**

Advantages include:
- Removal of larger regions of breast tissue to achieve wider surgical margins [p.155] [250]
- Better preservation of natural breast shape and appearance compared to standard breast resections [p.155] [250]

Limitations include:
- Lack of standardization among centers [p.155]
- Performance at only a limited number of sites in the United States [p.155]
- Possible necessity for subsequent mastectomy if pathologic margins are positive [p.155]

These issues should be considered prior to surgery for individuals likely to have cosmetically unsatisfactory surgical defects [p.156].

**Post-Lumpectomy Management**

Those who undergo lumpectomy and are dissatisfied with the cosmetic outcome should be offered consultation with a plastic surgeon to address breast defect repair [p.156]. Patients should be informed of the possibility of positive margins and potential need for secondary surgery, which could include re-excision, segmental resection, or mastectomy with or without loss of the nipple [p.156].

Oncoplastic procedures can be combined with surgery on the contralateral unaffected breast to minimize long-term asymmetry [p.156].

## Special Considerations

### General Principles

Breast reconstruction should not interfere with appropriate surgical management of breast cancer, as this may increase the risk of overall and cancer-related death, especially in those with late-stage disease [p.153] [218]. Coordinating consultation and surgical treatment with a reconstructive surgeon should be executed within a reasonable timeframe [p.153].

Reconstruction is an optional procedure that does not impact the probability of recurrence or death but is associated with improved quality of life for many patients [p.153]. It is sometimes necessary to perform surgery on the contralateral breast (breast reduction, implantation) to achieve optimal symmetry between the ipsilateral reconstructed breast and the contralateral breast [p.153].

### Smoking and Obesity

Smoking and obesity increase the risk of complications for all types of breast reconstruction, whether with implant or flap [p.153] [219-223]. Patients should be informed of increased rates of wound healing complications and partial or complete flap failure among patients who smoke and have obesity [p.153].

### Patient Selection and Multidisciplinary Coordination

Many factors must be considered in decision-making about breast reconstruction, including patient preference, body habitus, smoking history, comorbidities, plans for irradiation, and expertise and experience of the reconstruction team [p.153].

An evaluation of the likely cosmetic outcome should be performed prior to surgery [p.155]. Decisions regarding breast reconstruction should primarily focus on treatment of the tumor, and such treatment should not be compromised [p.156].

### Quality of Life and NAC Reconstruction

The loss of the breast following mastectomy for cosmetic, body image, and psychosocial purposes may be partially overcome through breast reconstruction with or without reconstruction of the nipple-areolar complex [p.153]. Possible advantages of NAC-sparing procedures include improvements in breast cosmesis, body image, and nipple sensation following mastectomy, although the impact on these quality of life issues has not been well-studied [p.154] [234-236]. Reconstructed nipples are devoid of sensation [p.155]. Reconstruction of the NAC may be performed in a delayed fashion if desired by the patient [p.155].

### Clinical Trials

Several prospective trials are underway to evaluate NAC-sparing mastectomy in the setting of cancer, and enrollment in such trials is encouraged [p.155].
