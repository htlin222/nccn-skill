## Summary of Key Evidence

- Marginal zone lymphomas (MZLs) originate from marginal zones of lymphoid follicles in mucosa-associated lymphoid tissues (MALT), spleen, and lymph nodes [p.196]
- Three distinct subtypes: extranodal MZL (EMZL) of stomach (gastric MALT), EMZL of non-gastric sites, nodal MZL (NMZL), and splenic MZL (SMZL) [p.196]
- United States incidence (SEER database 2001-2009): NMZL 5.7 per 100,000; extranodal MZL 12.3 per 100,000; gastric MALT 3.8 per 100,000 [p.196]
- Etiology: chronic immune stimulation from infectious agents; Helicobacter pylori in gastric MALT; hepatitis C virus (HCV) seroprevalence 22-35% in NMZL, SMZL, and non-gastric EMZL [p.196]
- MYD88 L265P mutation highly specific for WM/LPL (67%) versus 0-7% across MZL subtypes; IGHV gene sequencing distinguishes SMZL from WM/LPL [p.196]

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### EMZL of Stomach

#### H. pylori-Positive Early-Stage Disease

**H. pylori Eradication Therapy:**
- H. pylori eradication is effective initial therapy for H. pylori-positive early-stage EMZL of stomach, resulting in lymphoma regression in 70-95% of patients [p.199]
- Standard regimen: proton pump inhibitor plus combination of clarithromycin and amoxicillin (or metronidazole for penicillin-allergic patients) [p.199]
- Multicenter cohort follow-up study (420 patients): 77% response rate to H. pylori eradication therapy; 10-year freedom from treatment failure (FFTF) 90%, overall survival (OS) 95%, event-free survival (EFS) 86% [p.199]
- Predictors of resistance to eradication therapy: t(11;18) translocation, H. pylori-negativity, and submucosal invasion [p.199]
- **NCCN Category: Preferred for H. pylori-positive early-stage disease [p.199-200]**

**For t(11;18)-Positive Disease:**
- Antibiotic therapy combined with involved-site radiation therapy (ISRT) (preferred) or rituximab (if ISRT contraindicated) [p.199-200]

#### H. pylori-Negative Disease

**Involved-Site Radiation Therapy:**
- ISRT is effective treatment after failure of H. pylori eradication therapy or as initial treatment for H. pylori-negative disease [p.199]
- Randomized controlled study (241 patients with localized EMZL): 10-year EFS rates 52% (surgery), 52% (RT), 87% (chemotherapy); 10-year OS rates 80% vs. 75% vs. 87% respectively (not significantly different) [p.199]
- German multicenter study: extended-field RT (30 Gy + 10 Gy boost) resulted in 88% 10-year EFS and 93% OS in EMZL of stomach subgroup [p.199]
- Retrospective study (192 patients stage I-II): 99% complete response (CR) rate with involved-field RT (IFRT) (30-35 Gy); 10-year relapse-free survival (RFS) 76%, disease-free survival (DFS) 68%, OS 87% [p.199]
- Reduced-dose RT (25 Gy) effective for H. pylori-negative stage I-II disease [p.199]
- **NCCN Category: Preferred for H. pylori-negative disease [p.200]**

**Rituximab:**
- Rituximab demonstrated activity in H. pylori-negative disease or relapsed/refractory to eradication therapy [p.199]
- Prospective study (27 patients, majority stage I or II): 77% overall response rate (ORR), 46% CR; at 28-month median follow-up, 100% alive and 54% disease-free [p.199]
- Retrospective study (106 patients, 28 treated with rituximab): 73% ORR, 64% CR; 5-year progression-free survival (PFS) 70%, OS 95% [p.199]
- **NCCN Category: Option if ISRT contraindicated [p.200]**

#### Follow-Up and Restaging

- Endoscopy and biopsy recommended after 6 months to evaluate response and rule out diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL) [p.200]
- Clinical follow-up every 3-6 months for 5 years, then yearly or as clinically indicated [p.200]
- For H. pylori-negative persistent disease: ISRT recommended or active surveillance for 3 months if asymptomatic [p.200]

### EMZL of Non-Gastric Sites

**Involved-Site Radiation Therapy:**
- ISRT (24-30 Gy) is effective initial treatment for localized EMZL of non-gastric sites [p.201]
- Retrospective study (167 patients with localized EMZL): IFRT alone (25-35 Gy; 25 Gy for orbit) resulted in 10-year RFS 74%, OS 89% [p.201]
- Site-specific outcomes: thyroid 95% RFS, salivary gland 68%, orbital adnexa 67% [p.201]
- Retrospective study (208 patients, Ann Arbor stage III-IV in 44%): 76% ORR with RT; 5-year EFS 37%, OS 83% [p.201]
- **NCCN Category: Preferred treatment for stage I-II and IV disease [p.201-202]**

**Reduced-Dose ISRT:**
- Reduced-dose ISRT (4 Gy in 2 fractions) may be considered for orbital and salivary gland MZL [p.202]
- Requires careful follow-up with radiation oncologist and ophthalmologist [p.202]

**Rituximab:**
- Rituximab demonstrated activity in EMZL of non-gastric sites [p.201]
- Phase II study (IELSG): 80% ORR (55% CR, 25% PR) in non-gastric MALT lymphoma [p.201]
- **NCCN Category: Option for selected patients with stage I-II disease [p.201-202]**

**Active Surveillance:**
- May be considered for patients with excisional diagnostic biopsy or when RT would result in significant morbidity [p.202]

### Nodal Marginal Zone Lymphoma (NMZL)

#### Stage I-II Disease

**Involved-Site Radiation Therapy:**
- ISRT (24-30 Gy) is preferred treatment option for stage I or contiguous stage II disease [p.203-204]
- Initiation of systemic therapy improves failure-free survival (FFS) but has not improved OS [p.204]
- **NCCN Category: Preferred [p.203-204]**

**Anti-CD20 Monoclonal Antibody:**
- Anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody (mAb) with or without chemotherapy included as option [p.204]
- Can be considered in selected patients with bulky intra-abdominal or mesenteric stage I disease [p.204]
- Recommended for non-contiguous stage II disease [p.204]

**Active Surveillance:**
- Appropriate in circumstances where toxicity of ISRT or systemic therapy outweighs clinical benefit [p.204]

**Rituximab Outcomes:**
- Cohort analysis (56 patients treated with rituximab, 79% with advanced-stage disease): median PFS 42 months, median OS not reached; 120-month OS rate 72% [p.202]

#### Follow-Up

- Clinical follow-up with complete history, physical examination, and laboratory assessment every 3-6 months for first 5 years, then annually [p.204]
- Surveillance imaging (CT scans) no more than every 6 months for first 2 years, then annually thereafter [p.204]

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### EMZL of Stomach

**After Eradication Therapy Failure:**
- ISRT recommended for H. pylori-negative persistent disease after antibiotic therapy [p.200]
- Second-line antibiotic treatment recommended for persistent H. pylori infection with regressing or stable lymphoma [p.200]
- Persistent H. pylori with progressive/symptomatic disease: treat with ISRT plus second-line antibiotics [p.200]

### EMZL of Non-Gastric Sites

**Local Recurrence:**
- ISRT (if not previously given) or managed according to recommendations for advanced-stage NMZL [p.202]
- Patients with reduced-dose ISRT for ocular/salivary gland disease can receive additional RT for local recurrence [p.202]

**Systemic Recurrence:**
- Active surveillance recommended for patients without treatment indications [p.202]
- Systemic therapy for patients with indications for treatment [p.202]

### NMZL

**Inadequate Response to Initial Therapy:**
- Patients not responding to ISRT should receive treatment for stage III or IV NMZL [p.204]
- Rebiopsy recommended to rule out histologic transformation prior to second-line therapy [p.204]

## Special Considerations

### Diagnostic Markers and Testing

**Immunophenotype Assessment:**
- MALT lymphoma typical: CD5-, CD10-, CD20+, CD23-/+, CD43-/+, cyclin D1-, BCL2-; germinal centers [p.200]
- NMZL typical: CD5-, CD10-, CD20+, CD23-/+, CD43-/+, cyclin D1-, BCL2- [p.203]
- Recommended IHC panel: CD20, CD3, CD5, CD10, CD21 or CD23, kappa/lambda, cyclin D1, BCL2 [p.200, p.203]
- Flow cytometry: CD19, CD20, CD5, CD23, CD10 [p.200, p.203]

**Molecular Analysis:**
- IGHV gene rearrangement and t(11;18) detection may be useful [p.200, p.203]
- MYD88 mutation analysis may be useful in certain circumstances [p.200, p.203]
- Cytogenetics/FISH for t(11;18), t(3;14), t(11;14), t(14;18), del(13q), del(7q) may be considered [p.200, p.203]

### Workup Requirements

**Laboratory Testing:**
- Complete blood count (CBC) with differentials and platelets [p.201, p.203]
- Comprehensive metabolic panel [p.201, p.203]
- Serum LDH measurement [p.201, p.203]
- Hepatitis C virus (HCV) and hepatitis B virus (HBV) testing for all patients [p.201, p.203]
- HBV testing indicated due to risk of viral reactivation with chemoimmunotherapy [p.201, p.203]

**Imaging:**
- PET/CT preferred or chest/abdomen/pelvis CT with contrast of diagnostic quality [p.201, p.203]
- MUGA scan/echocardiogram if considering anthracycline or anthracenedione regimens [p.201, p.203]
- MRI with contrast for neurologic evaluation or if CT contraindicated [p.201]

**Additional Studies:**
- Bone marrow biopsy with aspirates for NMZL to document stage I-II disease [p.203]
- Endoscopy with multiple biopsies for selected circumstances (non-gastric EMZL) [p.201]
- Upper GI endoscopy for head/neck or lung primary sites [p.201]

### Clinical Features and Prognosis

**NMZL Characteristics:**
- Peripheral lymphadenopathy present in >95% of patients [p.202]
- Thoracic/abdominal lymph node involvement in ~50% [p.202]
- Bone marrow and peripheral blood involvement in 30-40% and 10% respectively [p.202]
- About two-thirds present with advanced-stage disease; most non-bulky [p.202]
- B symptoms present in only ~15% [p.202]
- Indolent disease course with less favorable long-term survival than EMZL [p.202]
## Summary of Key Evidence

- Splenectomy with or without chemotherapy demonstrates favorable outcomes with median OS exceeding 10 years and 10-year OS rate of 61% to 84% in patients with splenic marginal zone lymphoma (SMZL) [p.207]
- Pneumococcal and meningococcal vaccination should be administered at least 2 weeks before splenectomy [p.207]
- Bendamustine + rituximab (BR) and RCHOP are preferred first-line chemoimmunotherapy regimens with high overall response rates [p.207-208]
- Lenalidomide + rituximab demonstrates activity in both first-line and second-line settings with median PFS of 54 months in untreated patients with MZL [p.208]
- BTK inhibitors (acalabrutinib and zanubrutinib) are preferred second-line options with favorable efficacy and toxicity profiles [p.209]
- CAR T-cell therapy (axicabtagene ciloleucel) is included as third-line option with durable responses in relapsed/refractory MZL [p.210-211]

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Chemoimmunotherapy with anti-CD20 Monoclonal Antibody (Preferred Regimens)

**Bendamustine + Rituximab (BR)**

In the multicenter randomized phase III StiL NHL1 trial (67 patients with MZL), BR was evaluated against RCHOP; however, BR was not significantly superior to RCHOP in terms of PFS in MZL (P = 0.32) [p.207]

In the randomized phase III BRIGHT study evaluating noninferiority of BR compared to RCHOP/RCVP in previously untreated indolent lymphoma (46 patients with MZL; 28 to BR, 18 to RCHOP/RCVP), ORRs were 92% (20% CR) for BR and 71% (24% CR) for RCHOP/RCVP in the MZL subgroup [p.207]

In the multicenter phase II MALT 2008-01 trial (60 patients with previously untreated MALT lymphoma; 20 gastric, 34 non-gastric):
- After 3 cycles: ORR of 100% (75% CR), with significantly higher CR in gastric MALT versus non-gastric (90% vs. 62%; P = 0.023) [p.207]
- After 6 cycles: ORR and CR rates were 100% and 98%, respectively [p.207]
- After median active surveillance of 82 months: 7-year EFS rate was 88% overall (90% gastric, 84% non-gastric) [p.207]

In the phase II BRISMA trial (56 patients with SMZL treated with BR as first-line):
- ORR and CR rates were 91% and 73%, respectively [p.207]
- 5-year PFS and OS rates were 83% and 93%, respectively [p.208]

**RCHOP and RCVP**

In a phase II study (40 patients with advanced-stage MZL), RCVP resulted in ORR of 88% (60% CR); estimated 3-year PFS and OS rates were 59% and 95%, respectively, after median follow-up of 38 months [p.208]

RCVP is also effective for patients not candidates for H. pylori eradication therapy or those with gastric MALT lymphoma resistant to H. pylori eradication therapy [p.208]

**Obinutuzumab + Chemotherapy**

In the phase III randomized GALLIUM trial comparing obinutuzumab-based versus rituximab-based chemoimmunotherapy in previously untreated indolent B-cell lymphomas (195 patients with MZL; 99 to obinutuzumab, 96 to rituximab):
- After median follow-up of 59 months, no clinically relevant differences in ORR and PFS between arms [p.208]
- ORR: 80% (obinutuzumab) vs. 88% (rituximab) (P = 0.82) [p.208]
- 4-year PFS rates: 73% (obinutuzumab) vs. 64% (rituximab) (P = 0.35) [p.208]
- 4-year OS: 82% (obinutuzumab) vs. 78% (rituximab) [p.208]
- Grade 3-5 adverse events higher with obinutuzumab (86% vs. 77%) [p.208]
- Panel concluded obinutuzumab-based chemoimmunotherapy may not be appropriate first-line for MZL [p.208]

### First-Line Therapy: Other Recommended Regimens

**Lenalidomide + Rituximab**

In phase II trial evaluating lenalidomide + rituximab in untreated, advanced-stage indolent lymphoma (110 patients; 30 with MZL):
- ORR was 89% (67% CR; 22% PR) in the MZL subgroup [p.208]
- Median PFS was 54 months [p.208]

In patients with MALT lymphoma, lenalidomide + rituximab resulted in ORR of 80% (54% CR; 26% PR) [p.208]

Included with category 2B recommendation for first-line therapy [p.208]

**Rituximab Monotherapy**

Rituximab has demonstrated activity in MALT lymphomas and SMZL [p.208]

Rituximab is the preferred initial treatment for patients with SMZL [p.208]

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### Chemoimmunotherapy with anti-CD20 Monoclonal Antibody (Preferred Regimens)

In the GADOLIN study (413 total patients; 47 with MZL; 28 to bendamustine + obinutuzumab, 19 to bendamustine monotherapy), bendamustine + obinutuzumab followed by obinutuzumab maintenance significantly prolonged PFS compared to bendamustine monotherapy in rituximab-refractory indolent B-cell lymphomas [p.208]
- After median follow-up of 32 months: median PFS 26 months (bendamustine + obinutuzumab) vs. 14 months (bendamustine monotherapy) (P < 0.001) [p.208]

Alternate non-cross resistant chemotherapy regimens (bendamustine, CHOP or CVP) with obinutuzumab or rituximab are included as preferred options [p.209]

### Lenalidomide + Rituximab (Preferred Regimens)

In the multicenter, double-blind, randomized phase III AUGMENT trial comparing lenalidomide + rituximab versus rituximab monotherapy in relapsed/refractory indolent lymphoma (358 patients; 63 with MZL):
- ORR higher with lenalidomide + rituximab (64% [29% CR; 35% PR]) vs. rituximab monotherapy (44% [13% CR; 31% PR]) in MZL subgroup [p.209]
- PFS improvements favored lenalidomide + rituximab in most indolent subtypes except MZL [p.209]
- After median follow-up of 28 months: estimated 2-year OS rates were 82% (lenalidomide + rituximab) vs. 94% (rituximab monotherapy) for MZL subgroup [p.209]

In the MAGNIFY trial (394 patients; 76 with MZL) evaluating lenalidomide + rituximab followed by maintenance therapy in relapsed/refractory indolent B-cell lymphomas:
- Induction therapy resulted in ORR of 64% (39% CR) in MZL subgroup [p.209]

Included as option for preferred regimens for second-line and subsequent therapy [p.209]

### BTK Inhibitors (Preferred Regimens)

**Acalabrutinib**

In phase I/II trial (43 patients with relapsed/refractory MZL):
- ORR of 53% (13% CR) among 40 evaluable patients [p.209]
- After median follow-up of 13 months: estimated 12-month PFS rate was 67% [p.209]

**Zanubrutinib**

FDA-approved for relapsed/refractory MZL after at least one prior anti-CD20-mAB-based regimen based on phase II MAGNOLIA trial (68 patients with relapsed/refractory MZL after one or more prior therapies including anti-CD20-mAb-based regimen) [p.209]
- ORR of 68% (26% CR) by independent review committee [p.209]
- Responses observed in all subtypes: 64% ORR (40% CR) in extranodal, 76% (20% CR) in nodal, 67% (8% CR) in splenic, 50% (25% CR) in indeterminate subtypes [p.209]
- At median follow-up of 27 months: 24-month PFS and OS rates were 71% and 86%, respectively [p.209]
- 24-month PFS rates higher in CR patients (87% vs. 65%) [p.209]

**Pirtobrutinib**

Non-covalent BTK inhibitor approved for relapsed/refractory MCL and CLL/SLL; demonstrated safety and efficacy in phase I/II BRUIN study (36 patients; 17 NMZL, 13 SMZL, 6 EMZL) [p.209]
- ORR of 50% (3% CR and 47% PR) [p.209]
- ORR of 46% in patients previously treated with covalent BTKi [p.209]
- ORR in NMZL, SMZL, and EMZL were 59%, 31%, and 67%, respectively [p.209]
- Median PFS was 17 months after median follow-up of 16 months [p.209]
- After median follow-up of 22 months: median OS not reached, estimated 24-month OS rate 78% [p.209]

Included as option for second-line therapy in patients with relapsed/refractory disease after prior covalent BTKi [p.210]

### Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy: Other Recommended Regimens

**Ibrutinib**

In multicenter phase II study (60 patients with relapsed/refractory MZL after prior anti-CD20 MAB-based regimen):
- ORR of 48% (3% CR and 45% PR) [p.210]
- ORRs: 63% EMZL, 47% NMZL, 62% SMZL [p.210]
- After median follow-up of 33 months: median PFS 16 months, median OS not reached [p.210]
- Estimated 33-month PFS rates: 32% EMZL, 24% NMZL, 42% SMZL [p.210]
- Corresponding OS rates: 76% EMZL, 69% NMZL, 66% SMZL [p.210]
- Atrial fibrillation incidence (8%) and major bleeding (3%) higher than with other covalent BTKi [p.210]

FDA accelerated approval granted in 2021 based on aforementioned phase II results; accelerated approval status withdrawn April 2023 following confirmatory phase III studies [p.210]
- SHINE study met primary endpoint of superior PFS but OS not different; more deaths from adverse events in ibrutinib group [p.210]
- SELENE study did not meet primary endpoint of improved PFS with ibrutinib plus chemoimmunotherapy [p.210]

Panel consensus to continue listing ibrutinib monotherapy based on earlier phase II efficacy data, but moved to other recommended regimens due to more favorable toxicity profiles for acalabrutinib and zanubrutinib in head-to-head trials [p.210]

**Other Options**

Rituximab monotherapy (for relapse after longer duration of remission) and lenalidomide + obinutuzumab are included as options under other recommended regimens [p.210]

## Third-Line Therapy

### PI3K Inhibitors

Umbralisib received accelerated FDA approval in 2022 for relapsed/refractory MZL after one or more prior lines with anti-CD20 mAb-based regimen based on UNITY-NHL trial results [p.210]

Other PI3K inhibitors (copanlisib, duvelisib, idelalisib) showed promising activity in relapsed/refractory MZL; however, all subsequently had voluntary withdrawal of FDA indications due to substantial toxicity with detrimental impact on OS in recent trials [p.210]

Not recommended for treatment of relapsed/refractory MZL [p.210]

### CAR T-Cell Therapy

**Axicabtagene Ciloleucel**

Included as option for third-line therapy based on phase II ZUMA-5 trial (158 patients with relapsed/refractory indolent lymphoma; 127 FL, 31 MZL) [p.210-211]

Among 152 patients who received axicabtagene ciloleucel:
- Overall ORR 90% (75% CR) [p.211]
- In MZL subgroup (31 patients) after median follow-up of 32 months: ORR 77%, median PFS not reached [p.211]
- Estimated 36-month OS and lymphoma-specific PFS rates: 75% and 65%, respectively, for all patients with indolent lymphomas [p.211]
- 4-year follow-up data confirmed durable responses and long-term survival [p.211]

## Special Considerations

### Follow-Up and Monitoring

Patients should be monitored on regular basis following treatment [p.207]

Clinical follow-up (including repeat diagnostic tests and imaging studies as clinically indicated) should be performed every 3 to 6 months for 5 years and then annually or as clinically indicated thereafter [p.207]

In patients with recurrence, active surveillance is recommended for those without indications for treatment [p.207]

Splenectomy, ISRT, or systemic therapy are recommended for patients with indications for treatment [p.207]

### Treatment for Older or Infirm Patients

**Preferred Treatment**

Rituximab monotherapy is the preferred treatment option for untreated as well as relapsed/refractory disease in patients who are older or infirm [p.211]

**Other Recommended Regimens**

Alkylating agent-based chemotherapy (cyclophosphamide or chlorambucil) with or without rituximab are included as options for previously untreated and relapsed/refractory disease [p.211]

In study of 49 patients with EMZL of stomach, chlorambucil + rituximab was more efficient than rituximab alone in t(11;18)-positive disease, whereas rituximab alone is as efficient as chlorambucil + rituximab in t(11;18)-negative disease [p.211]

In international randomized IELSG-19 trial evaluating chlorambucil + rituximab versus monotherapies in untreated EMZL patients:
- 5-year EFS rates: 68% (chlorambucil + rituximab) vs. 51% (chlorambucil) vs. 50% (rituximab monotherapy) [p.211]
- 5-year OS rates not significantly different between treatment arms [p.211]

Cyclophosphamide + rituximab effective in SMZL patients who are frail and not fit for splenectomy:
- ORR of 87% with median PFS of 20 months [p.211]

Acalabrutinib, zanubrutinib, lenalidomide + rituximab, and rituximab monotherapy are included as preferred treatment options for relapsed/refractory disease in older/infirm patients [p.211]

Pirtobrutinib included as option for second-line therapy in relapsed/refractory disease after prior covalent BTKi [p.211]

Ibrutinib included as option under other recommended regimens [p.211]

## Summary of Key Evidence

- Rituximab monotherapy and rituximab-based combinations are superior treatment options for splenic marginal zone lymphoma compared to chemotherapy alone or splenectomy [p.218]
- Bendamustine plus rituximab demonstrates efficacy as first-line treatment for marginal zone lymphoma subtypes, including nodal and splenic variants [p.219]
- Bruton tyrosine kinase (BTK) inhibitors including zanubrutinib, ibrutinib, and acalabrutinib show safety and efficacy in relapsed/refractory marginal zone lymphoma [p.219-220]
- PI3K inhibitors (umbralisib, copanlisib, duvelisib, idelalisib) provide options for relapsed indolent lymphomas, though PI3K inhibitors require careful safety monitoring [p.220]
- CAR T-cell therapy (axicabtagene ciloleucel) demonstrates durable responses in relapsed/refractory indolent non-Hodgkin lymphoma [p.220]
- For gastric MALT lymphoma, rituximab-based combination therapy with chlorambucil shows improved outcomes compared to monotherapy [p.221]

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

### Rituximab Monotherapy and Combinations
Rituximab as monotherapy and in combination regimens is a foundational approach in marginal zone lymphomas. Rituximab monotherapy produces prolonged responses with potential benefit from maintenance therapy [p.218]. When combined with chemotherapy regimens such as CVP (cyclophosphamide, vincristine, prednisone), rituximab shows efficacy in advanced-stage marginal zone lymphoma as first-line therapy [p.219].

### Bendamustine Plus Rituximab
Bendamustine plus rituximab has demonstrated activity across marginal zone lymphoma subtypes as first-line treatment [p.219]:
- Efficacy established in primary nodal marginal zone B-cell lymphoma [p.219]
- Activity in intermediate-/high-risk splenic marginal zone lymphomas [p.219]
- Long-term outcomes with early unmeasurable minimal residual disease attainment in symptomatic splenic marginal zone lymphoma from the BRISMA/IELSG36 phase II study [p.219]

### Rituximab Plus Lenalidomide
Rituximab combined with lenalidomide shows activity in mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT) lymphoma [p.219].

### Obinutuzumab-Based Immunochemotherapy
The GALLIUM trial evaluated obinutuzumab versus rituximab immunochemotherapy in previously untreated indolent NHL, with final results reported in 2023 [p.219]. Immunochemotherapy with obinutuzumab or rituximab maintenance in previously untreated marginal zone lymphoma demonstrated outcomes in the randomized GALLIUM trial [p.219].

### Rituximab Plus Chlorambucil for Gastric MALT
For gastric mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue lymphoma, the IELSG-19 randomized trial showed improved event-free and progression-free survival with rituximab plus chlorambucil versus either chlorambucil or rituximab monotherapy [p.221].

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### BTK Inhibitors
Multiple BTK inhibitors demonstrate efficacy in relapsed/refractory marginal zone lymphoma:

**Zanubrutinib:** The MAGNOLIA trial, a next-generation Bruton tyrosine kinase inhibitor, demonstrated safety and efficacy in relapsed/refractory marginal zone lymphoma [p.219]. Zanubrutinib monotherapy shows activity in relapsed/refractory indolent non-Hodgkin lymphoma [p.219-220], with final analysis confirming safety and efficacy in relapsed/refractory marginal zone lymphoma [p.220].

**Ibrutinib:** Produces durable responses in relapsed/refractory marginal zone lymphoma with long-term follow-up and biomarker analysis available [p.220]. Ibrutinib plus bendamustine and rituximab was studied in the SELENE trial for previously treated patients with follicular or marginal zone lymphoma [p.220].

**Acalabrutinib:** A phase 2 trial (ACE-LY-003) of acalabrutinib in patients with relapsed or refractory marginal zone lymphoma demonstrated activity [p.219].

**Pirtobrutinib:** A highly selective, non-covalent (reversible) BTK inhibitor showed results in relapsed/refractory marginal zone lymphoma in the phase 1/2 BRUIN study [p.220].

### Lenalidomide-Based Combinations
Lenalidomide with rituximab demonstrates activity in relapsed/refractory disease:
- Safety and efficacy of lenalidomide and rituximab in untreated indolent lymphoma [p.219]
- Efficacy and safety in recurrent indolent non-follicular lymphoma from a phase II study conducted by the Fondazione Italiana Linfomi [p.219]
- The AUGMENT phase III study of lenalidomide plus rituximab versus placebo plus rituximab in relapsed or refractory indolent lymphoma [p.219]
- The Magnify phase 3b study of lenalidomide plus rituximab followed by maintenance in relapsed/refractory indolent non-Hodgkin lymphoma [p.219]

Lenalidomide in combination with obinutuzumab in patients with relapsed indolent non-Hodgkin lymphoma showed safety and activity in a single group, open-label, phase 1/2 trial [p.220].

### PI3K Inhibitors
Multiple PI3K delta inhibitors provide options for relapsed indolent lymphomas, though safety considerations are important [p.220]:
- **Umbralisib:** A dual PI3K delta/CK1 epsilon inhibitor demonstrated activity in patients with relapsed or refractory indolent lymphoma [p.220]
- **Copanlisib:** Demonstrated efficacy and safety in patients with relapsed or refractory marginal zone lymphoma [p.220]
- **Duvelisib:** The DYNAMO phase II study of duvelisib (IPI-145) in patients with refractory indolent non-Hodgkin lymphoma [p.220]
- **Idelalisib:** Up to 6 years of follow-up from a phase 2 study in relapsed indolent lymphomas [p.220]

PI3K inhibitors require careful safety monitoring, as survival is the ultimate safety endpoint in haematological malignancies [p.220].

### CAR T-Cell Therapy
Axicabtagene ciloleucel (Axi-Cel) in patients with relapsed/refractory indolent non-Hodgkin lymphoma showed 4-year follow-up data from the phase 2 ZUMA-5 trial [p.220].

### Rituximab-Based Therapy for Relapsed MALT
Rituximab plus cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin/mitoxantrone, vincristine and prednisone demonstrated activity in patients with relapsed MALT lymphoma [p.219]. Rituximab plus bendamustine is active in pretreated patients with extragastric marginal zone B cell lymphoma of the mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT lymphoma) [p.219].

## Special Considerations

### Splenic Marginal Zone Lymphoma Management
Treatment selection for splenic marginal zone lymphoma may include surgical versus medical approaches. Splenectomy with chemotherapy versus surgery alone as initial treatment has been studied [p.218]. Long-term follow-up analysis of 100 patients with splenic marginal zone lymphoma treated with splenectomy as first-line treatment spans up to 14 years [p.218]. Analysis of 107 patients treated in British Columbia examined outcomes in splenic marginal zone lymphoma [p.218]. A prospective observational study evaluated surgery as first-line therapy for splenic marginal zone lymphoma [p.218].

For patients unfit for surgery or more aggressive therapies, treatment approaches have been established with experience in 30 cases [p.221].

### MALT Lymphoma and t(11;18) Status
Treatment decisions for gastric mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue lymphoma may consider t(11;18) status, which was evaluated in a monocentric non-randomized observational study comparing rituximab and chlorambucil versus rituximab alone [p.221].
