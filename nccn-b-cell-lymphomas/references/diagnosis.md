## Diagnosis and Additional Testing

### Diagnostic Biopsy

All recommendations are (Category 2A) unless otherwise indicated [p.13].

- **Excisional or incisional biopsy** is preferred for the definitive diagnosis or histologic grading of lymphoma [p.13].

- **Fine-needle aspiration (FNA) biopsy** is insufficient for the initial diagnosis or histologic grading of lymphoma [p.13].

- **Core needle biopsy** (multiple biopsies preferred) is an appropriate alternative to excisional or incisional biopsy, especially in certain circumstances:
  - When a lymph node is not easily accessible for excisional or incisional biopsy [p.13]
  - If surgical biopsy would cause significant morbidity or substantial treatment delays [p.13]

- A **combination of core needle biopsy** (multiple biopsies preferred) and **FNA biopsy** in conjunction with appropriate ancillary techniques for the differential diagnosis may be sufficient for diagnosis:
  - Immunohistochemistry (IHC) [p.13]
  - Flow cytometry [p.13]
  - Molecular analysis to detect immunoglobulin (Ig) gene rearrangements [p.13]
  - Karyotype or fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) for major translocations [p.13]

### Hematopathology Review

- **Hematopathology review** of all slides with at least one paraffin block representative of the lesion [p.13].

- **Rebiopsy if consult material is nondiagnostic**, preferably the most FDG-avid, accessible lymph node [p.13].

### Clonal Process Detection

- If a high suspicion of a clonal process remains and other techniques have not resulted in a clear identification of a clonal process, then **next-generation sequencing (NGS)** can be used [p.13].
