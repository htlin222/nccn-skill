## Disease Overview

Mantle cell lymphoma (MCL) comprises about 4% of all newly diagnosed non-Hodgkin lymphomas (NHL) [p.222][1]. MCL is a heterogeneous subtype of B-cell NHL with unfavorable characteristics of both indolent and aggressive NHL, owing to the incurability with conventional chemoimmunotherapy and a typically more aggressive disease course compared to indolent NHL [p.222][2].

MCL is characterized by the reciprocal chromosomal translocation t(11;14), juxtaposing the CCND1 gene locus with the IGHV gene locus, resulting in cyclin D1 overexpression; diagnosis generally requires cyclin D1 expression [p.222][3]. Cyclin D1-negative MCL (<5% of cases) has been reported [p.222][4,5]. Rearrangements involving the CCND2 gene are present in 55% of cyclin D1-negative MCL and are associated with high cyclin D2 mRNA expression [p.222][6].

Gene expression profiling and microRNA profiling showed genomic signatures of cyclin D1-negative MCL were similar to cyclin D1-positive MCL [p.222][5-7]. Pathologic features and clinical characteristics of cyclin D1-negative MCL appear similar to cyclin D1-positive MCL [p.222][6,7]. In the absence of data suggesting otherwise, cyclin D1-negative MCL should not be managed differently than cyclin D1-positive MCL [p.222].

## MCL Subtypes

The 2022 WHO Classification of Hematolymphoid Tumors and International Consensus Classification recognize three subtypes of MCL with different clinicopathologic and molecular features [p.222][8,9]:

### Classical MCL
Classical MCL (nodal or extranodal) is SOX11-positive and IGHV unmutated with a generally aggressive clinical course [p.222][10]. The blastoid/pleomorphic variant is characterized by cytologic features resembling lymphoblasts or large cell lymphomas, more aggressive clinical course, and is often associated with poor prognostic features such as TP53 mutations and high Ki-67 proliferation index [p.222][10,11].

### Leukemic and Non-Nodal MCL
The leukemic and non-nodal subtype is typically SOX11-negative, IGHV mutated, with an indolent disease course [p.222][10]. This variant most commonly presents with peripheral blood, bone marrow, and splenic involvement, as well as low tumor burden and Ki-67 proliferation index <10% [p.222][10,12,13]. Some presentations have gastrointestinal (GI) or blood/bone marrow involvement only, which may express SOX11 [p.222].

### In Situ Mantle Cell Neoplasia (ISMCN)
ISMCN is characterized by preservation of lymph node architecture and presence of cyclin D1-positive B cells restricted to the mantle zones with minimal expansion [p.222][14,15]. An unusual presentation with scattering of cyclin D1-positive cells in germinal centers (but not mantle zones) of a lymph node specimen retrospectively evaluated several years prior to symptomatic MCL diagnosis has been reported [p.222][16].

Development of overt MCL in patients with ISMCN has been reported, although this appears very uncommon [p.223][15]. The occurrence of ISMCN in studies of reactive lymph nodes from patients without lymphoma history is very rare, and significance or malignancy potential of ISMCN in patients without known MCL remains uncertain [p.223][15,17]. These instances appear to have very indolent disease course with long-term survival even without treatment intervention [p.223][15].

## Diagnosis

The diagnosis is established by histologic examination combined with adequate immunophenotyping using immunohistochemistry (IHC) or flow cytometry [p.223]. Typical immunophenotype of MCL is CD5+, CD10-/+, CD20+, CD23-/+, CD43+, and cyclin D1+ [p.223]. Occasionally, MCL may be CD5- or CD23+ [p.223].

Currently available reagents for cyclin D1 evaluation by IHC yield good staining [p.223]. In rare instances of cyclin D1 and t(11;14) negative MCL, overexpression of cyclin D2 or cyclin D3 may be observed [p.223][7,21]. However, IHC for cyclin D2 or cyclin D3 is not helpful in establishing cyclin D1-negative MCL diagnosis, as these proteins are expressed in other B-cell malignancies [p.223].

SOX11 is positive in most patients with MCL, except in the indolent subtype, regardless of cyclin D1 expression level [p.223]. IHC for SOX11 or IGHV gene sequencing potentially aid in differentiating cyclin D1-negative MCL from other B-cell lymphomas [p.223][22-24].

In certain circumstances, fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) for CCND2 and CCND3 rearrangements or t(11;14) can be helpful for diagnosing cyclin D1-negative MCL [p.223][6,25]. FISH for t(14;18) and FISH panel for chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL) may be useful to distinguish cyclin D1-negative MCL from CLL [p.223].

### Prognostic Markers

Ki-67 proliferation index is an independent predictor of outcome in patients with advanced-stage MCL treated with chemoimmunotherapy [p.223][26-31]. Ki-67 proliferation index <30% has been associated with more favorable prognosis [p.223]. Ki-67 has been integrated into the MCL International Prognostic Index (MIPI) for risk stratification of patients with advanced-stage disease in clinical trials evaluating chemoimmunotherapy and high-dose therapy with autologous stem cell rescue (HDT/ASCR) [p.223][32-35]. Ki-67 should be included in the IHC panel for initial diagnostic workup, but should not be used to guide treatment decisions at this time [p.223].

Genetic aberrations involving the TP53 gene are associated with poor prognosis [p.223].

## Workup

The initial workup should include: thorough physical examination with attention to node-bearing areas, evaluation of performance status and constitutional symptoms, and laboratory assessments (complete blood count [CBC] with differential, comprehensive metabolic panel, serum lactate dehydrogenase [LDH]) [p.224].

Patients with high tumor burden and elevated LDH should be assessed for spontaneous tumor lysis syndrome (TLS), including measurements of uric acid level [p.224]. Measurement of serum beta-2-microglobulin levels may be useful in some circumstances [p.224]. Testing for hepatitis B virus (HBV) should be performed for all patients due to risk of viral reactivation in patients receiving chemoimmunotherapy [p.224]. Testing for hepatitis C virus (HCV) may be useful under certain circumstances [p.224].

MCL is a systemic disease with frequent bone marrow involvement; bone marrow biopsy with or without aspirate will be useful in certain circumstances [p.224]. PET/CT scan (preferred) or chest/abdomen/pelvis CT with contrast of diagnostic quality are recommended as part of initial diagnostic workup if systemic therapy is planned [p.224]. CT scan of the neck with contrast may be useful in selected circumstances [p.224]. In patients with blastic variant or those presenting with central nervous system (CNS) symptoms, lumbar puncture should be performed to evaluate cerebral spinal fluid for potential disease involvement [p.224]. MUGA scan or echocardiogram is recommended for patients being considered for treatment regimens containing anthracyclines or anthracenediones [p.224].

### Gastrointestinal Involvement

GI involvement has been reported in 15% to 30% of patients with MCL [p.224]. In two prospective studies, the frequency of GI tract involvement in patients with MCL was higher than literature reports [p.224][36,37]. In the Romaguera et al study, MCL was histologically present in lower and upper GI tract in 88% and 43% of patients respectively, and 26% of patients presented with GI symptoms at diagnosis [p.224][36]. Despite high frequency of GI tract involvement (primarily at microscopic level), endoscopy with biopsies led to changes in clinical management in only 4% of patients [p.224][36]. Salar et al reported upper or lower GI tract involvement in 38% and 54% of patients respectively at diagnosis [p.224][30].

The NCCN Guidelines Panel does not recommend endoscopy or colonoscopy as part of routine initial workup, but suggests it may be useful in certain circumstances [p.224]. However, endoscopic or colonoscopic evaluation of the GI tract is necessary for confirmation of stage I-II disease and for response assessment to initial therapy [p.224].

## Treatment Options for Stage I-II MCL

Few patients present with localized MCL and available published literature on localized disease management is retrospective and anecdotal [p.224]. In a retrospective analysis of 26 patients with limited bulk, early-stage (stage IA or IIA) MCL, radiation therapy (RT) with or without chemotherapy was associated with significantly improved progression-free survival (PFS) at 5 years (68% vs. 11%; P = .002) and a trend towards improved overall survival (OS) [p.224][38].

Less aggressive chemoimmunotherapy with or without involved-site RT (ISRT) is included as an option for induction therapy [p.224]. Involved-site RT (30-36 Gy) alone is an appropriate treatment option for patients with stage I or contiguous stage II, non-bulky disease [p.224]. In highly selected patients with asymptomatic disease (noncontiguous stage II, non-bulky), close observation with deferred therapy is a reasonable option, especially for those with good performance status and lower risk scores on standard International Prognostic Index (IPI) [p.224][39,40]. These recommendations are based on treatment principles in the absence of more definitive clinical data [p.224].
