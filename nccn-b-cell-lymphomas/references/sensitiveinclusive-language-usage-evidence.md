## Language Standards and Principles

NCCN Guidelines endeavor to use language that is person-first, non-stigmatizing, and inclusive of anti-racist, anti-classist, anti-misogynist, anti-ageist, anti-ableist, and anti-weight-biased perspectives [p.144]. Guidelines incorporate non-gendered language, instead focusing on organ-specific recommendations for greater accuracy and inclusivity [p.144]. When citing statistics or data from external sources that do not use inclusive terms, NCCN Guidelines will continue to use the terms men, women, female, and male as provided by those sources [p.144].

Most studies do not report how sex and gender data are collected and use these terms interchangeably or inconsistently [p.144]. If sources do not differentiate gender from sex assigned at birth or organs present, the information is presumed to predominantly represent cisgender individuals [p.144]. NCCN encourages researchers to collect more specific data in future studies and organizations to use more inclusive and accurate language in their future analyses [p.144].

## Classification Framework

The 2001 World Health Organization (WHO) Classification of Hematopoietic and Lymphoid Neoplasms represented the first international consensus on classification of hematologic malignancies [p.144]. Classification subdivides lymphoid neoplasms based on cell lineage (B, T, or NK), distinguishing precursor versus mature lymphomas [p.144]. Classification was further refined based on clinical, cytogenetic, and molecular features to define specific lymphoma subtypes [p.144].

Cytogenetic features detected by karyotype or fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) and molecular analysis are increasingly important in defining specific subtypes [p.144]. Detection of viruses, particularly Epstein-Barr virus (EBV), HHV8, and HTLV1, is often necessary to establish a specific diagnosis [p.144]. The WHO Classification was revised in 2008, 2017, and 2022 to include new disease entities and better define heterogeneous and ambiguous subtypes, based on the evolving genetic and molecular landscape of B-cell lymphomas [p.144].[8-10]

In 2022, the newly revised WHO Classification of Hematolymphoid Tumors (WHO5) and the International Consensus Classification (ICC) were published [p.144].[10,11] Both the ICC and WHO5 classify lymphomas based on morphology, clinical features, cell lineage (immunophenotype), and cytogenetic and molecular features [p.144]. There are differences between the two classifications in terms of nomenclature and diagnostic criteria [p.144].

## Diagnosis

An accurate pathologic diagnosis of the subtype is the most important first step in the management of B-cell lymphomas [p.144]. The basic pathologic evaluation is the same for each subtype, although some further evaluation may be useful in certain circumstances to clarify a particular diagnosis [p.144].

### Biopsy Approach

An excisional or incisional lymph node biopsy is preferred for the definitive diagnosis and histologic grading of B-cell lymphomas [p.144-145]. Fine-needle aspiration (FNA) biopsy alone is insufficient for the initial diagnosis or histologic grading of lymphoma but can be a valuable method for the initial screening of enlarged lymph nodes [p.144-145].[12-15]

Core needle biopsy (multiple biopsies preferred) is an appropriate alternative to excisional or incisional biopsy, especially when a clinical situation dictates that this is the only safe means of obtaining diagnostic tissue (when a lymph node is not easily accessible for excisional or incisional biopsy or if surgical biopsy would cause significant morbidity or substantial treatment delays) [p.145]. In such instances, a combination of core needle biopsy (multiple biopsies preferred) and FNA biopsies in conjunction with appropriate ancillary techniques (immunohistochemistry [IHC], flow cytometry, karyotype or fluorescence in-situ hybridization [FISH], and molecular studies) may be sufficient for the differential diagnosis [p.145].[16-20]

Next-generation sequencing (NGS) can be used if a high suspicion of a clonal process remains and other techniques have not resulted in a clear identification of a clonal process [p.145].

Hematopathology review of all slides (with at least one paraffin block representative of the tumor) is recommended [p.145]. Rebiopsy of lymph node (preferably the most fluorodeoxyglucose [FDG]-avid, accessible lymph node) should be done if consult material is nondiagnostic [p.145].

### Immunophenotyping and Genetic Analysis

Immunophenotyping is essential for the differentiation of various subtypes of B-cell lymphomas to establish the proper diagnosis [p.145]. It can be performed by flow cytometry and/or IHC; the choice depends on the antigens as well as the expertise and resources available to the hematopathologist [p.145].[21]

Cytogenetic or molecular genetic analysis may be necessary under certain circumstances to identify chromosomal rearrangements or gene mutations that are characteristic of some subtypes of B-cell lymphomas or to establish clonality [p.145].

The NCCN Guidelines Panel developed a series of algorithms for the use of immunophenotyping and genetic testing to provide guidance for surgical pathologists and clinicians in the interpretation of pathology reports [p.145]. These algorithms should be used in conjunction with clinical and pathologic correlation [p.145].

## Workup Procedures

### Essential Workup

Essential workup procedures include a complete physical exam with particular attention to node-bearing areas and the size of liver and spleen, symptoms present, performance status, and laboratory studies including complete blood count (CBC), serum lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), hepatitis B virus (HBV) testing, comprehensive metabolic panel, and chest/abdomen/pelvis CT with oral and intravenous contrast (unless coexistent renal insufficiency) [p.145]. Multigated acquisition (MUGA) scan or echocardiograms are recommended when anthracyclines and anthracenedione-containing regimens are used [p.145].

### Hepatitis B Virus Testing

HBV reactivation (resulting in liver failure and death) has been reported with anti-CD20 mAb-based regimens [p.145].[22] HBV carriers with lymphoid malignancies have a high risk of HBV reactivation and disease, especially those treated with anti-CD20 mAb-based regimens [p.145].[23] The Panel has included testing for hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg) and hepatitis B core antibody (HBcAb) as part of essential workup prior to initiation of treatment in all patients who will receive anti-CD20 mAb-based regimens [p.145].

### Hepatitis C Virus Testing

Large population-based or multicenter case-control studies have demonstrated a strong association between seropositivity for hepatitis C virus (HCV) and the development of B-cell lymphomas [p.145].[24-31] The prevalence of HCV seropositivity was consistently increased among patients with DLBCL and MZL [p.145].[24,25,29,30] HCV testing is needed in patients with high-risk disease and in patients with splenic MZL [p.145].

### Optional Workup

Optional workup procedures (depending on specific lymphoma type) include beta-2-microglobulin, CT or PET/CT scans, endoscopic ultrasound, head CT, or brain MRI and lumbar puncture to analyze cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) [p.145]. Discussion of fertility issues and sperm banking should be addressed in the appropriate circumstances [p.145].[32]

## PET Scan Utilization

### Staging

PET has a high positivity and specificity when used for the staging and restaging of lymphoma [p.146].[33] PET is nearly universally positive at diagnosis in DLBCL, FL, and nodal MZL, but less sensitive for extranodal MZL [p.146].[34,35] However, a number of benign conditions including sarcoid, infection, and inflammation can result in false-positive PET scans, complicating the interpretation [p.146]. Lesions less than 1 cm are not reliably visualized with PET scans [p.146].

Although PET scans may detect additional disease sites at diagnosis, the clinical stage is modified only in 15% to 20% of patients and a change in treatment in only 8% of patients [p.146].

The combined PET/CT scans have distinct advantages in both staging and restaging compared to full-dose diagnostic CT or PET alone [p.146].[36,37] In a retrospective study, PET/CT performed with low-dose non-contrast-enhanced CT was found to be more sensitive and specific than contrast-enhanced CT for the evaluation of lymph node and organ involvement in patients with Hodgkin lymphoma or high-grade B-cell lymphomas [p.146].[36] Preliminary results of a prospective study (47 patients; patients who had undergone prior diagnostic CT were excluded) also showed a good correlation between low-dose non-contrast-enhanced PET/CT and full-dose contrast-enhanced PET/CT for the evaluation of lymph nodes and extranodal disease in lymphomas [p.146].[37]

PET/CT should be done with contrast-enhanced diagnostic CT and is recommended for initial staging and restaging of all fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG)-avid lymphomas [p.146].[38,39] PET/CT is particularly important for staging before consideration of radiation therapy (RT), and baseline PET/CT will aid in the interpretation of post-treatment response evaluation based on the 5-point scale (5-PS) [p.146].[39]

### Response Assessment

The guidelines for response criteria for lymphoma, first published in 1999 by the International Working Group (IWG), were revised in 2007 by the International Harmonization Project to incorporate IHC, flow cytometry, and PET scans in the definition of response for lymphoma [p.146].[40,41] In the revised guidelines, the response category of complete response uncertain (CRu) was essentially eliminated and response is categorized as complete response (CR), partial response (PR), stable disease (SD), and relapsed disease or progressive disease (PD) based on the result of a PET scan [p.146].

In 2014, revised response criteria, known as the Lugano response criteria, were introduced for response assessment using PET/CT scans according to the 5-PS [p.146].[38,39] The 5-PS is based on the visual assessment of FDG uptake in the involved sites relative to that of the mediastinum and the liver [p.146].[42-44]

A score of 1 denotes no abnormal FDG avidity, while a score of 2 represents uptake less than the mediastinum [p.146]. A score of 3 denotes uptake greater than the mediastinum but less than the liver, while scores of 4 and 5 denote uptake greater than the liver, and greater than the liver with new sites of disease, respectively [p.146]. Different clinical trials have considered scores of either 1 to 2 or 1 to 3 to be PET-negative, but a score of 1 to 3 is now widely considered to be PET negative [p.146]. Scores of 4 to 5 are universally considered PET positive [p.146]. A score of 4 on an interim or end-of-treatment restaging scan may be consistent with a PR if the FDG avidity has declined from initial staging, while a score of 5 denotes PD [p.146].

The application of PET/CT to response assessment is limited to FDG-avid lymphomas and the revised response criteria have thus far only been validated for DLBCL and Hodgkin lymphoma [p.146-147].[38,39,40,41] The application of the revised response criteria to other histologies requires validation and the original IWG guidelines should be used [p.147].[40,41] False-positive PET scans may be observed related to infectious or inflammatory conditions [p.147]. Biopsy of affected sites remains the gold standard for confirming new or persistent disease at end of treatment (EOT) [p.147].

## Principles of Radiation Therapy

RT can be delivered with photons, electrons, or protons, depending upon clinical circumstances [p.147].[45] Advanced RT techniques emphasize tightly conformal doses and steep gradients next to organs at risk (OAR) [p.147]. Therefore, accurate target delineation and careful monitoring of treatment delivery are critical to avoid the risk of underdosing the clinical target volume (CTV) and subsequent decrease in tumor control [p.147]. Image guidance may be required to facilitate accurate treatment delivery [p.147].

### Dose Reduction to Organs at Risk

Preliminary results from single-institution studies have shown that significant dose reduction to OAR (including lungs, heart, breasts, kidneys, spinal cord, esophagus, carotid artery, bone marrow, stomach, muscle, soft tissue, and salivary glands) can be achieved with advanced RT planning and delivery techniques such as 4D-CT simulation, image-guided RT, intensity-modulated RT (IMRT)/volumetric modulated arc therapy (VMAT), and proton therapy [p.147].[46-54] In mediastinal and abdominal lymphoma, the use of respiratory motion management techniques such as respiratory gating or breath-hold techniques, and image-guided RT have been shown to decrease incidental dose to OAR such lung, heart, liver, and kidneys [p.147].[55-58]

The use of advanced RT and motion management techniques offer significant and clinically relevant advantages in specific instances to spare OAR and reduce the risk of late complications from normal tissue damage [p.147]. This is especially important for patients being treated with curative intent or who have long life expectancies following therapy [p.147].

Randomized prospective studies to test these concepts are unlikely to be done since these techniques are designed to decrease late effects, which usually develop greater than or equal to 10 years after completion of treatment [p.147]. Therefore, the guidelines recommend that RT delivery techniques that are found to best reduce RT dose to OAR in a clinically meaningful manner without compromising target coverage should be considered [p.147].

### Involved-Site Radiation Therapy (ISRT)

Involved-site RT (ISRT) is recommended as the appropriate field for B-cell lymphomas as it limits the radiation exposure to adjacent uninvolved organs (such as lungs, bone, muscle, or kidney) when lymphadenopathy regresses following chemotherapy, thus minimizing the potential long-term complications [p.147].[59,60] ISRT targets the initially involved nodal and extranodal sites detectable at presentation [p.147].[59,60] Larger RT fields utilizing elective nodal irradiation should be considered for limited-stage indolent B-cell lymphomas, often treated with RT alone [p.147].[59]

Treatment planning for ISRT requires the use of CT-based simulation [p.147]. The incorporation of additional imaging techniques such as PET and MRI often improves the accuracy of treatment planning [p.147]. The OAR should be outlined for optimizing treatment plan decisions [p.147]. The treatment plan is designed using conventional, 3D conformal, or IMRT/VMAT techniques using clinical treatment planning considerations of coverage and dose reductions for OAR [p.147].[59]

The principles of ISRT are similar for both nodal and extranodal disease [p.147]. The gross tumor volume (GTV) defined by radiologic imaging prior to biopsy, chemotherapy, or surgery provides the basis for determining the CTV [p.147].[61] Possible movement of the target by respiration as determined by 4D-CT or fluoroscopy should also influence the final CTV [p.147]. Larger CTV should be considered for the treatment of early-stage indolent B-cell [p.147].
