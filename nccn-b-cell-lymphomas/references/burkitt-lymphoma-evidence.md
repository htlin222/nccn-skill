## Summary of Key Evidence

- Burkitt Lymphoma (BL) is a rare aggressive B-cell lymphoma with extranodal involvement and improved survival in recent years [p.314]. Five-year survival increased from 43% in pre-2002 patients to 56% in 2002-2008 cohort (1922 patients from NCI SEER database) [p.314].
- Three clinical variants exist: endemic, sporadic, and immunodeficiency-associated BL [p.314]. Sporadic BL accounts for 1-2% of adult lymphomas in United States and Western Europe [p.314].
- Characterized by MYC gene rearrangement: t(8;14) in 80% of patients, with variants t(2;8) and t(8:22) in remaining 20% [p.314].
- Approximately 60% of adult patients with BL may achieve durable remission [p.314].
- Addition of rituximab to multiagent chemotherapy has improved survival outcomes [p.315].

## Diagnosis

**Immunophenotyping and Pathology** [p.315]

Adequate immunophenotyping using immunohistochemistry (IHC) with or without flow cytometry is essential for BL diagnosis [p.315]. Typical immunophenotype: sIg+, CD10+, CD19+, CD20+, CD22+, TdT-, Ki-67+ (>95%), BCL2-, BCL6+, with simple karyotype containing MYC rearrangement without BCL2 or BCL6 rearrangement [p.315].

IHC panel should include: CD3, CD10, CD20, CD45, TdT, Ki-67, BCL2, and BCL6 [p.315]. Flow cytometry should include: CD5, CD10, CD19, CD20, CD45, TDT, and kappa/lambda markers [p.315].

Fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) for detection of t(8;14) or variants, BCL2, and BCL6 rearrangements should be performed in all patients; MYC::IGH probe recommended [p.315].

EBV-encoded RNA in situ hybridization (EBER-ISH) may be useful to evaluate EBV infection status, although treatment is identical for EBV-positive and EBV-negative cases [p.315].

## Workup

**Initial Diagnostic Evaluation** [p.315-316]

Initial diagnostic workup includes detailed physical examination (with special attention to node-bearing areas, liver, and spleen), chest/abdomen/pelvis CT with contrast of diagnostic quality, and/or PET/CT scan [p.315]. PET/CT scans may identify extranodal sites not visible on CT; however, therapy initiation should not be delayed for PET/CT [p.315]. Multigated acquisition (MUGA) scan or echocardiogram is recommended, particularly for older patients receiving anthracycline-based regimens [p.315].

**Laboratory and Special Assessments** [p.315-316]

Adult patients commonly present with bulky abdominal masses, B symptoms, and laboratory evidence of tumor lysis [p.315]. Bone marrow involvement occurs in ≤70% and leptomeningeal CNS involvement in ≤40% of patients at diagnosis [p.315]. Lumbar puncture and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) flow cytometry are essential; brain MRI and bone marrow aspirate/biopsy may be useful in certain circumstances [p.315].

Serum lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) level has prognostic significance in these highly aggressive lymphomas [p.315-316].

**Prognostic Indices** [p.316]

BL-International Prognostic Index (based on age ≥40 years, performance status ≥2, elevated serum LDH >3 times upper limit of normal, and CNS involvement) stratifies patients into three risk groups [p.316]. Estimated 3-year PFS rates were 92%, 72%, and 53%, respectively for low, intermediate, and high-risk groups; corresponding 3-year OS rates were 96%, 76%, and 59% [p.316].

BL-IPI validation in prospective DA-EPOCH-R trial (113 patients): 5-year EFS rates were 90% and 67% for low-/intermediate-risk versus high-risk disease [p.316].

**Infectious Disease Screening** [p.316]

HIV serology should be part of diagnostic workup as BL can be HIV-associated [p.316]. Hepatitis B virus (HBV) testing is recommended prior to anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody treatment due to increased reactivation risk [p.316].

## First-Line Therapy Evidence

**General Principles** [p.315-317]

BL is curable in significant subset with dose-intensive, multiagent chemotherapy incorporating CNS prophylaxis [p.316]. About 60-90% of pediatric and young adult patients achieve durable remission [p.316]. However, older adults show less favorable survival compared with younger patients [p.316].

Most contemporary adult regimens developed from pediatric protocols include intensive multiagent chemoimmunotherapy with CNS prophylaxis using systemic and/or intrathecal chemotherapy [p.316]. Addition of rituximab to multiagent chemotherapy improved survival [p.316].

**Risk Stratification for Treatment Selection** [p.318-319]

Low-risk disease defined as: normal serum LDH and stage I disease (single extraabdominal mass <10 cm), OR completely resected abdominal lesion [p.318]. All other patients considered high-risk (stage I with abdominal mass, or extra-abdominal mass >10 cm, or stage II-IV) [p.318].

Adequate CNS prophylaxis with high-dose systemic methotrexate and intrathecal methotrexate and/or cytarabine recommended with all regimens [p.318].

### CODOX-M/IVAC (Cyclophosphamide, Vincristine, Doxorubicin, High-Dose Methotrexate alternating with Ifosfamide, Etoposide, High-Dose Cytarabine)

**Original CODOX-M/IVAC** [p.316-317]

CODOX-M/IVAC is highly effective with 1-year EFS rate of 85% in pediatric and adult patients with previously untreated BL or Burkitt-like lymphoma [p.316]. Patients with high-risk disease received 4 alternating treatments (ABAB) of CODOX-M and IVAC; low-risk disease received 3 cycles of CODOX-M; both included intrathecal chemotherapy for CNS prophylaxis [p.316].

**Modified CODOX-M/IVAC (With Rituximab)** [p.317]

International phase II study (n=52; 12 low-risk, 40 high-risk) with modified CODOX-M/IVAC (vincristine dose capped at 2 mg) + rituximab: Overall 2-year EFS and OS rates were 65% and 73%, respectively [p.317]. For low-risk patients: 2-year EFS 83%, OS 81%; for high-risk: 2-year EFS 60%, OS 70% [p.317].

Lowering systemic methotrexate dose to 3 g/m² (from 7 g/m²) maintained efficacy with decreased toxicity: 2-year PFS 62-64%, 2-year OS 71% [p.317].

Small study (n=15) of modified CODOX-M/IVAC with or without rituximab showed 5-year PFS and OS rates of 87% [p.317].

Retrospective study (n=80) of CODOX-M/IVAC + rituximab: 3-year PFS 74%, OS 77% with rituximab versus 3-year PFS 61%, OS 66% without (trend for improvement but not statistically significant) [p.317].

Phase II trial (n=38, high-risk BL with IPI score 3-5; 15% with CNS involvement) with CODOX-M/IVAC + rituximab: 2-year PFS 77%, OS 81% [p.317].

### HyperCVAD (Hyperfractionated Cyclophosphamide, Vincristine, Doxorubicin, Dexamethasone alternating with Methotrexate and Cytarabine)

**R-hyperCVAD Long-Term Outcomes** [p.317-318]

Retrospective analysis (n=79; BL n=54, HGBL n=25; 25% aged ≥60 years; 73% bone marrow involvement, 28% CNS involvement) with R-hyperCVAD: Complete response rate 91% overall (96% for BL patients) [p.317].

After median follow-up of 50 months for BL patients: 5-year OS 55%, RFS 58% [p.317]. Cumulative incidence of relapse (CIR) 21% overall (14% for BL); higher in patients with bone marrow or CNS involvement (27% and 42%, respectively) [p.317].

5-year CNS CIR was 6% overall (4% for BL); CNS relapses were 16% in patients with baseline CNS involvement [p.317]. R-hyperCVAD effective in preventing CNS relapse, especially in high-risk disease [p.317].

### Dose-Adjusted EPOCH (Etoposide, Prednisone, Vincristine, Cyclophosphamide, Doxorubicin) + Rituximab

**DA-EPOCH-R in Non-HIV Patients and SC-EPOCH-RR in HIV Patients** [p.317]

Prospective study comparing DA-EPOCH-R in non-HIV patients (n=19) and short course EPOCH with dose-dense rituximab (SC-EPOCH-RR) in HIV patients (n=11): Median follow-up 86 months with DA-EPOCH-R showing freedom-from-progression (FFP) 95%, OS 100% [p.317]. Results may reflect higher proportion of low-risk disease (approximately 53% with normal LDH) [p.317].

**Risk-Adapted DA-EPOCH-RR Trial** [p.317-318]

Multicenter phase II trial (n=113; 87% high-risk disease; stage ≥III, ECOG PS ≥2, elevated LDH, tumor ≥7 cm; 28 HIV-positive; 62% aged ≥40 years; 26% with bone marrow/CSF involvement) with risk-adapted DA-EPOCH-RR [p.318]:

- Low-risk patients (Stage ≤II, ECOG PS ≤1, normal LDH, tumor <7 cm): 2 cycles DA-EPOCH-RR without intrathecal therapy followed by PET scan; one additional cycle if interim PET negative; 4 additional cycles if interim PET positive [p.318]
- High-risk patients with no CNS involvement: 6 cycles DA-EPOCH-RR without intrathecal therapy [p.318]

After median follow-up of 59 months: EFS 85%, OS 87% for entire population [p.318]. High-risk patients (n=98): 4-year EFS 82%, OS 85% [p.318].

CSF involvement associated with lower EFS (4-year EFS 46% vs. 90% without CSF involvement) [p.318]. Bone marrow or peripheral blood involvement in high-risk disease associated with lower EFS (4-year EFS 67% vs. 92%) [p.318]. CNS relapses occurred in only 2% of high-risk patients without baseline CSF involvement [p.318].

**Comparative Trial: CODOX-M/IVAC vs. DA-EPOCH-R** [p.318]

Phase III randomized trial comparing R-CODOX-M/R-IVAC (n=46) and DA-EPOCH-R (n=43) in high-risk BL without CNS involvement; median follow-up 29 months: 2-year PFS 70% vs. 76%, respectively [p.318]. DA-EPOCH-R associated with fewer side effects (infection rate 34% vs. 56%) [p.318]. DA-EPOCH-R is appropriate option for high-risk BL without CNS involvement in patients unable to tolerate aggressive regimens [p.318].

### NCCN Treatment Recommendations for Induction

**Low-Risk BL** [p.319]

- CODOX-M (original or modified) + rituximab (3 cycles), OR
- DA-EPOCH-RR (3 cycles for CR; 6 cycles for PR), OR
- R-hyperCVAD
- For patients <60 years [p.319]

- DA-EPOCH-RR (3 cycles for CR; 6 cycles for PR) also appropriate for patients ≥60 years [p.319]

**High-Risk BL** [p.319]

- CODOX-M/IVAC (original or modified) with rituximab, OR
- R-hyperCVAD
- For patients <60 years [p.319]

- DA-EPOCH-R (6 cycles) appropriate for any age patient with high-risk BL unable to tolerate aggressive regimens [p.319]

Patients with symptomatic CNS disease should start with systemic therapy portion containing CNS-penetrating drugs [p.319].

### Consolidation Therapy

**Autologous HCT Not Recommended** [p.319]

Studies evaluating autologous HCT as consolidation in untreated BL patients in remission showed 5-year EFS 73-78% and 5-year OS 81-83% [p.319]. However, real-world analyses reported comparable survival without autologous HCT consolidation [p.319].

Real-world analysis of 641 BL patients treated with CODOX-M/IVAC, hyperCVAD, or DA-EPOCH-R without autologous HCT: At median follow-up of 45 months, 3-year PFS 64%, OS 70% [p.319]. Predictors of poor survival: age ≥40 years, elevated LDH, ECOG PS ≥2, CNS involvement [p.319].

Presence of 0 or 1 risk factor associated with excellent prognosis: 3-year PFS 91% and 73%, respectively; corresponding 3-year OS 95% and 77% [p.319].

Consolidation with autologous HCT not recommended for patients in remission after induction therapy [p.319].

**Follow-Up After CR** [p.319]

Patients with CR to induction therapy should be followed every 2-3 months for 1 year, then every 3 months for next 1 year, then every 6 months thereafter [p.319]. Disease relapse after 2 years is rare following CR; follow-up should be individualized [p.319].

**Non-CR Management** [p.319-320]

Patients with less than CR to induction therapy should be treated in clinical trial context; in absence of suitable trials, involved-site radiation therapy (ISRT) may be considered [p.320].

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

**General Approach** [p.320]

Patients with relapsed or refractory disease should be treated in clinical trial context [p.320].

**Outcomes by Timing of Relapse** [p.320]

Among relapsed/refractory patients, superior outcomes occur in those with disease relapse ≥6 months after first-line therapy with chemosensitive disease (CR to second-line therapy) [p.320].

For disease relapse >6 to 18 months after first-line therapy: second-line chemoimmunotherapy followed by consolidation with high-dose therapy and autologous stem cell rescue (HDT/ASCR) or allogeneic HCT (if donor available) recommended for patients achieving second-line response [p.320].

CIBMTR database analysis (n=241) showed autologous HCT resulted in 5-year PFS 44% for patients in second remission [p.320].

Earlier CIBMTR retrospective analysis in children/adolescents with relapsed BL (n=41, aged ≤18 years) showed similar 5-year EFS between autologous and allogeneic HCT (27% vs. 31%) [p.320]. EFS rates were lower in non-CR patients at transplant, though vast majority of relapsed BL patients lack chemotherapy-sensitive disease [p.320].

### Second-Line Chemotherapy Options [p.320]

The following are options for second-line therapy [p.320]:
- DA-EPOCH-R
- R-IVAC (Ifosfamide, carboplatin, etoposide combined with rituximab)
- R-GDP (Gemcitabine, dexamethasone, cisplatin combined with rituximab)
- R-ICE (Ifosfamide, carboplatin, etoposide combined with rituximab)
- High-dose cytarabine + rituximab

Treatment options remain undefined for relapsed/refractory disease; these suggestions based on very limited, retrospective studies with only a few patients [p.320].

**Progression/Early Relapse Management** [p.320]

Clinical trial or best supportive care including palliative ISRT recommended for patients with disease not responding to second-line therapy, progressive disease, or disease relapse <6 months after first-line therapy [p.320].

## Special Considerations

**Age and Treatment** [p.316]

Survival of older adults with BL appears less favorable compared with younger patients [p.316]. Although SEER database shows BL diagnosed in 60% of adults aged >40 years (about 30% aged >60 years), this population is underrepresented in published clinical trials [p.316].

**Tumor Lysis Syndrome** [p.318]

Tumor lysis syndrome more common in BL and should be managed per Supportive Care section guidelines [p.318].

**Treatment Setting** [p.318]

Treatment at centers with expertise in BL management is preferred [p.318]. Participation in clinical trials recommended for all patients [p.318].

**EBV and Other Associated Infections** [p.314-315]

Endemic BL is most common childhood malignancy in equatorial Africa; majority associated with Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) [p.314]. Sporadic BL associated with EBV infection in about 30% of patients [p.314]. Biology of EBV-positive and EBV-negative BL may differ, although treatment approaches are identical at present [p.314].

**HGBL with 11q Aberration** [p.314]

Burkitt-like lymphoma with 11q aberration (2017 classification) renamed large B-cell lymphoma (LBCL) with 11q aberration (ICC), or HGBL with 11q aberration (WHO5) [p.314]. Characterized by 11q gene deregulation but lacks MYC rearrangements [p.314]. Optimum management undefined; most often treated like BL [p.314].
