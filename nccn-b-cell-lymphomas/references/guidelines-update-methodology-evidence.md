## Summary of Key Evidence

- Non-Hodgkin lymphomas (NHL) are a heterogeneous group of lymphoproliferative disorders originating in B lymphocytes, T lymphocytes, or natural killer (NK) cells [p.143][1]
- In 2025, an estimated 80,350 people will be diagnosed with NHL with approximately 19,390 deaths due to the disease [p.143][1]
- The major B-cell lymphoma subtypes diagnosed in the United States (1998-2011) were: Diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL; 32%), follicular lymphoma (FL; 17%), marginal zone lymphoma (MZL; 8%), and mantle cell lymphoma (MCL; 4%) [p.143][2]
- The incidence of NHL has declined worldwide, and mortality rates have decreased since 2000 with the introduction of anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody rituximab [p.143][3][4]
- Higher mortality rates persist in regions with limited resources, related to prevalence of underlying risk factors and access to diagnostic techniques and treatment approaches [p.143][3]

## Special Considerations

### HIV-Related B-Cell Lymphomas

- NHL (along with Kaposi sarcoma and lung cancer) are the most common cancer types diagnosed in people with HIV in the United States, with largest declines in incidence rates projected for NHL and KS through 2030 [p.143][5][6]
- DLBCL, Burkitt lymphoma (BL), and primary central nervous system lymphoma (PCNSL) are the most common subtypes of B-cell lymphomas in people with HIV [p.143][7]

### Guideline Scope

The NCCN Guidelines for B-Cell Lymphomas cover diagnostic workup, treatment, and surveillance strategies for the following subtypes [p.143]:

- Follicular lymphoma (FL)
- Marginal zone lymphomas (MZLs):
  - Extranodal marginal zone lymphoma (EMZL) of the stomach
  - EMZL of non-gastric sites (noncutaneous)
  - Nodal MZL
  - Splenic MZL
- Mantle cell lymphoma (MCL)
- Diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL)
- ALK-positive large B-cell lymphomas
- Mediastinal gray zone lymphomas (MGZL)
- Primary mediastinal large B-cell lymphoma (PMBL)
- Histologic transformation of indolent lymphomas to DLBCL
- High-grade B-cell lymphomas (HGBL)
- Burkitt lymphoma (BL)
- HIV-related B-cell lymphomas
- Post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorders (PTLD)

### Global Disease Burden

In 2020, an estimated 544,000 people were diagnosed with NHL worldwide, with approximately 260,000 deaths from the disease [p.143][3]
