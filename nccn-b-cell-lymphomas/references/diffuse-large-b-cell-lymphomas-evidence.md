## Summary of Key Evidence

- Diffuse large B-cell lymphomas (DLBCL) are the most common lymphoid neoplasms in adults, representing approximately 30% of non-Hodgkin lymphomas (NHL) diagnosed annually [p.251]
- DLBCL, not otherwise specified (NOS) encompasses all nodal and extranodal large B-cell lymphomas (LBCL) that do not belong to specific diagnostic categories [p.251]
- Gene expression profiling (GEP) identifies two distinct subtypes based on cell of origin (COO): germinal center B-cell (GCB) subtype and activated B-cell (ABC) subtype, with GCB DLBCL associated with improved outcomes [p.251]
- Classification of DLBCL based on COO is retained in both the International Consensus Classification (ICC) and WHO Classification of Hematolymphoid Tumors (WHO5) [p.251]
- LBCL with IRF4 rearrangement is recognized as a definite entity in ICC and WHO5 and should be managed as DLBCL [p.251]
- Multiple DLBCL variants including EBV-positive DLBCL NOS, intravascular LBCL, and T-cell/histiocyte-rich LBCL are managed according to DLBCL algorithms [p.251]

## Diagnostic Classification and Subtype Recognition

### Molecular Subtypes [p.251]

Gene expression profiling (GEP) has identified distinct subtypes within DLBCL, NOS based on cell of origin (COO):

- **Germinal center B-cell (GCB) subtype**: Associated with improved clinical outcomes compared to ABC subtype [p.251]
- **Activated B-cell (ABC) subtype**: Associated with inferior outcomes [p.251]

Additional subtypes with distinct clinical characteristics have been identified through molecular profiling [p.251]

### Special DLBCL Variants

**LBCL with IRF4 Rearrangement** [p.251]
- Recognized as a definite entity in both ICC and WHO5 classifications
- Should be managed as DLBCL [p.251]

**LBCL with 11q Aberration** [p.251]
- Previously designated as "Burkitt-like lymphoma with 11q aberration" (provisional entity in 2017 WHO classification)
- Now called LBCL with 11q aberration (ICC) or high-grade B-cell lymphoma (HGBL) with 11q aberration (WHO5) [p.251]
- Genetically distinct from Burkitt lymphoma (BL) despite morphologic and clinical similarities [p.251]
- LEF1 is not expressed in LBCL/HGBL with 11q aberration but is uniformly expressed in BL, helping distinguish these entities [p.251]
- Optimal management is uncertain; some evidence suggests less aggressive behavior than BL, though often treated similarly [p.251]

**EBV-Positive DLBCL, NOS** [p.251]
- Aggressive lymphoma associated with poor prognosis [p.251]
- Can occur in any age group [p.251]
- Underlying causes of immune suppression must be excluded clinically [p.251]
- Expression of multiple pan-B-cell markers in >50% of tumor cells and extranodal presentation help differentiate from EBV-positive classic Hodgkin lymphoma (CHL) [p.251]

**Primary Effusion-Based DLBCL** [p.251-252]
- DLBCL presenting as isolated body-cavity effusions without solid masses
- Occurs more often (but not exclusively) in patients with underlying fluid-overload states [p.251]
- New categories introduced: HHV8-negative, EBV-negative, primary effusion-based lymphoma (ICC) and fluid overload-associated LBCL (WHO5) [p.251]
- Both require HHV8-negativity for inclusion [p.251]
- Optimal treatment uncertain; some patients demonstrate indolent disease course even without cytotoxic therapy [p.251]

**Other DLBCL Variants** [p.251]
- Intravascular LBCL
- DLBCL associated with chronic inflammation
- Fibrin-associated DLBCL
- T-cell/histiocyte-rich LBCL

All of these variants are managed according to DLBCL algorithms [p.251]

## Diagnostic and Staging Approach

### Literature Search Methodology [p.251-252]

Prior to the update of the NCCN Guidelines v3.2025 for B-Cell Lymphomas, an electronic search of the PubMed database was performed to identify key literature in DLBCL published since the previous Guidelines update [p.252]

**Search Terms Used** [p.252]
- Diffuse large B-cell lymphoma
- Aggressive B-cell lymphoma
- Primary mediastinal B-cell lymphoma
- Gray zone lymphoma
- HGBL [p.252]

**Search Strategy** [p.252]
- Database: PubMed (most widely used resource for medical literature indexing peer-reviewed biomedical literature)
- Language: English
- Study population: Humans
- Article types: Clinical Trial (Phase II, III, IV), Guideline, Randomized Controlled Trial, Meta-Analysis, Systematic Reviews, Validation Studies [p.252]

**Data Inclusion** [p.252]
- Key PubMed articles deemed relevant to NCCN Guidelines were included
- Data from e-publications ahead of print and meeting abstracts were considered [p.252]
- Recommendations lacking high-level evidence are based on Panel review of lower-level evidence and expert opinion [p.252]

## Special Considerations

### Classification and Management Framework [p.251-252]

The NCCN Guidelines utilize current international classifications (ICC and WHO5) for DLBCL diagnosis and management. All DLBCL variants listed above are integrated into comprehensive DLBCL treatment algorithms, ensuring consistent evidence-based approach across disease subtypes [p.251]

## Summary of Key Evidence

- Lisocabtagene maraleucel achieved 73% ORR (53% CR) in the TRASCEND NHL 001 study among 256 patients with relapsed/refractory DLBCL [p.262], with significantly improved CR rates, EFS, and PFS compared to second-line therapy plus HDT/ASCR in the phase 3 TRANSFORM study [p.262]
- Tisagenlecleucel demonstrated 53% ORR (39% CR) in the JULIET phase II study of 115 patients with relapsed/refractory DLBCL [p.263], though the BELINDA trial showed it was not superior to standard second-line therapy plus HDT/ASCR [p.263]
- CD3 x CD20 bispecific antibodies (epcoritamab and glofitamab) are FDA-approved for relapsed/refractory DLBCL after ≥2 prior systemic therapies [p.263], with glofitamab monotherapy achieving 52% ORR (39% CR) in heavily pretreated patients [p.263]
- Second-line chemoimmunotherapy regimens (R-ICE, R-DHAP, GDP) followed by HDT/ASCR remain standard for chemosensitive disease, though patients with MYC gene rearrangement have poor outcomes regardless of regimen [p.267]
- CAR T-cell therapy and bispecific antibodies are category 1 recommendations for primary refractory disease or relapsed disease <12 months after completion of first-line therapy [p.267]
- Cytokine release syndrome (CRS) and immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS) are the most common serious adverse events with CAR T-cell therapy, managed with tocilizumab and corticosteroids [p.269-270]

## Second-Line and Subsequent Therapy Evidence

### Lisocabtagene Maraleucel

The TRASCEND NHL 001 study evaluated lisocabtagene maraleucel in 344 patients with relapsed/refractory lymphomas, with 256 patients evaluable for efficacy [p.262]. The trial included patients with DLBCL, HGBL with MYC and/or BCL2/BCL6 rearrangements, DLBCL transformed from indolent lymphomas, PMBL, and FL grade 3B [p.262]. Lisocabtagene maraleucel resulted in an ORR of 73% (53% CR), with similar response rates across all subgroups [p.262]. With a median follow-up of 19 months, 1-year PFS and OS rates were 44% and 58%, respectively [p.262].

The PILOT study evaluated lisocabtagene maraleucel in 61 patients with relapsed/refractory DLBCL who were not candidates for HDT/ASCR, including 33 with refractory disease, 13 with relapse <12 months of first-line therapy, and 15 with relapse >12 months [p.262]. The ORR was 80% (54% CR) [p.262]. At a median follow-up of 16 months, median PFS was 23 months and median OS was not reached in patients achieving CR [p.262]. Final analysis with median follow-up of 23 months confirmed durability: median DOR was 23 months overall and not reached for CR patients; median PFS was 9 months and median OS was not reached [p.262].

The TRANSFORM study was a phase 3 randomized trial comparing lisocabtagene maraleucel to second-line chemoimmunotherapy followed by consolidation HDT/ASCR in 184 patients with primary refractory disease or early relapse (≤12 months after first-line chemoimmunotherapy) [p.262]. At median follow-up of 18 months, median EFS was not reached for lisocabtagene maraleucel versus 2 months for HDT/ASCR group [p.262]. CR rates were significantly higher with lisocabtagene maraleucel (74% vs. 43%; P < .0001) [p.262], and median PFS was not reached versus 6 months (P < .0001) [p.262]. Median OS was not reached for lisocabtagene maraleucel and 30 months for HDT/ASCR; after adjusting for crossover, estimated 18-month OS rates were 73% and 54%, respectively [p.262].

Lisocabtagene maraleucel is a category 1 option for eligible patients with primary refractory disease or relapsed disease <12 months [p.267]. For patients not eligible for transplant but eligible for CAR T-cell therapy, lisocabtagene maraleucel is recommended [p.268].

### Tisagenlecleucel

The JULIET multicenter phase II study evaluated tisagenlecleucel in 115 patients with relapsed/refractory DLBCL (after ≥2 prior lines including rituximab and anthracycline) and transformed FL [p.263]. Patients with prior CD19-directed CAR T-cell therapy, allogeneic HCT, PMBL, or active CNS involvement were excluded [p.263]. After median follow-up of 40 months, best ORR was 53% (39% CR), with median PFS of 3 months and median OS of 11 months [p.263]. Among patients achieving CR at 3-6 months, median PFS and OS were not reached [p.263].

The BELINDA trial showed tisagenlecleucel was not superior to second-line therapy followed by HDT/ASCR in a similar patient population [p.263].

Tisagenlecleucel is an appropriate option for patients achieving PR following second-line therapy [p.268], and is category 1 for eligible patients with primary refractory disease or relapsed disease <12 months [p.267].

### Bispecific Antibodies (CD3 x CD20)

CD3 x CD20 bispecific antibodies have shown promise for heavily pretreated DLBCL, including disease relapse after CAR T-cell therapy [p.263]. Epcoritamab and glofitamab are FDA-approved for relapsed/refractory DLBCL after ≥2 systemic therapy regimens [p.263]. Clinical trial results suggest addition of bispecific antibodies to chemoimmunotherapy improves outcomes in relapsed/refractory DLBCL after ≥1 systemic therapy, including primary refractory disease [p.263].

#### Glofitamab

Glofitamab was evaluated in a phase II study as intravenous fixed-duration treatment for 12 cycles in 154 patients with relapsed/refractory DLBCL (including transformed FL, HGBL, or PMBL) after ≥2 lines of therapy, prior HDT/ASCR failure, or CAR T-cell therapy [p.263]. All patients received pretreatment with one dose of obinutuzumab to mitigate CRS [p.263]. At median follow-up of 13 months, 52% had objective response (39% CR) by independent review committee [p.263]. Median PFS was 5 months, with estimated 12-month PFS and OS rates of 37% and 50%, respectively [p.263].

GemOx plus glofitamab is a preferred second-line therapy option for relapsed disease >12 months and is category 1 for this indication [p.268]. GemOx with or without rituximab is appropriate for patients unable to receive epcoritamab or glofitamab [p.268].

### Mosunetuzumab and Polatuzumab Vedotin

Mosunetuzumab plus polatuzumab vedotin is included as a preferred second-line therapy option for relapsed disease >12 months in patients not candidates for transplant, or for relapsed disease <12 months or primary refractory disease in patients not candidates for CAR T-cell therapy [p.268].

### Second-Line Chemoimmunotherapy Regimens for Intention to Proceed to Transplant

R-ICE (rituximab, ifosfamide, carboplatin, etoposide), R-DHA/R-DHAP (rituximab, dexamethasone, and cytarabine plus platinum), or R-GDP (rituximab, gemcitabine, dexamethasone, carboplatin, cisplatin, or oxaliplatin) are effective second-line regimens for transplant candidates [p.267].

The CORAL study evaluated 469 patients with chemosensitive relapsed/refractory DLBCL treated with second-line chemoimmunotherapy (R-ICE vs. R-DHAP) followed by ASCR [p.267]. No significant difference in outcome was found between arms [p.267]. ORRs were 63% after R-ICE and 64% after R-DHAP [p.267]. After median follow-up of 44 months, 4-year EFS was 26% with R-ICE versus 34% with R-DHAP (P = .20), and 4-year OS was 43% and 51%, respectively (P = .30) [p.267]. Notably, patients relapsing <1 year after initial R-CHOP had poor outcome with 3-year PFS of 23% [p.267]. Rituximab maintenance (every 2 months for 1 year) post-ASCR showed no benefit versus observation [p.267].

Bio-CORAL subgroup analysis showed for GCB phenotype patients, R-DHAP resulted in improved PFS (3-year PFS 52% vs. 31% with R-ICE); this difference was not observed in non-GCB phenotype (3-year PFS 32% with R-DHAP vs. 27% with R-ICE) [p.267]. Patients with MYC gene rearrangement had poor outcomes regardless of arm: 4-year PFS was 18% with MYC rearrangement versus 42% without (P = .032); 4-year OS was 29% and 62%, respectively (P = .011) [p.267]. Among MYC-rearranged patients, 4-year PFS was 17% with R-DHAP and 19% with R-ICE; OS was 26% and 31%, respectively [p.267].

A randomized study of 619 patients with relapsed/refractory aggressive lymphoma (419 with DLBCL) compared GDP (gemcitabine, dexamethasone, cisplatin) to DHAP [p.267]. GDP was non-inferior to DHAP in ORR (45% vs. 44%) and transplantation rate (52% vs. 49) [p.267]. GDP was associated with fewer grade 3-4 toxicities (P < .001) [p.267]. After median follow-up of 53 months, no differences in EFS (P = .95) or OS (P = .78) were detected [p.267].

Second-line combination chemotherapy is recommended for transplant candidates, with rituximab included if relapse occurs after >6 months remission with continued CD20 expression on biopsy; rituximab may be omitted in primary refractory disease [p.267]. Consolidation with HDT/ASCR (category 1 for CR) with or without RT is recommended for CR or PR to second-line therapy [p.267]. ISRT before HDT/ASCR improves local disease control and outcomes [p.267]. Additional RT may be given to limited sites with prior positive disease before or after HDT/ASCR [p.267].

CAR T-cell therapy (axicabtagene ciloleucel, tisagenlecleucel, or lisocabtagene maraleucel) is an appropriate option for patients achieving PR following second-line therapy [p.268]. Allogeneic HCT should be considered in selected patients with mobilization failures and persistent bone marrow involvement or inadequate second-line response, with patients in CR or near CR at transplant time [p.268].

### Second-Line Therapy for Chemotherapy-Ineligible or CAR T-Ineligible Patients

For relapsed disease <12 months, primary refractory disease in non-CAR T candidates, or relapsed disease >12 months with no intention to proceed to transplant, optimal treatment is not established and clinical trial is preferred [p.268].

GemOx plus glofitamab or epcoritamab is included (category 1 for GemOx + glofitamab in relapsed disease >12 months) [p.268]. Mosunetuzumab plus polatuzumab vedotin is included as preferred second-line option [p.268]. Tafasitamab plus lenalidomide is included as preferred treatment for relapsed/refractory DLBCL, excluding primary refractory disease [p.268]. The L-MIND trial showed 30-month PFS was 34% in primary refractory disease versus 42% in non-primary refractory [p.268].

GemOx with or without rituximab is appropriate for patients unable to receive epcoritamab or glofitamab [p.268]. Lenalidomide (with or without rituximab) and ibrutinib are appropriate options, particularly for non-GCB DLBCL [p.268].

In patients with relapsed disease <12 months or primary refractory disease without access to CAR T or bispecific antibodies, optional consolidation with HDT/ASCR may be considered if CR achieved with second-line chemoimmunotherapy [p.268].

### Third-Line and Subsequent Therapy

Patients with PR to second-line therapy, progressive disease to second-line therapy (regardless of transplant eligibility), or disease relapse following HDT/ASCR or allogeneic HCT should receive third-line systemic therapy, palliative ISRT, or best supportive care [p.268]. Repeat biopsy should be strongly considered before additional therapy in patients with FDG-PET-positive PR to second-line therapy, as PET positivity may represent post-treatment inflammation [p.268].

Second-line systemic therapy options not previously used may be considered for relapsed/refractory disease following second-line therapy [p.268-269]. Patients with progressive disease after ≥2 prior systemic therapy lines are unlikely to benefit from currently available systemic therapy except those with long disease-free interval [p.268-269]. Novel chemotherapy-free regimens should be considered for progressive disease after ≥2 prior lines [p.269].

CAR T-cell therapy (if not previously used) is an option for disease relapse after CR to second-line therapy or progressive disease on second-line therapy [p.269].

#### Loncastuximab Tesirine and Selinexor

Loncastuximab tesirine and selinexor are included as third-line or subsequent therapy options for relapsed/refractory DLBCL [p.269].

Loss of CD19 and CD20 tumor antigen expressions have been reported in relapsed/refractory lymphomas and may represent a mechanism of resistance to mAb therapy including bispecific antibodies [p.269]. In the LOTIS-2 trial, 10% of patients achieved 47% ORR (40% CR) to subsequent CD19-directed CAR T-cell therapy after loncastuximab tesirine, and another small study showed CD19 antigen loss after loncastuximab tesirine is not common [p.269]. These findings suggest loncastuximab tesirine does not preclude subsequent CD19-directed CAR T-cell responses, though results need confirmation in larger cohorts and unclear if loncastuximab tesirine would negatively impact efficacy of subsequent CD19-directed CAR T [p.269].

Selinexor was evaluated in the SADAL multicenter randomized phase IIb study in 267 patients with heavily pretreated relapsed/refractory DLBCL (2-5 prior therapies with progression after autologous HCT or HCT-ineligible) [p.266]. Patients were assigned to 60-mg (n = 175) or 100-mg (n = 92) groups; the 100-mg group was discontinued due to improved therapeutic window with 60 mg [p.266]. In the 60-mg group (127 patients) with median follow-up of 11 months, ORR was 29% (12% CR, 17% PR), and median OS was 9 months (not reached for PR or better; 18 months for stable disease) [p.266]. In GCB DLBCL, ORR was 34% (14% CR); in non-GCB DLBCL, ORR was 21% (10% CR) [p.266]. Grade 3-4 adverse events included thrombocytopenia (46%), neutropenia (25%), anemia (22%), fatigue (11%), hyponatremia (8%), and nausea (6%) [p.266].

#### Post-CAR T and Post-Transplant Options

Despite novel treatment availability, relapse occurs in ~60% of patients following CAR T-cell therapy and treatment options are very limited [p.269]. Epcoritamab and glofitamab are recommended as third-line therapy options for relapsed/refractory DLBCL including those with disease progression after transplant or CAR T-cell therapy [p.269]. Selinexor is also appropriate as third-line and subsequent therapy for disease progression after transplant or CAR T-cell therapy [p.269]. BV, lenalidomide, and rituximab is included as an option for third-line therapy and would be appropriate for relapsed/refractory DLBCL patients not candidates for or without access to CAR T or bispecific antibodies [p.269].

## Special Considerations

### CD19/CD20 Antigen Loss

Activity of CD3 x CD20 bispecific antibodies in CD20-negative lymphomas is unclear; rebiopsy to confirm CD20 positivity is recommended prior to initiating CD3 x CD20 bispecific antibody therapy [p.268]. Similarly, rebiopsy to confirm CD19 and CD20 positivity is recommended before CD19-directed CAR T-cell or CD3 x CD20 bispecific antibody therapy [p.269].

### Primary Refractory Disease and Early Relapse

ZUMA-7 and TRANSFORM trials confirmed axicabtagene ciloleucel and lisocabtagene maraleucel are effective for primary disease or relapsed disease <12 months, included as category 1 options for eligible patients [p.267]. Bridging therapy (as needed) is recommended until CAR T product is available; if bridging results in CR or very good PR, proceeding with HDT/ASCR is an appropriate alternative [p.267]. ISRT can be used as bridging monotherapy or sequentially with systemic therapy [p.267].

Prior to CAR T-cell therapy approval, retrospective studies reported extremely poor outcomes to conventional second-line therapies for primary refractory disease or relapse <12 months after first-line therapy [p.266].

Bendamustine should be avoided (prior to leukapheresis) in patients potentially eligible for CAR T-cell therapy [p.268].

### CAR T-Cell Therapy Toxicity Management

Cytokine release syndrome (CRS) and immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS, previously CRES) are the most common serious adverse events of special interest (AESI) with CAR T-cell therapy [p.269]. CD19-directed CAR T-cell therapies should only be dispensed and administered in health care facilities enrolled in and complying with REMS requirements [p.269].

#### CRS and ICANS Incidence by Trial

In ZUMA-1, CRS and neurologic toxicity of any grade occurred in 93% (grade ≥3: 11%) and 64% (grade ≥3: 30%) of patients, respectively [p.269-270]. Median time from infusion to symptom onset was 2 days and 5 days, with median duration of 8 days and 17 days [p.270]. Pyrexia (11%), hypoxia (9%), and hypotension (9%) were most common grade ≥3 CRS symptoms [p.270]. Encephalopathy (21%), confusional state (9%), aphasia (7%), and somnolence (7%) were most common grade ≥3 neurologic events [p.270]. Median CRS onset was 2 days post-infusion with 8-day median resolution [p.270].

In ZUMA-7, among patients randomized to axicabtagene ciloleucel, grade ≥3 CRS and neurologic events were reported in 6% and 21% of patients, respectively [p.270].

In TRASCEND NHL 001, CRS and neurologic toxicity of any grade occurred in 42% and 30% of patients, respectively [p.270]. Grade ≥3 CRS and neurologic events occurred in 2% and 10%, respectively [p.270].

In TRANSFORM, lisocabtagene maraleucel was associated with lower rates: grade ≥3 CRS and neurologic events in 1% and 4%, respectively [p.270].

In JULIET, CRS and neurologic toxicity of any grade occurred in 57% and 20% of patients, respectively [p.270]. Grade ≥3 CRS and neurologic events occurred in 23% and 11% [p.270]. Median time to symptom onset was 2 days and 6 days, with median duration of 7 days and 14 days, respectively [p.270].

#### CRS/ICANS Management

Tocilizumab (anti-IL-6 receptor mAb) is highly effective for CRS management, inducing rapid symptom reversal in most patients [p.270]. Tocilizumab is approved for treatment of CRS after CAR T-cell therapy and does not impact CAR T efficacy in response rates or DOR [p.270]. Corticosteroids are important adjunctive therapy with tocilizumab [p.270]. Corticosteroids are preferred for neurologic toxicity management if not associated with CRS; tocilizumab plus corticosteroids is recommended for CRS occurring with neurologic toxicity [p.270]. Anakinra (IL-1 receptor antagonist) has shown promising results in CRS and steroid-refractory ICANS management [p.270].

Corticosteroids for CAR T toxicity management did not impact CAR T efficacy, though shown to impair T-cell function [p.270]. CRS and neurologic toxicity should be managed based on toxicity grade per the NCCN Guidelines for Immunotherapy-Related Toxicities [p.270].

In addition to CRS and ICANS, severe and prolonged cytopenias are very common grade 3-4 adverse events with CAR T-cell therapy, possibly associated with bridging chemotherapy, lymphodepleting chemotherapy, or immune-mediated CAR effects [p.270]. Rapidly progressing disease and lack of access to certified CAR T-cell therapy centers limit CAR T use in many patients [p.270].

### Bispecific Antibody Toxicity

CRS (mostly grade 1-2) was the most common AESI with all three bispecific antibodies; ICANS were less common [p.270]. CRS and ICANS were graded using ASTCT criteria [p.270].

In EPCORE NHL-1, CRS (any grade) was reported in 50% of patients (grade ≥3: 3%) [p.270]. CRS occurred after first full dose of epcoritamab; prophylactic high-dose steroids were given during initial 4 weeks of step-up dosing [p.270]. Tocilizumab was used for CRS management in 28% of patients [p.270]. ICANS (any grade) was reported in 6% (grade ≥3: 3%) [p.270]. Pyrexia was reported in 24% [p.270].
